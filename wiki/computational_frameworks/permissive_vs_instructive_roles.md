## Key evidence

- Transient inactivations of brain areas can disrupt behavior by perturbing downstream circuit dynamics even when the targeted area is dispensable for the behavior, as demonstrated by motor cortex inactivation disrupting lever-pressing despite permanent lesions leaving the skill intact ([Otchy2015](../../raw/summaries/otchy2015_neural_circuit_motor.md)).

- The distinction between permissive (acutely required for expression but not providing essential computation) and instructive (providing non-redundant information or computation) circuit roles provides an operationally useful framework for interpreting neural manipulation studies ([Otchy2015](../../raw/summaries/otchy2015_neural_circuit_motor.md)).

- Recovery from transient inactivation deficits can occur via homeostatic regulation rather than experience-dependent learning, as shown by HVC dynamics recovering over 2-3 days without practice, consistent with synaptic homeostasis hypothesis of sleep ([Otchy2015](../../raw/summaries/otchy2015_neural_circuit_motor.md)).

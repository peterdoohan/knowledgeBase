# Hippocampal-Cortical Interaction

## Current understanding

Hippocampal-cortical interactions during decision-making involve coherent coding of spatial position across hippocampal and prefrontal populations on a theta-cycle timescale. Prefrontal cortex population activity encodes spatial position coherently with hippocampal CA1 populations, and theta phase-associated spiking significantly improves spatial decoding accuracy in both regions. This suggests that theta oscillations coordinate not just temporal synchrony but also content-specific information transfer between these memory-critical regions. The interaction supports the constructive episodic simulation hypothesis, where hippocampal mechanisms support imagination and exploration of possibilities beyond immediate experience.

## Key evidence

- Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples (SWRs) reactivate memory sequences that serve as choice options during deliberative decision making, with the prefrontal cortex evaluating these options against task rules and previous experience ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

- SWR interruption during learning impairs performance on outbound decisions (which require memory of the previous trial) but not inbound decisions (which follow a simple place-response rule), demonstrating that SWRs specifically support deliberation requiring memory retrieval ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

- Replayed trajectories during SWRs include both chosen and unchosen paths, suggesting they provide potential choice options rather than simply predicting the animal's future path ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

- Replay can include trajectories from remote or even novel environments, suggesting hippocampal replay supports imagination and flexible exploration of possibilities beyond immediate experience ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

- Theta oscillations during exploration show increased hippocampal-PFC coherence at choice points, particularly after learning, and may support execution of well-learned rules rather than deliberation per se ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

- Non-local place cell activity during theta at choice points (during vicarious trial and error) may represent another mechanism for exploring future trajectories, but its influence on PFC remains unclear ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

- The PFC receives hippocampal input during SWRs and could maintain working memory representations of previous choices and task rules to evaluate replayed options ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

- Prefrontal cortex population activity encodes spatial position coherently with hippocampal CA1 populations on a theta-cycle timescale, and theta phase-associated spiking significantly improves spatial decoding accuracy in both regions ([Zielinski 2019](../../raw/summaries/zielinski2019_theta_hippocampus_prefrontal.md))

- Both CA1 and PFC population activity encoded the animal's current spatial position on a theta-cycle timescale during memory-guided behavior, with CA1 showing significantly higher spatial specificity (median spatial specificity: CA1 = 0.10, PFC = 0.37; p < 1e-99) ([Zielinski 2019](../../raw/summaries/zielinski2019_theta_hippocampus_prefrontal.md))

- CA1 and PFC position representations were coherent, with correlated decoding errors within theta cycles (r = 0.10, p = 0.001; significantly higher than shuffled controls, p = 0.01), suggesting shared processing of spatial information ([Zielinski 2019](../../raw/summaries/zielinski2019_theta_hippocampus_prefrontal.md))

- Incorporating theta phase information significantly improved spatial decoding accuracy in both CA1 (~2-fold improvement with 7 phase bins, p < 0.02) and PFC (~4-fold improvement with 7 phase bins, p < 0.0013) ([Zielinski 2019](../../raw/summaries/zielinski2019_theta_hippocampus_prefrontal.md))

- Theta phase-associated spiking in PFC (phase-locking) rather than phase precession underlies improved spatial coding, unlike CA1 where phase precession dominates; 83.5% of CA1 neurons and 51.4% of PFC neurons were significantly phase-locked to hippocampal theta oscillations ([Zielinski 2019](../../raw/summaries/zielinski2019_theta_hippocampus_prefrontal.md))

## History of ideas

Prior to this work, the field understood that hippocampal-prefrontal interactions are necessary for memory-guided behavior, supported by both anatomical connectivity and inactivation studies. Theta oscillations (~8 Hz) were known to mediate physiological coupling between hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, manifesting as oscillatory coherence and phase-locked prefrontal spiking. Hippocampal CA1 place cells were known to encode spatial position via both rate coding and theta phase precession (temporal coding), with phase precession significantly improving spatial decoding accuracy. Prefrontal neurons were known to exhibit spatial selectivity and prominent phase-locking to hippocampal theta, but whether theta phase similarly modulated prefrontal spatial representations was unknown. Whether hippocampal and prefrontal populations coherently encode spatial position on a theta-cycle timescale was unexplored. This work established coherent spatial coding across hippocampal-prefrontal networks and demonstrated that theta phase serves as a temporal coordination mechanism for spatial coding across both regions.

## Open questions

- Whether ventral hippocampus (which projects directly to PFC) plays a specific role in the observed coherent coding
- The specific anatomical pathways supporting coherent spatial coding (direct ventral CA1→PFC projections vs. indirect routes via nucleus reuniens)
- The directionality of information flow during coherent coding (CA1→PFC vs. PFC→CA1 vs. bidirectional)
- Whether phase-locking in PFC improves spatial coding through enhanced signal-to-noise or through specific circuit mechanisms
- Whether coherence varies with task demands or performance
- Whether similar coherent coding extends to other cortical areas and generalizes beyond spatial representations

## Key evidence

- The striped field mouse (*Apodemus agrarius*) exhibits efficient, stereotyped hunting behavior toward insects that is comparable to specialized insectivores and distinct from generalist predators, as quantified using a novel data compression method applied to behavioral sequences ([Reznikova2017](../../raw/summaries/reznikova2017_hunting_behavior_mouse.md)).

- Striped field mice displayed hunting efficiency (0.36 ants/minute) rivaling specialized predators, with 65% success rate in complete hunting stereotypes compared to 52% in rats and 100% in shrews ([Reznikova2017](../../raw/summaries/reznikova2017_hunting_behavior_mouse.md)).

- Mice had 86% key behavioral elements vs 66.7% in rats, with strong negative correlation between compression ratio and key elements (r=-0.693, P<0.01), indicating efficient hunting relies on streamlined action sequences rather than behavioral flexibility ([Reznikova2017](../../raw/summaries/reznikova2017_hunting_behavior_mouse.md)).

- Data compression ratios can quantify behavioral complexity and distinguish hunting strategies across species, with mice (0.596) and shrews (0.59) showing significantly lower compression ratios (more stereotyped behavior) than rats (0.631) ([Reznikova2017](../../raw/summaries/reznikova2017_hunting_behavior_mouse.md)).

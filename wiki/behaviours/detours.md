## Key evidence

- The prefrontal cortex, not the hippocampus, shows consistent increased activity during forced detours in navigation, with all 9 neuroimaging studies reviewed reporting increased PFC activity during detour conditions ([Spiers 2015](../../raw/summaries/spiers2015_detour_problem_navigation.md))

- Frontopolar PFC (BA 10) is the most consistently activated region during detours and supports subgoal generation and goal-subgoal integration during route replanning ([Spiers 2015](../../raw/summaries/spiers2015_detour_problem_navigation.md))

- Lateral PFC detects model-based prediction errors when expected routes are blocked and initiates route replanning; activated in response to unexpected environmental changes requiring route adjustment ([Spiers 2015](../../raw/summaries/spiers2015_detour_problem_navigation.md))

- Superior PFC (BA 6) resolves conflict between alternative route options during detour planning; active during conflict between alternative route options ([Spiers 2015](../../raw/summaries/spiers2015_detour_problem_navigation.md))

- The hippocampus tracks path distance to goals rather than detecting detours per se; right posterior hippocampal activity correlates with magnitude of change in path distance to goal at detours ([Spiers 2015](../../raw/summaries/spiers2015_detour_problem_navigation.md))

- Dorsolateral PFC and ventrolateral PFC support inhibition and replanning during detours and environmental changes; dlPFC shows increased activity during active route choice compared to guided navigation ([Patai 2021](../../raw/summaries/patai2021_versatile_wayfinder_prefrontal.md))

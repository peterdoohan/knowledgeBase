## Key evidence

- A belief-instability parameter (κ₁) derived from attractor network principles best accounts for schizophrenia's nonlinear belief-updating pattern, outperforming simple learning-rate and disconfirmatory-bias models across two independent datasets. ([Adams 2018](../../raw/summaries/adams2018_attractor_schizophrenia.md))
- Belief instability (κ₁) and response stochasticity (ζ) are co-elevated in schizophrenia and negatively correlated across subjects, consistent with a shared neurobiological origin in unstable attractor dynamics. ([Adams 2018](../../raw/summaries/adams2018_attractor_schizophrenia.md))
- Nonpsychotic mood disorder patients showed the same elevated belief instability and response stochasticity as schizophrenia patients when acutely unwell, but these parameters normalised after recovery, contrasting with the persistent trait-like differences in schizophrenia. ([Adams 2018](../../raw/summaries/adams2018_attractor_schizophrenia.md))
- Group differences in belief instability and response stochasticity persisted after controlling for IQ and working memory, suggesting these computational abnormalities are not secondary to general cognitive impairment. ([Adams 2018](../../raw/summaries/adams2018_attractor_schizophrenia.md))

- Delusions emerge from conditional dependencies among multiple parameters in active inference, with no single parameter being both necessary and sufficient; reduced likelihood precision, policy precision, habit strength, and affective state interact to produce self-reinforcing attractor states. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Reduced likelihood precision is the strongest individual determinant of delusion formation (ρ = −0.64) but acts as a necessary rather than sufficient condition; it requires interaction with habits and/or affect to drive the system into delusional attractor states. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Simulated antipsychotic treatment (reducing policy precision after 10 false inferences) reduced delusional agents from 31 to 5 out of 972, without directly changing habits; the therapeutic effect arises through recurrent updating among policy precision, policies, habits, and beliefs, breaking the self-reinforcing attractor. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Increased self-inhibition of superficial pyramidal cells—reflecting reduced synaptic gain—is the consistent circuit abnormality across resting-state EEG, MMN, 40-Hz ASSR, and rsfMRI in schizophrenia. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams2021_pyramidal_synaptic_gain.md))
- Pyramidal cell synaptic gain loss is a primary deficit in schizophrenia, whereas interneuron pathology is likely a secondary compensatory response rather than a primary cause. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams2021_pyramidal_synaptic_gain.md))
- Psychotic symptoms in schizophrenia—including positive symptoms and auditory perceptual abnormalities—correlate with cortical disinhibition (decreased self-inhibition), opposing the primary group-level hyperinhibition pattern. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams2021_pyramidal_synaptic_gain.md))
- A single computational mechanism—reduced pyramidal synaptic gain—can explain diverse electrophysiological biomarkers in schizophrenia including reduced MMN, reduced 40-Hz ASSR, and resting-state EEG spectral abnormalities. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams2021_pyramidal_synaptic_gain.md))
- Compensatory interneuron downregulation (disinhibition) may destabilise cortical circuit dynamics, creating a narrow therapeutic window for glutamatergic treatments that restore pyramidal gain without addressing the compensatory rebalancing. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams2021_pyramidal_synaptic_gain.md))
- First-degree relatives of people with schizophrenia show intermediate or similar patterns of increased pyramidal self-inhibition, but results are inconsistent across paradigms and confounded by age in rsfMRI. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams2021_pyramidal_synaptic_gain.md))

- A quantitative meta-analysis of post-mortem studies finds a small but significant reduction in parvalbumin interneuron density in the pre-frontal cortex of patients with schizophrenia (Hedges' g = −0.27; z = −2.17; p = 0.03), while the parallel reduction in parvalbumin mRNA does not reach significance (g = −0.44; p = 0.12). ([Kaar 2019](../../raw/summaries/kaar2019_parvalbumin_schizophrenia.md))

- The effect size for parvalbumin cell density reduction in schizophrenia is fragile: significance depends on two high-influence studies (Hashimoto et al. 2003; Bitanihirwe and Woo 2014); removing either renders the result non-significant, and the mRNA meta-analysis is underpowered (only 4 studies) and highly heterogeneous (I² = 79.56%). ([Kaar 2019](../../raw/summaries/kaar2019_parvalbumin_schizophrenia.md))

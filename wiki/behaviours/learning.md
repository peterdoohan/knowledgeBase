# Learning

## Current understanding

Learning is the process by which the brain acquires, updates, and stores information through experience. In the context of spatial navigation and memory, learning involves the modification of neural representations across multiple brain regions, with distinct learning stages characterized by different patterns of neural activity and behavioral dependence.

---

## Key evidence

- RSC spatial representations develop progressively across learning stages, with position coding improving early, trajectory coding emerging mid-learning, and selective future-goal coding appearing only at asymptotic performance. Bayesian decoding accuracy reached ~40% within 4.5 cm at asymptote; spatial coding errors decreased by 64% from early to asymptotic performance ([Miller 2019](../../raw/summaries/miller2019_retrosplenial_spatial_learning.md))

- RSC lesions have no effect during early learning but selectively impair asymptotic performance, with lesion size strongly correlated with impairment magnitude (interaction F(6,90) = 2.832, p < 0.05; r = -0.90, p < 0.01) ([Miller 2019](../../raw/summaries/miller2019_retrosplenial_spatial_learning.md))

- ConSink positions redistribute from the original goal to a new goal after goal switching, with cells moving their ConSinks significantly toward the new goal (Wilcoxon signed-rank, z = -2.48, p = 0.013); ConSinks move progressively closer to the goal with continued training ([Ormond 2022](../../raw/summaries/ormond2022_goal_oriented_vector_fields.md))

## History of ideas

The study of learning in the brain has evolved from behaviorist theories emphasizing stimulus-response associations to cognitive theories emphasizing internal representations. In spatial navigation, early work by Tolman (1948) proposed cognitive maps as internal representations of space. The discovery of place cells by O'Keefe (1970s) and grid cells by Hafting et al. (2005) provided neural substrates for these maps. Recent work has emphasized that these representations are not static but dynamically modified by learning, with distinct brain regions contributing at different learning stages.

---

## Open questions

- What are the specific circuit mechanisms driving learning-related changes in spatial representations across different brain regions?
- How do different learning stages map onto distinct patterns of neural activity and synaptic plasticity?
- What is the relationship between representational dynamics and behavioral performance during learning?

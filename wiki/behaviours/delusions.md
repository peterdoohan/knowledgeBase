## Key evidence

- Delusions — false, certain, and incorrigible beliefs — emerge from conditional dependencies among multiple parameters in active inference, with no single parameter being both necessary and sufficient; reduced likelihood precision, policy precision, habit strength, and affective state interact to produce self-reinforcing attractor states. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Reduced likelihood precision is the strongest individual determinant of delusion formation (ρ = −0.64) but acts as a necessary rather than sufficient condition; it requires interaction with habits and/or affect to drive the system into delusional attractor states. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Adding affective states to the active inference model tripled the delusion rate compared to non-affective models (from 0% to 1.3% in consistent trustworthiness; from 1.0% to 3.2% in changing trustworthiness), demonstrating that affect is a critical enabling factor for delusional belief formation. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Mood primarily determines the direction (theme) of delusions — whether they are distrusting (paranoid) or trusting (gullible) — rather than their frequency or certainty; this domain-general affective bias becomes domain-specific through coupling to social inference policies. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Simulated antipsychotic treatment (reducing policy precision after 10 false inferences) reduced delusional agents from 31 to 5 out of 972, without directly changing habits; the therapeutic effect arises through recurrent updating among policy precision, policies, habits, and beliefs, breaking the self-reinforcing attractor. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- State-space analysis reveals three agent trajectories: those forming habits that can be revised (low false inferences), those forming habits that partially fail to revise (intermediate), and those forming very strong habits with increasing policy precision that create self-reinforcing attractor states (high false inferences). ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Domain-general likelihood imprecision produces domain-specific (social) delusions when affect is coupled to social inference policies; the same precision reduction would not produce delusions in non-social domains where different policies apply. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

## Open questions

- Why do delusions resist "second-factor" correction from memory or reasoning systems (cf. two-factor theories of delusions)?
- How do Jaspers's "ununderstandable" delusions — those involving abrupt, experience-near alterations in semantic meaning — fit within active inference frameworks?
- Can the predicted conditional dependencies between inference about states, affects, policies, and delusion scores be tested in longitudinal experimental designs?
- Do D2 receptor antagonists reduce delusions specifically via policy precision reduction (as the model predicts) rather than via aberrant salience (Kapur, 2003)?

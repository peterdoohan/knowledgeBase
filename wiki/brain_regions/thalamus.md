# Thalamus

## Current understanding

The thalamus is not merely a relay station but actively drives functionally distinct subnetworks within frontal cortex during decision-making. Thalamic inputs to anterior lateral motor cortex (ALM) have stronger impact on task selectivity than cortico-cortical inputs, suggesting thalamocortical loops are the primary drivers of frontal cortical computation during behavior. Thalamic inputs provide both tonic (sustained excitability) and phasic (transient UP-state) influences on cortical networks, with the phasic component coordinating timing and content of hippocampal sharp-wave ripples. The thalamus is required for maintaining stimulus information, choice selectivity, and action-related activity in frontal cortex, suggesting the thalamus drives subnetworks within frontal cortex coding distinct decision features.

## Key evidence

- Thalamic inputs to ALM have higher connection probability and elicit stronger EPSPs than cortico-cortical inputs from S1/S2 or contralateral ALM; while all inputs contact all functional populations (stimulus, choice, action coding), thalamic inputs have the strongest impact on task selectivity ([Yang 2022](../../raw/summaries/yang2022_thalamus_frontal_decision.md))

- Photoinhibition of thalamus (ThalALM) has stronger effects on ALM activity modes than photoinhibition of S1/S2 or cALM: thalamic inputs are required for stimulus selectivity, choice selectivity, and action mode; thalamic photoinhibition abolishes task selectivity even in neurons with unchanged spike rates ([Yang 2022](../../raw/summaries/yang2022_thalamus_frontal_decision.md))

- Stimulus information in ALM persists after S1/S2 photoinhibition but is abolished by thalamic photoinhibition, suggesting redundant subcortical pathways signal stimulus information via thalamus rather than through direct cortico-cortical transmission ([Yang 2022](../../raw/summaries/yang2022_thalamus_frontal_decision.md))

- ALM neurons exhibit 94 prototypical response profiles (clusters) that are consistent across mice and datasets, contrary to the notion of randomly mixed selectivity; stimulus, choice, and action are encoded by distinct but partially overlapping functional populations delineated by their response profiles ([Yang 2022](../../raw/summaries/yang2022_thalamus_frontal_decision.md))

- Choice and ramping signals share the same neuronal population in ALM, supporting models where ramping plays a permissive role for choice development; neurons with similar response profiles exhibit significant trial-to-trial noise correlation, suggesting they belong to subnetworks ([Yang 2022](../../raw/summaries/yang2022_thalamus_frontal_decision.md))

- Mediodorsal thalamus (MD) provides strong excitatory drive to L3 IT cells in mPFC, with reciprocal connections forming specific thalamocortical loops ([Anastasiades 2021](../../raw/summaries/anastasiades2021_circuit_medial_prefrontal.md))

- Ventromedial thalamus (VM) enriches inputs at distal apical dendrites of L5 PT cells in mPFC, regulating cortical state and arousal ([Anastasiades 2021](../../raw/summaries/anastasiades2021_circuit_medial_prefrontal.md))

- Thalamic nucleus reuniens provides an indirect pathway from prefrontal cortex to hippocampus, positioned in midline thalamus ([Yu 2015](../../raw/summaries/yu2015_hippocampal_cortical_interaction.md))

## History of ideas

The traditional view of the thalamus was as a simple relay station passing sensory information to cortex. However, recent work has established that the thalamus actively participates in cortical computation, with thalamocortical loops driving functional subnetworks within cortical areas. The finding that thalamic inputs have stronger impact on frontal cortical selectivity than cortico-cortical inputs represents a significant shift in understanding of cortical circuit organization.

## Open questions

- The specific thalamic nuclei or cell types involved in driving frontal cortical selectivity are not fully identified
- How thalamic and cortical inputs interact to produce task selectivity remains to be fully understood
- Whether the findings generalize to other frontal regions and tasks
- The directionality of information flow during coherent coding (thalamus→cortex vs. cortex→thalamus vs. bidirectional)
- How thalamic inputs "select" specific neuronal trajectories from preexisting cortical repertoires

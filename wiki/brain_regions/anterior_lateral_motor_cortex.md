# Anterior Lateral Motor Cortex (ALM)

## Current understanding

The anterior lateral motor cortex (ALM) is a premotor area in mice that plays a critical role in motor planning and preparation. ALM neurons exhibit persistent activity during delay periods that bridges sensory cues and motor responses. This persistent activity is organized as discrete attractor states, where the network maintains one of several stable firing patterns that encode pending motor decisions.

## Key evidence

- Premotor area ALM exhibits discrete bistable attractor dynamics during delay periods: perturbations are either corrected or flip state, consistent with a two-attractor landscape. Optogenetic perturbations during delay-response tasks reveal bistability; network tends to return to or flip between two discrete attractor states. ([Khona 2022](../../raw/summaries/khona2022_attractor_integrator.md))

## History of ideas

The concept of discrete attractor dynamics in motor cortex emerged from theoretical work on Hopfield networks (Hopfield, 1982), which established how recurrent neural networks could maintain multiple stable states. The application of these ideas to motor planning was advanced by studies showing persistent activity in premotor areas during delay periods. The direct demonstration of discrete attractor dynamics through perturbation studies in ALM provided empirical validation of these theoretical predictions in a mammalian motor planning circuit.

## Open questions

- What are the specific connectivity patterns in ALM that give rise to the discrete attractor landscape?
- How do multiple attractor states scale to represent more complex motor sequences?
- What is the relationship between ALM attractor states and the final motor command execution in M1?
- How does the attractor dynamics in ALM interact with decision-making processes in other prefrontal regions?

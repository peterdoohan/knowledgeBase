# Astrocytes

## Current understanding

Astrocytes form extensive gap junction-coupled networks that enable intercellular trafficking of metabolites, providing activity-dependent metabolic support to neurons. These astroglial metabolic networks are essential for sustaining synaptic transmission during periods of high neuronal activity or glucose deprivation.

## Key evidence

- Gap junctions formed by connexin 43 (Cx43) and connexin 30 (Cx30) enable activity-dependent intercellular trafficking of glucose and its metabolites through astroglial networks to sustain glutamatergic synaptic transmission during glucose deprivation ([Rouach 2008](../../raw/summaries/rouach2008_astrocyte_metabolism.md))

- Glucose trafficking is selective for non-phosphorylated glucose over glucose-6-phosphate (~70% decrease for phosphorylated form), suggesting gap junction selectivity for specific metabolic substrates ([Rouach 2008](../../raw/summaries/rouach2008_astrocyte_metabolism.md))

- Neuronal activity via AMPA receptors increases glucose diffusion by ~50% through astroglial networks, demonstrating activity-dependent metabolic coupling between neurons and glia ([Rouach 2008](../../raw/summaries/rouach2008_astrocyte_metabolism.md))

- Local energetic demands reshape the spatial extent of metabolic networks—glucose diffusion extends to regions with high synaptic activity, creating dynamic metabolic supply zones ([Rouach 2008](../../raw/summaries/rouach2008_astrocyte_metabolism.md))

- Astroglial metabolic networks sustain synaptic transmission during glucose deprivation via lactate shuttling—direct glucose delivery to astrocytic networks prevents fEPSP depression during exogenous glucose deprivation ([Rouach 2008](../../raw/summaries/rouach2008_astrocyte_metabolism.md))

- Cx43 contributes ~55% and Cx30 ~40% of gap junctional coupling in adult hippocampal astrocytes, with double knockout abolishing glucose trafficking completely ([Yi 2018](../../raw/summaries/yi2018_gap_junction_astrocytes.md); [Rouach 2008](../../raw/summaries/rouach2008_astrocyte_metabolism.md))

- Intracellular Ca2+ triggers ATP release via Cx43 hemichannels through a multi-step signaling cascade: CaM → CaMK-II → p38 MAPK → cPLA2 → arachidonic acid → ROS → NO ([De Vuyst 2009](../../raw/summaries/devuyst2009_calcium_connexin43_hemichannels.md))

- ATP release via Cx43 hemichannels shows a bell-shaped concentration-response to Ca2+, peaking at ~500 nM and disappearing at higher concentrations due to competing inhibitory mechanisms ([De Vuyst 2009](../../raw/summaries/devuyst2009_calcium_connexin43_hemichannels.md))

## History of ideas

The classical model of astroglial energy metabolism viewed astrocytes as single entities that take up glucose from blood vessels and provide metabolic support to neurons. Rouach et al. (2008) established that astrocytes are extensively coupled through gap junctions, forming functional metabolic networks that enable intercellular trafficking of glucose and lactate. This extended the astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle (ANLS) hypothesis to include gap-junction-mediated networks for more efficient metabolite delivery from vasculature to active synapses.

## Open questions

- Whether gap junction-mediated metabolic networks operate similarly in brain regions beyond hippocampus
- The exact molecular mechanism linking neuronal activity (via AMPA receptors) to increased glucose trafficking
- The relative contribution of lactate versus glucose as the final energetic substrate delivered to neurons
- Whether hemichannels contribute to metabolite release under pathological conditions
- Long-term consequences of disrupting astroglial metabolic networks for neuronal survival and circuit function

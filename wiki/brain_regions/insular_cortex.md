# Insular Cortex

The insular cortex (IC) is a region of the cerebral cortex folded deep within the lateral sulcus, involved in interoception, emotion, and the representation of incentive value for instrumental action.

## Key evidence

- Insular cortex lesions preserve contingency sensitivity but abolish reward devaluation in extinction and impair incentive learning. IC-lesioned rats showed normal contingency discrimination (all 8/8 performing correctly) but indiscriminate responding in extinction devaluation tests; crucially, they showed normal selective devaluation when rewards were present during the test, suggesting IC is required to store updated incentive value for offline use, not for computing value changes per se ([Balleine 1998](../../raw/summaries/balleine1998_goal_directed_instrumental_action.md))

- Insular cortex lesions impair incentive learning for motivational-state modulation. IC-lesioned rats failed to transfer motivational-state-dependent incentive learning to control of subsequent sated-state instrumental performance, consistent with IC housing incentive memory for food rewards ([Balleine 1998](../../raw/summaries/balleine1998_goal_directed_instrumental_action.md))

- The IC is specifically required for storing incentive value memories that can be retrieved in the absence of direct reward contact, establishing a double dissociation with prelimbic cortex (which is required for contingency learning but not incentive learning) ([Balleine 1998](../../raw/summaries/balleine1998_goal_directed_instrumental_action.md))

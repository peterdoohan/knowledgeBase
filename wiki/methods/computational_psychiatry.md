## Key evidence

- A belief-instability parameter (κ₁) derived from attractor network principles best accounts for schizophrenia's nonlinear belief-updating pattern, outperforming simple learning-rate and disconfirmatory-bias models across two independent datasets. ([Adams 2018](../../raw/summaries/adams2018_attractor_schizophrenia.md))
- The attractor-based model (Model 6 with κ₁) was the winning model even among healthy controls, suggesting the belief-instability parameter captures a genuine feature of normal belief updating that is exaggerated in schizophrenia. ([Adams 2018](../../raw/summaries/adams2018_attractor_schizophrenia.md))
- Group differences in belief instability and response stochasticity persisted after controlling for IQ and working memory, suggesting these computational abnormalities are not secondary to general cognitive impairment. ([Adams 2018](../../raw/summaries/adams2018_attractor_schizophrenia.md))

- Simulated antipsychotic treatment (reducing policy precision after 10 false inferences) reduced delusional agents from 31 to 5 out of 972, without directly changing habits; the therapeutic effect arises through recurrent updating among policy precision, policies, habits, and beliefs, breaking the self-reinforcing attractor. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

- Domain-general likelihood imprecision produces domain-specific (social) delusions when affect is coupled to social inference policies; the same precision reduction would not produce delusions in non-social domains where different policies apply. ([Adams 2021](../../raw/summaries/adams_delusions_inference_attractors.md))

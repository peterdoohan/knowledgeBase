## Research report 

## Inactivation of the infralimbic prefrontal cortex reinstates goal-directed responding in overtrained rats 

## Etienne Coutureau[a] _[,]_[b] , Simon Killcross[a] _[,]_[∗] 

> a _School of Psychology, Cardiff University, P.O. Box 901, Park Place, Cardiff CF10 3YG, UK_ 

> b _Laboratoire de Neurosciences Cognitives, UMR 5106 CNRS, Université de Bordeaux, Bordeaux, France_ 

## **Abstract** 

Over the course of extended training, instrumental responding in rats shows a transition from goal-dependent performance to goal-independentperformance,asassessedbysensitivitytoreward-devaluationinducedbytasteaversionsorspecificsatiety.Ithasbeensuggestedthat this reflects the gradual dominance of reflexive, habit-based responding over voluntary, goal-directed actions. Previous research suggests that lesions of the medial prefrontal cortex disrupt this interaction between goal-directed and habitual responding. More specifically, whereas lesions of the prelimbic prefrontal cortex appear to disrupt normal goal-directed responding, lesions of the infralimbic prefrontal cortex cause animals to remain goal-directed even after substantial overtraining. The current experiment explored further the nature of this interaction between actions and habits. Rats were given extended training of an instrumental lever press response before bilateral intracerebral cannulae giving access to the infralimbic cortex were implanted. Following further reminder training all animals were given a test of goal sensitivity by specific-satiety devaluation of the instrumental outcome, or a matched reward, prior to extinction tests. Before these tests, half of the animals received bilateral infusions of muscimol into the infralimbic cortex, and the remainder, control vehicle infusions. As expected after extended instrumental training, control-infused animals showed habitual performance that was not selectively influenced by devaluation of the instrumental outcome. In contrast, animals receiving temporary inactivation of the infralimbic cortex by muscimol showed selective sensitivity to devaluation of the instrumental outcome, indicating a reinstatement of goal-directed responding in these animals. This suggests that the development of habitual responding reflects the active inhibition of goal-directed responses that are mediated by action-outcome associations.

## **1. Introduction** 

Folk psychology suggests that when novel behavioural responses are learnt, processing is initially effortful and cognitively demanding, but with time comes to be easier and relatively independent of competing cognitive load [12,27]. This notion is formalized in a two-process view of instrumental action [11,15] which suggests that initial responding is based on the acquisition of goal-directed action-outcome (A-O) associations requiring active and continued representation of the goal object in order to mediate performance. With further experience, an alternative stimulus-response (S-R) associative structure comes to control performance, removing the need for an actively maintained representation

Substantial evidence for the existence of these processes in rats has been presented in recent years [15,18,43]. Instrumental devaluation procedures have allowed an assessment of the goal-direct nature of instrumental responding that has afforded empirical insight into different forms of response control. Adams [1] found that after limited training of a lever press response in rats, instrumental performance in an extinction test was decreased following devaluation of the instrumental outcome by repeated pairings with nausea-inducing injections of lithium chloride (LiCl). Hence, rats were sensitive not only to the current value of the goal of their actions, but also to the causal relationship between their actions and this goal. Further evidence suggests that the development of habits depends not on repetition of the rewarded response per se, but rather on an animal’s perceived correlation of response performance and delivery of reward [15–17,19]. Hence, development of habitual performance reflects the gradual increment of S-R processes with repeated stimulus-reward pairings according to classical S-R reinforcement theory, combined with a decreasing contribution of A-O processes based on the perceived correlation of instrumental action and outcome. Within this hypothesis, two scenarios are possible. First, the decline in the contribution of the A-O process reflects a diminution of the actual strength of associative links between action and outcome. This would be in line with results indicating that extended exposure to the outcome prior to training of the instrumental action also leads to the emergence of habitual responding [1,19], as prior exposure to the outcome may retard the development of subsequent A-O associations. Second, although the perceived correlation of action and outcome declines with extended training, this may not itself reflect any diminution of the strength of A-O associations, but may instead parallel the declining _influence_ of existing A-O associations over behavioural responses, perhaps under the control of direct inhibitory processes. 

Recent research has provided some preliminary evidence for a neural dissociation of mechanisms involved in the twin processes governing instrumental action. Following work by Balleine and Dickinson [4], we recently reported [31] that bilateral excitotoxic lesions of the prelimbic (PL) medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) in rats led to habit-based instrumental performance that was insensitive to goal devaluation after only minimal training. In contrast, we also reported that lesions of the more ventral infralimbic (IL) mPFC led to performance that remained sensitive to goal devaluation even following extended training. That is, PL lesions produced solely habit-based animals whereas IL lesions produced rats that were solely goal-directed. Following the theory outlined above, this double dissociation confirms the existence of response control by both habit-based and goal-directed processes. However, these findings cannot provide a resolution of the alternative views of the role of perceived A-O correlation in either the changing strength of A-O associations themselves or the changing influence of intact A-O associations. The current experiment addresses this important issue. If extended instrumental training leads to a degradation of A-O associations, then we would expect lesions of the IL cortex made after this training to be without effect on the lack of sensitivity to goal-devaluation. If overtraining actually degrades A-O associations, then there is no reason to believe that post-training lesions of the IL cortex might reinstate them. However, if extended training leads instead _intact_ A-O associations on 

behavioural responding, then we might expect post-training lesions of the IL cortex to re-instate goal-directed responding in overtrained animals. Here we examine the role of post-training IL lesions on the sensitivity of instrumental responding to goal devaluation following extended training. Due to the nature of the overtraining effect, and the size and location of the IL cortex, animals were given extensive instrumental training prior to the surgical implantation of bilateral cannulae giving access to the IL cortex. Infusions of muscimol were then used to induce local cortical deactivation prior to test sessions. As we were concerned that cannulation and infusion might produce damage to the overlying PL cortex that in itself would lead to changes in the goal-sensitivity of instrumental action, we conducted a between-subject comparison of the sensitivity of muscimoland sham-infused groups to goal devaluation after extended instrumental training. 

## **2. Materials and methods** 

## _2.1. Subjects_ 

Sixteen male, Lister-hooded rats (275–300 g) were used. Rats were accustomed to the temperature and humidity-controlled laboratory vivarium for 1 week. They were housed two per cage. During pre-surgical behavioural training and following recovery from surgery, animals were maintained at ca. 90% free-feeding weight. The vivarium was maintained at 21[◦] C with the light on from 8 a.m _._ to 8 p.m. All experiments were carried out during the light portion of the cycle. All procedures involving animals and their care conformed to institutional guidelines that comply with international (Directive 86-609, 24 November 1986, European Community) and national (UK Animals (Scientific Procedures) Act 1986) laws and policies.

## **4. General discussion** 

This experiment addressed the issue of whether or not instrumental performance mediated by goal-directed A-O associations could be re-established in animals given extended instrumental training normally sufficient to ensure behavioural control by goal-insensitive S-R habit processes. Whereas in control animals overtraining led to lever-pressing that was insensitive to selective goal-devaluation by specific-satiety, lever-press performance was sensitive to specific-satiety goal-devaluation following infusion of the

GABAA agonist muscimol into the IL cortex. That is, neurochemical inactivation of the IL mPFC produced goal-directed responding in overtrained animals, relative to vehicle-infused control animals. As the control group received identical surgical procedures and infusions, the effect observed is unlikely to be due to damage to the overlying PL mPFC following cannulation, or damage to the IL cortex due to infusions themselves. Two alternative scenarios were highlighted by the dual process view introduced earlier: first, the development of behavioural autonomy could be due to the increase in strength of S-R associations, combined with a decline in the _actual strength_ of A-O associations; second, the behavioural dominance of S-R associations could be a consequence of a decreasing _influence_ of intact A-O associations that are (actively) suppressed. The results presented here clearly favour the latter hypothesis. Animals that would normally show lever press performance dominated by S-R habit processes can be made to show goal-directed performance dominated by A-O associations following inactivation of the IL mPFC.

It appears that this process of development of behavioural autonomy may be more active than suggested by previous work. Previous findings demonstrated that the development of habits is facilitated by training on reward schedules that decrease the correlation between instrumental response rate and the rate of reward delivery. That is, habits develop more rapidly following training on interval schedules where there is little relationship between response and reward rates once a certain level of performance is achieved, than on ratio schedules where there is always a linear relationship between response and reward rates, regardless of performance levels. This led to the suggestion that changes in the contribution of A-O processes (as opposed to S-R processes) to behavioural performance were largely due changes in knowledge about the A-O relation, which in turn was dependent on the perceived correlation between instrumental responses and rewards [8,15,16,19]. Extended instrumental training and extended pre-training exposure to the instrumental reward, both manipulations that lead to domination of behaviour by S-R habits, also degrade this relationship; the former by changing the range over which experienced response and reward rates vary (see [15]), and the latter by processes akin to those thought to underpin learned helplessness [33]. The current study suggests that the contribution of A-O processes to instrumental performance is not irretrievable lost by decreases in perceived or actual correlation, but instead is actively suppressed by a mechanism that allows habit-based responding to dominate behavioural performance. It remains to be seen whether the mechanisms by which the influence of A-O associations decreases is the same following all manipulations that lead to the development of habits. 

Previous research has ascribed the development of S-R habits to processes taking place in the subcortical dorsolateral striatum [29,35,42,49], and this work has recently been extended to an assessment of sensitivity of overtrained responses to reward devaluation following excitotoxic lesions of this region. Yin et al. [50] reported that NMDA-induced lesions of the dorsolateral striatum produced goal-directed responding in overtrained animals in a manner analogous to the effects we previously observed following lesions of the IL cortex [31]. This suggests that whereas the dorsolateral striatum may underpin the development of S-R habits, the IL cortex is responsible for processes whereby these S-R associations come to dominate A-O associations. That is not to say, of course, that the IL cortex is necessarily involved in all behavioural situations in which responding is mediated by S-R associations. The current work demonstrates only that the IL cortex is important in procedures in which there is competition between instrumental goal-directed actions and S-R habits. Nevertheless, the ventral region of the mPFC has been implicated in a variety of inhibitory roles, particularly in the context of fear-conditioning and animal models of anxiety [22,28,37,38,41] and in affective inhibition [14]. The precise role played by the IL cortex appears to depend very much on the specific requirements of the task being investigated, although at a general level it appears to be involved in mechanisms whereby previous learning is interfaced or reconciled with currently acting contingencies. As such, it may underpin selective aspects of a contention-scheduling system [47], modulating competition between different response tendencies evoked by previous experience and the current environment. 

One issue that remains unresolved concerns the possible interface between the dorsolateral striatal systems mediating the development of S-R habits, and the prefrontal systems likely to be responsible for governing the nature of associations controlling performance. There appear to be no direct connections between the ventral aspects of the mPFC and the dorsolateral striatum. Rather, the IL cortex projects to the shell of the nucleus accumbens [9,34] as well as to a wide range of limbic and autonomic regions implicated in behavioural inhibition [26,46]. However, the IL mPFC projects reciprocally to the PL mPFC [21] and this region in turn projects to more dorsal aspects of the mPFC including anterior cingulate and medial agranular cortices, and from there to premotor and motor cortices [7,32,36]. A similar hierarchy exists between accumbens shell, core, medial/central striatum and dorsal striatum [23], and these may represent parallel systems governing the interaction of goal-directed and reflexive responding, influenced not only by S-R and A-O associations, but also by the presence of Pavlovian cues known to modulate instrumental performance [44,48]. Recent research has demonstrated the importance of these striatal sub-regions in incentive processes and in the influence of Pavlovian cues on performance [13,39].

motivated behaviour assessed in this experiment—approach to the food magazine—although this must be tempered by the variable nature of the results and their lack of statistical significance. Magazine approach responses that occur during instrumental performance have been dissociated from instrumental responding at a behavioural level, following demonstration that instrumental lever presses, but not accompanying magazine approach, is sensitive to incentive learning manipulations [2]. The frequent assumption is that magazine approach is under the control of Pavlovian associations between the training context and the instrumental outcome, and as these S-O associations are directly influenced by current motivational state, magazine approach responding reflects this direct sensitivity (see [3] for a discussion). Previous research has suggested that Pavlovian magazine approach remains sensitive to outcome devaluation despite extensive overtraining [5,24,31]. However, although not significant, the current experiment suggested that whereas magazine approach was selectively sensitive to devaluation following inactivation of the IL cortex, performance was markedly less sensitive following overtraining in control vehicle-infused animals. We believe this reduced is likely to be due to the context extinction procedure employed after extended instrumental training. This procedure was explicitly designed to reduce the impact of Pavlovian associations on instrumental performance [44] that may have masked the sensitivity of instrumental responding to reward devaluation by generally suppressing all behaviours following specific-satiety treatment. It is possible that magazine approach behaviour that survives the extinction of contextual S-O associations no longer reflects the influence of Pavlovian conditioning, but may reflect aspects of magazine approach that are due to instrumental associations acquired during extended training. These weak instrumental associations between magazine approach and food delivery are then subject to the same processes of transition to habitual performance as other, more explicit, instrumental associations (see [30] for a discussion), and also, interestingly, to the effect of IL cortex inactivation in reversing this transition. 

In summary, we have shown that the transition of control of instrumental performance from goal-sensitive to goal-insensitive processes can be reversed by inactivation of the IL region of the mPFC in rats. This suggests that normal habitual performance may emerge due to the active inhibition of goal-directed processes that otherwise remain intact during extended training. It has been suggested that the adjacent PL mPFC is responsible for the active maintenance of this goal-sensitive representation [4,31] perhaps in conjunction with the basolateral amygdala [6,10]. This interaction of goal-directed and habitual performance is likely to be of great importance in executive functioning in health and disease. For example, as the dominance of habitual responding over goal-directed action is thought to play a central role in the progressive development of drug addiction [20], it is likely that drugs of abuse not only induce activity in basic (dopaminergic) reinforcement systems [45], 

but also induce changes in the propensity of drug-directed actions to become habitual. The current study demonstrates one mechanism by which such habits might be reversed.

## Concept-based behavioral planning and the lateral prefrontal cortex 

## Jun Tanji[1] , Keisetsu Shima[2] and Hajime Mushiake[2] 

> 1 Brain Science Institute, Tamagawa University, Machida, Tokyo 194-8610, Japan 

> 2 Department of Physiology, Tohoku University School of Medicine, Sendai 980-8575, Japan 

Many lines of evidence implicate the lateral prefrontal cortex (LPFC) in the executive control of behavior. In early studies, neuronal activity in this area was thought to retain information about forthcoming movements for a short period until they were executed. However, later studies have stressed its role in the cognitive aspects of behavioral planning, such as behavioral significance, behavioral rules and behavioral goals. The consequence of the intended action (i.e. a change in the state of the target object), rather than the intended movement, is primarily represented in the LPFC during planning. Recent studies show that the LPFC is involved in more abstract aspects of conceptual processes, such as in representing categories of multiple actions at the stage of behavioral planning. 

## Introduction 

The lateral prefrontal cortex (LPFC) possesses a wealth of anatomical connectivity with multiple cortical and subcortical areas [1,2] and is involved in broad aspects of behavioral control [3–5]. Classical theories on the function of the LPFC have centered on working memory [6,7], attentional control [8] and behavioral inhibition [9]. Recent studies of this area, however, have revealed its role in the control of a much broader spectrum of behaviors [10–12]. Beyond the short-term retention of information, much interest has grown in its role in cross-modal and cross-temporal association of information [13], in the executive control of behavior [14,15], and in the top-down control of neural networks involving the cortical and subcortical areas [16]. This article, based on recent physiological findings, focuses on the involvement of the LPFC in behavioral planning that requires information processing at the conceptual level, and is not intended to provide a comprehensive review of studies on the LPFC. 

## Behavioral planning in the prefrontal cortex 

Because of its location in the frontal lobe, and its efferent connections to higher-order motor areas, the LPFC plays important roles in regulating aspects of volitional motor behavior [3,5,17]. Therefore, it is rather surprising that relatively few experimental studies have explored its role in the motor aspects of behavior compared with the number of studies on perceptual, mnemonic and attentional aspects of behavior. Studies using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) in humans have demonstrated 

prefrontal activity that represents forthcoming actions [18–20]. Studies examining single cells of the LPFC in macaques have also reported activity that might represent the parameters of forthcoming movements [21]. This raises the question as to whether intended movements are represented in the LPFC in ways that are similar to how they are represented in the cortical motor areas. 

A series of experiments has revealed that behavioral factors other than the specification of variables for intended movements crucially influence neuronal activity in the LPFC. Hoshi et al. [22] found that neuronal activity (recorded from the periprincipal portion of the LPFC) during a preparatory period differed considerably depending on whether the monkeys were intending to capture a circle or a triangle appearing in a specific spatial location. Furthermore, they found that activity in the LPFC during the preparatory period differed greatly depending on the behavioral rule that specified the intended motor task. Neuronal activity during the preparatory period might reflect a shape-match or locationmatch rule [23], a matching-to-a-sample rule or a spatialmatch rule [24], or a match or non-match to sample rule [25]. Human studies in this direction appear in a comprehensive review [26]. 

In a more recent study, the issue of the nature of motor representation within the LPFC was addressed by designing a behavioral condition that dissociated two behavioral factors specifying either motor variables (i.e. arm movement) or the motion of an object to be acted upon [27]. Monkeys were trained to move a cursor on a video screen by two hand-operated manipulanda. Supination (palm up) or pronation (palm down) of the right or left hand moved the manipulanda in one of four directions, which were then linked to the movements of the cursor (up, down, right or left). The direction of the cursor’s motion was assigned to each of four different hand movements in three different ways. It was found that, during the preparatory period, the vast majority of LPFC neurons exhibited activity reflecting the movement of the cursor, but not the movement to be performed (i.e. use of the right or left hand, or the direction of the movement). In other words, the LPFC neurons primarily represent the movement of an object that will occur as a consequence of an intended limb movement, but not the limb movement per se. This raises the possibility that motor behavior planning in the LPFC is performed in terms of the end result, but not in terms of the intended movement.

Averbeck et al. [28] studied the LPFC while monkeys were required to copy geometrical shapes from the right half of a screen to the left half of the screen. They reported that the ensemble activity of LPFC neurons reflected sequential segments of movement being planned. However, this interesting report could also be interpreted as if the neuronal activity reflected the shape of the geometric figure that the monkey intended to draw, rather than reflecting the limb movements planned. In this experiment, these two behavioral variables were not dissociated. It may well be that the behavioral planning in the LPFC is achieved in terms of the end result (i.e. an object intended to be drawn), rather than in terms of the movements required to perform the task. 

## Identifying behavioral goal independent of reward contingency 

The LPFC is influenced by information concerning the quality, quantity and content of reinforcement that is expected as the outcome of a specific behavior [29], although the orbitofrontal and medial prefrontal cortexes are the principal divisions involved in reinforcementguided decision making [30]. The primary importance of reinforcer-related activity in the LPFC might well be the modulation of behavioral control, reflecting the level of attention and motivation contingent on the expected positive–negative reinforcer [31]. The term behavioral goal, or goal-based action selection, is sometimes used to signify the presence or absence of a reward as the outcome of an action [32]. However, in view of the ubiquitous nature of reward expectancy in influencing neural activity throughout the central nervous system, we propose a more limited use of the term ‘behavioral goal’ when discussing prefrontal functions, referring specifically to gaining access to an object or place or attaining a specific behavioral state in a given environment, independent of reward relevance. 

The neglect of behavioral goals is a hallmark of frontal lobe deficiencies [33,34]. Frontal patients often fail to achieve the behavioral goal during tasks that require cognitive control such as multistep tasks or the concurrent execution of dual tasks [35,36]. When attempting the Tower of Hanoi task [37,38], the prefrontal cortex is implicated in resolving goal–subgoal conflicts. Such clinical studies of and reports on human subjects prompted researchers to determine the representation of behavioral goal information in the PFC in experimental studies. Saito et al. [27] constructed a spatial maze requiring monkeys to achieve multiple behavioral goals in a temporal sequence (Box 1). During a preparatory period, the monkeys were required to plan a path to acquire intermediate goals stepwise in order to reach the final goal. During the planning period, neuronal activity in the LPFC initially represented the final goal and subsequently represented the first, and then the second, of the intermediate goals. Interestingly, the information specifying these intermediate-goal representations was not available in externally given instruction signals, but instead was generated internally. These findings demonstrate that LPFC neurons take part in governing goal-oriented behavior by the generation of goal and subgoal representations while conforming to an appropriate behavioral schedule (see Box 1). 

## Box 1. Representation of behavioral goals explored during a path-planning task 

In a study designed to explore the neural representation of behavioral goals in the LPFC [27], monkeys were trained to perform a path-planning task in which multiple movements of a cursor were required to reach a goal within a maze (Figure Ia). A checkerboardlike maze and a cursor were displayed on a screen. The movement of the cursor was linked to the movement of manipulanda. At the beginning of the task, the animal was required to hold the two manipulanda in a neutral position for 1 s until a cursor appeared at the center of the maze, indicating the starting position. One second later, a goal position was presented for 1 s (goal display). After a delay period, the color of the start-position cursor changed from green to yellow, which served as a task-initiation signal (first GO signal). The animal was required to move the cursor within 1 s, using the manipulanda. The cursor moved in the direction specified by the animal to a position that was defined as the immediate goal. After holding the cursor there for 1 s, the next GO signal was presented. The animal was required to move the cursor in a stepwise manner to reach the goal that had been presented during goal display. No visual cues were provided to indicate the direction in which the cursor should be moved during the task. Thus, the monkey selected the direction of each cursor motion based solely upon its intended moves to reach the final goal. When the cursor reached the final goal, the animal received a reward. The population analysis of prefrontal neurons revealed that neuronal activity reflecting the final goal rose sharply at first and then subsided to a sustained level (Figure Ib, red line). In the later part of the delay period, during which the monkey was still formulating a plan, activity reflecting the immediate goal gradually increased and then peaked before the initiation of the first move (blue line). This finding implicates the prefrontal cortex in planning goal-oriented sequential behavior.

## Box 2. Categorization and the lateral prefrontal cortex 

Living systems must categorize; objects and events in the environment are assigned to separate classes or categories [61,62]. Categorization allows the recognition of old or new entities without the need for new associative learning. Numerous brain structures are involved in different types of category learning [63,64]. Although brain imaging studies in humans have determined which brain areas are involved in different classes or categories [65–68], studies in non-human primates have revealed that individual cells take part in representing category information. In the lateral prefrontal cortex (LPFC), cells responded selectively to a class of objects based on shape resemblance [56]. In this study, prefrontal cells not only differentiated between ‘dog-like’ or ‘cat-like’ shapes but also responded differently to two classes of objects whose shape boundaries were altered by the experimenters. Thus, category selectivity was based on the similarity of shapes, and category learning was based on explicit training with reinforcement. Furthermore, these category-selective responses were coupled to two different actions: match response (GO) and non-match response (NO-GO). By contrast, category selectivity could also be based on the temporal structure of actions to be performed [45]. In this case, subjects learned categories through a procedural learning process; no external cues were given to prompt the development of category-selective responses. The appearance of category selectivity was not immediately coupled to any reward-laden motor responses. These studies demonstrate that different classes of categories learned in different behavioral contexts are represented in the LPFC to guide discriminative behavior and to plan complex behavioral sequences.

Categorization is at the core of concept formation (Box 2), and the categorization of visual stimuli is represented in the LPFC [56]. LPFC neural activity was examined in monkeys that had been trained to categorize animal forms as being either ‘dog-like’ or ‘cat-like’. We propose a conceptual flow of processes (Figure 2) in which neural structures use category information to plan multiple sequences of actions.

## Box 3. Questions for future research 

Various issues must be investigated to further clarify the involvement of the LPFC in concept-based behavioral planning: 

- In what way do different subregions within the LPFC take part in different aspects of executive behavioral control? The issue of a functional parcellation of the LPFC remains to be solved. On the one hand, current techniques of brain imaging in human subjects have not provided sufficient spatial resolution to reveal the exact site of measurement [70]. On the other hand, the placing of electrodes in animal studies is not generally consistent. Possible functional subdivisions along the dorsolateral axis and along the rostrocaudal axis are of much interest. 

- What are the neuronal mechanisms that allow category learning with respect to action sequences? A multidisciplinary approach is required to analyze neuronal activity during learning and to develop computational models that explain the development of category specificity. 

- Here, we describe category learning as an example of the abstract structure of behavior. What is the limit of such abstract representation in nonhuman primates? 

- Although reward relevance is currently the most popular research topic in neuroscience, a major part of higher-order behavioral goal characterization in primate behavior is independent of the acquisition of primary reinforcers. One such example is presented here. Does this apply to other aspects of behavioral goal regulation? ## Conclusions 

Neurophysiological evidence indicates that the LPFC is primarily involved in behavioral planning that also requires cognitive information processing, but that this area is not substantially concerned with the specification of the motor aspects of behavior. In addition to behavioral factors such as task rules, task objectives, and task demands, two aspects of cognitive processing are deemed of particular interest. First, the steps required to attain a behavioral goal are processed individually in the LPFC. Second, when a large number of behavioral sequences must be planned, LPFC cells can represent categories of action sequences that possess a common temporal pattern of sequences. This representation of behavioral categories is an example of high-level processing of cognitive information that provides effective guidance for multi-phased behavioral sequences (see Box 3 for remaining, unanswered questions).

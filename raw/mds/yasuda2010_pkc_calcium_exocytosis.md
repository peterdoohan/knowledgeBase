## ABSTRACT 

Astrocytes release various bioactive substances via Ca[2][1] - and soluble N-ethylmaleimide-sensitive factor attachment protein receptor (SNARE)-dependent exocytosis; however the regulatory mechanisms of glial exocytosis are still poorly understood. In the present study, we investigated the effect of protein kinase C (PKC) on exocytosis in glial cells using primary cultured astrocytes and clonal rat glioma C6 cells. Mass spectrometry and Western blot analysis using phospho-specific antibodies revealed that phorbol 12myristate 13-acetate (PMA) treatment induced the phosphorylation of synaptosomal-associated protein of 23 kDa (SNAP-23) on Ser[95] , Ser[120] , and Ser[160] in cultured astrocytes and C6 cells. Phosphorylation at these sites was suppressed by treatment with the PKC inhibitor, bisindolylmaleimide I (BIS). In contrast, Ser[110] of SNAP-23 was constitutively phosphorylated in these cells and was dephosphorylated in a PKC-dependent manner. Exogenously expressed human growth hormone (hGH) accumulated in cytoplasmic granular structures in cultured astrocytes, and its release after ATP-treatment was Ca[2][1] - and SNARE-dependent. PMA treatment suppressed the ATP-induced hGH release from astrocytes and this inhibition was reversed by BIS. We also observed PMA-dependent suppression and an attenuation of that suppression by BIS in ionomycin-induced hGH release from C6 cells. These results suggest that intracellular activation of PKC suppresses Ca[2][1] - and SNARE-dependent exocytosis in astroglial cells. **V** C 2010 Wiley-Liss, Inc. 

## INTRODUCTION 

Astrocytes play many important roles in the brain, including support of the blood-brain barrier, provision of nutrients to nervous tissue, and maintenance of extracellular ion balance. They also play a principal role in repair and scarring processes of the brain and spinal cord following traumatic injury (Wang and Bordey, 2008). Astrocytes express a variety of neurotransmitter receptors (Kettenmann and Steinh€auser, 2005) and respond to neuronal activity by increasing intracellular calcium levels and producing second messengers (Agulhon et al., 2008). Furthermore, astrocytes release neurotransmitters such as glutamate and ATP (Coco et al., 2003; Pasti et al., 2001) and many bioactive peptides 

including growth factors and cytokines (Krzˇan et al., 2003; Ramamoorthy and Whim, 2008) by Ca[2][1] -dependent exocytosis. Thus, it is widely believed that astrocytes perform additional important functions in the brain, and may regulate the activity of synapses and neural networks through bidirectional interactions with neurons (Araque et al., 1999; Pascual et al., 2005; Perea and Araque, 2007). 

Exocytosis is a fundamental function of eukaryotic cells, and SNARE proteins play an essential role in secretory vesicle membrane fusion (Lin and Scheller, 2000; Rizo and Rosenmund, 2008). The specific SNARE proteins involved in neurotransmitter release from neurons are syntaxin 1 and synaptosomal-associated protein of 25 kDa (SNAP-25) in the plasma membrane, and vesicle-associated membrane protein 2 (VAMP-2) in the synaptic vesicle membrane. Various SNARE proteins are expressed in astrocytes; however, SNAP-25 is absent or present at very low levels under normal conditions, and SNAP-23 supports exocytosis in astrocytes (Hepp et al., 1999; Jeftinija et al., 1997; Li et al., 2008; Maienschein et al., 1999; Osborne et al., 2009; Parpura et al., 1995). 

In neurons, exocytotic release of neurotransmitters is both positively and negatively regulated by various protein kinases (Snyder et al., 2006; Takahashi and Ohnishi, 2002; Turner et al., 1999). This regulation is likely to be an important mechanism of synaptic plasticity underlying higher brain functions such as learning and memory. However, little is known about the modulation of exocytosis in astrocytes. In the present study, we investigated protein kinase C (PKC)-dependent regulation of exocytosis in cultured rat astrocytes and clonal rat glioma C6 cells. We found that PKC negatively regulates exocytotic release in astrocytes, in contrast to its action as a positive effector in neurons.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS Drugs and Chemicals

Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM), Ca[2][1] -Mg[2][1] -free Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), DNase I, 50% polyethylenimine (PEI) solution, D- biotin, hydrocortisone, heparin sulfate, transferrin, insulin, fibronectin, epidermal growth factor, basic fibroblast growth factor, dithiothreitol (DTT), iodoacetamide, and ATP were obtained from Sigma. Bovine serum (BS), fetal bovine serum (FBS), horse serum, goat serum, penicillin, streptomycin, trypsin, Coomassie Brilliant Blue (CBB) staining solution (SimplyBlue SafeStain), and Protein G-coupled magnetic beads (Dynabeads Protein G) were obtained from Invitrogen. Sequencing grade modified trypsin was obtained from Promega. Phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (PhosSTOP) was obtained from Roche. Ionomycin was obtained from Nacalai Tesque. 

## Antibodies 

Anti-SNAP-23 antibodies were raised in rabbits to synthetic peptides conjugated to keyhole limpet hemocyanin. Sequences of the peptide antigens were: SNAP-23 C-terminus, (C)KNRIDIANTRAKKLIDS; phospho-Ser[95] -SNAP-23, (C)TKNFEpSGKNYK; phospho-Ser[110] -SNAP-23, (C)DGG DSpSPSNVV; phospho-Ser[120] -SNAP-23, (C)VSKQPpSR ITNG; and phospho-Ser[160] -SNAP-23, (C)TQVGpSILGNLK. A phospho-Ser residue is indicated as ‘‘pS,’’ and a Cys residue (C) was added to the N-terminus of each peptide for conjugation. We purified each antibody from antisera using the respective peptide antigen conjugated to Sepharose 6B. The anti-SNAP-23 C-terminus antibody and the anti-phospho-SNAP-23 antibodies were further purified by passage through SNAP-25 C-terminus peptide- and nonphosphorylated peptide-conjugated Sepharose 6B, respectively, to remove cross-reactive antibodies. A monoclonal antibody to Na[1] /K[1] -ATPase (mAb 9A7) was prepared as described (Choi et al., 1997). The monoclonal anti-c-Myc antibody (9E10) was purchased from Berkeley Antibody Company; fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-conjugated Fab-fragment anti-mouse IgG and horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated donkey anti-rabbit IgG (H1L) were purchased from Jackson Immuno Research Laboratories; and the monoclonal anti-connexin 43 antibody was purchased from BD Transduction Laboratories. 

## Cell Culture 

We isolated astrocytes from the cerebral cortex of postnatal day 2 Wistar rats. Brain cells were prepared from the cortices by trypsinization (0.25% trypsin, 0.01% DNase in PBS), suspension in buffered DMEM (bDMEM) containing 2.5 U mL[2][1] penicillin, 25 lg streptomycin, 25 mM 4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1piperazineethanesulfonic acid [HEPES], adjusting to pH 7.4 with NaOH, and incubation in a plastic culture dish (Sumitomo Bakelite) for 10 min to remove microglia. Unattached cells were evenly seeded on four 90-mm culture dishes (Sumitomo Bakelite) coated with PEI, and cultured in 10% heatinactivated BS, 2.5% heat-inactivated FBS, and 87.5% bDMEM for 6 days at 37[�] C in a humidified incubator with 10% CO2. The culture medium was changed every 3 days. Cells were subcultured to six 75-cm[2] tissue culture flasks (Greiner Bio-One), cultured for 629 days, and then the culture flasks were shaken at 210 rpm for 18 h at 37[�] C to remove nonastrocytic cells. We subcultured the adherent cells on either PEI-coated 35-mm culture dishes or chamber slides at a density of 1 3 10[6] cells per dish or 0.5 3 10[5] cells per chamber, and used them for experiments after 2 days. Astrocytes used for hGH release assays (mentioned later) were cultured in 10% heat-inactivated FBS/bDMEM and subcultured on PEI-coated 35-mm dishes at a density of 0.9 3 10[6] cells per dish in astrocyte-defined medium (10 ng mL[2][1] D-biotin, 10 nM hydrocortisone, 0.5 lg mL[2][1] heparin sulfate, 40 nM sodium selenite, 50 lg mL[2][1] transferrin, 5 lg mL[2][1] insulin, 1.5 lg mL[2][1] fibronectin, 10 ng mL[2][1] epidermal growth factor, and 5 ng mL[2][1] basic fibroblast growth factor/bDMEM). C6 glioma cells were maintained in DMEM containing 10% heat-inactivated FBS at 37[�] C in a humidified incubator with 10% CO2. PC12 cells were maintained in DMEM with 5% heat-inactivated precolostrum newborn calf serum (Mitsubishi Kagaku, Japan) and 5% heat-inactivated horse serum at 37[�] C in a humidified incubator with 10% CO2. 

## PMA Treatment 

Cells were washed three times with a low-K[1] solution (140 mM NaCl, 4.7 mM KCl, 1.2 mM KH2PO4, 2.5 mM CaCl2, 1.2 mM MgSO4, 11 mM glucose, 15 mM HEPESNaOH, pH 7.4) at 37[�] C, and were incubated in the low-K[1] solution in the presence or absence of 2.5 or 5 lM BIS for 5 or 10 min as indicated in the figure legends. Cells were then incubated in low-K[1] solution in the presence or absence of 0.5 or 1 lM PMA with or without BIS for various periods as indicated in the figure legends. After treatment, cells were washed three times with cold PBS, homogenized in sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) sample buffer (2% SDS, 10% glycerol, 50 mM 2-amino-2-hydroxymethyl-1,3-propanediol [Tris]HCl, pH 6.89) by sonication, and stored at 280[�] C before use.

## Mass Spectrometric Analysis

Beads were washed five times with wash buffer (0.5% Triton X-100, 1 M NaCl, 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4), and bound proteins were eluted in SDS sample buffer. Eluted proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE and stained with CBB. The polyacrylamide gel containing SNAP-23 was excised into 1–2 mm pieces. Gel pieces were destained with 50% acetonitrile (ACN)/100 mM NH4HCO3, reduced in 10 mM DTT/100 mM NH4HCO3 at 56[�] C for 1 h, alkylated with 55 mM iodoacetamide at room temperature for 45 min in the dark, and then rehydrated in 1 mg mL[2][1] trypsin/100 mM NH4HCO3 at 45[�] C for 3 h. ## Plasmid Construction and Transfection 

The coding region of the human growth hormone (hGH) gene was subcloned into pSI (Promega), pcDNA3 (Invitrogen), and pcDNA3.1/myc-His (Invitrogen) vectors, and were designated as the pSI/hGH, pcDNA3/hGH, and pcDNA-mycHis/hGH constructs, respectively. A DNA fragment corresponding to the tetanus toxin (TeTX) light chain gene was isolated from the pOG-7 vector and subcloned into pcDNA3. The resulting construct was designated as pcDNA3/TeTX. Rat brain astrocytes, C6 cells, and PC12 cells were plated on either PEI-coated 35-mm culture dishes or chamber slides and cultured for 18 to 24 h. We transfected the expression constructs using Lipofectamine 2000 (Invitrogen) or FuGENE 6 (Roche) in the presence of serum according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Cells were then cultured for 24 to 48 h and used for experiments.

## DISCUSSION 

In the present study, we found that the phosphorylation of multiple serine residues on SNAP-23 was regulated by PKC in cultured rat astrocytes and clonal rat glioma cells, and that Ca[2][1] -induced exocytotic release from these cells was negatively regulated by PKC-dependent phosphorylation. 

Small molecules like ATP and glutamate are released from astrocytes not only by exocytotic mechanisms, but also by nonexocytotic mechanisms, possibly via connexin hemichannels, maxi-anion channels, and reverse operation of transporters (Malarkey and Parpura, 2008). Thus, to evaluate exocytotic release from astrocytes, we used the hGH assay system that has been successfully employed in adrenal chromaffin cells and PC12 cells (Wick et al., 1993). hGH is a protein of �25 kDa, which is unlikely to be released via nonexocytotic pathways. In our study, hGH accumulated in granular structures in the cytoplasm of astrocytes (see Fig. 4), and ATPinduced hGH release was markedly suppressed by BAPTA-AM as well as the TeTX light chain. The suppression of hGH release by TeTX was partial (Fig. 5B); however, several possibilities should be considered. One simple explanation is that TeTX expression was not high enough for complete suppression, since we also observed only partial suppression of hGH release in PC12 cells (Fig. 5C). Another possibility is that TeTX-insensitive VAMP family proteins such as TI-VAMP/VAMP-7 and VAMP-8 are partially involved. Thus, these results indicate that the hGH assay is an ideal system for the quantitative evaluation of Ca[2][1] - and SNARE-dependent exocytosis in astrocytes. 

The most striking finding of the present study is that PKC negatively regulates Ca[2][1] -dependent exocytosis in astrocytes and glioma cells. In neurons and many endocrine cells, neurotransmitter and hormone release are enhanced by PKC activation (Breukel et al., 1998; Morgan et al., 2005; Turner et al., 1999). Furthermore, PKC-dependent phosphorylation is indispensable for degranulation in mast cells and blood platelets (Hepp et al., 2005; Polg�ar et al., 2003). Whereas we demonstrated previously that nonreceptor tyrosine kinases modulate exocytotic release from neurons and PC12 cells in an inhibitory manner (Kawakami et al., 2001; Ohnishi et al., 2001; Takahashi and Ohnishi, 2002), to our knowledge this is the first report of inhibitory regulation of exocytosis by a serine-threonine kinase. PKC phosphorylates a variety of proteins and several mechanisms have been postulated for the PKC-mediated regulation of exocytotic release, including inhibition of K[1] channels (Coffey et al., 1993), phosphorylation of presynaptic proteins (Barclay et al., 2003; Shimazaki et al., 1996), and depolymerization of actin filaments (Ros�e et al., 2001). Thus, it is not surprising that there might be a mechanism for negative regulation in some cell types. 

To elucidate the molecular mechanisms of PKC-dependent regulation of exocytotic release, it is important to identify a PKC substrate essential for such regulation. In secretory cells like PC12 cells, adrenal chromaffin cells, and insulin secreting cells, PKC phosphorylates Ser[187] of SNAP-25, and this phosphorylation event is essential for PKC-dependent enhancement of neurotransmitter and hormone release (Nagy et al., 2002; Shu et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2007). SNAP-25 is not expressed in astrocytes and the ubiquitously expressed homologous protein, SNAP-23, is likely to play an essential role in exocytotic release from these cells. We found that PKC regulates SNAP-23 phosphorylation in astrocytes by two different mechanisms. In cultured astrocytes and C6 cells, Ser[95] , Ser[120] , and Ser[160] are not phosphorylated under basal conditions and robust phosphorylation of these residues is induced by PMA. In contrast, Ser[110] is constitutively phosphorylated in cultured astrocytes and C6 cells, and dephosphorylation is induced in a PKC-dependent manner. Phosphorylation of Ser[95] and Ser[120] of SNAP-23 is essential for the degranulation of mast cells (Suzuki and Verma, 2008); however, the physiological significance of Ser[110] phosphorylation is not yet known. Ser[110] is located in the SNARE motif of SNAP-23 and is conserved from fish to humans. Further studies will be necessary to elucidate the PKC-dependent inhibition of exocytotic release from astrocytes, as well as the physiological significance of PKC-dependent regulation in brain function.

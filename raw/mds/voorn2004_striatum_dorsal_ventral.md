## Putting a spin on the dorsal–ventral divide of the striatum 

Pieter Voorn[1] , Louk J.M.J. Vanderschuren[2] , Henk J. Groenewegen[1] , Trevor W. Robbins[3] and Cyriel M.A. Pennartz[4] 

> 1Department of Anatomy, Research Institute Neurosciences, VU University Medical Center, MF-G-102, PO Box 7057, 1007 MB, Amsterdam, The Netherlands 

> 2Rudolf Magnus Institute of Neuroscience, Department of Pharmacology and Anatomy, University Medical Center Utrecht, 3584 CG Utrecht, The Netherlands 

> 3Department of Experimental Psychology, University of Cambridge, Downing Street, Cambridge CB2 3EB, UK 

4Department of Animal Physiology and Cognitive Neuroscience, Neurobiology section of Swammerdam Institute for Life Sciences, University of Amsterdam, Faculty of Science, 1098 SM Amsterdam, The Netherlands 

Since its conception three decades ago, the idea that the striatum consists of a dorsal sensorimotor part and a ventral portion processing limbic information has sparked a quest for functional correlates and anatomical characteristics of the striatal divisions. But this classic dorsal–ventral distinction might not offer the best view of striatal function. Anatomy and neurophysiology show that the two striatal areas have the same basic structure and that sharp boundaries are absent. Behaviorally, a distinction between dorsolateral and ventromedial seems most valid, in accordance with a mediolateral functional zonation imposed on the striatum by its excitatory cortical, thalamic and amygdaloid inputs. Therefore, this review presents a synthesis between the dorsal–ventral distinction and the more mediolateral-oriented functional striatal gradient. 

The concept of a unified striatal complex incorporating dorsal and ventral divisions has tremendously advanced our knowledge of basal ganglia function [1]. At the same time, the distinction of dorsal and ventral striatum on the basis of their cortical, thalamic and dopaminergic inputs, which do not segregate along discrete lines, has served as an invitation to continue use of the boundary between the nucleus accumbens and caudate–putamen complex as a demarcation line. In fact, this line and the dorsal–ventral boundary have practically become synonymous (Figure 1). A further segregation is imposed on the ventral striatum, in which core and shell regions are distinguished [2]. This review addresses the question of whether recent behavioral, neuroanatomical and neurophysiological findings support these distinctions or whether the striatal map needs re-ordering, to match better the different domains of function and/or specialized neurophysiological processes.

Psychostimulant drugs exert their effects on reward, locomotion and response to conditioned reinforcement mainly through the ventral striatum [4,6,7]. However, to some extent, these effects can also be elicited from the dorsal striatum [8–10], slightly blurring the dorsal– ventral division. Likewise, infusion of opioids throughout the ventral striatum elicits feeding, hypothetically by enhancing the palatability of food, but feeding behavior can also be elicited from several dorsal sites [5]. 

More functional overlap between dorsal and ventral striatum is seen in cognitive functions. The dorsolateral striatum mediates procedural or stimulus–response learning [11,12], whereas the dorsomedial striatum is involved in spatial learning [13]. The latter function has also been demonstrated for the ventral striatum [14], although dorsomedial and ventral striatum could mediate distinct components of spatial learning. A comparable overlap between dorsomedial and ventral striatum exists in the inhibitory control of behavior, although different aspects are regulated by dorsomedial and ventral striatum [15,16]. Similarly, different dimensions of cognitive flexibility are mediated by dorsal and ventral striatum: data from nonhuman primates and humans suggest that task setshifting depends on the dorsal and reversal learning on the ventral striatum [17–19]. 

Thus, ventral portions of the dorsal striatum can also mediate the motivational functions ascribed to the ventral striatum. With respect to cognitive functions, a distinction between dorsolateral striatum on the one hand and dorsomedial and ventral on the other appears most valid. 

## Behavioral differences and similarities: coherence with striatal anatomy 

Cytology emphasizes the similarity of the dorsal and ventral striatal regions. The same neuronal cell types are present throughout the striatum. The projection neurons, the medium-sized spiny neurons (MSNs), make up the vast majority (95%) of the neuronal cell population; a minority consists of several classes of interneurons [20]. The densities and relative proportions of the various cell types do not differ strongly between striatal regions, although cytoarchitectonic and chemoarchitectonic complexity is greater in ventral striatum [21,22]. However, no clear boundary between the dorsal and ventral striatum can be established on the basis of cytoarchitecture, myeloarchitecture or chemoarchitecture [21–23] (Figure 2). Instead of a dorsal–ventral separation, findings in the human striatum suggest a different functional organization. A dorsolateral-to-ventromedial graded density of MSNs has been observed that strikingly resembles not only the zonal organization of several neurochemical gradients but also the pattern of primate corticostriatal inputs [24–26]. Judging from the similarity between the distribution of dynorphin and the pattern of prefrontal cortical inputs in the rat, such a match in organizational patterns might be a more general phenomenon [27]. ## Striatal inputs and outputs conform to a dorsolateral-toventromedial gradient 

The original notion of Heimer and Wilson [1] of a very similar and parallel organization of dorsal and ventral striatal connectivity is still valid. No excitatory cortical, amygdaloid, midline thalamic or intralaminar thalamic fibers, inhibitory pallidal afferents, or modulatory dopaminergic or serotoninergic afferents definitively distinguish between dorsal and ventral striatum [28–31]. This is caused by the slightly oblique dorsomedial-toventrolateral orientation of the longitudinal striatal zones that are reached by the cortical, amygdaloid and thalamic inputs (Figure 3). Typically, the dorsolateral striatum receives predominantly sensorimotor-related information, the ventromedial part collects visceral-related afferents, and striatal areas lying between these extremes receive higher order ‘associational’ information. This functional differentiation can be recognized in all three inputs (Figure 3).

## Regional differences in striatal neurophysiology 

The basic neurophysiological machinery appears to function similarly for dorsal and ventral striatum, in accordance with cytology. MSNs of both striatal sectors exhibit a common set of membrane properties, such as relatively hyperpolarized resting membrane potentials and strong inward rectification. Fast excitatory inputs to MSNs in dorsal and ventral striatum are uniformly mediated by glutamate receptors, with AMPA and kainate subtypes predominating transmission near resting membrane potential and NMDA receptors contributing at depolarized membrane potentials. In vivo, MSNs in both dorsal and ventral striatum exhibit characteristic ‘up’ and ‘down’ states, the first state being attributable to a barrage of glutamatergic inputs [36]. Furthermore, inhibition by way of monosynaptic GABAA-receptor-mediated connections between MSNs has been demonstrated in both striatal sectors [37,38]. Current data also support additional GABA-mediated inhibition of MSNs in dorsal and ventral striatum by fast-spiking GABAergic interneurons [39] (S. Taverna et al., unpublished). 

Although the general neurophysiological characteristics are uniform throughout the striatum, dorsal– ventral differences do exist. They concern more subtle, fine-tuned physiological neuronal properties. Dopamine attenuates glutamatergic inputs to the ventral but not to the dorsal striatum in vitro by a presynaptic mechanism involving D1-like receptors [40,41]. In dorsal striatum, dopamine receptors attenuate AMPA-receptor-mediated input but enhance NMDA-receptor-mediated input by a postsynaptic mechanism involving voltage-gated Ca[2][þ] channels [42]. This combination of actions does not occur in the ventral striatum [40,41]. Furthermore, both striatal regions can sustain long-term synaptic plasticity under particular protocols: high-frequency tetanization of corticostriatal inputs in vitro induces long-term potentiation in the ventral striatum but long-term depression in the dorsal striatum [43,44]. These differences might not only be intrinsic to postsynaptic neural elements but also depend on the properties of afferent pathways differentially innervating dorsal and ventral striatum. 

Although the neurophysiological data seem to conform to a dorsal–ventral division of the striatum, they are far from incompatible with a dorsolateral-to-ventromedial functional organization. This awaits further, systematic comparisons between dorsal–ventral and medial–lateral coordinates – studies that have not been carried out as yet. 

The preceding discussion clearly demonstrates the many similarities between the dorsal and ventral striatum. Structural and functional differences within the 

striatum appear to conform to a dorsolateral-to-ventromedial, rather than a strict dorsal–ventral organizational gradient. The next section will review evidence suggesting that the shell and core of the ventral striatum are in fact an embodiment of this dorsolateral-to-ventromedial functional striatal pattern. 

## Core and shell: protagonists in a dorsolateral-toventromedial striatal organization Behavioral distinctions and similarities between core and shell 

What sets the shell apart from the core and the rest of the striatum is its involvement in the expression of certain innate, unconditioned behaviors. Shell lesions disrupt maternal behavior [45], and infusion of GABAA receptor agonists or AMPA receptor antagonists into the shell evokes, depending on the infusion site, feeding or defensive behavior [5,46]. These shell-elicited affective actions depend on its projection to the lateral hypothalamus, and probably subserve fast adaptive switching of behavior under changeable circumstances [5]. 

Similarities between core and shell concern the prominent role that both regions play in learned behaviors. These regions mediate various forms of Pavlovian and instrumental conditioning [4,5,47–49] but there appears to be a certain distinction regarding the particular tasks subserved by core and shell. For example, acquisition of lever pressing for food is a function of the core but not the shell [5], whereas the opposite holds true for the acquisition of conditioned taste aversion [48]. The enhancement by psychostimulant drugs of behavior influenced by active or passive presentations of conditioned stimuli is also a function of the shell [50,51]. Lesions of the core impair control over the response to conditioned reinforcers, whereas enhancement of this control by psychostimulant drugs depends on the shell [50]. Thus, the shell functions to invigorate certain behavioral responses that are coordinated through the core. 

The same holds true for conditioned stimulus-controlled cocaine seeking: core lesions disrupted performance of this behavior, whereas shell lesions blunted its enhancement by cocaine [52]. Consistent with such roles for core and shell are the observations that cocaine-conditioned locomotion [53] and expression of context-specific psychomotor sensitization [54] depend on the core. Moreover, the acute, unconditioned psychomotor and reinforcing effects of psychostimulants are a more prominent, albeit not exclusive, function of the shell [50,55]. However, exposure to cocainepaired stimuli increased cellular activation in both regions [56] and extinction of cocaine self-administration seems to rely on the shell [57], suggesting that behavioral plasticity in the context of drug addiction is not just a function of the core. 

In conclusion, the functional similarities between core and shell indicate that they process strongly related information, which fits well with the proposed dorsolateral-to-ventromedial functional organization of the striatum. However, the question remains of how certain behaviorally unique functions of the shell fit into this organizational scheme.

## Unique anatomical and neurophysiological features of the shell 

Differential densities of dopamine receptors and dopaminergic fibers parallel shell–core differences in psychostimulant action [2,58] (Figure 2). Indeed, the very first identification of the shell stressed its neurochemical and cytoarchitectonic exclusivity [2]. The unique identity of the shell is also apparent in its ontogeny and its highly complex compartmental organization [58], and differences have been noted between synaptic wiring and morphological features of core and shell MSNs. For example, the density of dendritic spines is higher in core than shell and, compared with the shell, dopaminergic inputs to the core arrive more often on spines than dendritic shafts or somata [59]. 

Electrophysiology shows shell–core differences in neuromodulation of glutamatergic and GABAergic inputs by dopamine, opioids and adenosine [40]. However, core and shell are very similar in terms of ‘skeletal’ microcircuitry, membrane properties and functioning of glutamatergic and GABAergic inputs, as well as in behavioral correlates of single-unit activity [36,60,61]. 

At first sight, the differences between shell and core in neurophysiology, histology and behavior are borne out by differences in afferent and efferent connections. However, a closer look shows the differences in inputs and outputs not to be very strict at all. Thus, prefrontal cortical inputs to the shell arise primarily in the infralimbic and ventral agranular insular areas, whereas the core receives cortical inputs predominantly from the prelimbic, anterior cingulate and dorsal agranular insular areas [29], as part of the dorsolateral-to-ventromedial organizational plan already described (Figure 3). A medial–lateral functional organization is seen in afferents from the hippocampus, the amygdala and the mesencephalic dopaminergic neurons. Hippocampal and parahippocampal fibers both reach shell and core, the parahippocampal afferents dominating in the core, hippocampal afferents in the shell. The ventral hippocampus (representing viscerolimbic functions) projects caudomedially and successively more dorsal hippocampal areas (involved in spatial tasks) project to progressively more lateral parts of rostral shell and core [62]. A similar medial–lateral functional–anatomical organization is found in the afferents from basal amygdaloid subnuclei and from midline and intralaminar thalamic nuclei (Figure 3), and also in the dopaminergic projections [63–65]. VTA (the A10 cell group) projects most medially and the retrorubral area (the A8 cell group) reaches the most lateral areas, both to a large degree ignoring shell-core boundaries. However, the caudomedial shell has an exclusive relationship with the medial part of the VTA [65]. 

So far, core and shell seem part of the dorsolateralto-medial organizational pattern. Nevertheless, clear-cut differences between shell and core are seen in their outputs. The core projects to the classical striatal targets – that is, to the pallidal and nigral complex: the subcommissural ventral pallidum, the most medial part of the entopeduncular nucleus and the dorsomedial substantia nigra pars reticulata [66]. The shell, in addition, reaches areas that are unique for a striatal output: lateral preoptic 

and lateral hypothalamic areas, dopaminergic cell groups and caudal mesencephalic areas associated with locomotor functions. These non-classical projections originate primarily in the (caudo)medial shell, the ventral and lateral shell forming a transitional area that, in a lateral direction, exhibits more and more classical striatal output characteristics [66]. 

In conclusion, core and shell represent the ventral-most sector in the dorsolateral-to-ventromedial striatal functional organization. The ventral and medial extreme in this pattern comprises the caudomedial shell, a region with unique anatomical and neurophysiological characteristics. 

## Striatal divisions: serial or parallel function? 

The reviewed behavioral data indicate similarities between the dorsal and ventral striatal regions, concomitant with medial–lateral differences that support a more graded, dorsolateral-to-ventromedial functional organization. This agrees well with the functional division of the striatum imposed by the mediolateral zonal topography of its excitatory inputs from cortex, thalamus and amygdala. Within this division there are, in turn, dorsal–ventral differences, for example in somatotopy, in density of amygdaloid afferents and in modulatory, plastic and neurochemical characteristics. Such differences could form the basis for the dorsal–ventral distinctions that are seen from a behavioral vantage point. Core and shell are an integral part of the dorsolateral-to-ventromedial functional organizational pattern. The shell, however, seems endowed with special anatomical, neurophysiological and behavioral characteristics. Its efferent projections allow direct behavioral control via hypothalamic and mesencephalic centers, and direct access to the A9 dopaminergic cell group. The latter projection puts the shell in control of dopaminergic input to the entire striatum. Indeed, recent behavioral data support such a dopamine-mediated modulation of processes in other striatal regions, namely the nucleus accumbens core, invigorated by the shell [50]. 

The pattern of striatonigral and nigrostriatal connections has led to the proposal of spiraling striatonigrostriatal pathways in the primate [24], which might argue for a serial, hierarchical organization of behavior involving successively more dorsal parts of striatum. However, neither behavioral nor anatomical or neurophysiological findings in the rat support such a mode of operation. Although the shell might invigorate behaviors mediated by other striatal regions, no data show that the shell orchestrates functions ascribed to more dorsal striatal sectors. The dopaminergic nigrostriatal afferents constitute slow, modulatory (not orchestrating) inputs to a striatal network that processes information propagated by fast glutamatergic inputs supplied in parallel by cortex, thalamus and amygdala. In fact, both dorsal and ventral striatal neurons exhibit firing correlates to all temporal segments of a task sequence, from instructive signals to generation of active movement and ending with reinforcement. Although this does not deny the possibility of additional serial interactions between striatal subregions, it does emphasize the essentially parallel nature of information processing across the striatal complex, with its subregions operating largely in simultaneous fashion.

Instead of a hierarchy, the reviewed data are much more in line with a ‘heterarchical’ arrangement, in which interactions might be mediated not only by striatonigral circuits but also by corticostriatothalamic loops and by intrastriatal interactions. In this scenario, interactions between individual parallel circuits, which could grow stronger in time and with learning [67], might well be essential. 

In conclusion, although dividing the striatum into dorsal and ventral extremes has greatly enhanced our understanding of striatal function, it might now be more appropriate to rotate this cadre of reference by , 458 and include a mediolateral template in the interpretation of future experimental data.

## Free journals for developing countries 

The WHO and six medical journal publishers have launched the Access to Research Initiative, which enables nearly 70 of the world’s poorest countries to gain free access to biomedical literature through the Internet. 

The science publishers, Blackwell, Elsevier, the Harcourt Worldwide STM group, Wolters Kluwer International Health and Science, Springer-Verlag and John Wiley, were approached by the WHO and the British Medical Journal in 2001. Initially, more than 1000 journals will be available for free or at significantly reduced prices to universities, medical schools, research and public institutions in developing countries. The second stage involves extending this initiative to institutions in other countries. 

Gro Harlem Brundtland, director-general for the WHO, said that this initiative was ’perhaps the biggest step ever taken towards reducing the health information gap between rich and poor countries’. 

See http://www.healthinternetwork.net for more information.

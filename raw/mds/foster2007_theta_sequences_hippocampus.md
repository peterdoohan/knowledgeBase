HIPPOCAMPUS 17:1093–1099 (2007) 

## Hippocampal Theta Sequences 

David J. Foster[1,2,3] and Matthew A. Wilson[1,2,3] 

ABSTRACT: The activity of individual hippocampal principal neurons is spatially localized such that each neuron is active only when the animal occupies an area of the environment known as the cell’s place field. Additionally, the activity of hippocampal neurons exhibits a particular temporal relationship to the hippocampal EEG, such that spikes fired by the neuron occur at progressively earlier phases of the co-occurring theta rhythm in the EEG as the animal traverses the place field. This relationship is known as theta precession. A long-standing prediction following the observation of theta precession has been that among a collection of hippocampal neurons recorded simultaneously, the neurons will fire in sequences reflecting the behavioral order of the place fields. Here we examine this prediction. We show that clear, ordered sequences occur during theta, which we name theta sequences, in which a portion of the animal’s spatial experience is played out in forwards order. We further investigate the relationship of theta sequences to phase precession by shuffling spike phases in such a way as to preserve the relationship between spike phase and position. This jitter significantly reduces the prevalence of theta sequences while leaving theta phase precession intact, suggesting that the presence of theta phase precession is not trivially predictive of theta sequences. Finally, we discuss the relationship between theta sequences and individual place fields, and the possible functional role of theta sequences in navigational learning. **V** C 2007 Wiley-Liss, Inc. 

KEY WORDS: place cells; memory; theta precession; navigation; replay 

## INTRODUCTION 

The hippocampus has long been known to be involved in navigational learning (O’Keefe and Nadel, 1978; Morris et al., 1982), and in memory for events (Scoville and Milner, 1957; Olton and Samuelson, 1976; Gaffan, 1994; Steele and Morris, 1999). A key aspect of both navigation and event memory is a requirement for dealing with temporally sequenced information. In the case of navigating rodents, it is known that the principal, excitatory neurons of the hippocampus exhibit spatially selective activity, in that typically each cell is active only within a localized region of the experimental environment known as the cell’s place field (O’Keefe and Dostrovsky, 1971; O’Keefe and Nadel, 1978).

A persistent mystery has been how this spatially restricted activity might relate to the sequential nature of the navigation problem—place cells might tell the rat about where it is, but not about where it’s been or where it’s going. 

Increasing evidence suggests that in fact hippocampal place cells are active in precisely ordered temporal sequences at various times. Following demonstrations of sequential activity during sleep (Skaggs and McNaughton, 1996; Louie and Wilson, 2001; Lee and Wilson, 2002), it was demonstrated that sequential reactivation occurs robustly during the awake state, at the goal or endpoint of a rat’s trajectory (Foster and Wilson, 2006). In these events, the precise sequence of place cell activity corresponding to the recent trajectory is replayed in its entirety, in temporally reversed order, and in a temporally compressed fashion. We now examine a second instance of awake sequential activation that has been proposed to arise from the single-cell phenomenon of ‘‘theta precession.’’ 

The theta rhythm in the EEG recorded from rodent hippocampus is strongly correlated with locomotion, orientation, rearing, and exploratory sniffing (Vanderwolf et al., 1975; Skaggs et al., 1996), as well as with REM sleep. Individual place cell activity has a particular temporal relationship to EEG theta, in that the phase of the theta cycle at which a place cell emits spikes is negatively correlated with the position of the animal within the place field (O’Keefe and Recce, 1993; Skaggs et al., 1996). This phenomenon is known as ‘‘theta precession,’’ because the phase of spikes advances (or ‘‘precesses’’) as the animal runs forward through the place field. 

A long-standing hypothesis about theta precession holds that a corollary should be the occurrence of temporal sequences across a population of several neurons (Skaggs et al., 1996; Wallenstein and Hasselmo, 1997; Hasselmo, 2005; Dragoi and Buzsaki, 2006). There are arguments against the hypothesis. First, it has been reported that place cell firing rates are highly variable from lap to lap (Fenton and Muller, 1998), and so it is possible that activity across cells fails to match up because of differences in spiking probability. Second, although it is clear that sequences follow if, in addition to firing probabilities being stationary, the sizes and shapes of place fields are all the same, as in the model of Skaggs et al. (1996), it is not clear whether a similar effect would follow given the more heterogeneous forms of real place fields. Third, however, is the fact that the

hypothesis assumes a simple and deterministic relationship between position and spike time, when in fact the relation described as phase precession is noisy. In particular, any given position is typically associated with a range of phases. 

The main result of this paper is a demonstration that precise sequences of place cell activity—theta sequences—occur during running behavior. In these sequences, spike times are correlated with the rank positions of the place fields of the cells, over a short timescale of tens of milliseconds. We further investigate the relationship of theta sequences to phase precession by shuffling spike phases in such a way as to preserve the relationship between spike phase and position. This jitter significantly reduces the prevalence of theta sequences while leaving theta phase precession intact, suggesting that the presence of theta phase precession is not trivially predictive of theta sequences. We discuss the possible functional roles of theta sequences, and contrast theta sequences with other forms of sequential place cell activity. Together, these results suggest that all hippocampal unit activity is fundamentally sequential in nature, reflecting its role in navigational learning and memory for events. 

## METHODS 

## Surgery and Electrophysiology 

Three rats were implanted with a multiple electrode microdrive array consisting of either 18 (Rat 1) or 17 (Rats 2 and 3) independently adjustable tetrodes. The array was positioned above region CA1 of the right dorsal hippocampus (4 mm posterior, 2.2 mm lateral with respect to bregma). The postsurgical technique was the same as had been used previously (Wilson and McNaughton, 1993; Lee and Wilson, 2002; Foster and Wilson, 2006). Briefly, the tetrodes were lowered over the course of several days until they rested in the CA1 pyramidal cell layer. Subsequently, over the course of several more days, fine tetrode adjustment was carried out to attempt to maximize the number of pyramidal cells on each tetrode, as indicated by the spread of clusters in spike amplitude space. For stability, recordings were made at least 24 h after the last adjustment. 

Following recording, individual cells were isolated from each tetrode by cluster cutting by hand using XCLUST (Wilson and McNaughton, 1993; Skaggs et al., 1996). Spike times were recorded with precision 0.1 ms. The head position and head direction were measured using two tracker diodes mounted to the front and rear of the drive array, with a sampling rate of 30 Hz. Broadband EEG from the hippocampal CA1 pyramidal cell layer was recorded from each tetrode. All procedures were performed in accordance with institutional guidelines. 

## Behavior 

All the data presented in this paper comes from recordings during running along linear tracks, or along linear portions of a more complex track. In particular, Rats 1 and 2 ran back and forth along a linear track (162 cm long), while the data from Rat 

3 is taken from traversal in both directions of the central arm only of a T-shaped track (132 cm long). In all cases, rats ran for crushed food pellets which could be licked up from food wells placed at either end (except for the outward bound traversals of the central arm of the T track, which were not followed by reward). For both analysis of theta phase precession, and of theta sequence events (see below), a minimum velocity criterion was enforced, to prevent analysis being complicated by coarse changes in behavioral state. Spikes for phase precession analysis, and events for sequence analysis, were only considered during times when the animal’s velocity was at least 10.8 cm/s.

## Individual Place Fields and Theta Precession 

Each of the three rats ran back and forth regularly along the linear track (grey line, Fig. 1a), stopping between runs to eat food from a foodwell at either end, which was replenished after 

every visit. Individual units showed spatially localized activity (red dots, Figs. 1a,b). 

Leftward and rightward directions along the tracks were analyzed separately, yielding two sessions per rat. The numbers of fields satisfying the criteria for inclusion were as follows for each rat (in the format Rightwards, Leftwards): Rat 1: 26, 28; Rat 2: 21, 19; Rat 3: 18, 10 (see Methods). 

Concurrent EEG recording revealed clear theta modulation during running (upper panel, Fig. 1c). Theta precession relative to the bandpass filtered EEG was clear (lower panel, Fig. 1c). Analysis of theta precession yielded a correlation between position and phase, across the entire recording session, for each session for each rat (e.g. Figs. 1d,e). For each animal, the mean value across fields of the linear correlation between phase and position was negative and significantly different from zero, as assessed by t-test (Rat 1: r 5 20.22, t(53) 5 15.16, P < 0.0001; Rat 2: r 5 20.20, t(39) 5 10.75, P < 0.0001; Rat 3: r 5 20.30, t(27) 5 12.34, P < 0.0001).

## DISCUSSION 

Sequential activity across neurons during theta is a longstanding prediction in the hippocampal literature, and yet has not to our knowledge been demonstrated before. Skaggs et al. (1996) suggested that the observation of theta phase precession in the timing of spikes from individual hippocampal neurons might imply that sequential ordering would emerge across collections of hippocampal neurons. Dragoi and Buzsaki (2006) have shown that spikes from pairs of neurons exhibit a temporal separation within each theta cycle that correlates with the physical distance between their corresponding place fields, in line with the general prediction. Interestingly, their results suggest that subsets of place cells may enjoy a more closely coordinated temporal relationship with each other than with the remainder of the population. Indeed, phase precession is interpreted by Dragoi and Buzsaki as a less faithful measure of the underlying correlational structure of place cell activity that averages across, and hence muddles up, functionally different subsets of cells. This argument goes much further than that presented in the current paper, since we do not identify subsets of cells in this way. However, it remains a key point that Dragoi and Buzsaki stop short of demonstrating directly that large numbers of place cells fire in temporal sequences during theta. 

We have demonstrated that theta sequences—clear, forwards ordered sequences of spikes across multiple neurons—occur during the theta state. Therefore, the firing rate within an event is effectively constant—so that there would be no expected correlation between cell number and time reflective of sequential ordering, if spike times were distributed randomly solely on the basis of firing rate. 

Part of the motivation for this work is that sequences may not follow trivially, despite expectations, because the relationship between position and phase is noisy. This issue was investigated by adding phase-jitter to the spike trains. This jitter was so constructed as to replicate the observed relationship between phase and position exactly, while disrupting any further temporal relationships between spikes, by shuffling across spikes from different times. The result was that the occurrence of theta sequences was significantly reduced. Hence, a demonstration of phase precession is not sufficient to infer the presence of theta sequences to the extent to which they actually occur. 

A simple graphical model illustrates how theta sequences across multiple cells are compatible with the observation of phase precession of individual cells (Fig. 7). The left-hand side shows the ordered spiking of several cells in theta sequences. The right-hand side shows just the spikes from one cell. These spikes occur at progressively earlier phases of the theta rhythm. Moreover, the firing rate of the cell as a function of position is skewed (Mehta et al., 2000). One result of this model is that extending the theta sequences so as to look further ahead in time results in the backward elongation of the individual place fields (e.g. see Mehta et al., 2000; Hasselmo and Eichenbaum, 2005). This scheme has the happy consequence that representational changes, such as place field size changes, do not overwrite the original representation, which is effectively preserved among spikes fired in early phases of theta. 

Theta sequences across hippocampal place cells can be contrasted with other forms of sequential activity exhibited by this same neural population. During sleep, place cells are active in temporal sequences, corresponding to prior behavioral experience (Louie and Wilson, 2001; Lee and Wilson, 2002), a phenomenon known as replay. Replay also occurs, however, during the awake state, at the end of each running trajectory, in which

case the behavioral sequence is replayed in reverse temporal order. Taken together, sleep and awake replay share a number of characteristics which distinguish them from the sequential activity observed during theta. Firstly, sleep replay occurs long after the experience, several hours after in the case of slow wave sleep replay, and 24 h after in the case of REM sleep replay, and awake replay also occurs after the experience, although separated by only a few seconds. In contrast, theta sequences occur during experience. Secondly, replay sequences correspond to entire behavioral trajectories between stopping points, whereas theta sequences span only that area in which place fields overlap, typically a much shorter distance. 

It has been proposed that one function of theta sequences would be to recapitulate in a temporally compressed form sequences of events, such as the occupancy of successive locations during navigation, that originally take place on a behavioral timescale of seconds, for the purposes of encoding (Jensen and Lisman, 1996; Skaggs et al., 1996; Jensen and Lisman, 2005). This then brings the spiking activity of the cells involved within the time range of synaptic plasticity mechanisms (Bi and Poo, 1998; Mehta et al., 2000). The present data relates to this proposal directly, since spike times tend to be correlated with cell order within a short, plasticity-relevant time window. It has further been proposed that a second function for theta sequences would be that of information retrieval. Theta sequences provide a short look ahead that can allow, for example, a rat to navigate, by retrieving information about locations immediately ahead of it (Buzsaki, 2005; Hasselmo, 2005). The present data are consistent with this hypothesis, however with the following caveat. It remains to be demonstrated that theta sequences correspond to hypothetical future trajectories rather than simply an ongoing record of the immediate past. 

The hippocampus has been implicated in navigational learning, and memory for events, both fundamentally sequential problems, but it has been difficult to understand exactly how the classical place cell contributes to their solution, given that the activity of a place cell is by definition localized and hence restricted to the current location or behavioral state. However, it is becoming increasingly clear that place cell activity, both within and outside of the classical place field, is itself fundamentally sequential in nature, allowing the exploration of events both ahead of, and behind, the current event. Understanding the full relationship between this dynamic representation and the mnemonic and navigational roles of the hippocampus is the key task ahead.

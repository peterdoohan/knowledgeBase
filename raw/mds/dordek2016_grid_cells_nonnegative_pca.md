|**Abstract**|15|
|Many recent models study the downstream projection from grid cells to|16|
|place cells, while recent data has pointed out the importance of the feedback|17|
|projection. We thus asked how grid cells are affected by the nature of the input from|18|
|the place cells.|19|
|We propose a single-layer neural network with feedforward weights|20|
|connecting place-like input cells to grid cell outputs. Place-to-grid weights were|21|
|learned via a generalized Hebbian rule. The architecture of this network highly|22|
|resembles neural networks used to perform Principal Component Analysis (PCA).|23|
|Both numerical results and analytic considerations indicate that if the components of|24|



1 

|the feedforward neural network were non-negative, the output converged to a|25|
|---|---|
|hexagonal lattice. Without the non-negativity constraint the output converged to a|26|
|square lattice. Consistent with experiments, grid spacing ratio between the first two|27|
|consecutive modules was ~1.4. Our results express a possible linkage between place|28|
|cell to grid cell interactions and PCA.|29|
|**Introduction**|30|
|The system of spatial navigation in the brain has recently received much attention|31|
|[1,2,3]. This system involves many regions, which seem to divide into two major|32|
|classes: regions such as CA1 and CA3 of the hippocampus which contain place cells|33|
|[4,5], vs. regions, such as the medial-entorhinal cortex (MEC), the presubiculum and|34|
|the parasubiculum, which contain grid cells, head-direction cells and border cells|35|
|[6,7,8,9,10]. While the phenomenology of those cells is described in many studies|36|
|[11,12], the manner in which grid cells are formed is quite enigmatic. Many|37|
|mechanisms have been proposed. The details of these mechanisms differ, however|38|
|they mostly share in common the assumption that the animal’s velocity is the main|39|
|input to the system [11,13,14], such that positional information is generated by|40|
|integration of this input in time. This process is termed "path integration" (PI)[15]. A|41|
|notable exception to this class of models was suggested in a paper by Kropff &|42|
|Treves [16], and in a sequel to that paper [17], in which they demonstrated the|43|
|emergence of grid cells from place cell inputs without using the rat's velocity as an|44|
|input signal.|45|
|We note here that generating grid cells from place cells may seem at odds with the|46|
|architecture of the network, since it is known that place cells reside at least one|47|



2 

|synapse downstream of grid cells [18]. Nonetheless, there is current evidence that|48|
|---|---|
|the feedback from place cells to grid cells is of great functional importance.|49|
|Specifically, there is evidence that inactivation of place cells causes grid cells to|50|
|disappear [19], and furthermore, it seems that, in development, place cells emerge|51|
|before grid cells do [20,21]. Thus, there is good motivation for trying to understand|52|
|how the feedback from hippocampal place cells may contribute to grid cell|53|
|formation.|54|
|In the present paper we thus investigated a model of grid cell development from|55|
|place cell inputs. We showed the resemblance between a feedforward network from|56|
|place cells to grid cells to a neural network architecture previously used to|57|
|implement the PCA algorithm [22]. We demonstrated, both analytically and through|58|
|simulations, that the formation of grid cells from place cells using such a neural|59|
|network could occur given specific assumptions on the input (i.e. zero mean) and on|60|
|the nature of the feedforward connections (specifically, non-negative, or excitatory).|61|
|**Results**|62|
|**Comparing Neural-Network results to PCA**|63|
|We initially considered the output of a single-layer neural network and of the|64|
|PCA algorithm in response to the same inputs. These consisted of the temporal|65|
|activity of a simulated agent moving around in a two-dimensional (2D) space (Figure|66|
|1A; see Methods for details). In order to mimic place cell activity, the simulated|67|
|virtual space was covered by multiple 2D Gaussian functions uniformly distributed at|68|
|random (Figure 1B), which constituted the input. In order to calculate the principal|69|



3 

|components we used a [Neuron x Time] matrix (Figure 1C) after subtracting the|70|
|---|---|
|temporal mean, generated from the trajectory of the agent as it moved through the|71|
|place fields. Thus we displayed a one-dimensional mapping of the two-dimensional|72|
|activity, transforming the 2D activity into a 1D vector per input neuron. This resulted|73|
|in the [Neuron X Neuron] covariance matrix (Figure 1D), based on which PCA was|74|
|performed by evaluating the appropriate eigenvalues and eigenvectors.|75|
|To learn the grid cells based on the place cell inputs, we implemented a|76|
|single-layer neural network with a single output (Figure 2). Input to output weights|77|
|were governed by a Hebbian-like learning rule. As described in the Introduction (see|78|
|also analytical treatment in the Methods section), this type of architecture induces|79|
|the output’s weights to converge to the leading principal component of the input|80|
|data.|81|
|The agent explored the environment for a sufficiently long time allowing the|82|
|weights to converge to the first principal component of the temporal input data.  In|83|
|order to establish a spatial interpretation of the eigenvectors (from PCA) or the|84|
|weights (from the converged network) we projected both the PCA eigenvectors and|85|
|the network weights onto the place cells space, producing corresponding spatial|86|
|activity maps. The leading eigenvectors of the PCA and the network’s weights|87|
|converged to square-like periodic spatial solutions (Figure 3A-B).|88|
|Being a PCA algorithm, the spatial projections of the weights were periodic in|89|
|space due to the covariance matrix of the input having a Toeplitz structure [23] (a|90|
|Toeplitz matrix has constant elements along each diagonal). Intuitively, the Toeplitz|91|
|structure arises due to the spatial stationarity of the input. In fact, since we used|92|
|periodic boundary conditions for the agent’s motion, the covariance matrix was a|93|



4 

|circulant matrix, and the eigenvectors were sinusoidal functions [24] [a circulant|94|
|---|---|
|matrix is defined by a single row (or column), and the remaining rows (or columns)|95|
|are obtained by cyclic permutations. It is a special case of a Toeplitz matrix].|96|
|The covariance matrix was heavily degenerate with approximately 90% of the|97|
|variance accounted for by the first 15% of the eigenvectors (Figure 4B). The solution|98|
|demonstrated a 4-fold redundancy. This was apparent in the plotted eigenvalues|99|
|(from largest to smallest eigenvalue, Figure 4A and 4C), which demonstrated a|100|
|fourfold grouping-pattern. The 4-fold redundancy can be explained analytically by|101|
|the symmetries of the system – see analytical treatment of PCA in Methods section|102|
|(specifically Figure 15C).|103|
|In summary, both the direct PCA algorithm and the neural network solutions|104|
|developed periodic structure. However, this periodic structure was not hexagonal|105|
|but rather had a square-like form.|106|
|**Adding a non-Negativity constraint to the PCA**|107|
|It is known that most synapses from the hippocampus to the MEC are|108|
|excitatory [18]. We thus investigated how a non-negativity constraint, applied to the|109|
|projections from place cells to grid cells, affected our simulations. As demonstrated|110|
|in the analytical treatment in the Methods section, we could expect to find hexagons|111|
|when imposing the non-negativity constraint. Indeed, when adding this constraint,|112|
|the outputs behaved in a different manner and converged to a**hexagonal grid**,|113|
|similar to real grid cells. While it was straightforward to constrain the neural|114|
|network, calculating non-negative PCA directly was a more complicated task due to|115|
|the non-convex nature of the problem [25,26].|116|



5 

|In the network domain we used a simple rectification rule for the learned|117|
|---|---|
|feedforward weights, which constrained their values to be non-negative. For the|118|
|direct non-negative PCA calculation, we used the raw place cells activity (after spatial|119|
|or temporal mean normalization), as inputs to two different iterative numerical|120|
|methods: NSPCA (Nonnegative Sparse PCA) and an AMP (Approximate Message|121|
|Passing) based algorithms (see Methods section).|122|
|In both cases we found that hexagonal grid cells emerged in the output layer|123|
|(plotted as spatial projection of weights and eigenvectors: Figure 5A-B, Figure 6A-B).|124|
|When we repeated the process over many simulations (i.e. new trajectories and|125|
|random initializations of weights) we found that the population as a whole|126|
|consistently converged to hexagonal grid-like responses, while similar simulations|127|
|with the unconstrained version did not (compare Figure 3 to Figure 5 -Figure6).|128|
|In order to further assess the hexagonal grid emerging in the output, we|129|
|calculated the mean (hexagonal) Gridness scores ([8], which measure the degree to|130|
|which the solution resembles a hexagonal grid (see Methods)). We ran about 1500|131|
|simulations of the network (in each simulation, the network consisted of 625 place|132|
|cell-like inputs and a single grid cell-like output), and found noticeable differences|133|
|between the constrained and unconstrained cases. Namely, the Gridness score in|134|
|the non-negatively constrained-weight simulations was significantly higher than in|135|
|the unconstrained-weight case (Gridness = 1.07 ± 0.003 in the constrained case vs.|136|
|0.302 ± 0.003 in the unconstrained case. see Figure 7). A similar difference was|137|
|observed with the direct PCA methods (1500 simulations, each with different|138|
|trajectories, Gridness = 1.13 ± 0.0022 in the constrained case vs. 0.27 ± 0.0023 in the|139|
|unconstrained case).|140|

|Another score we tested was a "Square Gridness" score (see Methods) where|141|
|---|---|
|we measured the "Squareness" of the solutions (as opposed to "Hexagonality"). We|142|

|All in all, these results suggest that when direct PCA eigenvectors and neural|148|
|network weights were unconstrained they converged to periodic square solutions.|149|
|However, when constrained to be non-negative, the direct PCA, and the|150|
|corresponding neural network weights, both converged to a hexagonal solution.|151|
|**Dependence of the result on the structure of the input**|152|
|We investigated the effect of different inputs on the emergence of the grid|153|
|structure in the networks' output. We found that some manipulation of the input|154|
|was necessary in order to enable the implementation of PCA in the neural network.|155|
|Specifically, PCA requires a zero-mean input, while simple Gaussian-like place cells|156|
|do not possess this property. In order to obtain input with zero-mean we either|157|
|performed differentiation of the place cells’ activity in time, or used a Mexican-hat|158|
|like (Laplacian) shape (See Methods for more details on the different types of|159|
|inputs). Another option we explored was the usage of positive-negative disks with a|160|
|total sum of zero activity in space (Figure 8). The motivation for the use of Mexican-|161|
|hat like transformations is their abundance in the nervous system [27,28,29].|162|



7 

|We found that usage of simple 2-D Gaussian-functions as inputsdid not|163|
|---|---|
|generate hexagonal grid cells as outputs (Figure 9). On the other hand, time-|164|
|differentiated inputs, positive-negative disks or Laplacian inputsdidgenerate grid-|165|
|like output cells, both when running the non-negative PCA directly (Figure 6), or by|166|
|simulating the non-negatively constrained Neural Network (Figure 5). Another|167|
|approach we used for obtaining zero-mean was to subtract the mean dynamically|168|
|from the every output individually (see Methods). The latter approach, related to|169|
|adaptation of the firing rate, was adopted from Kropff & Treves [16], who used it in|170|
|order to control various aspects of the grid cell's activity. In addition to controlling|171|
|the firing rate of the grid cells, if applied correctly, the adaptation could be exploited|172|
|to keep the output's activity stable, with zero-mean rates. We applied this method in|173|
|our system and in this case the outputs converged to hexagonal grid cells as well,|174|
|similarly to the previous cases (e.g. derivative in time, or Mexican hats as inputs;|175|
|data not shown).|176|
|In summary, two conditions were required for the neural network to|177|
|converge to spatial solutions resembling hexagonal grid cells: (1) non-negativity of|178|
|the feedforward weights and (2) an effective zero-mean of the inputs (in time or|179|
|space).|180|

|**Effect of place cell parameters on grid structure**|208|
|---|---|
|A more detailed view of the resulting grid spacing showed that it was heavily|209|
|dependent on the field widths of the place cells inputs. When the environment size|210|
|was fixed and the output calculated per input size, the grid-spacing (distance|211|
|between neighboring peaks) increased for larger place cell field widths.|212|
|To enable a fast parameter sweep over many place cell field widths (and|213|
|large environment sizes), we took the steady state limit of a high density of place cell|214|
|locations, and used the fast FISTA algorithm to solve the non-negative PCA problem|215|
|(see Methods section).|216|
|We performed multiple simulations, and found that there was a simple linear|217|
|dependency between the place field size and the output grid scale. For the case of|218|
|periodic boundary conditions (i.e. place cells vanish at the boundaries), we found|219|
|that grid scale was S = 7.5sigma+0.85, where sigma was the width of the place cell|220|
|field (Figure 12A). For a different set of simulations with zero boundary conditions|221|
|we achieved a similar relation: S=7.54sigma+0.62 (figure not shown). Grid scale was|222|
|more dependent on place field size and less on box size (Figure 12H). We note that|223|
|for very large environments the effects of boundary conditions diminishes. At this|224|
|limit, this linear relation between place field size and grid scale can be explained|225|
|from analytical considerations (see Materials and methods section). Intuitively, this|226|
|follows from dimensional analysis: given an infinite environment, at steady state the|227|
|length scale of the place cell field width is the only length scale in the model, so any|228|
|other length scale must be proportional to this scale. More precisely, we can provide|229|
|a lower bound for the linear fit (Figure 12A), which depends only on the tuning curve|230|
|of the place cells (see Methods section). This lower bound was derived for periodic|231|



10 

|boundary conditions, but works well even with zero boundary conditions (not|232|
|---|---|
|shown).|233|
|Furthermore, we found that the grid orientation varied substantially for|234|
|different place cell field widths, in the possible range of 0-15 degrees (Figure 12C,D).|235|
|For small environments, the orientation strongly depended on the boundary|236|
|conditions. However, as described in the Methods section, analytical considerations|237|
|suggest that as the environment grows, the distribution of grid orientations becomes|238|
|uniform in the range of 0-15 degrees. Intuitively, this can be explained by rotational|239|
|symmetry – when the environment size is infinite, all directions in the model are|240|
|equivalent, and so we should get all orientations with equal probability, if we start|241|
|the model from a uniformly random initialization. In addition, grid orientation was|242|
|not a clear function of the gridness of the obtained grid cells (Figure 12B). For large|243|
|enough place cells, gridness was larger than 1 (Figure 12E-G).|244|
|**Modules of grid cells**|245|
|It is known that in reality grid cells form in modules of multiple spacings [32,33]. We|246|
|tried to address this question of modules in several ways. First, we used different|247|
|widths for the Gaussian/Laplacian input functions: Initially, we placed a|248|
|heterogeneous population of widths in a given environment (i.e., uniformly random|249|
|widths) and ran the single-output network 100 times. The distribution of grid|250|
|spacings was almost comparable to the results of the largest width if applied alone,|251|
|and did not exhibit module like behavior. This result is not surprising when thinking|252|
|about a small place cell overlapping in space with a large place cell. Whenever the|253|
|agent passes next to the small one it activates both weights via synaptic learning.|254|



11 

|This causes the large firing field to overshadow the smaller one. Additionally, when|255|
|---|---|
|using populations of only two widths of place fields, the grid spacings were dictated|256|
|by the size of the larger place field (data not shown).|257|
|The second option we considered was to use a multi-output neural network,|258|
|capable of computing all “eigenvectors” rather than only the principal “eigenvector”|259|
|(where by "eigenvector" we mean here the vectors achieved under the positivity|260|
|constraint, and not the exact eigenvectors themselves). We used a hierarchical|261|
|network implementation introduced by [34] (see Methods). Since the 1stoutput’s|262|
|weights converged to the 1st“eigenvector”, the network (Figure 13 A-B) provided to|263|
|the subsequent outputs (2nd, 3rd, and so forth) a reduced-version of the data from|264|
|which the projection of the 1st“eigenvector” has been subtracted out. This process,|265|
|reminiscent of Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization, was capable of computing all|266|
|“eigenvectors” (in the modified sense) of the input's covariance matrix. It is|267|
|important to note though that, due to the non-negativity constraint, the vectors|268|
|achieved in this way were not orthogonal, and thus it cannot be considered a real|269|
|orthogonalization process, although, as explained in the Methods section, the|270|
|process does aim for maximum difference between the vectors.|271|
|When constrained to be non-negative, and using the same homogeneous|272|
|"place cells" as in the previous network, the networks' weights converged to|273|
|hexagonal shapes. Here, however, we found that the smaller the “eigenvalue” was|274|
|(or the higher the principal component number) the denser the grid became. We|275|
|were able to identify two main populations of grid-distance "modules" among the|276|
|hexagonal spatial solutions with high Gridness scores (>0.7, Figure 14A-B). In|277|



12 

|addition, we found that the ratio between the distances of the modules was ~1.4,|278|
|---|---|
|close to the value of  1.42 found by Stensola et al. [33]. Although we searched for|279|
|additional such jumps, we could only identify this single jump, suggesting that our|280|
|model can yield up to two “modules” and not more. The same process was repeated|281|
|using the direct PCA method, utilizing the covariance matrix of the data after|282|
|simulation as input for the non-negative PCA algorithms, and considering their ability|283|
|to calculate only the 1st“eigenvector”. By iteratively projecting the 1st“eigenvector”|284|
|on the simulation data and subtracting the outcome from the original data, we|285|
|applied the non-negative PCA algorithm to the residual data obtaining the 2nd|286|
|“eigenvector” of the original data. This “eigenvector” now constituted the 1st|287|
|“eigenvector” of the new residual data (see Methods). Applying this process to as|288|
|many "outputs" as needed, we obtained very similar results to the ones presented|289|
|above using the neural network (data not shown).|290|



|**Discussion**|291|
|---|---|
|In our work we explored the nature and behavior of the feedback projections|292|
|from place cells to grid cells. We shed light on the importance of this relation and|293|
|showed, both analytically and in simulation, how a simple single-layer neural|294|
|network could produce hexagonal grid cells when subjected to place cell-like|295|
|temporal input from a randomly-roaming moving agent. We found that the network|296|
|resembled a neural network performing PCA [22], with the constraint that the|297|
|weights were non-negative. Under these conditions, and also under the|298|
|requirements that place cells have a zero mean in time or space, the first principal|299|
|component in the 2D arena had a firing pattern resembling a hexagonal grid cell.|300|



13 

|Furthermore, we found that that in the limit of very large arenas, grid orientation|301|
|---|---|
|converged to a uniform distribution in range of 0-15°. When looking at additional|302|
|components, grid scale tends to be discretely clustered, such that two modules|303|
|emerge. This is partially consistent with current experimental findings [31,33].|304|
|Furthermore, the inhibitory connectivity between multiple grid cells is consistent|305|
|with the known functional anatomy in this network [35].|306|
|**Place-to-Grid as a PCA network**|307|
|As a consequence of the requirements for PCA to hold, we found that the|308|
|place cell input needed to possess the quality of zero-mean, otherwise the output|309|
|was not periodic. Due to the lack of the zero-mean property in 2D Gaussians we used|310|
|various approaches to impose zero-mean on the input data. The first, in the time|311|
|domain, was to differentiate the input and use the derivatives (a random walk|312|
|produces zero-mean derivatives) as inputs. Another approach was to dynamically|313|
|subtract the mean in all iterations of the simulation. This approach was reminiscent|314|
|of the adaptation procedure suggested in the Kropff & Treves paper [16]. A third|315|
|approach, applied in the spatial domain was to use inputs with a zero-spatial mean|316|
|such as Laplacians of Gaussians (Mexican hats in 2D, or differences-of-Gaussians) or|317|
|negative – positive disks. Such Mexican-hat inputs are quite typical in the nervous|318|
|system [27,28,29], although in the case of place cells it is not completely known how|319|
|they are formed. They could be a result of interaction between place cells and the|320|
|vast number of inhibitory interneurons in the local hippocampal network [36].|321|
|Another condition we found crucial, which was not part of the original PCA|322|
|network, was a non-negativity constraint on the place-to-grid learned weights. While|323|



14 

|rather easy to implement in the network, adding this constraint to the non-convex|324|
|---|---|
|PCA problem was harder to implement. Since the problem is NP-hard [25]  we|325|
|turned to numerical methods. We used three different algorithms [25,37,38] to find|326|
|the leading “eigenvector” of every given temporal based input. As shown in the|327|
|results section, both processes (i.e. direct PCA and the neural network) resulted in|328|
|hexagonal outcomes when the non-negativity and zero-mean criteria were met.|329|
|We note that while our network focused on the projection from place cells to|330|
|grid cells, we cannot preclude the importance of the reciprocal projection from grid|331|
|cells to place cells. Further study will be needed in order to ‘close the loop’ and|332|
|simultaneously consider both of these projections at once.|333|
|**Similar studies**|334|
|We note that similar work has noticed the relation between place-cell-to-|335|
|grid-cell transformation and PCA. Notably, Stachenfeld et al. [39] have|336|
|demonstrated, from considerations related to reinforcement learning, that grid cells|337|
|could be related to place cells through a PCA transformation. However, due to the|338|
|unconstrained nature of their transformation, the resulting grid cells were square-|339|
|like. Furthermore, there has been an endeavor to model the transformation from|340|
|place cells to grid cells using independent-component-analysis [40].|341|
|We also note that there is now a surge of interest in the feedback projection|342|
|from place cells to grid-cells, which is inverse to the anatomical downstream|343|
|direction from grid cells to place cells [18] that has  guided most of the models to-|344|
|date [13,14]. In addition to several papers from the Treves group, in which the|345|
|projection from place cells to grid cells is studied [16,17] , there has been also recent|346|



15 

|work from other groups as well exploring this direction [41,42]. As far as we are|347|
|---|---|
|aware, none of the previous studies noted the importance of the non-negativity|348|
|constraint and the requirement of zero mean input. Additionally, to the best of our|349|
|knowledge, the analytic results and insights provided in this work are novel, and|350|
|provide a mathematically consistent explanation for the emergence of hexagonally-|351|
|spaced grid cells.|352|
|**Predictions of our model**|353|
|Based on the findings of this work, it is possible to make several predictions.|354|
|First, the grid cells must receive zero-mean input over time in order to produce|355|
|hexagonally shaped firing patterns. With all feedback projections from place cells|356|
|being excitatory, the lateral inhibition from other neighboring grid cells might be the|357|
|balancing parameter to achieve the temporal zero-mean [35]. Alternatively, an|358|
|adaptation method, such as the one suggested in Kropff & Treves [16] may be|359|
|applied. Second, if indeed the grid cells are a lower dimensional representation of|360|
|the place cells in a PCA form, the place-to-grid neural weights distribution should be|361|
|similar across identically spaced grid cell populations. This is because all grid cells|362|
|with similar spacing would have maximized the variance over the same input,|363|
|resulting in similar spatial solutions. As an aside, we note  that such a projection may|364|
|be a source of phase-related correlations in grid cells [43]. Third, we found a linear|365|
|relation between the size of the place cells and the spacing between grid cells.|366|
|Furthermore, the spacing of the grid cells is mostly determined by the size of the|367|
|largest place cell – predicting that the feedback from large place cells is not|368|
|connected to grid cells with small spacing. Fourth, we found modules of different|369|



16 

|grid spacings in a hierarchical network with the ratio of distances between|370|
|---|---|
|successive units close to√2. This result is in accord with the ratio reported in|371|
|Stensola et al. [33]. However we note that there is a difference between our results|372|

|**Why Hexagons?**|386|
|In light of our results, we further asked what is special about the hexagonal|387|
|shape which renders it a stable solution. Past works have demonstrated that|388|
|hexagonality is optimal in terms of efficient coding. Two recent papers have|389|
|addressed the potential benefit of encoding by grid cells. Mathis et al. [45]|390|
|considered the decoding of spatial information based on a grid-like periodic|391|
|representation. Using lower bounds on the reconstruction error based on a Fisher|392|

|information criterion, they demonstrated that hexagonal grids lead to the highest|393|

|spatial resolution in two dimensions (extensions to higher dimensions were also|394|
|provided). The solution is obtained by mapping the problem onto a circle packing|395|
|problem. The work of Wei et al. [46] also took a decoding perspective, and showed|396|
|that hexagonal grids minimize the number of neurons required to encode location|397|
|with a given resolution. Both papers offer insights into the possible information|398|
|theoretic benefits of the hexagonal grid solution. In the present paper we were|399|
|mainly concerned with a specific biologically motivated learning (development)|400|
|mechanism that may yield such a solution.   Our analysis suggests that the hexagonal|401|

|non-negativity constraints. In Fourier space, the solution is a hexagonal lattice with|403|
|lattice constant near the peak of the Fourier transform of the place cell tuning curve|404|
|(Figures 15 and 16).|405|

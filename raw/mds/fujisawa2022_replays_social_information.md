## Replays of socially acquired information in the hippocampus 

## Shigeyoshi Fujisawa[1][,][2][,] * and Ayako Ouchi[1] 

1Laboratory for Systems Neurophysiology, RIKEN Center for Brain Science, 2-1 Hirosawa, Wako, Saitama, Japan 

2Department of Complexity Science and Engineering, Graduate School of Frontier Sciences, The University of Tokyo, 5-1-5 Kashiwanoha, Kashiwa, Chiba, Japan 

*Correspondence: shigeyoshi.fujisawa@riken.jp 

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2022.02.005 

Replays of place cell sequences in the hippocampus are thought to underlie memory consolidation for spatial learning. In this issue of Neuron, Mou et al. show that not only self-running but also social observation experiences promote awake remote replays for planning future journeys. 

The hippocampus has been proposed to organize a cognitive map, a mental representation of the spatial information as well as the relationships between entities in the world, which contribute to flexible planning of future actions and recalling of past experiences (O’Keefe and Nadel, 1978; Eichenbaum, 2004). Place cells in the hippocampus are thought to embody this representation for spatial navigation. During active states such as exploration, activities of place cell assemblies register the current location of the self in the environment, as if pinning to your current position on a map (O’Keefe and Dostrovsky, 1971). During resting states, ‘‘replays’’ of place cell sequences of past or future trajectories emerge, as if tracing the routes with your fingers on a map (Foster and Wilson, 2006; Carr et al., 2011). These place cell activities, in active and resting states, seem to reflect the ‘‘semantic’’ and ‘‘episodic’’ memory functions of the hippocampus; map representations by place cells reflect ‘‘general knowledge’’ of the environments, and replays of place cell sequences reflect ‘‘autobiographical experiences’’ in a specific spatiotemporal context (Buzsa´ ki and Moser, 2013). However, past rodent studies on hippocampal functions have mainly focused on spatial navigation based on self-running experiences; humans and animals have the ability to learn not only from self-centered experiences but also from observations of others’ behaviors. How do hippocampal neurons contribute to memorizing and utilizing information acquired through social observations and interactions? This is an important question for understanding the mechanisms of hippocampal functions for orga- 

nizing memories and knowledge that are not limited to spatial navigation. 

In this issue of Neuron, Mou et al. (2022) addressed this question by focusing on the replay phenomena in CA1 during social observation behavior. In this study, they first developed an observational task in which an observer rat (OB) was required to watch the spatial trajectory of a demonstrator rat (Demo) to acquire information about the position of a reward (Figure 1). A single trial of the task consisted of two phases: (1) in the observation phase, the Demo ran in a T-maze and made a random choice for the left or right arm. During the Demo’s trial, the OB stayed in an observation box placed near the T-maze and watched the spatial trajectory of the Demo’s running. (2) In the self-running phase, the Demo was confined to the resting box, and the OB was placed in the T-maze. In this phase, the OB could receive a reward on the same side (left or right) in which the Demo had selected in the previous run. The key to this task was that the OB was required to acquire the information of the rewarded arm through observations of the Demo’s behavior (observation phase) and to memorize the information for self-running in the T-maze (self-running phase). This task was dependent on the hippocampus as pharmacological inactivation of the hippocampus significantly impaired task performance. 

The question that the authors tackled to address was whether the knowledge acquired from observational experiences can induce replays for the planning of the future trajectory of the self. To this end, the authors recorded neuronal activ- 

ities from the hippocampal CA1 region of the OB during task behavior. In particular, the authors focused on the neuronal activity patterns during the observation period. Due to the design of the task, the OB’s future goal position was determined at the moment of the Demo’s choice of either the left or right arm. They found that replay activities associated with sharp wave ripples emerged after the Demo reached the goals. Decoding analysis revealed that the contents of the replay preferentially reflected the OB’s future trajectories from the start to the correct rewarded site in the T-maze, with both forward and reverse replay directions. Moreover, they found that in the control conditions without Demo remote replay events were significantly reduced, and the representations for future reward trajectories disappeared. Altogether, this work by Mou et al. (2022) demonstrated that learning of rewarded positions from social observation promotes awake remote replays for future trajectories for self-planning. 

The information processes of observational learning may include: (1) understanding the actions of others’ behaviors; (2) anticipating the intentions and mental states of others; and (3) planning the self’s actions based on this information. The study by Mou et al. (2022) indicates that awake remote replays in the hippocampus play essential roles in the third process mentioned above in observational learning. However, whether and how cell assembly activities in the hippocampus are involved in the first and second processes are still unknown.

## Evolution of Declarative Memory 

## Joseph R. Manns* and Howard Eichenbaum 

ABSTRACT: The present review considers research on the hippocampus and related areas from humans and experimental animals and makes three main points. First, many of the anatomical details of the hippocampus and adjacent cortical areas in the parahippocampal region are conserved across mammals. Second, the functional role of these areas in declarative memory is also conserved across species. Third, an evolutionary approach will be key to understanding exactly how the local circuitry of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region supports declarative memory. To highlight the utility of this approach, a schematic model is described in which separate streams of spatial and nonspatial information converge on the hippocampus. By this view, a fundamental function of the mammalian hippocampus is to combine incoming information about spatial context from the postrhinal (parahippocampal in primates) cortex and medial entorhinal area with incoming information about nonspatial items from the perirhinal cortex and lateral entorhinal area. The underlying neurobiological computations that arise from local circuitry enable item-in-context memory and are proposed to be fundamental to many examples of declarative memory, including episodic memory in humans and spatial memory in experimental animals. **V** C 2006 Wiley-Liss, Inc.

## INTRODUCTION 

The characterization of declarative and nondeclarative memory systems in the brain has benefited greatly from a comparative, cross-species approach. In particular, some of the most successful attempts to understand the role of the hippocampus in declarative memory have been those that have considered evidence from both humans and experimental animals (Sherry and Schacter, 1987; Squire, 1992; Brown and Aggleton, 2001; Eichenbaum and Cohen, 2001). However, the relevance of findings across species has not always been clear. The mammalian taxon represents a tremendous diversity of habitats, means of locomotion, preferred diets, and social structure (see Fig. 1 for an evolutionary tree of selected species). Mammals also show considerable diversity in neuroanatomy, particularly in the size and organization of neocortical areas. For example, the cerebral cortex of the hedgehog is predominately composed of primary sensory areas and is relatively small when compared with that of primates (Catania et al., 2000). In comparison, the human brain contains numerous large neocortical areas that are devoted to integrating information across modalities. Thus, over the course of hundreds of millions of years of evolution it is quite possible that the hippocampus could have adapted to perform different functions in different mammals, as has been the case for the neocor-

The present review is divided into three sections, each advancing the view that, despite considerable diversity of other cerebral cortical areas, the anatomy and functional role of the hippocampus and neighboring parahippocampal cortical areas are largely conserved. First, the organization and neural pathways of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region are similar across species. In all mammals, a cross-section of the hippocampus reveals the dense cell layers of the CA fields and dentate gyrus (DG) (see Fig. 2), and the intrinsic connectivity of these areas is also remarkably conserved. The connectivity of the cortical areas in the parahippocampal region are less conserved but show an organizational scheme that is shared across species. Second, studies using behavioral paradigms that were adapted for diverse species indicate that the fundamental functional role of the hippocampus is also similar in humans and experimental animals. Across species, the machinery of the hippocampus and adjacent cortices enables rapid formation of stable associations between previously unrelated information from disparate cortical areas. The manner in which these associations are expressed through behavior can vary substantially across species. In humans, the resulting capability for declarative memory is reflected in the conscious recollection of facts and events. In other mammals, memories dependent on the hippocampus are characterized by rapid formation, complex associative properties, and flexible expression. 

Third, we advance the argument that an evolutionary approach is the key to understanding the fundamental functional role of the mammalian hippocampus. In each mammalian species, a remarkably conserved hippocampus is wrapped in a unique neocortex. The divergent neocortex cloaks the conserved hippocampal function by requiring a species-specific translation of incoming sensory information before it reaches the hippocampus and of retrieved associations before they can be reflected in behavior. Thus, results from experimental animals may appear incompatible with results from humans unless the differences in neocortical organization are taken into account. ## CIRCUITRY OF THE MAMMALIAN HIPPOCAMPUS AND PARAHIPPOCAMPAL REGION 

The evolution of the hippocampal memory system is best understood as a contrast between conserved internal circuitry and diversified neocortical inputs. The internal anatomical details of the subdivisions of the hippocampus, that is, the CA fields, DG, and subiculum, vary little from mammal to mammal. These 

structures are connected by a serial, unidirectional path, starting with the DG, and continuing through CA3, then CA1, and then the subiculum (Amaral and Witter, 1995). Yet the similarity among species is not a byproduct of the simplicity of this circuitry. Indeed, the conservation is somewhat surprising, given the complex patterns of connectivity with each projection of this forward pathway. The projection from the DG to CA3 involves a lamellar organization such that, at successive septo-temporal levels, dentate cells project to the entire transverse extent of CA3 at about the same level (Swanson et al., 1978; Gaarskjaer, 1986). In contrast to this dispersion of dentate-CA3 connectivity in the transverse axis, the projection from CA3 to CA1 includes a dispersion of connections in the septo-temporal axis (Ishizuka et al., 1990; Li et al., 1994). The projection from CA1 to subiculum is perhaps the most structured of the intrahippocampal projections and is organized along both the transverse axis and the septo-temporal axis (Amaral et al., 1991). Three nearly discrete transverse columns project to the subiculum such that the third of CA1 cells closest to the subiculum project to the third of the subiculum that is immediately adjacent to CA1. The middle third of CA1 projects to the middle third of the subiculum, and the third of CA1 closest to CA2 (farthest from the subiculum) projects to the third of the subiculum farthest from CA1. Like the CA3-to-CA1 projection, the CA1 to subiculum projection is also distributed along the septo-temporal axis such that CA1 cells at any septo-temporal level project to about one third of the septo-temporal extent of the subiculum. The contrast that emerges from these considerations is that CA3 and the DG tend to intermix information whereas the CA1-subiculum projection segregates information. The anatomy of the hippocampus region has been studied in the most detail in the rat, cat, and monkey (see Amaral and Witter, 1995; Witter et al., 2000b for reviews), but similarities have also been observed in species as diverse as humans (Duvernoy and Bourgouin, 1998), tree shrews (Keuker et al., 2003), and hedgehog tenrecs (Kunzle and Radtke-Schuller, 2001). 

In sharp contrast to the conservation of hippocampal circuitry, the neocortical regions that are the ultimate origin of hippocampal inputs differ substantially from species to species. For example, there are numerous dissimilarities in the neocortex that reflect general differences between small-brained and big-brained mammals, such as cortical size, laminar stratification, and number of polymodal association areas [(Krubitzer and Kaas, 2005); also see Fig. 2]. Further, the amount of tissue devoted to a particular sensory modality also varies substantially between species, such that olfaction (e.g., rats), vision (e.g., primates), audition (e.g., bats), or somatosensation (e.g., star nosed moles) have become disproportionately represented in the brain in different animals (Krubitzer and Kaas, 2005). 

Differences in the organization of the cerebral cortex between mammals are particularly relevant because the hippocampus sits at a confluence of cortical (and subcortical) inputs. Yet, the majority of cortical inputs do not arrive directly in the hippocampus but instead arrive indirectly through the surrounding entorhinal, perirhinal, and postrhinal (parahippocampal in primates) cortices, here collectively called the parahippocampal region. In both the rat and the macaque, many unimodal and polymodal cortical regions project to the perirhinal cortex or to the postrhinal/parahippocampal cortex (Suzuki and Amaral, 1994; Burwell and Amaral, 1998). The incoming information is presumably processed and then passed on to the entorhinal cortex (EC), where it is likely further processed before being relayed to the hippocampus. Thus, the hippocampus and parahippocampal region can be described as a hierarchy of connectivity in which the perirhinal and postrhinal/parahippocampal cortices are positioned near the top of the hierarchy and funnel information into the EC and the hippocampus (Lavenex and Amaral, 2000; Witter et al., 2000a). 

Although the characterization of these pathways as hierarchical applies to both the rat and the macaque, several details of anatomy differ between the species. In particular, compared with that in the macaque, the patterns of connectivity in the rat less clearly conform to a strict hierarchy. In the macaque, more than two-thirds of the cortical input to the EC originates in either the perirhinal or postrhinal/parahippocampal cortices (Suzuki and Amaral, 1994). In comparison, less than one-fourth of cortical afferents of the EC in rats originate in these regions (Burwell and Amaral, 1998). Some, but not all, of this difference can be accounted for by considering the prominent olfactory input of the rat that bypasses the perirhinal cortex and projects directly to the EC (Insausti et al., 2002). Thus, the more rigidly serialized hierarchy in the monkey suggests that the primate EC (and therefore the hippocampus) receives information that is on aver- 

age even more highly processed than it is in the rodent. Other details of the parahippocampal region also differ somewhat from species to species. For example, the rat EC has been divided into five anatomically defined regions whereas the macaque EC has been divided into six subregions (Insausti, 1993). 

Nevertheless, the overall pattern of connectivity of the parahippocampal region is similar for rats and monkeys. In addition to the shared pattern of hierarchical connectivity, the available anatomical data from both species sketch out parallel pathways that arise in the perirhinal and postrhinal/parahippocampal cortices and terminate in the hippocampus. That is, although the perirhinal, postrhinal/parahippocampal, and entorhinal cortices funnel information to the hippocampus, they appear to do so through two partially distinct channels. First, in both the rat and the macaque, the perirhinal and parahippocampal/postrhinal cortices receive different complements of neocortical input (Suzuki and Amaral, 1994; Burwell and Amaral, 1998). The parahippocampal/postrhinal cortex receives inputs from many areas that appear to be involved in processing the spatial content of sensory information. In contrast, the perirhinal cortex receives inputs from areas concerned with identifying the nonspatial identity of stimuli. For example, the monkey parahippocampal cortex receives more inputs from areas along the dorsal visual stream thought to be important for visually-guided actions, whereas the monkey perirhinal cortex receives more inputs from areas along the ventral visual pathway thought to be important for object recognition (Suzuki and Amaral, 1994). Second, the separation of spatial and nonspatial information is at least partially maintained until it is combined in the hippocampus. In both the rat and the macaque, the perirhinal cortex tends to project more to the lateral entorhinal area (LEA) of the EC, and the postrhinal/parahippocampal cortex tends to project more to the medial entorhinal area (MEA) (Witter et al., 2000a). 

Further, LEA and MEA send separate projections to the hippocampus in both the rat and the macaque. Both pathways project to each of the four hippocampal subregions, but the pattern in which the fibers from both pathways terminate in hippocampal targets differs between CA3 and DG on one hand, and CA1 and subiculum on the other (Witter et al., 2000a). The projections from LEA and MEA target the same subsets of dentate and CA3 cells. In contrast, the projections from LEA target portions of CA1 and subiculum differ from the portions targeted by the projections arising in MEA. In particular, projections arising in LEA target CA1 and subiculum cells near the subiculum/CA1 border, whereas projections arising in MEA target CA1 and subiculum cells farthest from the subiculum/CA1 border. Thus, information passing through LEA and MEA appears to be combined in the DG and CA3 but kept separate in the subiculum and CA1. The specific topography of LEA and MEA inputs to the hippocampus is similar not only for the rat (Witter, 1993) and macaque (Witter and Amaral, 1991) but for all species for which detailed anatomical information is available, including bats (Buhl and Dann, 1991), mice (Van Groen et al., 2002), and cats (Witter and Groenewegen, 1984). 

Based on these observations, the hierarchical characterization of the mammalian hippocampus and parahippocampal region may need to be balanced with a depiction of parallel and partially distinct pathways for incoming information. By this view, a channel of nonspatial information reaches the hippocampus by way of the perirhinal cortex and LEA, and a channel of spatial information arrives via the parahippocampal/postrhinal cortex and MEA (Witter et al., 2000a). However, the notion of parallel, functionally distinct, input streams is tempered by the presence of a substantial projection from parahippocampal/postrhinal cortex to perirhinal cortex (and a smaller return projection) in addition to connections between LEA and MEA (Burwell, 2000). That is, a depiction of the region’s anatomy that includes a hierarchical component and a component of parallel pathways may be the description that captures best the commonalities across species. 

In sum, the conservation of the hippocampus contrasts with the diversity of the mammalian neocortex. The cross-species similarity of the parahippocampal region sits somewhere in between, with the entorhinal-hippocampal projections being much more similar across species than the neocortical-parahippocampal projections. Yet even if the parahippocampal region was identical between rats and monkeys, differences between the species in 

terms of the kinds of information processed by the perirhinal and postrhinal/parahippocampal cortices would be virtually assured by the substantial neocortical differences. Furthermore, because the inputs to the parahippocampal region largely determine the input to the hippocampus, one might also expect to observe differences in the content of information that is available to the rat and the monkey hippocampus. Although the rat and macaque parahippocampal regions may share a separation of spatial and nonspatial inputs between postrhinal/parahippocampal cortex and perirhinal cortex, respectively, the details of the spatial and nonspatial information reaching these structures likely differs markedly between the species. 

## FUNCTIONAL HOMOLOGY OF THE HIPPOCAMPUS AND PARAHIPPOCAMPAL REGION ACROSS MAMMALS 

A wealth of findings from functional studies agree with the anatomical data in that both lines of evidence reveal a strong evolutionary conservation for the hippocampus and parahippocampal region. However, unlike in the anatomical studies, an understanding of the functional similarity was not clear in the earliest studies and was achieved only through considerable effort to reconcile apparent inconsistencies in the evidence from studies on humans and experimental animals. Indeed, some the initial work in experimental animals that followed the seminal report on the amnesic patient H.M. (Scoville and Milner, 1957) suggested that the hippocampus might be important for a cognitive ability outside the province of memory, such as response inhibition (reviewed in O’Keefe and Nadel, 1978; Cohen and Eichenbaum, 1993; Eichenbaum and Cohen, 2001). Subsequent work in experimental animals identified a role in memory for the hippocampus (Hirsch, 1974; O’Keefe and Nadel, 1978; Olton et al., 1979), yet even these revised ideas did not entirely agree with the ideas generated by the work with amnesic patients. For example, one prominent view based on data from rats was that the hippocampus was involved in the subset of memories that contained spatial information (O’Keefe and Nadel, 1978). This view was inspired by the observation of severe deficits in some types of spatial memory following hippocampal damage and by the observation that many hippocampal pyramidal cells fire selectively when animals occupy a particular location in space. In comparison, the prevailing view based on work with humans was that the hippocampus was important for both spatial and nonspatial memory. 

The functional homology of the hippocampus was established most clearly by considering data from tasks that have been adapted for use across species. Several tasks have been successfully administered to both humans and experimental animals, but a challenge has been that experimental animals approach some tasks with a different strategy than human participants. For example, in the concurrent discrimination task, human participants often attempt to memorize the pairs of rewarded and unrewarded objects (Hood et al., 1999; Bayley et al., 2005). ## Trace Eyeblink Conditioning 

One task that has been particularly informative has been classical conditioning of the eyeblink response, in which repeated presentations of a tone conditioned stimulus (CS) followed by an airpuff unconditioned stimulus (US) result in eyeblink conditioned responses (CRs) emitted in response to the tone. Standard delay eyeblink conditioning, in which the CS and US overlap and coterminate, is supported by a carefully described circuit in the cerebellum and hindbrain (Christian and Thompson, 2003). Insertion of a 1-s silent trace interval between the CS and US leads performance to depend additionally on forebrain structures, including the hippocampus. The importance of the hippocampus for trace eyeblink conditioning has been established for rabbits (Solomon et al., 1986; Moyer et al., 1990), mice (Tseng et al., 2004; Ohno et al., 2005; Sakamoto et al., 2005), rats (Weiss et al., 1999), and humans (McGlinchey-Berroth et al., 1997; Clark and Squire, 1998). It is unclear exactly what contribution the hippocampus and parahippocampal region make to trace eyeblink conditioning, but one possibility is that the hippocampus interacts with other forebrain structures such as the prefrontal cortex to maintain a representation of the CS over the trace interval so that information about the tone CS and airpuff US arrive together in the cerebellum (Clark et al., 2002). 

## Recognition Memory 

Another task that has helped clarify the similarity in hippocampal function has been the delayed nonmatch to sample (DNMS) task and other similar recognition memory tests that employ unique stimuli on each trial (Gaffan, 1974; Mishkin and Delacour, 1975). The typical finding is that participants with damage to hippocampus and parahippocampal region perform normally when a short interval separates the sample and test stimuli but show impaired performance when the study-test interval outstrips the capabilities of frontal lobe-dependent working memory. This pattern of delay-dependent memory impairment on the DNMS task has been observed for rats (Mumby and Pinel, 1994; Clark et al., 2001), monkeys (Meunier et al., 1993; Zola et al., 2000; Nemanic et al., 2004), and humans (Reed and Squire, 1997; Manns et al., 2003a). More recently, researchers have debated whether the hippocampus itself is necessary for normal recognition memory or whether the parahippocampal region can support recognition in some circumstances even in the absence of hippocampal function (Vargha-Khadem et al., 1997; Brown and Aggleton, 2001; Squire et al., 2004). Nevertheless, the results from the DNMS task and other tests of recognition memory have indicated that the mammalian hippocampus and parahippocampal region are important for at least some component of recognition memory. 

## Transitive Inference 

A third task that has been administered to humans and experimental animals is the transitive inference task. In this task, participants learn a series of overlapping stimulus pairings that contain a hidden hierarchy. For example, in one version of the task, rats were rewarded for selecting odor B when presented with odor A and were rewarded for selecting odor C when presented with odor B (Bunsey and Eichenbaum, 1996). Both healthy rats and rats with hippocampal damage learned these overlapping stimulus pairs, but only the healthy rats responded to odor C when presented with odor A, demonstrating that they had learned the two paired associates and could infer the relationship between the indirectly related items A and C. The finding of a critical role for the hippocampus or parahippocampal region in transitive inference extends to other studies in both rats and monkeys (Dusek and Eichenbaum, 1997; Buckmaster et al., 2004). In addition, several functional imaging studies in humans have identified increased activity in the medial temporal lobe during transitive inference judgments (Nagode and Pardo, 2002; Heckers et al., 2004; Preston et al., 2004). There is debate as to how the components of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region contribute to transitive inference (Frank et al., 2003; Driscoll et al., 2004), but one possibility is that the hippocampus is important for retrieving information that can be used in a flexible manner, such as in the inference that A relates to C. 

## Retrograde Amnesia 

Another finding that supports the idea of functional conservation of the mammalian hippocampus is the observation of retrograde amnesia. This evidence comes not in the form of a specific task that has been examined across species but as a pattern of results from studies using a variety of tests of premorbid memory in amnesic patients and experimental animals with damage to the hippocampus. The typical finding from studies with experimental animals is that damage to the hippocampus immediately after training impairs subsequent performance, whereas damage to the hippocampus delayed 1 week to 1 month after training has no subsequent effect (for a review, see Squire et al., 2001). These findings are qualitatively very similar to the pattern of temporally graded retrograde amnesia in H.M. and other amnesic patients (Kapur, 1999). For example, one study assessed amnesic patients’ memory for newsworthy incidents that occurred a different times prior to the patients sustaining damage thought to be limited to the hippocampus (Manns et al., 2003b). The results from this study with humans suggested that the impact of the hippocampal damage extended much farther into the timeline of premorbid memories than was the case with experimental animals, up to 10 years prior to onset of damage. Despite the difference in the age of affected memories between humans and experimental animals, the consistent pattern of impaired memory for information obtained shortly before hippocampal damage and spared memory for information obtained remotely prior to damage suggests that the hippocampus is needed for some period, but eventually memories become independent of the hippocampus through a process of consolidation (Squire et al., 2001). ## Remaining Areas of Discontinuity? 

Taken together, the results from eyeblink classical conditioning, the DNMS task, the transitive inference task, and temporallygraded retrograde amnesia, strongly suggest that the hippocampus and parahippocampal region serve a similar role in memory in both humans and in experimental animals. That is, it is clear that the extended hippocampal memory system, including the parahippocampal region, is important for examples of single-trial learning, including recognition memory judgments, for flexible expression of memory, as in the arbitrary relationships formed and expressed in the transitive inference task, and for trace eyeblink conditioning. It also seems clear from the observations on retrograde amnesia that the hippocampus plays a time-limited role in these examples of memory. These four points of contact between species argue in favor of evolutionary conservation of hippocampal function, yet they also illustrate the difficulty in characterizing the role of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region in declarative memory. Summarizing the common memory operations of trace eyeblink conditioning, recognition memory judgments, transitive inference, and consolidation is a difficult task. Moreover, any understanding of the function of the hippocampal memory system would have to first reconcile what appears to be a point of potential discontinuity between species; The prominence of spatial correlates in the firing patterns of hippocampal cells in rodents contrasts with the frequent characterization of the human hippocampus as engaged most strongly by episodic memory. We therefore next consider whether the hippocampus is specialized for spatial memory in rats and for episodic memory in humans. 

Evidence has emerged from several recent studies that the rat hippocampus is not specialized for spatial memory but instead, like the hippocampus in monkeys and humans, contributes to declarative memory in a more general manner. In particular, there is accumulating evidence that nonspatial as well as spatial factors modulate the activity of hippocampal neurons in rats, monkeys, and humans. For example, one study of rats performing an odorcued recognition memory task evaluated the firing patterns of CA1 and CA3 pyramidal neurons in relationship to either the animal’s location or to nonspatial factors including odor identity and whether the stimulus was a match or a nonmatch with a previously presented stimulus (Wood et al., 1999). The study found that similar proportions of cells had firing rates that were modulated by spatial and nonspatial factors. Perhaps as important, the study also found that many cells responded not to solely spatial or nonspatial factors but instead fired in association with a combination of place, odor, and match/nonmatch status. These findings are very similar to those observed in a study in which single hippocampal neurons were recorded while human participants navigated through a computer-generated virtual town (Ekstrom et al., 2003). Similar to the findings with rats, equivalent proportions of 

the cells fired in association with particular landmarks, views of the environment, or places occupied in the virtual town. Also, many of these cells fired selectively in association with specific combinations of a place and the view of a particular scene or a particular goal. The results of these studies suggest that, although the activity of hippocampal cells might be modulated by location, the activity is equally likely to be modulated by nonspatial factors or by a combination of spatial and nonspatial factors. 

Although studies such as those described earlier indicate that hippocampal activity might often be activated by specific combinations of spatial and nonspatial stimuli, it also appears to be the case that the factors driving activity can be much more abstract than particular locations, scenes, or odors. In another study (Hampson et al., 2004), monkeys learned several matching-tosample problems, then were shown novel stimuli that shared visual features with the trained cues. Many hippocampal neurons encoded meaningful categories of stimulus features across many situations. Kreiman et al. (2000a) characterized hippocampal firing patterns in humans during presentations of a variety of visual stimuli. They reported a substantial number of hippocampal neurons that fired when the subject viewed specific categories of material (e.g., faces, famous people, animals, scenes, houses) across many exemplars of each. A subsequent study showed that these neurons are activated when a subject simply imagined the exemplar, supporting a role for hippocampal networks in recollection of specific memories that goes beyond any perceptual function (Krieman et al., 2000b). Further, in another study, rats were trained to run on a T-shaped maze in such a way that portions of the two possible routes overlapped (Wood et al., 2000). In many instances, cells whose activity showed spatial specificity fired when the rat traversed the overlapping segment of the maze but did so for only one of the two routes. That is, trial type (Route 1 vs. Route 2) proved to have a large influence on the spatially correlated firing in this task. This combination of findings across species provides compelling evidence that some hippocampal cells represent abstract features of nonspatial stimuli that appear in different experiences. 

In addition to the observation that many hippocampal neurons are activated best by a combination of factors, another commonality across species is that these neural responses can emerge rapidly in the presence of new information. For example, in one study rats were trained on an auditory fear conditioning task in which a white noise was paired with a periorbital shock (Moita et al., 2003). Prior to fear conditioning, few hippocampal cells were activated by an auditory stimulus. Following one conditioning session, many cells fired briskly to the tone, yet did so only when the animal was in a particular place. Another recent study examined the firing properties of hippocampal neurons in monkeys performing a task where they rapidly learned new scene– location associations (Wirth et al., 2003). Just as the monkeys acquired a new response to a location in the scene, neurons in the hippocampus changed their firing patterns to become selective to particular scenes. These scene–location associations persist even long after learning is completed (Yanike et al., 2004). The task was adapted and administered to humans in a functional imaging study (Law et al., 2005). The results were similar to the monkey

The observation that hippocampal cells can rapidly acquire representations of new spatial and nonspatial information and that these firing patterns persist does not mean that those representations are unique or rigidly held. Indeed, the results of several experiments indicate that the activity of a single hippocampal neuron is capable of representing multiple pieces of information in a state or context-dependent manner. For example, single-unit recording studies in rats indicate that hippocampal neurons have quite different and sometimes unrelated spatial or nonspatial firing patterns when animals are in different or altered environments (Skaggs and McNaughton, 1988; Anderson and Jeffery, 2003; Leutgeb et al., 2005; reviewed in Jeffery et al., 2004). The large variety of patterns of context dependent activity of hippocampal neurons and their ability to reflect a wide range of spatial and nonspatial features of stimuli and behaviors suggests that the scope of information encoded by hippocampal neurons is as broad as that of all attended experience. This characterization seems to apply equally well to the scope of memory supported by the human hippocampus. 

Another potential point of discontinuity regarding the role of the hippocampus in humans and experimental animals involves the question of whether the hippocampus supports episodic memory in animals, as it does in humans. Episodic memory is characterized as the ability to replay in mind a particular episode in one’s life, and this capacity has been closely, sometimes uniquely, identified with hippocampal function in humans (Tulving, 1983, 2002). If episodic memory is an ability unique to the human hippocampus, this specialization would represent an important difference in the function of the hippocampus between humans and experimental animals. The difficulty in addressing this issue is that experimental animals are unable to report on their subjective experience. 

One approach to studying episodic memory in experimental animals has been to adopt less mentalistic operational definitions of episodic memory. For example, consistent with Tulving’s (1972) original characterization, Clayton and Dickinson (1998) emphasized that episodic memory contains information about the \what," \where," and \when" of an event. Damage to the hippocampus impairs this capacity (Ergorul and Eichenbaum, 2004), and thus, by this definition, it appears that animals other than humans are capable of episodic-like memory and that this ability depends on the hippocampus. 

Another approach has adopted signal detection theory to extract and quantify the relative contribution of episodic-like recollection and semantic-like familiarity to recognition memory judgments (Yonelinas, 2001). A recent study on rats (Fortin et al., 2004) used this method and observed that animals with damage to the hippocampus were disproportionately impaired on the measurement of episodic-like recollection, similar to the results from a study of amnesic patients with suspected hippocampal damage (Yonelinas et al., 2002). However, in another study, patients with damage thought to be restricted to the hippocampus were similarly impaired on measurements of recollection and familiarity (Wais et al., 2006). The discrepancy between these studies 

highlights the importance of an evolutionary perspective when considering data from humans and experimental animals. It is unclear whether the differences in results between studies occurred because of differences in methodological details, such as testing parameters or locus of brain damage, or because of differences between species. For example, it is possible that differences between humans and rats regarding cortical areas outside the hippocampus and parahippocampal region could have led to different patterns of performance as measured by estimates of recollection and familiarity. A key point is that the debate centers on the questions other than the homology of the hippocampus. For example, there is continuing debate as to whether the hippocampus plays an exclusive role in episodic memory in humans but not as to whether the role of the hippocampus in episodic memory differs between humans and experimental animals. 

## A SPECIES-GENERAL MECHANISTIC ACCOUNT OF THE HIPPOCAMPUS 

A challenge in memory research has been to provide a mechanistic account that could connect the anatomy of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region to the examples of memory that depend on these structures. That is, it is now clear that the hippocampus and parahippocampal region are important for the initial acquisition and temporary maintenance of declarative memory, memory that is often acquired rapidly and that can be associative, abstract, and context-dependent. Yet it is unclear exactly how these regions support this capacity. 

One promising approach has been the development of models of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region that depict computations arising from local circuitry. One particularly influential model was based on the hierarchical connectivity of the region and proposed that the hippocampus served as a central node of rapid synaptic change (McClelland et al., 1995). By this view, information could be stored quickly through a limited number of synaptic changes in the hippocampus. The model further specified that the stored information could then be gradually interleaved into existing networks of knowledge such that the original connections in the hippocampus would become unnecessary. That is, the model described how the process of consolidation might occur and was based on the idea that several neocortical nodes would be joined by the hippocampus into a subnetwork whose coactivity could be propelled by repetition, rehearsal, or spontaneous reinstantiation during sleep. 

Other models have considered different aspects of the type of memory supported by the hippocampus and parahippocampal region (Teyler and Discenna, 1986; Lorincz and Buzsaki, 2000; Hasslemo et al., 2002). For example, a model by Alvarez and Squire (1994) also addressed the concept of consolidation but in addition focused on how the hierarchical arrangement of the region might support binding of previously unrelated items into arbitrary associations. The idea was that the position of the hippocampus as a central node in a hierarchical network would allow linking of information from widespread neocortical sites. ## A Model Based on a Cross-Species Approach 

A benefit of models such as those described earlier is that the concepts should apply equally well to any mammal, provided that the anatomical constraints included in the model are not violated by any species-specific anatomical idiosyncrasies. Indeed, additional computational models will be the most useful to an evolutionary approach when they are based on facets of the anatomy that are shared across mammals. To this end, outlined in Figure 3 is a framework that incorporates many of the ideas highlighted in earlier models plus the anatomical evidence that information arrives at the hippocampus via two largely parallel pathways 

through the parahippocampal region. In this model, information that includes spatial content arrives via the parahippocampal cortex and medial EC. In contrast, information regarding stimulus identity arrives via the perirhinal cortex and lateral EC. Consistent with the anatomy, these two pathways are combined in area CA3 and DG but kept separate in area CA1 and subiculum. Central to the model is the idea that CA3 and DG are uniquely positioned to make associations between individual items (e.g., objects, odors, sounds) and the place in which the item was encountered, in other words, item-context associations. On the other hand, CA1 and subiculum might play a role in comparing or combining these associations with a more veridical representation of the sensory experience. One intriguing possibility is that this comparison might allow for multiple arbitrary associations to be linked in some organized way, such as along a temporal dimension. Thus, memory for a particular event, memory that includes information about particular items encountered at a particular time and place, would be supported by an interaction between neocortical, parahippocampal, and hippocampal structures. Although many structures would contribute in an important way to the acquisition of a detailed memory, the structures

## Findings in Support of the Model 

## Perirhinal cortex and LEA 

This model is based upon not only the cross-species similarities in anatomical pathways but also physiological and behavioral findings from several mammalian species. For example, considerable evidence indicates that neurons in the perirhinal cortex and parts of the EC are involved in the representation of individual stimuli. Electrophysiological studies on monkeys and rats performing simple recognition tasks have identified three general types of responses (Desimone, 1992; Fuster, 1995; Brown and Xiang, 1998; Suzuki and Eichenbaum, 2000). First, many cells exhibited selective tuning to memory cues such as odors or visual stimuli. Second, some cells continued firing in a stimulus-specific fashion during a memory delay, indicating the persistence of a stimulus representation. Third, many cells showed enhanced or suppressed responses to stimuli when they reappear in a recognition test, indicating involvement in the memory judgment. Complementary studies in animals with damage to the perirhinal cortex indicate that it may be critical to memory for individual stimuli in the DNMS task in rats (Otto and Eichenbaum, 1992; Mumby and Pinel, 1994) and monkeys (Suzuki et al., 1993), and in novel object recognition in rats (Kesner et al., 2001; Gilbert and Kesner, 2003; Winters and Bussey, 2005a,b). In addition, Herzog and Otto (1997) reported that perirhinal cortex is critical to cued but not contextual fear conditioning. These and other data have led several investigators to the view that the perirhinal cortex is specialized for identifying the memory strength of individual stimuli, and this role is evident in findings from single neuron recordings, experimental ablations, immediate early gene activation, and functional imaging in humans (Brown and Aggleton, 2001; Henson et al., 2003; Aggleton et al., 2004). 

## Parahippocampal/postrhinal cortex and MEA 

Conversely, the postrhinal/parahippocampal cortex and MEA may be specialized for processing spatial context. However perirhinal and LEA neurons have poor spatial coding properties, and postrhinal/parahippocampal and MEA neurons show strong spatial coding (Quirk et al., 1992; Burwell et al., 1998; Burwell and Hafeman, 2003; Fyhn et al., 2004; Hargreaves et al., 2005). Further, the immediate early gene fos is activated in perirhinal cortex by novel visual cues, but fos is activated in the postrhinal cortex by a spatial rearrangement of the cues (Wan et al., 1999) and in spatial learning (Vann et al., 2000). In addition, object recognition is impaired following perirhinal damage, whereas objectlocation recognition is deficient following postrhinal damage in rats (Gaffan et al., 2004) and monkeys (Malkova and Mishkin, 

2003; Alvarado and Bachevalier, 2005). Similarly, Norman and Eacott (2005) reported data suggesting that perirhinal lesions led to greater memory for object pairings whereas postrhinal lesions led to greater impairment in memory for the context in which an object was presented. These findings are consistent with functional imaging studies in humans that dissociate object processing in perirhinal cortex from spatial processing in the parahippocampal cortex (Bellgowan et al., 2004; Pihlajamaki et al., 2004). Furthermore, perirhinal cortex is activated in association with the memory strength of specific stimuli (Henson et al., 2003), whereas the parahippocampal cortex is activated during recall of spatial and nonspatial context (Davachi and Wagner, 2002; Bar and Aminoff, 2003; Ranganath et al., 2003). 

This putative functional segregation in the parahippocampal region is mitigated by additional polymodal inputs to perirhinal cortex and substantial interconnections between perirhinal cortex, postrhinal cortex, and entorhinal areas. Furthermore, Burwell and colleagues (2004a,b) and Bucci et al. (2000, 2002) found that perirhinal and postrhinal cortices both contribute to memory for context [but not spatial learning; see also (Aggleton et al., 2000, 2004)]. A possible explanation for these conflicting results is that the distinction between \objects" and \context" is difficult to operationalize, and the parsing of foreground cues and background context occurs differently depending on many as yet unclear task parameters (e.g., Frankland et al., 1998). Also, the extent to which the contextual information processed by postrhinal cortex, MEA, and the hippocampus is general or dedicated to spatial context is unclear (Rosenbaum et al., 2001; Bar and Aminoff, 2003; Anderson et al., this issue; Smith and Mizumori, this issue). 

## CA1 vs. CA3 

Several recent studies have suggested that subregions of the hippocampus may play distinct roles in memory. In particular, models of CA3 circuitry have highlighted its sparse and distributed input connectivity, high recurrency, and rapid synaptic plasticity (long-term potentiation (LTP)) as ideal ingredients to associate elements of a memory and mediate the completion of patterns from partial cues (e.g., McNaughton and Morris, 1987; Treves and Rolls, 1994; Hasselmo et al., 1995). These models are supported by recent findings showing that mice without NMDA receptor dependent plasticity in CA3 are impaired in rapid learning of new spatial patterns (Nakazawa et al., 2003) and in pattern completion from partial cues, resulting in reduced activity by downstream CA1 cells with the presentation of partial cues (Nakazawa et al., 2002). Also, the notion that CA3 is more involved than CA1 in the association of elements is supported by recent studies showing that CA3 neurons are more likely to orthogonalize, that is separate or complete altered or partial patterns of input, as compared to CA1 (Lee et al., 2004a; Leutgeb et al., 2004; Vazdarjanova and Guzowski, 2004) and that CA3 cells more rapidly change to a shift in the environment (Lee et al., 2004b). In addition, recent studies have shown that CA3, and not CA1, is critical at the earliest stage of composing a contextual representation (Lee and Kesner, 2004) and is critical

These observations have led to the idea that CA1 may play a differential role in memory for the order of events in unique experiences in contrast to the role of CA3 in concurrent associations (also see Kesner et al., 2004). 

## CONCLUSION 

The hippocampus and parahippocampal region show a high degree of anatomical conservation across the mammalian taxon. The available evidence indicates that this anatomical similarity is matched by a similarity in fundamental functional role across species. A key to this understanding has been the effort to make direct comparisons between species on similar tasks, and this effort is currently being pursued further with the help of computational models that are based on the anatomical data. One challenge in this process has been the struggle to find a language that can describe simultaneously the results of behavioral experiments in psychological terms and the findings from physiological and anatomical studies in mechanistic terms. The goal is to identify the unifying principles that apply to all examples of hippocampus-dependent memory, both in humans and experimental animals. To date, many ideas that have been proposed that contain elements of species-specific psychology, such as spatial memory in rats and episodic memory in humans. The difficulty is that often the elements of psychology vary markedly between species— despite the fact that the anatomical details of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region are remarkably similar. That is, it is possible that any apparent differences in terms of the psychological properties of hippocampus-dependent memory might 

be more related to the differences in neocortical input to the hippocampus and parahippocampal region rather than to any differences between species for these areas themselves. 

This contrast between a conserved hippocampus and a diverged neocortex highlights the importance of an evolutionary perspective. Any activity in the hippocampus and parahippocampal region will be influenced by both the conserved internal circuitry and the divergent neocortical inputs. Further, any product of the conserved circuitry in the hippocampus and parahippocampal region must pass through the divergent circuitry of the neocortex before being expressed in behavior. Thus, the results of any assay of the mammalian hippocampus must be decoded by a species-specific translation that takes into account differences between species. By this view, the prevalence of spatial representations in the firing rates of hippocampal cells in rats and the importance of the human hippocampus for episodic memory both reflect common memory operations. It is perhaps not surprising that an animal such as the rat, whose survival normally depends on nighttime foraging, would place a premium of remembering spatial locations of important items. It is also not surprising that the human hippocampus, spoiled by the wonderful elaborative ability of its complex neocortex, would more likely reflect the paradoxes of episodic memory, being abstract yet structured, detailed yet imperfect. 

We described here a framework for understanding the memory operations of the mammalian hippocampus and parahippocampal region that are common to all species. In this framework, the fundamental function of the mammalian hippocampus is described as combining spatial information arriving via the postrhinal (parahippocampal in primates) cortex and medial entorhinal area with nonspatial information arriving via the perirhinal cortex and lateral entorhinal area. We hypothesized that this convergence of spatial and nonspatial information might make the hippocampus especially well suited for item-location associations or even more generally for item-in-context memory. Further research in a variety of mammals will be required to assess the validity of this framework. In this effort, the differences between species in terms of neocortical organization is both a disadvantage and an advantage. The neocortical differences can obscure the true commonality of the conserved hippocampal circuitry but can also provide tests of the framework’s generality. For example, one might use the echolocation system in bats to investigate whether spatial and nonspatial auditory information converges on the hippocampus in this mammal in a way that resembles how spatial and nonspatial visual information converges on the hippocampus in primates.

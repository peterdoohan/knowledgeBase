## Cortico-basal ganglia circuits underlying dysfunctional control of motor behaviors in neuropsychiatric disorders Ana Mafalda Vicente, Gabriela J Martins and Rui M Costa 

**==> picture [29 x 29] intentionally omitted <==**

Neuropsychiatric disorders often manifest with abnormal control of motor behavior. Common symptoms include restricted and repetitive patterns of behavior (RRBs). Cortico-basal ganglia circuits have been implicated in the etiology of RBBs. However, there is a vast range of behaviors encompassed in RRBs, from simple explosive motor tics to rather complex ritualized compulsions. In this review, we highlight how recent findings about the function of specific basal ganglia circuits can begin to shed light into defined motor symptoms associated with neuropsychiatric disorders. We discuss recent studies using genetic animal models that advocate that different aspects of motor repetition in neurodevelopmental disorders, like obsessive-compulsive disorder and autism spectrum disorder, emerge from particular dysregulations in distinct cortico-basal ganglia circuits.

Furthermore, it is clear that neurological disorders can present with various psychiatric symptoms (such as depression in Parkinson’s Disease and psychosis in epilepsy), whereas neuropsychiatric disorders can include motor symptoms typical of neurological disorders (ex: dyskinesia in schizophrenic patients [2]). Hopefully, reclassification as disorders of the nervous system can lead to a general shift in the perception of these disorders, and change the stigma and the perception that people suffering from neuropsychiatric disorders are more responsible for their own disease and cure than people suffering from neurological ones.

## Introduction 

Neuropsychiatric disorders involve patterns of behavioral or psychological symptoms that affect the life of an individual. According to a survey by the National Institute of Mental Health (URL: https://www.nimh.nih.gov/ health/statistics/mental-illness.shtml), one in every five adults suffers from mental health disorders in their lifetime. However, the financial resources allocated to mental health remain relatively low (0.1% of total development assistance for health). Traditionally, psychiatric disorders have been considered functional disorders of the mind, while neurological disorders have been seen as organic disorders of the brain, with specific physical substrates [1]. ## The basal ganglia structure and function 

The basal ganglia are a group of deep brain nuclei, implicated in a variety of cognitive, motor, and learning functions. The striatum is the main input to these nuclei, receiving glutamatergic projections from cortical, thalamic, and limbic areas and dopaminergic projections from the midbrain (Figure 1). The output of the basal ganglia exerts inhibitory control over downstream brain areas, via the internal segment of the globus pallidus (GPi) and substantia nigra pars reticulata (SNr) to midbrain and brainstem areas, and to the thalamus, which in turn projects to several cortical regions, establishing cortico-basal ganglia-thalamic loops. Dysfunction of basal ganglia therefore results in dysfunctional inhibitory control of downstream brain areas.

Given these multiple interacting streams, it is not surprising that dysfunctions in cortico-basal ganglia circuitry have been associated with a variety of neuropsychiatric disorders with dysfunctional control of movement and reinforcement. 

The majority of striatal cells are GABAergic spiny projection neurons (SPNs). The remaining 5–10% of the striatum is composed by a variety of GABAergic and cholinergic interneurons. Since SPNs are kept under tight hyperpolarization, substantial convergent glutamatergic and dopaminergic input is needed to drive these neurons. SPNs are not, however, a homogeneous population and based on projection and expression profiles, they are often classified as being part of the direct pathway SPNs (dSPNs) or the indirect pathway SPNs (iSNPs). dSPNs project directly to the basal ganglia output nucleus and express D1-type dopamine receptors, while iSPNs form an indirect, multisynaptic pathway to the output structures of the basal ganglia, 

and express D2-type dopamine and Adenosine A2a receptors. These populations have differential responses to dopamine (DA): while D1 receptors modulate positively dSPNs excitability, D2 receptors reduce iSPNs excitability. The two major sources of DA in the brain are from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) and the substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc), which also has a conserved topography. DA neurons are known to respond to unexpected rewards and salient stimuli [3–5], as well as during action initiation [6–8]. Plasticity at cortico-striatal synapses is dopamine-dependent, can lead to both potentiation and depression, and is mediated by different mechanisms in dSPNs and iSPNs [9]. 

The direct-pathway and indirect-pathway were traditionally seen as having opposing effects on movement, with dSPNs promoting action and iSPNs suppressing actions [10,11]. However, this model has been challenged by observations that both populations are active during movement [12]. When animals perform a sequence of actions, stimulation of dSPNs will lead to continued or repetitive performance of that action, while iSPNs stimulation will lead to switching to other actions [13[��] ], suggesting that both populations are needed for movement, with dSPNs supporting particular actions and iSPNs inhibiting competing actions. The activity in dSPNs and iSPNs seems to be actionspecific [14,15]. In parallel, there is a ‘hyperdirect’ pathway whereby the cortex sends topographically organized glutamatergic projections directly to the subthalamic nucleus (STN), an excitatory region in the basal ganglia, which then stimulates the GPi directly, bypassing the striatum. This is proposed to disinhibit the direct pathway to facilitate a specific movement, preventing premature while inhibiting prepared responses [16]. 

Cortico-striatal circuits have been shown to contribute differently in action learning: while DMS seems to be involved in early action learning and goal-directed behaviors [17,18], DLS is associated with late skill learning [17], sequence chunking [6,19] and habitual actions [18,20]. Cortical and thalamic circuits are also involved in these two strategies, with PL and mediodorsal thalamus involved in goal-directed behavior and IL in habitual actions [18]. OFC has also been implicated in the balance between goal-directed (actions done to achieve a particular outcome) and habitual actions (elicited by antecedent stimulus) [21], and attenuation of OFC to DMS input is critical for habitual behavior [22]. The balance between goal-directed and habitual actions is thought to be critical in neuropsychiatric disorders involving certain types of motor repetition, but there is no consensus on how an imbalance occurs or its direct impact on motor function. ## Dysfunctional behavioral control and motor repetition in neuropsychiatric disorders 

Behavioral motor dysfunctions occur in a variety of neuropsychiatric disorders such as schizophrenia and OCD, and neurological conditions such as Parkinson’s disease and Tourette syndrome. Restricted and repetitive patterns of behavior (RRBs) are of particular interest in the context of cortico-striatal studies, since they have long been associated (both in pathological and physiological states, and in both humans and experimental animals). Many neuropsychiatric disorders present some forms of restricted and repetitive patterns of behavior (RRBs), but not others. However, when talking about dyscontrol, not all forms of repetitive behavior are the same, and different types can be distinguished based on their properties. Among some of the most commonly found in neuropsychiatric disorders are [23]: 

- Stereotypies: repetitive and apparently purposeless simple movements repeated with high precision and low execution variance; 

- Compulsion: repetitive, intentional behaviors that appear to follow certain rules; typically include complex motor acts (e.g. washing body in a ritualized manner) that are repeated, often in relation to distressing, intrusive thoughts (‘obsessions’); 

- Tics: considered involuntary brief, rapid, ‘explosive’ or ‘shock-like’ movements that can be simple or complex; 

- Perseveration: non-aberrant, adaptive responses are repeated beyond what is necessary for the completion of the goal. 

Although clinically there are several metrics and scales developed to deal with this diversity, many studies in animal models do not differentiate different patterns of repetitive behaviors as similar entities. When assessing RRBs in animal models, researchers usually evaluate animals on repetition of spontaneous behaviors (such as grooming, rearing, jumping, digging and back somersaulting) and on a variety of learning paradigms (such as reversal learning, goal-directed and habitual learning, etc). This highlights the variety of symptoms that can be associated with the concept of RRBs, and the distinctions between these is not always obvious especially in animal models. For example, OCD and ASD are both characterized by excessive and abnormal repetitive behaviors. However, the repetitive behaviors in each disorder are clinically different, with compulsions more associated with OCD and stereotypies with ASD. But in animal models of OCD and ASD, excessive grooming, for example is often used as a phenotype for both disorders. Detailed mapping of neuroanatomical circuits under 

carefully designed behavioral paradigms will be imperative to dissociate these variables. 

Given the distinction in these (and other) types of behavioral control dysfunction, it is not surprising that they originate from different circuitry changes in cortico-basal ganglia circuits. In the sections below we use OCD and ASD to illustrate this point. 

## OCD 

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is characterized by obsessions, which are persistent and recurrent thoughts; and/or compulsions, which are repetitive behaviors aimed at reducing anxiety or dread. As with other neuropsychiatric disorders, results from neuroimaging studies in OCD can be contradictory, but suggest the existence of aberrant frontal-striatal activity and connectivity [24]. 

Although the genetic underpinnings of OCD are still largely unknown, a mouse knockout of Sapap3 (Table 1), a gene associated with OCD in humans [25], seems to recapitulate some of the symptomatology of OCD. This model shows both increased grooming and anxiety phenotypes, which are reversed by fluoxetine treatment [26], an SSRI commonly used to treat OCD. Cortico-striatal synaptic function is altered in this model, while the thalamocortical pathway is normal relative to controls, suggestive of specific cortico-striatal abnormalities [27]. Other models of OCD based on human genetic studies, such as the Slitrk5 and Hoxb8, are also associated with changes in cortico-striatal activity/connectivity [28,29]. 

Data from OCD patients trained on an RL model shows decreases in the perseveration parameter, which means they were less likely to stay in the same action, regardless of value [30]. Accordingly, patients also show increased RPE-related activity in ACC, nucleus accumbens and putamen [30,31]. A lower perseveration could be indicative of an increased checking behavior linked to increased uncertainty [30]. Interestingly, in a reversal learning lever pressing task, Sapap3-ko showed a bimodal distribution of responses, with approximately half the population reversing and the other showing reversal impairments – however, reversal failing was not due to perseverance in the old action, but lower presses in the new active lever [32]. 

It has been proposed that compulsions are driven by habits which lead to the automatic eliciting of the same action under the same context via a stimulus-response mechanism and deficits in goal-direct behavior. However, others have suggested that compulsions stem from an overvaluation of goals/outcomes, which would lead to an overactivation of the goal-directed system. ## ASD 

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a neuro-developmental disorder with a core symptomatology consisting of impaired social and communication skills, and RRBs. An influential hypothesis in the field describes an imbalance between excitatory and inhibitory neuronal control [46]. Regardless, the neuronal circuits underlying the development and control of dysfunctional repetitive behaviors and stereotypies in ASD are still poorly understood but are assumed to impinge on the same circuitry involved in action learning and habitual control of movement. Several studies in humans, albeit sometimes contradictory, have found dysfunctions in basal ganglia volume, activity, and connectivity. 

A recent hypothesis suggests that the three main core symptoms of autism (RRBs and social and communication deficits) can be explained by a general dysfunction of the dopamine diffusion systems [47]. Accordingly, injections of DA or a DA agonist in rats striatum induced repetitive behavior. Likewise, amphetamine (which acts on the dopamine transporter (DAT), therefore increasing extracellular DA in striatum) can induce stereotypy [48]. A genetic mutation of DAT (mimicking a mutation found in an ASD patient) leads to hyperactivity in mice with social deficits and repetitive rearing [49]. All this evidence suggests that there is indeed a deficit in DA functioning in ASD. 

Some of the most used genetic experimental systems of ASD rely on genetic mutations found in the ASD human population (such as Shank3 and Del16p11.2), though some models are used for their spontaneous increase in RRBs in a laboratory setting (such as deer mice, the BTBR T+tf/J18 line, and C58J21 line) (Table 1). 

Shank3-ko mice, which exhibit increased self-grooming and decreased hole-board exploration, have impaired striatal LTD, increased SPN excitability and decreased sEPSC frequency [50,51,52[�] ]. These changes in SPN function seem to affect both neuronal subtypes, and the authors suggest that excitability increases could be due to a compensatory effect from reduced glutamatergic transmission. In fact, over the past decade, drugs targeting metabotropic glutamate receptor 5 (mGluR5) have been suggested as therapeutic intervention for ASD and other neurodevelopmental disorders based on studies showing that a genetic reduction of mGluR5 reversed some of the symptoms in Fmr1 mouse models of fragile X syndrome [53], in addition to studies showing that an mGluR5 antagonist treatment reversed Shank3 [50,54] and Del16p11.2 [55] phenotypes and repetitive self-grooming in BTBR mice [56]. Silencing of mGluR5 on AC5-ko and D2-ko animals suppressed excessive grooming and rescued the deficits in social preference and novel object preference. Interestingly, mGluR5 is a major modulated of LTD in the striatum [57]. These data suggest that some neuropsychiatric symptoms of ASD arise from altered synaptic signaling that is amenable to targeted drug therapy. This further strengthens the hypothesis that multiple causes of neuropsychiatric diseases converge on common pathophysiological processes, including synaptic function. 

An advantage of animal models is that we can look specifically at each striatal SPN subpopulation and try to tease apart their dynamics. In wild-type animals, activation of dSPNs projections to SNr is sufficient to increase frequency of repetitive behaviors [58]. Along the same lines, administration of a D1 agonist in wildtype mice leads to deficits in social behavior, as well as increases in grooming [59]. Conversely, knocking out the D1 receptor led to no changes in behavioral measures. A knockout of AC5 with increased grooming and rearing, as well as stereotyped food consumption, can be rescued with selective inhibition of projections from PL to dorsal striatum [60,61]. Interestingly, the PL cortex has been shown to project mostly to dSPNs [62]. 

Concomitantly, there is a string of evidence pointing to downregulation of iSPNs as a putative cause for highly stereotyped repetitive behavior. Enkephalin (marker for iSPNs) is decreased in high-stereotypy deer mice compared to low-stereotypy mice; and BTBR mice have reduced striatal A2a receptor function. ## Other neuropsychiatric disorders affecting basal ganglia and leading to dysfunctional motor behavior 

TS is a disorder characterized by motor and vocal tics, sometimes having intermittent explosive outbursts characterized as sudden episodes of verbal or physical aggression. TS has a high comorbidity with ADHD and ASD. In 

TS, the cortico-striatal network, specifically the sensorimotor loop, is associated with tics. Focal disinhibition in DLS induced tics in mice and non-human primates. In rats, microinjection of a GABAa antagonist in dorsal striatum resulted in motor tics and leads to locked increase activity of SPNs to LFP [68]. Interestingly, postmortem analysis of TS brain tissue demonstrates a selective loss of GABAergic and cholinergic interneurons of the striatum [69,70], possibly dis-inhibiting striatal pathways. Concomitant loss of these neurons in a mouse model of TS leads to enhanced stereotyped grooming and sniffing behaviors [71]. Additionally, tics are often treated with DA antagonists, suggestive of a dopaminergic substrate. 

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent inattention and/or hyperactivity/impulsivity, with patients usually showing an increase in motor activity described as ‘fidgetiness’ or ‘restlessness’, which are not typically characterized by repetitive, stereotypic actions [72]. Increases in reaction time variability (but not necessarily reaction time itself) and movement to movement variability, resulting in less smooth movement dynamics, are also common in ADHD responses to a variety of tasks [73,74]. Since this increase in activity is not achieved through repetitive behaviors, and in fact the variance of the actions seems to be increased rather than decreased, this could suggest instead an increase in switching between behaviors, which has been associated with iSPNs activity [13[��] ]. In fact, there are indications that ADHD children have increased D2 receptor availability which is reduced by a common ADHD treatment drug [75]. Accordingly, in mouse models of ADHD, D1R agonist reduces hyperactivity in a dose dependent manner [76], and the reduction of hyperactivity by psychostimulants is reversed by D2R blockage [77]. 

These disorders further demonstrate the diversity in repetitive behaviors involving the basal ganglia and highlight the potential for engaging different subcircuits during different types of repetitive behaviors. 

## Conclusion 

As discussed here, many neuropsychiatric disorders are associated with dysfunction in cortico-basal ganglia circuits and lead to changes in behavioral control and motor repetition. These disorders have several comorbidities, so it is not surprising that there are common circuit underpinnings. However, specific circuit changes appear to underlie different types of motor behavior dysfunction. Careful mapping of distinct projections and cell types and their activity under defined behavioral paradigms will be imperative to dissociate the underlying circuits leading to motor dyscontrol in neurodevelopmental disorders.

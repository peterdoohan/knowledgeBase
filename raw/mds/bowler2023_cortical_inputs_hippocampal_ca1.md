## SUMMARY 

The subregions of the entorhinal cortex (EC) are conventionally thought to compute dichotomous representations for spatial processing, with the medial EC (MEC) providing a global spatial map and the lateral EC (LEC) encoding specific sensory details of experience. Yet, little is known about the specific types of information EC transmits downstream to the hippocampus. Here, we exploit in vivo sub-cellular imaging to record from EC axons in CA1 while mice perform navigational tasks in virtual reality (VR). We uncover distinct yet overlapping representations of task, location, and context in both MEC and LEC axons. MEC transmitted highly location- and context-specific codes; LEC inputs were biased by ongoing navigational goals. However, during tasks with reliable reward locations, the animals’ position could be accurately decoded from either subregion. Our results revise the prevailing dogma about EC information processing, revealing novel ways spatial and non-spatial information is routed and combined upstream of the hippocampus. 

## INTRODUCTION 

The mammalian hippocampal formation plays a critical role in both spatial navigation and episodic memory.[1][,][2] The most prominent neural correlate of these phenomena is place coding, in which individual excitatory principal neurons are specifically active in restricted regions of the environment, termed ‘‘place cells,’’ with the preferred locations referred to as ‘‘place fields.’’[2] At the population level, place cells produce a map of external space.[3–5] Importantly, place maps are altered by proximal cues, which can serve as landmarks to support allothetic navigation[6–8] ; place maps also incorporate goal-related information.[9–13] The entorhinal cortex (EC), the primary cortical input region to the hippocampus, may provide critical spatial and sensory information for the hippocampal code.[14–17] Both EC and hippocampal spatial maps exhibit experience-dependent changes, even in familiar environments,[18–20] and respond to salience and novelty.[21–24] Models of place cells in hippocampal subregion CA1 describe how highly tuned spatial input arriving from CA3[10][,][25] might combine with an external depolarizing event to create or refine place fields.[26–30] The prominent direct projection from superficial EC (primarily layer III) to CA1[31] is ideally suited to provide these instructive signals for place field formation and transformation in CA1. Given the malleable nature of hippocampal representations, any truly mechanistic model of CA1 computations must rely on a comprehensive characteriza- 

tion of the activity in the EC-to-CA1 projections, as well as an understanding of how this activity changes with navigational context and task goals. 

Traditionally the medial EC (MEC) and lateral EC (LEC) subregions of the EC are thought to be functionally dissociated. The high proportion and diversity of spatially modulated MEC cells[32–37] suggest that this subregion primarily represents spatial information related to global frames of reference. In contrast, LEC is proposed to primarily process information related to individual items, such as sensory cues and location based on a ‘‘local’’ frame of reference.[14][,][38–40] LEC also responds during object-context association tasks[41] and displays object-trace representations.[42][,][43] Vector-based tuning is also reported in LEC with egocentric bearing-based coding.[44] Much less spatial selectivity has been observed in LEC compared with MEC, although significant levels of tuning have been reported in both.[38][,][39][,][45] Finally, temporal coding has been observed in both EC subregions.[44,46,47] These studies, however, did not specifically examine the subset of EC cells directly projecting to CA1, nor the degree to which information about navigation, context, place, or time could be reconciled from the EC-originating activity observed there. Lesioning or silencing EC disrupts internal representations of the external environment.[41][,][48][,][49] However, a clear dependency of CA1 tuning on the input from either EC subregion is yet to be identified; instead, manipulations revealed only partial deficits in tuning.[50–57]

Accumulating evidence suggests that EC circuits also participate in processing goal-related information.[58][,][59] LEC egocentric bearing cells show a particular affinity for goal locations.[45] Moreover, presentation of goal-related information increases coherence between LEC and CA1,[60] and dopaminergic input to superficial LEC from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) has been shown to be critical for cue-reward associations.[61] Recent studies of MEC revealed that both grid and non-grid spatial signals are modified by the presence of rewarded locations.[62][,][63] Although the impact of goals on MEC tuning contradicts the notion that MEC representations are purely spatial, changing the context (visual cues) while animals run on a linear track elicited an almost complete remapping of the MEC spatial inputs to CA1, even though the goal location remained fixed.[64] In CA1, context-independent reward tuning has been observed,[13] suggesting that other intrinsic or extrinsic mechanisms besides just the MEC projection might be important to goal representations. Importantly, the relationship between context and goal representations has not been studied for the LEC projection. Consequently, the relative contribution of MEC- and LEC-to-CA1 goal-related information processing remains unknown. 

Thus, we still lack a circuit-level mechanistic understanding of direct cortical influences on CA1 population dynamics and how responsibilities are shared between EC subregions. The vast majority of studies have focused on either the LEC or the MEC independently,[58][,][61][,][64][,][65] rather than comparing the two regions under identical conditions. Although a few studies have previously recorded directly from EC axons in CA1, they used limited behavioral tasks and focused on either the MEC axons specifically[64] or downstream plasticity mechanisms in CA1.[59] Additionally, especially for LEC, few studies have focused on goal-directed navigation, context change, and how spatial representations shift with navigational objectives.[66] To bridge these knowledge gaps, we conducted a direct characterization by performing two-photon Ca[2+] imaging of EC axons in CA1 during a battery of VR navigation tasks. 

## RESULTS 

We performed in vivo two-photon functional Ca[2+] imaging of EC axons within stratum lacunosum moleculare (SLM) of CA1 to characterize the information conveyed directly between the regions (Figures 1A–1D, S2, and S3). Mice performed virtual reality (VR) tasks designed to probe mnemonic and spatial information processing, traversing a 4-m virtual linear environment with a 2-s inter-trial interval (ITI) between each ‘‘lap’’ (Figure 1E). Mice were

exposed to a training context for 2–3 weeks while they learned to run on the wheel in VR and find reward zones (RZs, initially several, but with training, 1 per 4-m lap). Additionally, unless otherwise specified, mice had at least 1 training day prior to recording on the specified tasks and VR contexts used for analysis. We detect regions of interest (ROIs) corresponding to putative boutons or axonal segments, allowing us to record 41,617 LEC ROIs from 32 FOVs and 34,604 MEC ROIs from 25 fields of view (FOVs) across all tasks (n = 10 mice LEC and n = 6 mice MEC; Figure S1). We extracted fluorescence time series data from the ROIs and identified significant Ca[2+] transients for subsequent analysis (Figures 1B, 1C, and S1). 

## Stable representations of space in both LEC and MEC during 1D goal-directed navigation 

Wefirstuseda spatial goallearningtasktoinvestigate how avirtual environment was encoded by the direct EC-CA1 projection incorporating both ‘‘VR-Off’’ (cue-less) and ‘‘VR-On’’ (with visual cues) conditions (Figure 1E). Both conditions featured a water reward located near the end of the 4-m lap. Animals anticipated the location of the reward better when they had access to the visual cues, indicated by a reduced running velocity and increased rate of anticipatory licking (Figures 1H and S5A). Mice also exhibited these behaviors when there was no access to the VR cues, albeit with substantially less accuracy, presumably path integrating between visits to the rewarded location (Figures 1H and S5A). 

In both VR-Off and VR-On, both axon types exhibited transient Ca[2+] events of similar duration, height, and rate (similar by mouse mean in both conditions and between cohorts for each metric, Figures S4A–S4C). During VR-On laps Ca[2+] events exhibited more spatial structure and spatial population vectors (PVs) were significantly more correlated between VR-On laps, compared with VR-Off (Figures 1F, 1G, and S5B). Additionally, during VR-On, there was no significant difference between the lap-to-lap correlations between the LEC and MEC axons (Figure S5B). The increase in correlations appeared within the first laps with visual cues and then persisted, indicating that stable contextual representations in the EC form quickly and then maintain their tuning across many subsequent exposures (Figure 1G). The finding that EC axons quickly form stable representations of virtual environments is consistent with previous reports of MEC axons[64] and expands this to the LEC projection. 

Both LEC and MEC axons showed increased spatial tuning during the VR-On condition (Figures 1I and S5C–S5E). Compared with shuffled data, 43% of the LEC ROIs and 44% of the MEC ROIs were significantly tuned during VR-On.

Interestingly, without the visual cues, 21% of LEC ROIs were spatially tuned, whereas only 9% of the recorded MEC ROIs were. This confirms that the visual cues significantly increased the stability of responses in both projections but also highlights a task-dependent dissociation between the LEC and MEC activity (Figure 1I). A significant effect of VR condition on the reliability and specificity of spatial fields was observed in both axonal populations (Figures S5F and S5G). Additionally, MEC axons specifically showed a non-significant (when aggregated by mouse) trend toward increased field width in the VR-On condition; however, no difference was observed in the LEC axons or significant difference between LEC and MEC fields (Figure S5H). In order to ensure that there were no systematic imaging artifacts between the MEC and LEC cohorts, we calculated the imaging frame stability (after motion correction) and found no trend between average frame correlation and percentage of spatial fields, and there was also no significant difference in frame stability between the LEC and MEC FOVs (Figure S5I). 

It was unexpected that such a large proportion of the LEC axons would exhibit significant spatial tuning (Figures 1I and S5C). Additionally, we observed an enrichment of the LEC spatial fields just prior to the RZ in both conditions (Figure 1K). Notably, LEC axons active around the RZ in the VR-Off condition also tended to be tuned near the RZ in VR-On (Figure S5K). When the VR cues were present, reward proximity/anticipation tuning was observed in all LEC FOVs (Figure 1K) and the degree of the enrichment was significantly correlated with the animal’s anticipatory licking behavior (Figure S5J). In the VR-Off condition there was also increasedtuningwithinandimmediatelyfollowingtheRZobserved in both LEC and MEC axons, which was less surprising given that the reward delivery was the only spatially fixed cue during these laps (Figure 1J). These results demonstrate a goal anticipatory bias in the tuning of the LEC activity, which could be partially distinct from the presence (or absence) of the current contextual cues, whereas the spatial activity in the MEC axons might be, to a much higher degree, dependent on the VR environment. 

The observation that LEC-CA1 axons were spatially tuned, in some cases just as much or more than axons originating in MEC, is in stark contrast to studies that identified the foundations of the hippocampal place code purely targeting MEC cell populations.[64][,][67] We therefore sought to quantify the degree to which input from each EC subregion was mapping the virtual environment. Position decoders trained on either axonal population performed equally well during the VR-On condition, also significantly better than VR-Off (Figures 1L and S6A–S6D). Increasing the number of ROIs used for decoding improved the decoding accuracy, but this plateaued after 500–750 ROIs (Figure S6B). Position could be accurately predicted from both the LEC and MEC axons throughout the entire VR track, including locations far from the reward (Figures S6A and S6C), suggesting that LEC spatial coding during this task was not restricted to areas near the reward. Without visual cues, LEC axons were more informative about the animal’s location near the RZ, whereas with MEC axons, decoding error remained, on average, quite high (Figures 1L, S6C, and S6D). The position decoding corroborates our observations of the spatial tuning properties, and we find that better decoding performance is significantly correlated with the fraction of spatially tuned axons (Figures S6E and S6F). 

Given previous reports of velocity modulation of MEC neurons,[35][,][36] we finally asked whether it was possible to decode the animals’ velocity based on the activity of the EC-CA1 projection. Across mice, we found that it was possible to decode velocity from the LEC axonal activity, and there was a non-significant trend for successful decoding in the MEC axons restricted to the VR-On laps (Figure S6G). Thus, it is unlikely that a large proportion of MEC ‘‘speed cells’’ (or at least unlikely that a large proportion of cells purely selective for velocity) project to CA1. 

## LEC-CA1 projection transmits a reward-centric map of space during a goal alternation task 

The enrichment of LEC-CA1 axons in anticipation of reward locations suggests that a large portion of the spatial stability in this projection might be due to the presence of a consistent, familiar goal location, with the MEC projection more uniformly representing space. In order to test this, we recorded EC axons while mice performed a reward location alternation task, in which the goal would alternate every 10 laps between two locations (RZ1 and RZ2, Figure 2A). Both LEC and MEC cohorts of mice followed the reward translocation within a lap of the switch, as shown by the anticipatory licking (Figure 2B). Mice also tended to run at a similar velocity along the entire track at all locations, except for those adjacent to the RZ, and we observed no significant differences in the animals’ average running velocity between reward conditions (Figure S7A). 

Despite exhibiting similar performance on the behavioral indicators of task competency, differences in the tuning of the EC axons between reward conditions were drastic. Tuning curves derived from LEC axons reorganized considerably upon RZ translocation, whereas the MEC projection appeared more stable (Figures 2E and S7C). We observed similar spatial tuning properties between the subregions’ axons (Figures S7D and S7E), with slightly elevated percentages of tuned ROIs during the RZ2 condition compared with RZ1; however, this was only significant across animals for the MEC axons (Figure S7F). Additionally, for both subregions, about 30% of the ROIs that were stable in either RZ condition were stable in both (Figure S7G). However, there were differences in the stability of the tuned fields; tuned MEC axons shifted less between RZ conditions compared with LEC axons (Figure S7G). Also, more LEC axons were tuned proximal to the RZ in both conditions (Figure S7I). Interestingly, some LEC axons followed the RZ, resulting in a greater overlap of RZ-adjacent tuned fields compared with MEC (Figures 2F, S7I, and S7J). We also classified rewardtracking ROIs, as well as spatial ROIs, which did not change the location of their firing fields between the two reward conditions[13] (Figures 2C, 2D, S7K, S7L, S7N, and S7O). We find an increased percentage of reward-tracking neurons in the LEC projection compared with the MEC (11.7% of LEC ROIs paired in both reward conditions as opposed to just 3.5% of MEC). Additionally, there was a higher percentage of MEC ROIs with spatially consistent tuning between RZ conditions (70.7% of the MEC axons tuned in both reward conditions, compared with 41.4% of the LEC axons). This greater continuity of the spatial representations in the MEC data between RZ1 and RZ2 blocks indicates that MEC activity was more influenced by the VR/spatial cues than location of the reward (Figure S7B).

We next asked how the moving RZ impacted our ability to predict the animals’ location from the activity of the axons. LEC axons had notably worse performance decoding space on the first lap following an RZ switch, but accuracy was otherwise similar to MEC (Figure 2I). The difference in the first lap decoder performance indicates that a consistent reward location, and likely the animal’s awareness of it, had greater influence on LEC responses than MEC. Additionally, training the decoder on laps from the opposite reward condition yielded worse decoding performance from LEC axons than from MEC (Figure 2J). This reward translocation-related reorganization only took 1 lap following the reward location change, after which the decoder performance rapidly plateaued, indicating that spatial representations in the LEC projections reorganize quickly and have separable representations of the current RZ condition. 

Because LEC axons switched between two distinct RZdependent representations of the same virtual track, we hypothesized that there was an internal representation of the current task demands, asking the following question: is information about the current RZ location preserved within EC between laps? We found that it was, indeed, possible to decode the RZ location throughout the ITI timeout for all laps except the first lap of an RZ condition block (Figure 2G). RZ decoding during the ITI from both LEC and MEC projections was better than chance, and although the decoder performed better from the LEC axons (by mouse average performance LEC: 83.5%, MEC: 77.8%), this difference was not significant (Figure 2G). Importantly, there was no reward-location-dependent difference in the animals’ running or licking behavior during the timeout (Figure S7H). Similarly, we found that, for matched location prior to either RZ, velocity, and (no) licking epochs, it was possible to decode the current goal location during navigation from the activity of the LEC axons (excluding the first lap following RZ change; Figure 2H). Together, these results indicate that the EC inputs to CA1 contain information relevant to navigational 

variables that extend beyond the current position of the animals or VR cues. Rather, decoding a reliable ‘‘spatial’’ map from the LEC is possible but dependent on an understanding of the current navigational task. This implies a degree of prospective or retrospective coding[68] with ramifications for the animal’s route planning because this information could be resolved to a ‘‘reward vector,’’ tracking the distance to the next reward or from the previous one. 

There is believed to be a relationship between EC-CA1 axonal activity and increased place field formation in CA1.[59] Because a relationship between successful goal-oriented navigation and reward-proximal place field enrichment exists,[10][,][12][,][13] we hypothesized that the LEC projection could contribute to the success in navigational tasks, especially when learning of novel goal areas is required. In order to test this prediction we bilaterally injected mice with retrograde-infecting cre virus in CA1 (AAVretro-Cre) and then a cre-dependent hM4D-mCherry virus[69] in either LEC or MEC to selectively express the inhibitory channel (hM4D DREADD) in cells with direct projections to CA1 (Figures 2K and 2L). These animals were trained in a task that forced them to learn novel reward locations (Figures S8A– S8D; see also STAR Methods). Animals were tested on this task for 3 days prior to application of the DREADD agonist deschloroclozapine (DCZ) in order to establish a baseline of performance. Following DCZ injection, mice with selective inactivation of the LEC-CA1 projection showed a significant reduction in their ability to perform the task as compared with saline (percent success following DCZ injection compared with saline injection p % 0.001 for all days; Figure 2M); no deficit was observed in mice expressing the channel in MEC or in control mice expressing tdTomato in LEC (percent success following DCZ injection compared with saline injection p R 0.9 for all days; Figure 2M). Thus, selective inactivation of LEC-CA1, but not MEC-CA1 projection, affected the animals’ ability to form novel location-reward associations in this task.

## MEC axons remap following context change, while LEC axonal representations are more stable with fixed goal location 

Because CA1 has been shown to remap in response to changes in context,[19][,][70–73] we next asked how the EC-CA1 projections reorganize their activity if we completely alter the visual scenery while leaving the RZ at a fixed location relative to the start/end of the track[29] (Figures 4A and S9A; see also STAR Methods). Sorting MEC axonal activity by context A laps revealed that there was little relationship between the observed activity in the two contexts. Interestingly, some of the LEC axons appeared to maintain similar tuning, especially near the RZ (Figures 4B and S9B). Both LEC and MEC axons exhibited more similar tuning within the same context compared with between them (Figures 4C and 4G). However, LEC axonal activity was more correlated between different contexts than MEC—in particular, LEC responses were most strongly correlated at locations directly adjacent to the RZ (Figure 4G). 

As with the other tasks, we calculated spatial tuning scores for every ROI imaged during this task in order to look at spatial selectivity and remapping. Spatial tuning metrics were similar between EC projections (Figure S9K). Although the LEC axons had similar percentages of tuned ROIs across contexts (context A: 35.5%, context B: 39.2%), the MEC FOVs contained slightly more in the second context (context A: 35.5%, context B: 42.7%, Figure S9C), with a tendency toward multiple spatial fields (Figure S9O). Similar proportions of the tuned axons were significantly tuned in both contexts (Figure S9E). Following a context change, MEC spatial fields tended to move further than LEC (Figure S9E). LEC ROIs showed notable enrichment at locations just prior to the RZ location, whereas the distribution of the MEC centroids was more uniform (Figures S9D, S9F, and S9H). We found an increased percentage of reward-tuned axons in LEC compared with MEC (36.1% LEC, 10.3% MEC of axons tuned in both context; Figure 4D). LEC reward-tuned axons

No specific evidence for grid-like activity was observed in the MEC recordings compared with LEC.

## DISCUSSION 

Our study elucidates unique and overlapping roles of the LEC and MEC projections to CA1. LEC spatial maps exhibit robust goal-driven enrichment, with more spatially uniform representations in MEC. In 1D tasks, reward proximity is directly related to location, and either representation could be used to form and stabilize the ‘‘dual’’ mnemonic and spatial map that has been observed in CA1.[80][,][81] During goal-directed navigation, it is possible to decode current location from the LEC input to CA1 (in addition to MEC) at fairly good resolution and throughout the entire context. These results have important implications for the interpretation of previous studies that manipulated EC inputs to CA1, as well as for our understanding of the various routes spatial information takes as it propagates through the cortex to the hippocampus. 

LEC has previously been reported to exhibit a low prevalence of spatially tuned cells[38][,][39][,][42] ; additionally, a series of models[82–84] and experimental paradigms have focused specifically on how MEC activity (and grid cells in particular) might yield place cell activity in CA1.[32][,][50][,][85] The current study is unique in that we performed identical evaluations of both subregions. Our results confirm spatial selectivity in MEC-CA1 projections, although not necessarily grid-like tuning (Figure S12; see also STAR Methods). We also find that, when a clear navigational goal is established, LEC input to CA1 is also spatially stable. Furthermore, recordings from LEC and, as expected, MEC axons in CA1 reveal sufficient information to distinguish between distinct visual contexts despite identical task structure and reward location. Finally, although shuffling the goal location resulted in a reduction in spatial tuning in the LEC axons, it does not revert to chance levels—similar to previous reports that describe a low, but notable, baseline level of spatially informative content within the LEC during 2D foraging tasks.[38][,][39][,][45] Our results indicate that, during goal-directed navigation, LEC-CA1 projection is equally capable of providing the necessary tuned input to drive place field formation, stability, and subsequent remapping with context change. The potential for overlapping spatial representations between the EC subregions has implica-

tions for studies investigating cortical influence over CA1 through MEC specific perturbations,[51][,][52][,][86] instead suggesting that MEC and LEC may act in conjunction to support CA1 computations.[87] 

Although reward location in a linear environment also serves as a prominent proximal landmark, our results agree with models that MEC is involved in more allocentric coding, whereas LEC more egocentric.[44][,][88][,][89] Goal-related enrichment, specifically present in LEC axons and conserved between contexts, indicates a possible goal-distance representation of space. Modeling the axonal Ca[2+] activity, we find evidence for goal-distance tuning that is significantly more prevalent in the LEC axons compared with MEC. Similar to previously reported egocentricbearing vector activity in EC,[45] with the important distinction that, in our tasks, no visible or olfactory cues are specific to goal locations. In the VR task, an egocentric perspective of the visual cues is not sufficient to explain the LEC anticipatory tuning because an association between reward and location in global/ allocentric space is necessary. Lesions to LEC have previously been shown to impair both egocentric and allocentric associations[90] ; our results concur with a model for some conjunctive egocentric and allocentric tuning present in LEC. Of course, distinguishing vector-based or egocentric-bearing navigation is difficult in 1D because these strategies are more overlapping with purely spatial responses. 

When goal locations change, we observe a subsequent reorganization in the LEC-CA1 projection and, importantly, overlapping representations of the goal areas. LEC tuning shifts quickly, at around the same timescale it takes for animals to adjust their anticipatory behaviors. We only test familiar reward locations, however, and the shift in tuning appears related to the animal’s expectation of the reward (which we also observe in the VROff/no visual cues task, as well as by stochastically omitting rewards). When goals are paired with novel locations, we expect reward representations in LEC to arise alongside the animals’ awareness of the reward. Recent evidence shows dopaminergic reward expectation signals during non-spatial olfactory tasks, mitigated by a pathway between VTA and superficial LEC.[61] Besides this pathway, the LEC likely receives spatially tuned inputs from postrhinal cortex,[91] collateral input from the MEC,[92] and internally from deep layers via CA1.[93] The existence of these inputs suggests that features of memory and space may be integrated upstream of CA1 within LEC. It is notable that the stability of reward-proximal place cells in CA1 have been shown to be uncorrelated with pre- and post-experience activation during offline-memory consolidation epochs,[94] suggesting that there might be unique mechanisms that contribute to the maintenance of these place fields. An enrichment in stable LEC spatial fields proximal to goals implies that less synaptic plasticity within CA1 is necessary to maintain place fields in these locations. 

Previous studies have found task-dependent reward-based tuning in both MEC grid and non-grid spatial cells.[62][,][63] Although our results point to a prominent role for LEC, we also observed improved position decoding along with increased place field stability in the MEC projection around rewards. ## Surgery 

## Injections 

Mice were stereotactically injected in either the LEC or MEC with a mixture of 2/3 rAAV1-Synapsin-GCaMP6s[100] and 1/3 mixture of rAAV9-hSynapsin-DIO-tdTomato and h56d-Cre (LEC: relative to bregma 3.0 mm posterior, 4.7 mm lateral and -2.6 mm, -2.5 mm, -2.4 mm ventral from the surface of the brain, MEC: 0.2 mm anterior of the transverse sinus, 3.2 mm lateral relative to bregma and -1.3 mm, -1.2 mm, -1.1 mm ventral from the surface of the brain). 2 pulses of 

28.8 nL were injected at each DV location (depth) using a Nanoject syringe. Additionally, for all animals, 50x diluted CamKII-NLStdTomato was injected into CA1 to sparsely label the pyramidal cell layer (2.3 mm posterior of bregma, -1.5 mm lateral and, -1.1 mm, -1.0 mm, -0.9 mm ventral from the surface of the brain). 2 pulses of 32.2 nL were applied at each DV target in CA1. Separate cohorts of mice were injected bilaterally with 5 x diluted AAVretro-hSynapsin-Cre into CA1 and 2 x diluted AAV-hSynapsin-DIO-hM4D(Gi)mCherry[69] into either LEC or MEC in order to selectively express the inhibitory DREADD receptor in the CA1 projecting population of neurons. To serve as a control, additional animals were injected with 5 x diluted AAVretro-hSynapsin-Cre in CA1 and AAV9-hSynapsin-DIO-tdTomato at the LEC coordinates. Injection coordinates for CA1, LEC and MEC were the same as listed previously; 80 nL were injected at each DV location at a rate of 1 nL/s. 

## Window Implants 

Following the viral injections, mice were given 3 days to recover. Then, a craniotomy was performed directly over left dorsal CA1, and a 3 mm imaging window and cannula were implanted in accordance with the previously described procedures.[103][,][104] Custom 3D printed titanium head fixation bars were then attached to the skull with dental acrylic. For the DREADD inactivation mice, no craniotomy was performed, however, head fixation bars were attached to the skull immediately following the viral injection surgery. 

## Behavior 

After recovering from the craniotomy, mice were habituated to head fixation, then water deprived and trained to lick at a water spout while running on a wheel. Movement of the running wheel 

advanced the position of visual VR cues, which were displayed on 5 LCD monitors surrounding the mouse. The running wheel and VR setup were as previously described.[29][,][105] Mice were trained first to lick for water rewards spread across the entire track, and then, eventually, for a single fixed reward zone. Reward zones were fixed 20 cm sections of the track. When mice entered the reward zones, they were given an initial drop of water followed by a time period (‘‘reward duration’’) during which animals could lick at the reward spout and receive additional water droplets–provided they stayed within the 20 cm area. Mice that did not slow down would run past the rewarded area and not be able to get additional reward; thus, the animals had an incentive to learn the reward locations and not fully rely on the initial drop as a cue. Reward durations were initially 10–15 seconds and then reduced as animals learned the task. Animals were considered proficient to start experiments after they would run for at least 50 laps in 20 minutes while reward duration was 2 seconds. The training VR context was distinct from those used during experiments; however, for the data shown, all animals had at least 1 prior experience in the environments. All VR environments used during the experiments were 400 cm long with a start and end ‘‘tunnel’’ at each side that the animal would exit from and enter into at the beginning/end of each lap. Following each lap, there was a 2 second timeout when all of the visual cues were dark prior to the next lap starting. Mice that did not accomplish enough laps during a 20 minutes 2p imaging session to complete the behavioral task were removed from analysis (see Figure S11B for summary of mice included per task). 

VR Navigation 

Mice first had to traverse 25 laps in a VR-Off context without the visual cues: the only spatial cue was the reward zone, which was fixed at a location 360 cm after the start of the lap. Once mice reached 400 cm, there was a 2 second timeout before the next lap started. After 25 laps of running without visual cues, the animals completed 25 laps with access to the visual stimuli–the VR-On context. The reward zone remained fixed in the same location and the 2 second timeout did not change. After the VR-On laps, mice would continue to run in a subsequent VR-Off block of laps; however, data from this block was not included in our analysis (other than the lap-by-lap correlation plot, Figure 1G). 

Reward Alternation 

For the reward learning task, we used two reward zones, RZ1 at 360 cm and RZ2 at 110 cm relative to the start of the virtual track. The reward location was fixed at RZ1 for a block of 10 laps, then moved to RZ2 for 10 laps, then returned to RZ1 for 10 laps, and finally moved once again to RZ2 for 10 laps. As described above, upon entry into the reward zone, an initial drop of water cued the animals to the reward location; additional rewards were available if the mouse learned to stop and lick within 20 cm of the reward start for a period of 2 seconds. There was no visual cue that changed or indicated which reward zone was currently active. Novel Reward Learning

Each day 3 novel locations were used and the environment was novel to the animals on day 1. Each reward location was initially cued on the first lap it was active by presenting the animal with a drop of water when they entered it. Following the ‘‘cue’’ lap animals had to lick within the rewarded area into to trigger a water drop to be released. ## QUANTIFICATION AND STATISTICAL ANALYSIS 

## Preprocessing 

## Motion Correction 

Datasets were processed using the SIMA python package for handling sequential imaging data.[101] Motion correction was accomplished using a custom implementation of the NoRMCorre algorithm[106] which was modified in order to drop low-correlation patches or entire frames which were corrupted by z-motion, as identified by outliers during the template correlations step. In some cases, we also report frame stability, as measured after the motion correction and calculated as the individual frame correlation to the time average of the entire dataset as in Grosmark et al.[94] 

## Segmentation and Signal Extraction 

ROIs detection, neuropil subtraction, and signal extraction were achieved used the python implementation of the Suite2p[102] package. The visual interface was then used to inspect all of the ROIs to ensure there were no spurious components detected. Axon ROIs were taken to be either individual boutons or small segments of axons. 

DF/F and Correlations Calculation 

The fluorescence was smoothed with a Savitzky–Golay filter (window length 51, polynomial order 3) and then a baseline was calculated using a max min filter (window size 500, s 10); finally, DF/F was calculated. In order to ensure that axons were not oversegmented, Pearsons’s pairwise correlation coefficient was calculated between the DF/F traces for all pairs of ROIs detected in a FOV. ROI pairs with an extremely high correlation coefficient (> 0.7) were considered to be from the same axon segment and merged. Transient Detection 

Transients were detected and used for all subsequent analysis unless specifically annotated as otherwise. Transient detection was done with a threshold of height based on an estimate of the noise as the standard deviation of the signal. Transients started when the D F/F surpassed 2s, and were completed once the signal dropped below sigma. To be counted as a transient, the signal had to pass 2.5s and last for at least 1 s. Within-transient times were considered unobserved for the purposes of the subsequent analysis. Iteratively, the transients were removed and s was re-estimated in order to reduce any skewing effect of with-transient times, similar to previously used approaches.[107] Axons with very few detected transients (< 0.25/min) or very poor quality signals (median within transient maximum fluorescence < 3.25s or 0.35DF/F ) were not included in the analysis.

## Analysis 

## Statistics 

For paired data, unless otherwise indicated, significance was assessed with two-sided t-tests. For unpaired data (e.g. comparisons between LEC and MEC data) Welch’s unequal variances t-test was used. Multi-factor ANOVA performed when more than two groups were being compared; tables provided with the analysis. In all cases * - p < 0.05, ** - p < 0.01, *** - p < 0.001, n.s. – not significant. Spatial Tuning 

In order to identify axons as spatially tuned, we calculated the spatial information[108] based on the Ca[2+] transient onset times for each ROI. Then we circularly shuffled the position of the transients on each lap and recomputed the spatial information. Axons that had spatial information scores higher than 95% of shuffles (p < 0.05) were considered significantly spatially tuned. Significance of spatial tuning was calculated independently for the conditions considered (VR-On/VR-Off, RZ1/RZ2 Context A/Context B, and for the random foraging task Visual Only/Multi- Sensory). 

## Spatial Field Detection 

Spatial field locations for each of the tuned axonal ROIs were calculated using a method similar to those previously reported for CA1 pyramidal cells,[29] except using the transient event onset times we recorded from the EC axons. Briefly, we identified where in the virtual environment axons’ transient onsets occurred more often than expected by chance. In order to do this, we calculated the occupancy-normalized tuning curve for each axon, for epochs in which the animals were actively navigating through the virtual environment (velocity > 5 cm/s). Then, we calculated 1000 turning curves based on within-lap circular shuffles of the position. Positions where tuning curves that exceeded 95% of the shuffled data for at least 4 cm were considered candidates for place field locations. The place field center and width were assigned by fitting a Gaussian to the position of the animal at each of the transient Ca[2+] events (center as the mean of the distribution and width as 2s). Place fields were additionally required to have at least 3 Ca[2+] transients within them (to filter out low activity axons). Place fields were calculated independently for each of the conditions considered (VR-On/VROff, RZ1/RZ2 Context A/Context B, and for the random foraging task Visual Only/Multi-Sensory). 

## Position Decoding 

In order to make comparisons about the degree to which EC input to CA1 might be used for the propagation of spatially selective signals, we trained a multi-class (one-vs-one) Support Vector Machine (as in Stefanini et al.[79] ) on the extracted transients. For each dataset, a random sample of ROIs was used to ensure that the same number of ROIs were used per mouse for both LEC and MEC animals. For all position decoding analyses, the track was split into 4 cm bins each given a separate class label. Two laps were then held out of the dataset and the classifier was tested on these 2 while being trained on the rest. The train/test split was rotated across all laps until each lap was tested on once. ROIs used for decoding as well as train/test splits were determined psuedo-randomly for 3 different splits. Unless annotated, 1,000 ROIs were used per FOV for the VR navigation and random foraging tasks, 900 ROIs were used for the reward alternation task, and 750 for the context change tasks. FOVs without sufficient ROIs detected were excluded from this analysis. For the VR navigation task, each condition (VR-Off or VR-On) was considered separately, and only laps 5-25 for each condition were analyzed. For the reward alternation task, RZ1 and RZ2 conditions were also considered separately; meanwhile, for the context alternation task, positions in Context A and Context B were given different class labels, and all 40 laps were included in a single analysis (expect for the across context decoding). For all of the position decoding analysis, locations 0 - 25 cm and 375 - 400 cm were not included due to most of the visual cues being blocked by the end tunnel and for the randomized reward task, the first lap was excluded from the analysis (to make an even number for the train/test split). 

## Velocity Decoding 

To decode the speed of the mice from the Ca[2+] activity, we used Lasso Regression as in Stefanini et al.,[79] training on the DF/F from a randomly selected subset of 1000 axonal ROIs per mouse. Leave one out cross validation by lap was used to asses model performance. 

## Context/Reward Zone Decoding 

Previous lap context or reward zone were decoded similarly during the 2 second between-lap timeout period when the VR screens were dark. Decoding was performed by training a linear SVM on a smoothed (Gaussian smoothing s = 0.27 s) vector of the Ca[2+] transient onset times, specifically from the inter-lap period. The classifier was trained on 36 laps and tested on 4, one from each context/ RZ block (test laps were the identical lap-in-context relative to the last time the context switched). 

## Grid Classifier 

The grid tuning of axons was assessed as a two-step process. The first step was to find identify putative ‘‘grid-like’’ ROIs with activity that followed a spatially localized periodic firing pattern; the second step was to fit those ROIs to an idealized grid pattern, as in Yoon et al.[109] The identification in the first step was based on a series of criteria adapted from Domnisoru et al.[110] and similar to commonly used, established criteria used to classify MEC activity as ‘‘grid-like’’ in 1D environments.[64][,][109][,][111] Specifically, axons had to meet 5 criteria: (1) there must be 2 identified spatial fields on the track, (2) the number of transitions between in-spatial field and out-of-field firing must be L/ (5w) where L is the track length (in this case 400 cm) and w is the mean spatial field width of the axon’s 1D response, (3) the width of the widest spatial field for any axon must be less then 5w, (4) At least 30% of the spatial bins must be able to be assigned to either in-field or out-of-field periods, (5) the observed in-field activity must be twice that of out-of-field activity. Given that a lot of presumably non-grid LEC axons also passed these criteria, we took the additional step to fit the 1D tuning curves to the best matching idealized tuning curve by taking a linear slice through a 2D grid lattice. In order to check how well the ‘‘grid-like’’ axons matched grid tuning, we fit both the LEC and MEC axons’ tuning curves to 1D slices of idealized grid fields. We found no difference in how well they fit, on average (mean grid fit, r, LEC Context A: 0.7275, Context B: 0.7256, MEC Context A: 0.7131, Context B: 0.7345, Figure S12E), suggesting that the grid-like activity might be due to the periodic nature of the virtual cues (some are repeated along the track) and the fixed reward zone. Additionally, grid cells would be expected to have the same grid field spacing (l) regardless of the context, so we compared how much the grid spacing changed between contexts. We found that while the difference in the LEC grid-fit spacing was altered slightly less than would be expected by chance (likely due to the bias of LEC ROIs to form spatial fields around the reward zone), the MEC grid fits were identical to the shuffled data (mean by mouse, dl compared to shuffled l all ROIs, LEC p=0.0017, MEC p=0.3047, Figure S12F). The MEC activity is no more ‘‘grid-like’’ than we observe in any multi-peaked spatial cells constrained by the parameters of the 1D navigation task (with a 4m 1D environment). It is possible that deformations of the grid pattern around the goal locations[62][,][63] may be obscuring our ability to measure conclusively the percentage of grid-like axons in our datasets. Additionally, our results are limited by the length of the track and the resolution of the GCaMP6s indicator; given our observations, we can only report evidence for spatial, likely nongrid, cells with context dependent tuning curves participating the MEC projection to CA1.

Vertebrate gap junctions are composed of proteins encoded by the Cx gene family and consist of two hemichannels each contributed by the cells that share this junctional channel. Hemichannels are hexameric high-conductance plasma membrane channels that have a low open probability before incorporation into gap junctions. ## **2. Experimental procedures** 

## _2.1. Cell cultures and reagents_ 

We used C6 glioma wild type, C6 stably transfected with Cx43 [16], C6 stably transfected with Pannexin-1 (Panx1) coupled to a Myc-tag [17], HEK293 cells stably transfected with the P2X7 receptor [18] and HeLa cells stably transfected with Cx43 [19]. C6 cells were maintained in DMEM:Ham’s F12 (1:1—Invitrogen, Merelbeke, Belgium) and HeLa and HEK293 cells were grown in DMEM all supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 2 mM glutamine, 10 U/ml penicillin, 10 �g/ml streptomycin and 0.25 �g/ml fungizone. Cells were seeded at a density of 25,000 cells/cm[2] and used for experiments the next day (subconfluent cultures). 

Primary cultures of mixed glial cells were isolated from the cerebral cortex of newborn Sprague–Dawley rats according to the method of [20], resulting in astrocyte-enriched cultures as previously described [21]. These cells were grown in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 1% glutamine and 0.5% gentamycine for 12 days with the medium being refreshed every two days. 

Fura-2-AM, 4-Br-A23187, Sypro Ruby Orange Protein Blot Stain, propidium iodide, bis-(1,3-dibutylbarbituric acid)trimethine oxonol (DiBAC4(3)) and 1,2-bis( _o_ -aminophenoxy)ethane- _N_ , _N_ , _N_[′] , _N_[′] - tetraacetic acid (BAPTA-AM) were obtained from Molecular Probes (Invitrogen). ## _2.2. Extracellular ATP measurements_ 

ATP release was measured directly in the medium above the cells using a plate reader protocol (at room temperature) as previously described in [10]. Baseline measurements were carried out on separate cultures, according to the same procedure but with HBSS–HEPES (Hanks’ balanced salt solution, pH 7.4) vehicle only. All applied inhibitory agents were incubated in the CO2 incubator at 37[◦] C for the times indicated in the text, and were not present dur- 

ing stimulation to avoid interference with luminometry. Average baseline ATP release was 14.53 ± 4.02 pmol ( _n_ = 250) for C6-Cx43 cells and 17.87 ± 4.96 pmol ( _n_ = 77) for HeLa-Cx43 cells. 

## _2.3. Ca[2+] imaging_ 

Cell cultures were loaded with 5 �M Fura-2-AM in HBSS–HEPES (pH 7.4) for 1 h, followed by a 30 min de-esterification. Imaging was performed on an inverted epifluorescence microscope (Nikon Eclipse TE 300, Analis, Ghent, Belgium) with a 40× objective as described previously [10]. Excitation, alternating between 340 and 387 nm (Fura-2-B filterset from Semrock, AHF Analysentechnik, Tübingen, Germany), was done with a Lambda DG-4 filterswitch (Sutter Instrument Company, Novato, CA). A 430 nm long-pass dichroic mirror was used in combination with emission bandpassfiltering at 510 nm (40 nm bandwith). Images were acquired with an electron multiplying CCD camera (Quantem 512SC, Photometrics, Tucson, AZ) and stored in a PC equipped with a FireWire interface board (Turbo 1394, Photometrics, Tucson, AZ). Ratio images were generated with software written in Microsoft Visual C[++] 6.0, after standard background correction procedures. Fura-2 _in vitro_ calibrations were carried out with Ca[2+] -free (10 mM EGTA, 130 mM KCl and 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.2) and Ca[2+] -saturating solutions (10 mM CaCl2, 130 mM KCl and 10 mM HEPES pH 7.2) containing 5 �M pentapotassium fura-2 salt; a _K_ d of 224 nM was used to convert ratios to [Ca[2+] ] _i_ . 

## _2.4. Membrane depolarization measurements_ 

Membrane depolarization was determined with the voltage sensitive oxonol dye DiBAC4(3). For these experiments, C6-WT or C6-Cx43 cells, grown on glass coverslips, were preloaded with 50 nM DiBAC4(3) in HBSS–HEPES for 15 min at room temperature. Thereafter the coverslips were placed on the microscope (same setup as used for Ca[2+] imaging) and perfused with HBSS–HEPES, also containing 50 nM DiBAC4(3). Images were continuously taken (one per second) with 482 nm excitation, a 505 nm long-pass dichroic and 535 nm (35 nm bandwidth) emission bandpass filtering. Double labelling experiments used for probing propidium iodide dye uptake into the cells were performed with TRITC filter settings. 

## _2.5. siRNA transfection_ 

The cells were transiently transfected with ON-TARGETplus SMARTpool siRNA from Dharmacon (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Erembodegem, Belgium) containing a mixture of four siRNA duplexes (5[′] -CAACAACCUGGCUGCGAAAUU-3[′] ; 5[′] -UGAUUGAAAUGUCGAGUUAUU-3[′] ; 5[′] -CGUGAAGGGAAGAAGCGAUUU-3[′] ; and 5[′] -UUACUGAGAUUCUGCGAUAUU-3[′] ). The negative control siRNA was obtained from the same company (ON-TARGETplus siCONTROL, Dharmacon). Transfection efficiency with Dharmafect 1 transfection reagent was in the order of ∼85% as assayed with a fluorescent siGLO transfection efficiency indicator (Dharmacon). Briefly, the cells were seeded at a density of 37,500 cells/cm[2] and transfected 24 h later with 125 nM siRNA or the negative control siRNA, using 1.5 �l of transfection reagent per square centimeter. Twenty-four hours after transfection, the medium was replaced and the cells were incubated with culture medium for 24 h before the experiments were started.

## _2.9. Data analysis and statistics_ 

The data are expressed as mean ± s.e.m., with ‘ _n_ ’ (indicated on the graph) denoting the number of independent experiments on different cultures. The variations in the magnitude of triggered ATP signals observed in some figures represent normal variability between different experimental groups; in some cases it may be related to different experimental conditions, e.g. the presence of different vehicles. Comparisons between two groups were carried out using a one-tailed unpaired _t_ -test, with a _p_ -value below 0.05 indicating significance. Comparison of more than two groups was done with one-way ANOVA and a Bonferroni post-test. Statistical significance is indicated in the graphs with a single symbol (* or #) for _p_ < 0.05, two symbols for _p_ < 0.01 and three symbols in case _p_ < 0.001. 

## **3. Results** 

## _3.1. Ca[2+] ionophore triggered ATP responses in Cx43 expressing_ 

## _cells_ 

Exposing C6 or HeLa cells stably transfected with Cx43 (respectively C6-Cx43 and HeLa-Cx43 cells) to various concentrations of the Ca[2+] ionophore 4-Br-A23187 (further called A23187—applied during 5 min) induced transient [Ca[2+] ] _i_ changes that increased in amplitude with increasing A23187 concentrations (0.1–10 �M range; Fig. 1E and inset). Ionophore application (5 min) also triggered ATP release in the extracellular medium and these responses were maximal at 2.2 �M A23187 in C6-Cx43 and 2.0 �M A23187 in HeLa-Cx43 while fading away with lower or higher concentrations (Fig. 1A–B). Dye uptake studies in C6-Cx43 cells with propidium iodide (PI) gave a similar concentration-dependency profile (data not shown). The kinetics of the responses are illustrated in Fig. 1C, demonstrating maximal ATP release with 5 min exposure to A23187. A graph of ATP release as a function of the peak [Ca[2+] ] _i_ level obtained during ionophore stimulation, indicated that the responses were maximal at ∼500 nM [Ca[2+] ] _i_ , similar to observations in Cx32 expressing cells [10]. 

_3.2. Ca[2+] -triggered ATP release is blocked by gja1 suppression and Gap peptides and does not involve major contributions of vesicular release, pannexin 1 (Panx1) hemichannels or P2X7 receptor pores_ 

C6 wild-type cells (C6-WT) or C6 cells stably transfected with Panx1 containing the short (9 amino acid) Myc-tag at the carboxyterminus (C6-Panx1-Myc) displayed no detectable ATP release above baseline over the whole 0.1–5 �M A23187 concentration range applied (Fig. 1D), indicating that the responses in C6-/HeLaCx43 were related to the presence of Cx43. Exposing C6-Panx1-Myc cells to 100 �M Bz-ATP (2.5 min) triggered significant dye uptake (44.1 ± 6.2% PI-positive cells compared to 0.2 ± 0.1% without trigger; _p_ = 0.0001; _n_ = 18), demonstrating that Myc-tagged Panx1 forms functional hemichannels as reported by Bruzzone et al. [24]. Combined Ca[2+] and Mg[2+] -free (divalent-free) extracellular conditions also triggered significant dye uptake in these cells (data not shown), confirming data reported by Scemes et al. in astrocytes [25].

## _cascade_ 

The metabolism of AA by lipo- and cyclo-oxygenases is associated with the production of ROS, such as hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) and superoxide radicals (O2[−] ) [51]. Moreover, ROS can stimulate the synthesis of NO via NO synthase [52]. To test the involvement of ROS, we incubated the cells with the anti-oxidants and free radical scavengers N-acetyl-l-cysteine (1 mM, 1 h), �-tocopherol succinate (150 �g/ml, 1 h), and superoxide dismutase (SOD; O2[−] to H2O2 conversion, 200 �g/ml, 1 h). N�-nitro-l-arginine (100 �M, 1 h) was used as an inhibitor of NO synthesis. All these substances strongly and significantly inhibited ATP release triggered by A23187, CALP1 and AA (Fig. 8A–C). Ramachandran et al. have reported that ROS exposure depolarizes the cell membrane and thereby opens hemichannels [8]. We tested whether AA was, like ROS, able to trigger a depolarizing response by membrane potential measurements with the voltage-sensitive dye DiBAC4(3). Perfusion of C6-WT cells with a solution in which all extracellular sodium was replaced by potassium (giving an extracellular potassium concentration of 142 mM, applied 5 min) triggered a robust fluorescence increase, reflecting depolarization to an estimated 0 mV level (Fig. 8D). In a next set of experiments performed on C6-Cx43 cells, we included 100 �M PI dye in the extracellular solution (in addition to the voltage dye) to mark cells in which hemichannels opened. After 5 min exposure of the cells to high extracellular potassium, PI-positive cells appeared and subsequent analysis showed that the DiBAC4(3) fluorescence, measured in the cytoplasm, was much larger in the PI-positive cell population as compared to PInegative cells (Fig. 8E), indicating a relation between potassium depolarization and hemichannel opening. When cells were exposed to 50 �M AA, PI-positive cells displayed a stronger DiBAC4(3) signal (indicating depolarization) than PI-negative cells but the difference was smaller as compared to the high potassium effect (Fig. 8E).

## **4. Discussion** 

The present work demonstrates, in two cell lines stably transfected with Cx43 and in rat primary cortical glial cells that hemichannel-related responses like ATP release or dye uptake can be triggered by changes in [Ca[2+] ] _i_ . This is not due to a direct effect of intracellular Ca[2+] on the Cx43 subunit, but is mediated by a complex intermediate signaling cascade. Based on the strong blocking effects of Cx43 knock-down with siRNA (a major argument for hemichannel involvement [58]), combined with the exclusion of alternative release mechanisms and the bidirectionality of the pathway, we conclude that Cx43 hemichannels are a major source for ATP release recorded in this study. 

[Ca[2+] ] _i_ elevations are since long known to influence gap junctional communication and there is strong evidence that Ca[2+] –CaM interactions with the connexin protein play a central role in this regulation [59,60]. In this study, we demonstrate that Ca[2+] –CaM activation is also involved in controlling hemichannel responses, based on work with W7 and the CaM-activating peptide CALP1. Surprisingly, the dose-dependency of the responses triggered by CALP1 was S-shaped, while those triggered by A23187 and glutamate were bell-shaped and decreased again with higher concentrations (compare Fig. 1A and B to 5A and 9A). This indicates that the disappearance of the responses with high A23187 concentrations (and by inference high [Ca[2+] ] _i_ ) is mediated by CaM-independent signaling. The two parts of the bell-shaped response curve are thus mediated by distinct underlying mechanisms.

Experiments with CaMK-II antagonists, AIP and KN62, demonstrated the involvement of this kinase in hemichannel regulation. Active CaMK-II can phosphorylate and activate p38 MAPK [43]. The latter has recently been reported to stimulate hemichannelmediated dye uptake in HeLa-Cx43 cells [12]. SB202190, a p38 MAPK antagonist, suppressed ATP release triggered with A23187 or CALP1, while ATP release triggered by AA was not inhibited by SB202190. These results put p38 MAPK upstream of cPLA2 and downstream of Ca[2+] –CaM. Elevated [Ca[2+] ] _i_ , active CaMK-II and p38 MAPK may induce activation and translocation of cPLA2 from the cytosol to the intracellular membranes [45–47]. Ca[2+] thereby promotes the insertion of hydrophobic residues into the lipid bilayer, while p38 MAPK and CaMK-II phosphorylate cPLA2 on S505 and S515 respectively, increasing its intrinsic activity 2–3-fold [47], resulting in the release of AA from the membranes. AA has previously been reported to modulate Cx43 hemichannel related responses [7,61] and we here demonstrate that the AA signaling cascade is involved in Ca[2+] -triggered ATP release. Moreover, exogenously applied AA was able to trigger hemichannel responses by itself. Released AA is further metabolized by cyclo-oxygenases and lipoxygenases, a process that is associated with the production of ROS [51,62]. The strong effect of ROS antagonism with the various triggers applied (A23187, CALP1, AA and glutamate - see Figs. 8A–C, 9D and 10C) clearly supports involvement of ROS signaling. Additionally, ROS activation of NO synthase with subsequent NO release [52], a messenger that has been demonstrated to activate hemichannels via S-nitrosylation of the Cx43 protein [63], is also part of the signaling cascade (Fig. 8A–C). The finding that AA induces a small membrane depolarization in C6-WT cells (Fig. 8D) suggests a possible link toward hemichannel opening. The magnitude of the AA-triggered depolarization was however much smaller than the one observed with high potassium exposure. The double staining experiments performed in C6-Cx43 (Fig. 8E) furthermore demonstrate a relation between the degree of depolarization and hemichannel opening (with a possible extra contribution here of open hemichannels to the observed responses). While it is clear that the depolarization brought about by high potassium or AA is certainly below the strongly positive voltage steps (several tens of mV in the positive voltage scale) needed to open hemichannels in whole cell patch clamp experiments [64], it is conceivable here that a combination of factors – depolarization, altered redox status, S-nitrosylation and positive modulation by AA – is successful in opening hemichannels in intact (non-patch clamped) cells - a notion that needs to be further addressed in electrophysiological experiments. 

Many of the inhibitor substances applied displayed strong blocking effects, often suppressing the responses to baseline level. In the case of Cx43 knock-down, there was almost complete inhibition of the functional responses, while the Cx43 protein levels were only reduced by 50%. This apparent discrepancy may be the consequence of a drastic reduction of the presence of connexin protein in the plasma membrane while leaving a residual connexin pool inside the cell. In addition, positive feed-back actions combined with a threshold phenomenon in the intermediate signaling cascade may also add their part. If the signal strength is above threshold, positive feed-back will amplify the initially small signal to produce a measurable endpoint response. However, as soon as the signal drops below threshold, amplification is lost and the responses may disappear.

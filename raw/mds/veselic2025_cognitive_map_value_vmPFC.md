## SUMMARY 

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is crucial for economic decision-making. However, how PFC value representations facilitate flexible decisions remains unknown. We reframe economic decision-making as a navigation process through a cognitive map of choice values. We found rhesus macaques represented choices as navigation trajectories in a value space using a grid-like code. This occurred in ventromedial PFC (vmPFC) local field potential theta frequency across two datasets. vmPFC neurons deployed the same grid-like code and encoded chosen value. However, both signals depended on theta phase: occurring on theta troughs but on separate theta cycles. Finally, we found sharp-wave ripples—a key signature of planning and flexible behavior—in vmPFC. Thus, vmPFC utilizes cognitive map-based computations to organize and compare values, suggesting an alternative architecture for economic choice in PFC. 

## INTRODUCTION 

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is fundamental for learning and choice.[1–10] A dominant idea in economic decision-making has been that value is represented in a common currency format, which facilitates efficient action selection.[1][,][5][,][11] While these studies have led to great progress toward understanding the PFC’s neural code in relatively simple and overlearned decision contexts, humans and animals often face novel choices where they must infer or construct value based on previous experience.[12][,][13] Here, we investigated whether making choices of this kind require an alternative framework for representing choice options within a value space; one in which the representation of choice task structure becomes crucial. 

In contrast to research on the PFC, research on the medial temporal lobe (MTL) has investigated representations of structure and representations supporting inference.[14] In the MTL, cognitive maps encoding the relationships between entities in the world are built, supporting flexible behavior.[15][,][16] Two key neural mechanisms allow for this: the ability to infer vectors between different locations using a grid-like code[14] and planningrelated signals observed during replay and coinciding with sharp-wave ripples (SWRs).[17][,][18] Grid cells represent the structure of space[19–21] and such structural representations are 

thought necessary for inferential choices that go beyond direct experience.[14][,][16][,][21] Relatedly, ripples in MTL may reflect planning signals during model-based reinforcement learning,[18] as place cells encode sequences of locations during replay,[17] which may underlie our ability to compositionally bind information and help facilitate novel choice.[22] 

While investigating cognitive map-like representations and computations has historically focused on physical space and the MTL, such signals have now been found in abstract spaces and outside the MTL.[12][,][13][,][15][,][23–29] fMRI work in both human and non-human primates has implicated the ventral and medial parts of the PFC (mPFC and vmPFC),[12][,][13][,][15] perhaps due to their strong anatomical links with the MTL.[30][,][31] While this suggests the computations supporting inference, novel choice, and planning in spatial navigation may be a general neural mechanism implemented in the brain, their link to choice has not yet been shown. Motivated by recent findings in non-human primate fMRI showing a gridlike code in a value space as subjects passively navigated between trials,[12] we asked whether the same neural code was present during choice itself. Finding a grid-like code at choice together with SWRs in the PFC would suggest an alternative neural architecture for representing value and implementing optimal choice.

## RESULTS 

Behavior and encoding of chosen value across the PFC We first investigated the behavior and canonical value signals of two male rhesus macaques (Macaca mulatta) making decisions between two options (Figure 1A). In this task, an ‘‘option’’ had to be constructed compositionally from two previously learned cues (images) that never formed an option during training (see STAR Methods—dataset 1: Task and procedure). A set of five cues mapped to one out of five reward probability levels, while another set of five cues mapped to one out of five reward magnitude levels. The subjects’ choice accuracy was above chance (Figures 1B and 1C). We looked for canonical value signals in single neurons across the four regions we recorded from (see Figure S1A): anterior cingulate cortex (ACC, n = 198), dorsolateral PFC (dlPFC, n = 156), orbitofrontal cortex (OFC, n = 195), and vmPFC (n = 160), in line with how data from such experiments are typically analyzed. Neurons in ACC, dlPFC, and OFC significantly encoded chosen value (Figures 1D and 1E) and chosen value difference (Figures S3A and S3B) in a 300-ms window before subjects initiated their choice. The signal was overall strongest in ACC neurons compared with the other three regions, thereby matching similar studies.[3][,][33] In contrast, we found little evidence for a chosen value or chosen value difference code in vmPFC neurons during the same time period. This highlighted a frequent divergence between fMRI research, where choicerelated value signals in the vmPFC are commonly found,[12][,][34–36] and corroborates findings from electrophysiology, where choice-related activity is often weaker or absent relative to neurons in other regions.[37–39] 

## A cognitive map of value space in the vmPFC emerges at choice 

While we did not find canonical value signals in the vmPFC, we hypothesized the presence of map-like representations, based on previous work showing a grid-like code within the vmPFC and adjacent (medial PFC) regions.[12][,][13][,][15] We predicted the cues representing individual attributes would be composed into options and mapped onto a reward magnitude by reward probability value space (Figure 2A, left), where a choice option corresponds to a location in 2D space. Each choice between two options thus reflects a trajectory between two locations in this 2D space, analogous to vector-based navigation in physical space.[14] We tested the hypothesis that subjects construct a cognitive map of value space using a grid-like code. We did this by looking for periodic modulation of neural activity predicted to arise from firing rate properties of grid cells (Figure 2A, middle and right).[40][,][41] Based on previous work showing such modulation in theta frequency of local field potential (LFP) activity,[42] we first looked for a grid-like code in vmPFC LFP channel theta frequency (see STAR Methods—LFP preprocessing, value confound GLM, and hexadirectional analysis). 

We found a cognitive map of value space was represented with a grid-like code in vmPFC theta frequency in a 300-ms window before subjects initiated their choice (Figure 2B, t(15) = 3.20, pUncorrected = 0.006; pBonferroni = 0.03, Cohen’s d = 0.79) across recording sessions. The effect appeared before subjects made 

their choice (Figure 2C) and had distinct 6-fold periodicity, as in previous work investigating grid-like coding[15][,][41][,][42] (Figure 2D; see also Figures S2I and S2J for the strength of this effect based on a more detailed anatomical parcellation of vmPFC and Figure S3C for single channel examples). In contrast, there was no grid-like code in control LFP frequencies (Figure S2C; see STAR Methods—hexadirectional analysis control frequencies), as in Maidenbaum et al.[42] Individually, 25% of sessions (4/16) showed significant 6-fold modulation (Figure 2E, binomial test, pUncorrected < 0.001; pBonferroni < 0.001, see Figures 2F and 2G for an example session). We found no grid-like code in ACC (t(40) = 1.54, p = 0.13) or OFC (t(33) = 1.73, p = 0.09) despite previous reports.[15][,][43] Notably, while OFC contained a significant grid-like code within an earlier temporal window (Figure 2C), this effect did not survive cluster-based permutation testing. Last, we found no grid-like code in dlPFC (t(31) = 0.12, p = 0.182, see Figures S2A, S2B, and S2D for individual comparisons). 

In additional analyses, we observed two suggestive trends. The first trend implied the grid-like code in the vmPFC became stronger as a stimulus set became more familiar (Figures S2E– S2G), perhaps suggesting the cognitive map is constructed during choice and further consolidated during sleep by hippocampal SWRs.[44] The second trend suggested the neural geometry of the cognitive map in the vmPFC is better explained by an objective (i.e., veridical) compared with a subjective (i.e., distorted) representation of the cognitive map (p < 0.05, permutation testing, Figures S3D–S3F; see STAR Methods—distorting the cognitive map via behavioral model fitting). The latter was obtained by fitting prospect theory models to subjects’ behavior and using the magnitude and probability distortion parameters to generate subjectively defined cognitive maps. This trend implies brain regions downstream in the choice process may distort information contained within the veridical cognitive map found in the vmPFC, suggesting a double dissociation of value representations across the PFC. Based on exploratory analyses, a candidate region where this could happen is the dlPFC (see Figures S3G–S3I) where neural activity was better explained by a subjective (compared with objective) chosen value code (t(155) = 3.15, pUncorrected = 0.002; pBonferroni = 0.008). Examining distortions in the neural geometry of value representations based on behavioral distortions from prospect theory may offer a principled approach to linking prospect theory with its neural instantiation in the PFC. 

To demonstrate the robustness of our result, we replicated the main effect from Figure 2B using the same analysis approach in an independent dataset that is currently being collected (Figure 2H, t(10) = 2.74, p = 0.02, Cohen’s d = 0.83, see Figure S1B and STAR Methods—dataset 2: Task and procedure). The effect similarly occurred before choice (Figure 2I) and exhibited 6-fold periodicity (Figure 2J). Concatenating the data across both datasets revealed a large effect size for a grid-like code in vmPFC (t(26) = 4.17, p < 0.001, Cohen’s d = 0.80). 

These results show subjects construct a cognitive map of value space with a grid-like code in the vmPFC, echoing previous reports,[12][,][13] and suggest how it may be used for choice. Importantly, this cognitive map for value is a latent, abstract representation of task structure which can be generalized across sensory instantiations (i.e., sets of visual cues) of this task, or tasks similar to it.[13][,][16][,][21] Because this embedding occurs at choice on a trialby-trial basis, navigation trajectories between choice locations in the value space can be computed. This suggests a grid-like code may be used for the choice process itself, where it could facilitate inference of novel choices[13][,][26] analogous to how grid cells facilitate inference of spatial shortcuts.[14] Therefore, navigating in space and making optimal choices in value-guided decisionmaking tasks may be solved with common neural mechanisms, such as vector-based navigation.[14] 

## Grid orientations realign across stimulus sets but are stable within stimulus sets 

To deepen the parallels with spatial cognition, we next tested whether the observed grid-like code was consistent across sessions and stimulus sets.[15] Grid cell grid orientations are typically stable within the environments they are recorded in but realign their grid fields across different environments.[48] Therefore, if the signal we observed indeed arose from populations of grid cells, this would predict a systematic difference in grid orientations across sessions, but only when the stimulus sets (i.e., ‘‘environment’’) differed. Our study was unique in that every few sessions the stimuli denoting reward magnitude and reward probability were changed (Figure 3A). Subjects had to learn these in a separate experiment interleaved with the choice task reported here. This meant identical value spaces (i.e., identical task structure) were decorrelated from the sensory properties of stimuli that subjects observed, analogous to having different sensory experiences in different spatial environments with an identical underlying spatial structure.[48] As predicted, when we used grid orientations from one session to estimate a grid-like code in different sessions, we only observed a consistent gridlike code within (Figure 3B, t(8) = 4.77, pUncorrected = 0.0014; pBonferroni = 0.007) but not across stimuli sets (Figure 3C, t(15) = 0.06, p = 0.95). 

We further demonstrated differences in grid orientations did not occur due to noise by computing grid orientation angle distances between sessions, comparing within vs. between stimulus set sessions (see STAR Methods—grid orientation realignment and consistency analysis). Mean distances between grid orientations within stimulus sets were significantly smaller compared with ones across stimuli sets (Figures 3D and 3E, z = 2.42, p < 0.01). The result in neither Figure 3B nor Figure 3E 

could be explained by potential within-session grid orientation correlations across channels as these were removed from the analysis beforehand. It could also not be explained by time differences of pairwise comparisons (i.e., degree of exposure to a stimulus set) on which the distances were computed (Figure S3J). 

Overall, these results demonstrate the existence of nonspatial, or abstract, grid realignment. Given grid cell realignment occurs when generalizing across spatial contexts,[48] our results suggest similar mechanisms may be at play in non-spatial generalization. This deepens the connection between the known computational properties of grid cells measured in rodents during spatial navigation[48] and our understanding of grid-like codes in the non-spatial environments of other species. Since this is a specific prediction of grid cells, it provides further evidence that the hexadirectional analysis used to estimate grid-like codes is an indirect measure of grid cell activity—a critical assumption underlying this analysis approach which has not been proven yet. Hence, these results support the idea of PFC grid cells building cognitive maps of abstract space that share computational properties of MTL grid cells building cognitive maps of physical space. 

## A grid-like code in vmPFC neurons and its theta-phase dependency 

Having established a cognitive map of value space exists in vmPFC LFP theta frequency, we next sought evidence for such a representation in vmPFC neurons. Despite multiple studies demonstrating population-level grid-like coding in blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) and LFP activity,[12][,][13][,][15][,][41][,][42] it has not been shown whether neurons can represent information with a grid-like code as well. 

We first investigated whether vmPFC neurons were theta modulated, similar to neurons in the hippocampal formation and OFC.[49][,][50] We aligned vmPFC neuron-firing activity to theta frequency phase (see STAR Methods—neuronal phase-aligning analyses) and, indeed, found they were theta modulated. vmPFC neurons fired most at theta-phase troughs and fired least at theta-phase peaks—(Figures 4A–4C, F(9, 159) = 8.87, p < 0.001, see also Figures S4A–S4C). We therefore isolated our hexadirectional analyses in vmPFC neurons to specific phases of several theta cycles using grid orientations from vmPFC LFP channels (Figure 4D, see STAR Methods—phase-aligned grid-like code analyses). Across vmPFC neurons, we observed a significant grid-like code at the mean theta-phase trough (Figure 4E, t(159) = 3.08, pUncorrected = 0.002; pBonferroni = 0.012, region of interest [ROI] 2), when neurons fired most (see Figures S5A–S5C for comparable analyses in OFC neurons).

value. This implies both neuronal subpopulations may interact with one another to facilitate optimal choice. 

More broadly, these results show that vmPFC neurons may employ a theta-phase-dependent neural code—encoding different task variables sequentially (or in parallel)—based on the phase of ongoing theta oscillations. Finally, demonstrating the existence of a cognitive map of value space in vmPFC neuronal activity strongly suggests the vmPFC could contain grid cells. 

SWRs are present in vmPFC and are modulated by valueguided choice 

Considering subjects had constructed a cognitive map of value space that was used during choice, we looked for evidence of other MTL cognitive map-related computations in vmPFC. Another such phenomenon are SWRs—and associated replay—in awake animals occurring at choice points.[17][,][44][,][51] SWR-associated replay is suggested to be a neural mechanism for the retrieval of information,[44][,][52][,][53] and model-based planning.[18] 

We used a ripple detector similar to previously published work investigating ripples in the MTL and cortex[52–55] to demonstrate the presence of oscillatory events within the ripple frequency range (80–180 Hz) comparable to ones previously reported in non-human primates.[56] After identifying candidate events, we further filtered events with a high signal-to-noise ratio (see Figure S7 and STAR Methods—sharp-wave ripple detection) to avoid issues with artifacts or noisily defined events.[57] This procedure revealed candidate MTL-like SWRs (Figure 5A, n = 4,162, median duration = 103 ± 42.08 SD ms; see also Figure S7). Crucially, despite our wide frequency range, the ripples were localized to a narrow frequency range spanning 90–140 Hz (Figure 5B, bottom). This concurs with previous findings[24][,][53][,][56][,][58] 

and suggests these ripple events were generated by a specialized circuit rather than reflecting spurious high-frequency noise spanning the entire frequency range. The simultaneously recorded vmPFC neurons were found to elevate their firing rate around the ripple window (Figure 5C)—and more on trials with ripples (Figure S7E)—which coincided with a ripple sharp-wave component (Figure 5D). 

When we localized ripples relative to task epochs and compared their frequencies across the four regions, we observed they mostly occurred shortly before and after choice, and at feedback, in addition to occurring at a much higher frequency in the vmPFC compared with other brain regions (Figure 5E). The periods with higher frequency in the vmPFC did not coincide with periods of a general tonic increase in vmPFC firing activity, which might have suggested they simply indexed a tonic increase in vmPFC neuron firing, such as what occurred in the other brain regions (Figure S7F).

This result was not explained by mean session reaction time, stimulus set familiarity, or subject identity, as these were regressed out before performing this analysis. 

Last, we tested a more precise hypothesis related to trialby-trial coupling between ripple frequency and value-based decision-making. Ripple frequency increases after receiving reward, possibly to allow learning and consolidation of rewarded states via replay.[44][,][59][,][60] In our task, this would suggest a higher ripple frequency after the reward period (i.e., the fixation period of the subsequent trial). Notably, theta stimulation during the post-reward fixation period is also known to disrupt learning and adaptive choice in a reinforcement learning (RL) task.[49] Because the probability of detecting these events on channels was lower in fixation compared with during choice, we performed a median split on channels, picking ones where more ripples were detected, to improve our signal-to-noise ratio. We then tested our prediction and found a higher proportion of ripple events occurring on trials after subjects were rewarded for their choice (Figure 5H), similar to other work showing increased ripple frequency following reward.[60] 

While ripples have been previously described in the human cortex and rodent mPFC, these results demonstrate that they can be observed in a non-human primate vmPFC within a value-based decision-making context.[53] Detecting another common feature of MTL (i.e., SWRs) predominantly in vmPFC 

corroborates rodent work finding ripple events in mPFC,[24] as well as previous non-human primate work in relation to the frequency range within which they were found.[56] It remains unclear whether these ripples originate in the hippocampal formation or vmPFC itself. If the latter, the synchronization of ripples between the two regions would be interesting to observe.[24] While ripples were modulated by task events (e.g., when they occurred), it is not clear what their precise role might be for value-based decision-making. Work within the MTL would suggest a role in the retrieval and consolidation of information,[44][,][53][,][54][,][61] replay of task states and choice trajectories,[17][,][62] or model-based planning.[18] These roles would be important in this task considering subjects had to compositionally bind visual cues into options to make choices using continuously novel stimulus sets (Figure 1A). The relationship between ripples, choice accuracy, and reward on the previous trial implies these patterns may be a mechanism by which state-value associations are consolidated or reinforced. 

## DISCUSSION 

We investigated neural representations that facilitate flexible, value-guided choice in the PFC by taking inspiration from the well-characterized map-like representations in the MTL and spatial cognition. We demonstrated a grid-like code of value space at choice in the LFP of two separate vmPFC datasets. We showed the same grid-like code in vmPFC neurons and demonstrated how their representations depend on theta phase. Furthermore, we showed that grid orientations realigned across stimuli sets. Finally, we found SWRs in non-human primate vmPFC and their modulation by valueguided choice.

Within this perspective, vmPFC represents value-based decision-making tasks in a way that is neither predicted nor explained by pre-existing theories of value-guided choice.[1][,][2][,][5][,][63][,][64] Furthermore, it means choosing between two options cannot be accounted for only through a comparison of two or more options in a common currency format.[1][,][5][,][63] Rather, these results suggest that a cognitive map is constructed in the vmPFC along the dimensions relevant for choice, and this representation is employed when a choice needs to be made between options along those dimensions. ## Limitations of the study 

One limitation of our study is that we could not provide direct evidence of grid cells in the vmPFC in the same manner as traditionally done in rodent spatial navigation. We did, however, show that vmPFC neurons contain a grid-like code for value using an indirect measure typically used in LFP/magne- 

toencephalography (MEG) and fMRI.[41] By examining the periodic properties of rate maps in vmPFC neurons, we were further able to show that these were related to the grid-like code, as would be predicted if the grid-like code was generated by local vmPFC grid cells. Our findings in the vmPFC, when considered alongside other studies reporting grid-like coding in PFC, provide strong support for future investigations of grid cells in the PFC with analysis approaches used to define grid cells in spatial navigation.[48] 

Relative to other PFC regions, we found weak evidence for a chosen value code in vmPFC neurons without theta alignment, which contrasts with previous reports in vmPFC neurons[39] and fMRI work.[12][,][34–36] However, both[37][,][39] together with our results, highlight that value-related signals in vmPFC neurons are relatively weak compared with other value-coding regions. One possibility for the discrepancy between neuronal[39] and fMRI findings[12][,][34–36] could be phase-dependent neuronal coding in the vmPFC: if longer time windows of neuronal firing activity are averaged or heavily smoothed over time, as is common in the neuroimaging field, phasedependent signals appearing on one or a few cycles may average out. In contrast, those appearing consistently over several cycles may get stronger, leading to divergent inferences between fields. Notably, this issue may even apply to discrepancies across studies within electrophysiology— averaging neural activity with longer vs. shorter smoothing and temporal windows may yield different results compared with analyzing it over time and/or with an oscillation-aligned approach. This further raises the question of whether the strength of value-based signals in PFC neurons would improve if they were to be assessed against the phase of ongoing oscillations in which they may be embedded.[50] 

This work raises three questions. Given the anatomical connectivity between the vmPFC and entorhinal cortex (EC), does the grid-like code in vmPFC LFP originate in the EC,[29][,][65][,][66] or is it generated by local neural circuitry within the vmPFC? Since we observed a grid-like code in vmPFC neurons, this suggests that the signal may originate within the vmPFC. While currently unclear, understanding the exact nature of the relationship

Previous human fMRI work reported a grid-like code in both the vmPFC and OFC, while nonhuman primate fMRI work reported it in the mPFC (perhaps localizing closer to Brodmann area 10).[4] If grid-like coding in the PFC is a ubiquitous representational scheme, dissociating the contribution of such signals across subregions of the PFC will be crucial for advancing our understanding of value-based decision-making.

## Conclusions 

In sum, our results suggest that the well-described neural mechanisms in the MTL, which support cognitive maps during spatial navigation, have been redeployed by the vmPFC to build cognitive maps of abstract space used for value-guided choice.

## METHOD DETAILS 

## Dataset 1: Task and procedure 

Subjects performed a two-alternative forced-choice task in which both options were simultaneously presented, one on each side of the screen (Figure 1A). Each option was parametrised by two attributes, reward magnitude and reward probability. Each attribute had five reward levels, yielding a total of 10 possible cues (images). Magnitude cues represented juice amount in arbitrary units (0.15, 0.35, 0.55, 0.75, 0.95). Probability cues represented the probability of receiving the reward (10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 90%). Each attribute could appear either at the top or bottom of the screen. The presentation of the cues and the position of attributes with respect to the screen were sampled uniformly to avoid any biases in the stimuli presentation. On each trial, the subject therefore saw four cues appear on the screen, two on the left side indicating the magnitude and probability of the left option and two on the right side indicating the magnitude and probability of the right option. These four cues appeared simultaneously to the subjects, and they were free to saccade to sample information about each option before making a choice. Note that this is a separate dataset from ref Hunt et al.[7] with the same subjects. Subjects chose by moving the joystick in the direction of the option they were choosing. Following the cue presentation and subjects’ choice, a short pre-feedback period followed, where subjects saw the selected option but did not receive a reward. This was followed by the feedback period where they received the outcome for the selected option followed by a fixation cross. 

Across all recording sessions, this task was interleaved with a learning task in which subjects learned to associate a new set of five images with reward probability and another set of five images with reward magnitude. These images comprised a stimulus set - such as the one presented in Figure 1 – and were used for three to four recording days. After this period, a new stimulus set would be introduced in the subsequent learning task session and the previous stimulus set discarded. This was followed by another three

## Dataset 2: Task and procedure 

Subjects performed a two-alternative forced choice task with sequential presentation of two options. Each option was parametrised by two attributes represented by cues (images): reward magnitude and reward probability. Each attribute had ten reward levels, resulting in a total of 20 possible cues. Magnitude cues represented juice amount in arbitrary units (0.17, 0.34, 0.51, 0.68, 0.85, 1.02, 1.19, 1.36, 1.53, 1.70). Probability cues represented the probability of receiving the reward (9.1%, 18.2%, 27.3%, 36.4%, 45.5%, 54.6%, 63.7%, 72.8%, 81.9%, 91%). The first option (‘Option 1’) was presented for 700 msec on either the left or right side of the screen and the subject was allowed to freely fixate the option. Both attributes of the option were presented simultaneously, and the position of reward magnitude and reward probability were randomised across trials. The second option (‘Option 2’) was presented for 700 msec and the subject was allowed to freely fixate the option. If Option 1 appeared on the left side of the screen, Option 2 would appear on the right side of the screen. The order of option presentation was randomised across trials. Once central fixation was re-acquired, both options were presented for choice. Choice was required through fixation for the chosen option for 500 msec. The chosen option was then presented paired with reward or no reward according to its associated magnitude and probability. Feedback on correct/incorrect choices was provided via a change in background colour. 

## Electrophysiological recordings 

For Dataset 1, we recorded neurons in ACC (dorsal bank of anterior cingulate sulcus, area 24c), dlPFC (both dorsal and ventral banks of sulcus principalis, areas 9/46), OFC (primarily in the medial orbital gyrus, area 11/13), and vmPFC (a region from the rostral sulcus extending to the medial orbital sulcus and within the gyrus rectus; primarily comprising Brodmann areas 14, 10, 32, 13. See Table S1 for the frequency of recording sites based on two atlases[45][,][46] ). Recordings were done with single microelectrodes spaced apart at a minimum distance of 1 mm. For a detailed description of the recording protocol and recording locations from subjects from Dataset 1, see ref Hunt et al.[7] and Butler et al.[47] . In total, 724 neurons (198 – ACC [subject F = 97, subject M = 101], 156 – dlPFC [subject F = 107, subject M = 49], 195 – OFC [subject F = 108, subject M = 87], 160 – vmPFC [subject F 125, subject M = 35]) and 497 LFP channels (149 – ACC [subject F = 71, subject M = 78], 104 – dlPFC [subject F = 67, subject M = 37], 133 – OFC [subject F = 63, subject M = 70], 97 – vmPFC [subject F = 69, subject M = 28]) were analysed. 

For Dataset 2, the subject was implanted with a titanium head positioner for restraint. On the basis of preoperative 3T MRI and stereotactic measurements, a recording chamber was subsequently implanted on the left hemisphere and with its centre 26mm along the anterior-posterior (AP) coordinate plane. Postoperatively, gadolinium-attenuated MRI imaging and electrophysiological mapping of gyri and sulci confirmed chamber placement. Craniotomies were then performed inside the chamber to allow for neuronal recordings. During each recording session, up to 4 multi-channel linear electrodes (26-32 channel V- PROBES, Plexon, Dallas, TX) were lowered into the brain through a grid with custom-built manual microdrives to measure neuronal activity. A Plexon Omniplex system was used to record neuronal data at 40 kHz and local field potential data at 1kHz. Single-unit isolation was achieved with manual spike sorting with a Plexon Offline Sorter. Neurons were randomly-sampled, with no attempt to pre-screen for responsiveness. We recorded neuronal data from vmPFC, defined as all sites medial to the medial orbital sulcus and within the rectus gyrus (area 14) spanning AP 35-42mm. Recordings were also made in OFC defined as sites between the medial and lateral sulci (areas 11/13) spanning AP 35-42mm. In total 252 channels from vmPFC and 209 channels from OFC were analysed.

## Determining significance of the hexadirectional analysis 

For each channel, we first averaged the three independent regression betas that were obtained from the hexadirectional analysis. This yielded channel-level beta estimates which indicated the strength of a grid-like code on a per-channel basis. To avoid concerns about whether regression estimates from individual LFP channels within the same brain region can be statistically treated as independent units of analysis (e.g. due to volume conduction-induced correlations from nearby recording sites), we further averaged channel estimates within a brain region to obtain independent session-level estimates. This yielded session-level beta estimates for each brain region. To perform statistical inference on these quantities, we employed two separate methods. First, to determine a significant population-level effect across sessions, we performed one-sample t-tests across sessions for each symmetry to investigate whether the beta estimates were significantly different from zero. The crucial prediction is that these betas would yield a positive, significant t-value for sixfold symmetry but not control symmetries. That would imply a higher theta frequency LFP signal on angles aligned with the grid axis compared to angles that were misaligned with the grid axis. Second, to estimate the significance of the effect on a per-session basis, we compared session-level beta estimates to a null distribution by shuffling the dependent variable 1000 times on a per-session basis before running the hexadirectional analysis. The session-level betas were considered significant if they exceeded the 99th percentile of the null distribution. These analyses were always performed for control symmetries as well. Similar to the first approach described above, session-level significance was estimated based on session-level averages to avoid concerns about whether individual channels can be considered independent units of analysis. 

To further verify the signal was hexadirectionally modulated, we used the computed grid orientation estimates to align and bin the held-out trial angles into aligned and unaligned trials. This was done in a way where the trial-by-trial angles and neural activity were always aligned and subsequently binned with cross-validated grid orientations. For each channel, we therefore obtained a series of six angle bins for both aligned (0, 60, 120, 180, 240, 300) and misaligned (30, 90, 150, 210, 270, 330) trials that spanned the whole value space. We first averaged the trials for each bin of a channel and then averaged all channels within a session, similar to the procedure used to determine the significance of a grid-like code in individual regions. 

Across all recording sessions of Dataset 1, there was variance in the number of trials per session, spanning from 151 to 277 trials. Because the hexadirectional analysis is sensitive to angle sampling, we removed six sessions with the lowest number of trials to improve the grid orientation estimates and reliability of our results. This removed sessions with fewer than 200 trials per session (removing 11.27% of recorded channels with the lowest number of trials). 

## Hexadirectional analysis control frequencies 

To determine whether the grid-like code was present only in theta frequency (5 – 8Hz) or other frequencies as well, we used a similar approach as in ref Maidenbaum et al.[42] by bandpassing LFP signal into individual frequencies (beta: 12 – 40Hz, low gamma: 41 – 60 Hz, high gamma: 61 – 100Hz), transformed the data using Morlet wavelets within each frequency, summed the obtained signal within each frequency, and down-sampled the data to 100Hz to speed up analyses. We repeated the hexadirectional analyses described in the previous section on this data.

## Grid orientation realignment and consistency analysis 

A key assumption of hexadirectional analyses is that the signal being indexed is generated due to firing statistics of conjunctive grid cells which fire more when movement is aligned with the main axes of grid firing fields.[41] However, this has so far not been proven. One way in which this claim has been strengthened previously is by showing that grid orientations measured using the hexadirectional analysis approach are stable across days and brain regions.[13][,][15] Crucially, a computational property of grid cells is that their grid orientations realign when moved between environments. In other words, the measured grid orientation from one environment may change when the grid orientation from the same grid cell is measured in another environment.[48] We were able to test this prediction due to our task design where the same environment (i.e. a set of five reward magnitude and reward probabilities) was continuously assigned to new stimuli sets (i.e. different sensory observations). This allowed for a different type of cross-validation from the one presented in Figure 2. ## Neuronal phase-aligning analyses 

To estimate whether firing activity in neurons was phase-dependent, we first estimated theta phase in the same frequency range in which we found the grid-like code (5 – 8Hz) on every trial. On every trial, we then binned theta phase into 10-non overlapping phase bins within which the raw firing rates were binned. For each phase bin, we slid a larger 300-millisecond window over time and averaged the firing rates within this window for each neuron and trial at each window increment. This allowed us to estimate whether firing activity was theta phase-dependent, estimate how theta phase dependency may change over time, and crucially, allow capturing more than one theta cycle within the 300-millisecond time window before subjects initiated their choice, which increased the robustness of the relationship between firing activity and theta phase. Figure 4A shows the phase-dependent mean across vmPFC neurons collapsed across the choice epoch while Figure 4B shows the phase-dependent mean of an example neuron without collapsing across the choice epoch. To determine whether neurons’ firing activity was theta phase-dependent, we first mean-subtracted each neuron’s activity at individual phase bins using the mean across all phase bins and examined the effect of phase in a oneway ANOVA. In the supplementary results, we performed further post-hoc paired t-tests across neurons where we compared firing activity across neurons at each phase bin. The post-hoc paired t-tests across neurons were used to determine the ‘Preferred’ and ‘Non-Preferred’ phases that were used to illustrate the phase-dependent firing in Figure 4C; the ‘Preferred’ phase corresponded to the phase bin with the largest relative firing activity across neurons compared to other phase bins while the ‘Non-Preferred’ phase corresponded to the phase bin with the lowest relative firing activity across neurons.

## Determining significance of the phase-aligned grid-like code analyses 

To determine whether significant positive t-values at each time point could be obtained by chance we used cluster-based permutation testing. For each neuron, we randomly shuffled its firing activity across trials 1000 times – using identical shuffles across time to preserve temporal smoothness - before computing the null distribution betas at each time point. We then performed t-tests across neurons for each shuffle and compared the empirically obtained t-values against the null distribution (97.5th percentile of a length-corrected null distribution).

## Sharp wave ripple detection 

To detect sharp wave ripples, we pre-processed the raw LFP data by removing line noise and filtering it to the ripple frequency (80 – 180Hz) as in previous non-human primate work.[56] To detect candidate ripple events, we used ripple detectors like previous work.[53][,][55][,][56] In brief, the ripple frequencu-filtered LFP signal was smoothed with a 6SD Gaussian (±3s). The envelope of the smoothed trace was then computed by using the Hilbert transform. This envelope was z-scored. On the z-scored envelope we looked for events with a total length of at least 25 msec, of which at least 15 msec needed to exceed 3SD throughout their duration. If several events were recorded where peak-to-peak was separated by less than 40 msec, these were combined into one ripple event. These candidate events were then further processed by a non-negative matrix factorisation with the aim of improving our signal-to-noise ratio and selecting events that have clearer spectral properties[56] due to recent concerns with respect to the signalto-noise ratio of ripple estimation.[57] We subjected the wideband spectral signal of each ripple event to a non-negative matrix factorisation with three clusters and 10 replicates. We then proceeded to compute for each of the three obtained clusters their lowrank approximations of the original spectral signal. For each cluster, we then computed the estimated signal-to-noise ratio for each frequency (by dividing the spectral signal by the summed squared error the projection of each cluster gave rise to relative to the original spectral signal). We then defined a minimum signal-to-noise threshold that was used to discard events below this threshold. We used a signal-to-noise ratio of 5 compared to the original 3 used by Logothetis et al.[56] The events passing this criterion needed to further pass the final criteria by which we selected only events as ripples when the projection of the cluster with the highest signal-to-noise ratio had a clear unimodal peak that was within the ripple frequency, as defined in ref Logothetis et al.[56] Crucially, this allowed for ripple events where the projection of a cluster with high signal-to-noise ratio can also be observed in other frequencies (e.g. due to theta sequence activity).

Figure S6. Periodic activation patterns in vmPFC neurons, related to Figure 4 (A) Flowchart of the analysis procedure to compute the Fourier magnitude differences. For each neuron, a rate map was computed based on the chosen state. On that rate map, a spatial Fourier transform was computed, and the magnitude of all Fourier coefficients was extracted. The magnitude of Fourier coefficients was then averaged based on their frequency (median split) and termed ‘‘low’’ and ‘‘high’’ frequency. Low- and high-frequency captured rate-map variation for neighboring and non-neighboring states respectively. The Fourier magnitude difference represents the difference between both groups (low-high). (B) To validate the Fourier magnitude difference measure, the mean square error (MSE) between empirical Fourier magnitude difference values (obtained from rate maps described in A above) and theoretical Fourier magnitude difference values was computed. Theoretical Fourier magnitude difference values were obtained from several candidate rate-map templates (described in K below). These templates represented hypotheses that captured rate maps arising from value-related or grid cell-related activity. Across all templates, MSE across vmPFC neurons was minimized when the template was derived from grid cell-related activity, suggesting patterns of rate-map activations in vmPFC neurons were consistent with grid cell activity. (C) The Fourier magnitude difference and hexadirectional modulation betas from Figure 4G correlated with one another. Each dot represents a neuron. (D) Fourier magnitude difference split based on a split across neurons. Each bar represents neurons classified as containing a high grid-like code (top third of neurons) and neurons classified as containing a low grid-like code (bottom third of neurons). 

(E) More principal components needed to explain the neural activity of the high vs. low grid-like code group of neurons. Black line denotes a significant difference (p < 0.05) obtained through permutation testing where neuron identity was shuffled (n = 1,000 permutations). 

(F) Putative navigation trajectories on trials where options are far apart (‘‘long’’—red) vs. close together (‘‘short’’—blue) may elicit more periodic activity over time, as several grid fields are encountered within a putative cognitive map. 

(G) The Fourier magnitude coefficients for a sample neuron across frequencies, capturing periodic variation over time. For each neuron, we used a 100-ms time window centered on the time period with a significant grid-like code across vmPFC neurons. The firing activity of each neuron was first averaged across trials based on the Euclidean distance between options as a proxy for navigation trajectory length. The mean firing activity was then Z scored to ensure values across neurons and conditions were comparable. Next, the slope of the Fourier magnitude coefficients across frequencies was estimated on these firing rates. Last, the slopes were averaged into (long and short) navigation trajectories based on a median split of Euclidean distance levels. Periodicity (over time) was captured by subtracting the estimated slopes (long-short). Short trajectories were expected to yield more negative slopes compared with long trajectories due to less periodic variation over time, reflected in dominant lower frequency components. (H) The periodicity effect across all vmPFC neurons indicates a steeper decay from low to high frequencies for trials with short compared with long navigation trajectories. (I) Splitting (H) into the high vs. low grid-like group revealed this effect was primarily driven by neurons with a high grid-like code. (J) Sample neurons with a significant periodicity effect from the high grid-like code group. For each neuron, firing activity was first averaged within trials based on Euclidean distance and then further averaged across Euclidean distance levels based on a median split to create two conditions (long, short), as described in (G). (K) Templates used to compute theoretical Fourier magnitude difference values. Templates were derived from idealized ‘‘grid cells’’ (hypotheses 1:7) and idealized ‘‘value cells’’ (hypotheses 8:17). Within each family, we assumed a neuron could only respond to high-value states where rank value within an attribute (high attr. value’) or expected value across attributes (high EV’) is considered. Brighter colors mean higher firing activity. In the template fitting procedure, we computed the MSE between the empirical Fourier magnitude difference obtained from vmPFC neurons and theoretical Fourier magnitude differences obtained from these templates. For each neuron and each template, this procedure was repeated for all viable map rotations. This ensured we examined both the linear attribute code of reward magnitude (depicted in the figure) and reward probability (90[�] rotation), in addition to different grid orientations.

## Thought Disorder in Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder 

ROCHELLE CAPLAN, M.D., DONALD GUTHRIE, PH.D., BETH TANG, M.S., KEITH H. NUECHTERLEIN, PH.D., AND ROBERT F. ASARNOW, PH.D. 

## **ABSTRACT** 

**Objective:** This study compared thought disorder and associated cognitive variables in attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and schizophrenia. **Method:** Speech samples of 115 ADHD, 88 schizophrenic, and 190 normal children, aged 8 to 15 years, were coded for thought disorder. A structured psychiatric interview, the WISC-R, the Continuous Performance Test, and the Span of Apprehension task were administered to each child. **Results:** The ADHD and schizophrenic groups had thought disorder compared with the normal children. However, the subjects with ADHD had a narrower range of less severe thought disorder than did the schizophrenic subjects.The younger children with ADHD and schizophrenia had significantly more thought disorder than did the older children with these diagnoses.IQ, attention, and working memory were associated with thought disorder in the ADHD but not the schizophrenic group. **Conclusions:** Thought disorder in childhood is not specific to schizophrenia and reflects impaired development of children’s communication skills. _J. Am. Acad. Child Adolesc. Psychiatry_ , 2001, 40(8):965–972. **Key Words:** attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder, schizophrenia, thought disorder, development, cognition. 

Children with attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) have a wide range of pragmatic deficits (see reviews in Damico et al., 1999; Tannock and Shachar, 1996). They talk too much in unstructured settings (Cunningham et al., 1985; Zentall, 1988), but too little when expected to (Hamlett et al., 1987; Tannock et al., 1993; Zentall, 1988). They do not attend to the listener’s needs or maintain the conversational context without disruption (Zentall, 1988). Furthermore, they have difficulty with maintaining and changing the ongoing topic without impeding the flow of conversation, as well as with selecting and using words to convey information unambiguously (Purvis and Tannock, 1997; Tannock et al. 1993; Tannock and Shachar, 1996).

The findings of numerous studies with the Continuous Performance Test (CPT) provide evidence for impaired information processing in children with ADHD (Levy and Hobbs, 1997; Losier et al., 1996; Nuechterlein, 1983; Overtoom et al., 1998). Unlike normal age- and gendermatched children, children with ADHD make more errors of omission and commission and have slower reaction times. These information processing deficits reflect distractibility, impulsivity, impaired vigilance, and faulty motor inhibitory control (Barkley, 1997) and do not appear to be a function of general intellectual level (Aylward et al., 1997). The information processing deficits in schizophrenic children (Strandburg et al., 1994) and children of a biological parent with schizophrenia (Nuechterlein, 1983; Rutschmann et al., 1986) have also been studied with the CPT. Like children with ADHD, schizophrenic children and children at genetic risk for schizophrenia have reduced hit rates, more false alarms, and slower reaction times (Nuechterlein, 1983; Rutschmann et al., 1986; Strandburg et al., 1994). Nuechterlein (1983) has shown that children at risk for schizophrenia differentiate poorly between target and nontarget stimuli (i.e., signal-to-noise discrimination), whereas children with ADHD have lowered response criterion (i.e., have a lowered threshold for responding to a target stimulus). 

In addition, Asarnow and colleagues (1995) demonstrated that schizophrenic children are impaired compared with normal mental-age-matched children in a serial information processing strategy during the partial report version of the Span of Apprehension task. These deficits reflect inefficiencies in controlled attentional processes that normally develop during middle childhood (Asarnow et al., 1995). A study of 19 schizophrenic children demonstrated that attentional deficits, measured by the Span of Apprehension, were associated with illogical thinking (Caplan et al., 1990). Loose associations, however, were associated with the WISC-R distractibility factor score. These findings suggested that, in schizophrenic children, the components of formal thought disorder might reflect different aspects of attentional impairment. The study presented in this paper examined whether, like schizophrenic children, the communication deficits of children with ADHD reflect specific attentional deficits rather than global cognitive deficits. 

Finally, deficits in working memory play a role in the behavioral and attentional deficits of children with ADHD (Barkley, 1997; Tannock et al., 1995). Karatekin and Asarnow (1998) and Oie et al. (1999) found similar deficits in verbal working memory in children with ADHD and schizophrenia compared with normal children. Although thought disorder is associated with deficits in working memory in schizophrenic adults (Nestor et al., 1998), there have been no studies on the relationship between thought disorder and working memory deficits in childhood schizophrenia and ADHD. 

In addition to determining whether children with ADHD have thought disorder, this study had three goals. 

The first goal was to ascertain the similarities and differences in the clinical manifestations and cognitive correlates of thought disorder in children with ADHD and schizophrenia, and to determine whether children with ADHD have thought disorder scores between those of children with schizophrenia and normal children. The second goal was to examine whether, as described in schizophrenic and normal children (Caplan et al., 2000), younger children with ADHD have more severe thought disorder than older children with ADHD. The third goal was to determine whether subjects with ADHD and comorbid psychiatric diagnoses have more severe thought disorder than subjects without these disorders.

This study was performed in accordance with the University of California, Los Angeles, Human Subject Protection Committee policies, and informed consents were obtained from the parents and assents from the children in the study. 

## Formal Thought Disorder 

Two raters with no knowledge of the children’s diagnoses coded videotapes of the Story Game with the Kiddie Formal Thought Disorder Rating Scale (K-FTDS), according to the method of Caplan et al. (1989). The scores derived from the K-FTDS ratings are frequency counts of illogical thinking, loose associations, incoherence, and poverty of content of speech divided by the number of sentences (clauses) spoken by the child. The generalizability coefficient (Mitchell, 1979) for illogical thinking and κ values for loose associations were 0.75 (SE = 0.15) and 0.66 (SE = 0.01), respectively, in 39 children. Because of a low base rate, we did not compute interrater agreement for incoherence and poverty of content of speech. 

## Cohesion 

Two raters with no knowledge of the children’s diagnoses coded the rate (frequency/number of clauses) of the following cohesion devices from transcriptions of the videotaped Story Game (Caplan et al., 1989), using Halliday and Hassan’s (1976) analysis of cohesion. _Conjunction_ ties together contiguous clauses (sentences) by using additive, contrastive, causal, temporal, and continuative relationships. 

_Referential cohesion_ involves use of a pronoun, demonstrative, definite article, or comparative to refer back to people or objects in the preceding spoken text. _Lexical cohesion_ provides the listener with ties between notions through word repetition, a synonym, or an antonym. In _unclear reference_ the speaker uses a pronoun, demonstrative, definite article, or comparative to refer to a person or object that has not been previously mentioned in the spoken text. In _ambiguous reference_ the speaker uses a referent that can apply to more than one person or object. If the speaker makes reference to the immediate environment rather than to the verbal context, this is called _exophora_ . 

The interrater agreement (intraclass correlation) of the cohesion scores among 31 children was 0.97 for conjunction, 0.96 for referential cohesion, 0.99 for unclear/ambiguous reference, 0.97 for lexical cohesion, 0.99 for ellipsis, and 0.99 for exophora. We combined the unclear and ambiguous reference scores for each child because of their low base rate. 

## Psychological Testing 

_IQ._ The WISC-R administered to each child generated IQ scores and factor scores, according to Kaufman (1979). 

_Degraded Stimulus Continuous Performance Test._ By degrading the image of the numerals presented to the subject, the Degraded Stimulus Continuous Performance Test (DS-CPT) is more difficult and more demanding of initial perceptual encoding processes than is the conventional CPT (Nuechterlein, 1983, 1991). The dependent variables computed for each of the 162 successive trials for the DS-CPT were (1) hit rate; (2) false alarm rate; (3) perceptual sensitivity, “d,” a measure of signal/noise discrimination; and (4) response criterion level, β, which has been shown to be independent of “d” for the DS-CPT (Nuechterlein, 1983, 1991). 

_Memory Load Continuous Performance Test._ The Memory Load Continuous Performance Test (3–7 CPT) is an adaptation of the original A-X CPT (Rosvold et al., 1956), but it involves shorter stimulus durations (40 milliseconds) and additional partial targets to increase difficulty level and burden on working memory (Nuechterlein et al., 1986). 

_Span of Apprehension._ In the partial report, forced-choice Span of Apprehension stimulus presentation, a series of arrays of letters was presented briefly, and the subject was asked to determine for each array which of two target letters was included, according to Asarnow et al. (1995). The subject’s score was the number of correct detections of the target stimulus for the 10-letter arrays. 

## DATA ANALYSIS 

The K-FTDS and cohesion scores were logarithmically transformed because of their skewed distribution. The thought disorder scores, expressed as frequency per clause, constituted the outcome variables. 

The mean Full Scale IQ of the schizophrenic children was more than 1 SD less than that of the normal children (Table 1). It was therefore necessary to correct for this disparity to analyze the thought disorder scores. Analysis of covariance could not yield the proper correction because the slope of the regression of the thought disorder scores on Full Scale IQ was different for the ADHD, schizophrenic, and normal children. 

We therefore used the following approach for the analysis of the thought disorder scores. First, we computed the linear regression of the logarithmically transformed thought disorder scores, expressed as frequency per clause on Full Scale IQ scores, separately for the ADHD, schizophrenic, and normal groups. Using the data from these analyses, we then computed estimates (and their standard errors) of the mean thought disorder scores for each diagnostic group at three

_Age._ A linear regression of thought disorder scores with age for the ADHD, schizophrenic, and normal groups demonstrated a main effect of age for illogical thinking, loose associations, referential cohesion, lexical cohesion, and exophora ( _F_ 1,364 = 8.23–71.38, _p_ < .004). With age, 

illogical thinking and exophora scores decreased, but referential cohesion and lexical cohesion increased in all three groups of children. Loose associations, found only in the schizophrenic children, decreased with age. Above and beyond the differences in age, however, the ADHD and schizophrenic subjects had significantly more thought disorder than did the normal subjects ( _F_ 2,300 = 6.7–11.16, _p_ < .002).

In the schizophrenic group, other than loose associations and the distractibility factor score ( _r_ = –0.30, _p_ < .01), the thought disorder and IQ variables were not correlated. In the normal group, lexical cohesion alone correlated significantly with verbal IQ ( _r_ = 0.25, _p_ < .002) and the verbal comprehension factor score ( _r_ = 0.20, _p_ < .01).

## **DISCUSSION** 

This study demonstrates that, above and beyond differences in IQ, gender, ethnicity, and socioeconomic status, children with ADHD have poorly organized thoughts (i.e., increased use of illogical thinking, exophora) and impaired use of cohesive devices (i.e., decreased use of conjunctions, increased use of lexical cohesion) to connect ideas across sentences compared with normal children. Children with ADHD have a narrower range (i.e., no loose associations, referential cohesion, unclear/ambiguous reference) of less severe thought disorder than do schizophrenic children. These findings confirm and extend those of other studies, which have indicated that children with ADHD have communication deficits that involve difficulties maintaining the topic of conversation without disruption (Tannock and Shachar, 1996; Zentall, 1988) and conveying the spoken message unambiguously (Purvis and Tannock, 1997). 

## Clinical Implications 

The study’s findings have the following clinical implications. First, they indicate that certain aspects of the communication deficits of ADHD children can be described by the clinically familiar term, thought disorder, using reliable and valid measures. Second, as demonstrated in adults (Marengo and Harrow, 1997), this study revealed that thought disorder is found in pediatric neurobehavioral disorders other than schizophrenia. 

Third, clinicians have difficulty following the conversation topic with both schizophrenic and ADHD patients. Our findings suggest that this involves loose associations (i.e., unpredictable change in the topic of conversation without use of pragmatic devices to prepare the listener for the topic change) only in schizophrenic children. Moreover, they imply that cognitive and attentional deficits tapped by the verbal (i.e., arithmetic) and performance subscales (i.e., digit span, coding, mazes) in 

the WISC-R distractibility factor score might underlie loose associations. 

In contrast to the “internal distraction” involved in loose associations, digression from the conversational text to referents in the immediate external surrounding (i.e., exophora) can contribute to the clinician’s difficulty with following the topic of conversation in both ADHD and schizophrenia. In addition, when lexical cohesion is used in excess, the connections between the speaker’s ideas could appear to be tenuous and idiosyncratic because their link is primarily semantic and not ideational. Increased use of lexical cohesion in ADHD children might, therefore, contribute to the clinician’s difficulty with following these children’s conversations. 

Fourth, the study’s findings suggest that ADHD and schizophrenia affect the normal development of children’s ability to use language to formulate their thoughts. They also highlight how a developmental approach enhances our clinical understanding of thought disorder in children. For example, the occurrence of loose associations in normal children under, but not over, age 7 (Caplan et al., 1994, 2000) implies that developmental processes set in by this age that enable children to monitor their thoughts and the listener’s needs so that they can prepare the listener for an upcoming topic change. Children’s reasoning skills also undergo important advances during middle childhood. Furthermore, normal children increase the use and repertoire of conjunctions and referential cohesion and decrease the use of exophoric references in middle childhood (Gopnik, 1986; Pratt and Mackenzie-Keating, 1985). They also make more connections across sentences using similar word roots and synonyms (i.e., lexical cohesion) (Greenfield and Savage-Rumbaugh, 1993) and use fewer unclear and ambiguous references (Gopnik, 1986; Pratt and Mackenzie-Keating, 1985). Increased illogical thinking, loose associations, exophora, lexical cohesion, and unclear/ambiguous reference together with reduced use of conjunctions and referential cohesion with age imply developmental impairment of these communication skills in ADHD and schizophrenia. 

From the theoretical perspective the association of thought disorder with IQ, Span of Apprehension accuracy, CPT signal/noise discrimination, and digit span scores in ADHD suggests that the communication disturbances of children with this disorder reflect their cognitive and/or attentional deficits. More specifically, their impaired reasoning and distraction from the conversational text taps skills involved in CPT signal/noise discrimination. Their

The distinct patterns of the manifestations of thought disorder and its cognitive correlates in ADHD and schizophrenia led us to hypothesize that thought disorder might be a final common pathway for disorders that differentially involve frontotemporal neural networks. In support of this hypothesis, frontotemporal networks mature during childhood and adolescence (Giedd et al., 1996; Reiss et al., 1996) and are involved in higher linguistic processing (St. George et al., 1999), childhood schizophrenia (Jacobsen et al., 1998; Rapoport et al., 1999; Sowell et al., 2000), thought disorder in adult schizophrenia (Holinger et al., 1999; McGuire et al., 1998; Vita et al., 1995), as well as the executive function, working memory, attention (see review in Barkley, 1997; Berger and Posner, 2000; Purvis and Tannock, 1997), and structural (Castellanos et al., 1996; Filipek et al., 1997) and functional imaging abnormalities (Rubia et al., 1999; Schweitzer et al., 2000) found in ADHD. Differential involvement of these neural circuits by ADHD and schizophrenia might, therefore, underlie the differences and similarities in thought disorder in these two disorders. 

## Limitations 

We were unable to ascertain the association of comorbidity and thought disorder in children with ADHD because most of these children had comorbid axis I diagnoses. In addition, use of _DSM-III-R_ diagnostic criteria precluded examination of how severity of thought disorder differs in ADHD with and without hyperactivity. 

From the methodological perspective, the study included a large number of dependent variables and statistical tests. The differences between diagnostic groups, as well as the age-related and cognitive associations, however, were in the direction of the study’s hypotheses. Finally, compared with transcriptions, videotapes provide potentially biasing verbal and nonverbal diagnostic cues to coders. The large magnitude of differences between the patient and normal groups for both the videotape-based formal thought disorder ( _p_ < .008) and the transcription- 

based cohesion ( _p_ < .002) scores suggest that the potential biasing effect was minimal.

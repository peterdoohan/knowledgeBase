## **Abstract** 

The involvement of medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) in spatial learning was examined in two memory tasks using spatial components, the Morris water maze and the three-panel runway. Using the Morris water maze task, with an invisible platform, the effects of NMDA mPFC lesions were assessed in a procedure reflecting spatial learning and memory, including a spatial reversal. In the three-panel runway, a delayed matching-to-position procedure was used in which rats were required to find food at the end of the runway after passing through one of three panel gates set into four barriers spaced equally apart along the maze. In addition, mPFC lesions were assessed behaviorally in two behavioral tests known to be sensitive to mPFC dysfunction: the food hoarding paradigm and spontaneous locomotion in the open field. Consistent with the documented effects of mPFC damage, NMDA mPFC lesions impaired food hoarding behavior and increased spontaneous exploratory locomotion. In the Morris water maze and the three-panel runway, mPFC-lesioned rats showed relatively few effects, supporting the conclusion that the damage inflicted to the mPFC had no consequence for the processing of spatial information. However, mPFC lesioned animals showed slower acquisition during both the training trial in the three-panel runway and the reversal training in the Morris water maze. These results suggest that spatial memory did not depend on mPFC integrity in the Morris water maze and the three-panel runway experiments, and address the issue of deficits induced by mPFC lesions in memory tasks dependent on non-mnemonic processes such as attentional processes and/or a reduced behavioral flexibility to environmental changes. © 2002 Elsevier Science B.V. ## **1. Introduction** 

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) of the mammalian brain is commonly defined as the cortex of the anterior pole, characterized by the reciprocal projection with the mediodorsal nucleus of the thalamus [38,57,58]. Although behavioral studies have shown that the PFC is involved in functions such as behavioral flexibility [10,29], response inhibition [52], and attentional processes [43], memory processes have also been suggested as a key function of the PFC [17,19]. PFC has been generally associated with memory deficits involving delay and spatial information [63], and in this respect it is mainly the dorsolateral area of the PFC in humans [41,59] and in nonhuman primates [19,60] that has been implicated. 

In rats, the medial part of the prefrontal cortex (mPFC) is considered to be the homologue of the primate dorsolateral PFC [57,58]. Supporting this view, the deficits observed in memory tasks following mPFC lesions in rats are similar to those described following lesions of the dorsolateral PFC in primates [29,30]. This view is supported in rats by findings that mPFC lesions produce deficiencies in various delayed-response tests [26,37,62], as well as by evidence of sustained firing of mPFC neurons in rats throughout the brief delay period of delayed response tasks [2]. While mPFC lesions affect working memory, no impairment of reference memory has been shown, suggesting that mPFC is not involved in the storage of information that remains constant over time [20,50]. The classic example of delay-type tasks used to show behavioral deficiencies in mPFC-lesioned rats is the spatial delayed alternation in a T- maze or in operant procedures [9,13]. Also, deficiencies have been observed following mPFC lesions in other learning tasks requiring spatially defined responses such as the Morris water maze and the eight-arm radial maze [32,48,55]. Thus, both spatial and delay components have been suggested to be determining factors for the behavioral deficits observed following mPFC damage in rats. 

However, recent data have shown that rats with mPFC lesions are able to use the spatial relationships of the environment to locate a goal [10,50], suggesting that mPFC is not involved in the processing of spatial information. Also, the involvement of mPFC in working memory processes has been questioned by findings of transient or no deficits following lesions of the mPFC in tasks generally considered as typical working memory tasks, such as the radial arm maze [24,31]. Thus, to the extent that mnemonic-like deficits were observed in rats, they might be due to the disruption of non-mnemonic processes, like attention and discriminative abilities, which also result in impaired performance in learning tasks [7]. These considerations raise the issue of the contribution of the mPFC in mnemonic/representational processes, the temporal ordering of behavior, attention and the ruling out of inappropriate strategies. 

Therefore, to further document the issue of the role of mPFC in spatial learning involving working and reference memory abilities, the present study examined the effect of excitotoxic lesions of the mPFC on two spatial memory tests: the Morris water maze and the three-panel runway. The experimental design allowed for characterization of mPFC involvement in spatial learning and long-term memory in the Morris water maze. The effect of mPFC lesions in a delayed matching-to-position task in the three-panel runway allowed assessment of both working and reference memory processes [16,22,45]. In addition to these two spatial memory tasks, mPFC lesions were assessed behaviorally in two tests known to be sensitive to mPFC dysfunction: the food hoarding paradigm [9,34,54,61] and spontaneous locomotion in the open field [4,23,34,35]. 

## **2. Materials and methods** 

## 2.1. _Subjects_ 

Twenty male Wistar rats [Zur:WIST(HanIbm), ETH Research Unit Schwerzenbach, Switzerland], approximately 3 months old at the start of the experiment, were used in this study. Rats were housed individually in Macrolon type III cages (48×27 ×20 cm) under reversed cycle lighting (lights on 19:00 h in the evening–07:00 h in the morning) in a temperature and humidity (21� 1 °C, 55 � 5%, respectively) controlled animal facility. Food (Nafag 9431, Eberle Nafag AG, Gossau, CH) and water were available ad lib in the home cages, except for experimental procedures that required food deprivation. All behavioral testing was carried out in the dark phase of the cycle. All procedures were carried out in accordance with Swiss regulations for animal experimentation. 

## 2.2. _Surgery_ 

The animals allocated to the mPFC group were anaesthetized with a cocktail containing fentanyl citrate (0.079 mg/ml), fluanisone (2.5 mg/ml), medetomidine (2.5 mg/ml) and atropine (0.05 mg/ml), given subcutaneously in a volume of 1 ml/kg. The animals were placed in a stereotaxic frame (Kopf Instruments, Tajunga, CA) and an incision was made into the scalp to expose the skull. The skull was adjusted such that the bregma and lambda were in the same horizontal plane. A small square of bone above the mPFC was removed. A 34-gauge needle fitted to a 5 �l Hamilton syringe was used to inject either a 10 mg/ml solution of _N_ -methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA; SIGMA, Switzerland; dissolved in 0.1M phosphatebuffered saline with a final pH 7.4) or vehicle (0.1M phosphate-buffered saline pH 7.4). At each coordinate 0.2 �l was injected over a 2 min period. After completion of each injection the needle remained in place for 1 min to allow diffusion of solution. Bilateral injections were made 0.7 mm lateral to the midline using the following coordinates, anterior from bregma (AP) and ventral from dura (DV): AP= 3.0 mm, DV = 3.0 and 4.0; AP= 4.0 mm, DV = 3.0 mm. Sham lesioned animals underwent the same procedure, but were injected with vehicle. Bone wax was used to cover the hole in the bone, and the scalp incisions were sutured. Unoperated rats were anaesthetized, but not operated (UNOP). After surgery rats were given a cocktail containing naloxone (0.1 mg/ml) and atipamezole (2 mg/ml) injected intraperitoneally in a volume of 1 ml/kg. Rats were allowed to recover for 2 weeks with free access to water and food.

## 2.3. _Apparatus_ 

## 2.3.1. _Food hoarding_ 

Simultaneously with food deprivation, rats’ home cages were changed to different Macrolon cages type III with drinking and feeding devices in the front. The feeding device was blocked by an opaque perspex door, and water and food were given through the lid. The same type of cage, but with no sawdust, was used as the hoarding cage. On the test day, the hoarding cage was connected to the home cage with the feeding devices such that there was an entrance to the hoarding cage. The test room was illuminated with red light throughout the experiment. 

## 2.3.2. _Three_ - _panel runway_ 

The three-panel runway apparatus (170×40 × 25 cm) made of dark gray plastic, was divided into a start box (20× 10 cm) and goal box (30×40 cm) separated by four consecutive compartments (30×40 cm) accessible at choice points. Each choice point consisted of a gate with three panels (9×13.5 cm). Rats were required to find food at the end of the runway by passing through one of the three panels at each choice point. The animals were prevented from passing through two of the three panels in the gate by front stoppers, and they were prevented from returning to the start box or to a previous choice by rear stoppers affixed to each of the panels in all of the gates. Food pellets were placed in a glass food cup (2 cm high, 4 cm in diameter) fixed at the end of the goal box. 

## 2.3.3. _Locomotor acti_ � _ity in an open field_ 

Locomotor activity was measured in four square open field arenas (76.5×76.5 ×49 cm), made of dark gray plastic. Behavior in the arenas was recorded by a video camera mounted on the ceiling and relayed to a monitor and a Video tracking, Motion Analysis and Behavior recognition system (EthoVision[®] , Noldus, Wageningen, The Netherlands). Each open field was divided into two areas, the complete arena and a 20× 20 square center. The test room was dimly illuminated (20 lx). 

## 2.3.4. _The Morris water maze_ 

The water maze was a circular tank 2 m in diameter, 0.6 m in height, made of fiberglass and painted black. It was positioned in the middle of a dimly lit (30 lx) testing room (3× 4.9 ×2.9 m) enriched with distal visual stimuli. The bottom of the maze was raised 0.45 m above the room floor. At the beginning of each day, the tank was filled with a mixture of cold and hot tap water to a depth of 0.3 m, and the water temperature was maintained at 22�2 °C. Four points, equally spaced along the circumference of the pool, were arbitrarily designated as: N, E, S, W. These points served as the 

starting positions at which the rat was lowered gently into the water, with its head facing the wall of the water maze. The area of the pool was also conceptually divided into four quadrants (NE, SE, SW and NW) of equal size by two imaginary diagonal lines running through the center of the pool. 

A stable circular platform, measuring 11 cm in diameter and painted black, was submerged 2 cm below the surface of the water and hidden from the rat’s view. It had a rough surface that allowed the rat to climb easily onto it once its presence was detected. A separate white circular disk, also measuring 11 cm in diameter, could be mounted on top of the hidden platform so that it was 10 cm above the water surface. When in place, the white disk served as a distinct visual cue for the location of the otherwise hidden platform. A video camera, connected to an image analysis system (HVS Image, England), which in turn was connected to a microcomputer running the HVS maze software, was mounted above the center of the water maze. The swim path of the animal was tracked, digitized and stored for subsequent behavioral analysis using the same software. Each trial was started and ended manually by the experimenter, who operated a remote switch connected to the microcomputer. 

## 2.4. _Beha_ � _ioral procedures_ 

## 2.4.1. _General procedure_ 

Prior to surgery, a total of 20 rats were placed on a 23 h food deprivation schedule for 1 week and then tested in the food-hoarding paradigm. According to their hoarding performance, rats were matched into three treatment groups: medial prefrontal cortex lesion (mPFC; _N_ = 10), sham lesion (SHAM; _N_ = 5), and unoperated controls (UNOP; _N_ = 5). Surgery was performed on the following 3 days. At the end of a 2 week recovery period, rats were returned to food deprivation, which continued for 1 week prior to testing in food hoarding and throughout the three-panel runway experiment. At the end of the testing in the three-panel runway, rats were returned to ad lib food for 1 week prior to being tested in the open field and the Morris water maze. A period of at least 1 week was allowed between each behavioral test. 

## 2.4.2. _Food hoarding_ 

Animals were tested in squads of 10. Fifty food pellets (2–3 g per each) were weighed before being distributed in the hoarding cage. After a 15 min period of habituation to the experimental room, the entrance to the hoarding cage was opened, allowing the animal access to the hoarding cage for 60 min. After the end of this 60 min period, the entrance to the hoarding cage was closed, and the pellets in the hoarding cage and in the home cage were counted and weighed. The hoard-

ing score was calculated as the total number of food pellets removed by the animal from the hoarding cage. 

## 2.4.3. _Three_ - _panel runway task_ 

2.4.3.1. _Habituation to the apparatus_ . During the first 2 days, animals were allowed to explore the runway for 10 min without any panel gates separating the different compartments. The following day, the panel gates were put back with all front stoppers removed so that animals could pass through any one of the three panel gates at each choice point. Thereafter, animals were run daily on six consecutive trials with 2 min inter-trial intervals until the time that elapsed from leaving the start box to reaching the goal box was consistently below 20 s. Once this criterion was reached, the delayed matching-to-position task started. For the inter-trial interval, animals were returned to their home cage and water was freely available. 

2.4.3.2. _Delayed matching_ - _to_ - _position task_ . During the delayed matching-to-position task rats were required to find food at the end of the runway after passing through one of three panel gates set into four barriers equally spaced along the maze. The front stopper of only one of the three panel gates (defined as correct) was removed at each choice point. Animals received daily six consecutive trials with 2 min inter-trial intervals consisting of one training trial (trial 1) followed by five test trials (trial 2–6). During the training trial the panels that opened at each choice point were cued white and the closed panels were black. During the test trials, all panels were black. The sequence of the correct panel gates was changed every day, but remained the same within a day. Between each trial, animals were returned to their home cage with water available ad lib. 

Errors were defined as attempts to enter closed gate panels by pushes or nose pokes. Repetitive attempts to enter the same incorrect gate panel were counted as only one error, so that a maximum of two errors was possible at each choice point and thus eight errors in total per trial. The learning criterion to end the experiment was less than ten errors summed over the five test trials. 

## 2.4.4. _Locomotor acti_ � _ity in an open field_ 

Animals were run in squads of four. Spontaneous locomotion was assessed on 2 consecutive days. On each day, each animal was placed in the center of one of the arenas and allowed to explore it for 30 min. After this period they were returned to their home cages. The computer software calculated, in 5 min bins, the distance the rat traveled while in the arena, the number of entries into the center of the arena, and the time spent by the animal in the center. 

## 2.4.5. _The Morris water maze_ 

The Morris water maze procedure consisted of five stages organized as follows: 6 days of spatial training, 1 day of spatial training probe test, 3 days of reversal training, 1 day of reversal training probe test, and 1 day of visual-cued test. 

2.4.5.1. _Spatial training_ . Each animal was placed in the pool and was required to locate the fixed position of a platform hidden beneath the surface of the water. Animals received four consecutive daily trials for 6 days. Each animal was released into the pool from one of the four starting points. The order of the starting positions was pseudo-randomized and varied every day. Trials ended either when the platform was located or when 90 s had elapsed. If the platform was not located within the maximum trial duration of 90 s, the animals were placed on the platform. In either case, the animals were left on the platform for 30 s. Between successive trials of one session the animal was put in a transparent plastic bucket for a 30 s inter-trial interval. During this time fecal boluses were removed from the pool. Following the last trial of a session the animal was dried with a cloth towel and returned to its home cage. 

2.4.5.2. _Spatial training probe test_ . The day following the spatial training, the platform was removed from the pool. All animals received a probe test during which they were released into the pool for a 60 s period from the quadrant opposite to the one where the platform had been during the preceding spatial learning. 

2.4.5.3. _Re_ � _ersal training_ . The day following the probe test, animals were trained for 3 days in a reversal task. The platform was placed in the quadrant opposite to the one used during spatial training, and the procedure remained the same as for the spatial training phase. 

2.4.5.4. _Re_ � _ersal training probe test_ . The day following the last reversal trial, the platform was removed from the pool. All animals received a probe test during which they were released into the pool for a 60 s period from the quadrant opposite to the one where the platform had been during the preceding reversal training. 

2.4.5.5. _Visual_ - _cued test_ . One day after the completion of the second probe test, each animal was tested in the visually-cued task. The platform was submerged 1.5 cm below the water level and was made ‘visible’ by mounting a visual cue on top of it. Animals were given four trials similar to those described above for the spatial training, but while the release site of the animal remained the same, the platform position was varied from trial to trial.

## 2.5. _Histology_ 

After completion of the behavioral testing, lesioned and sham operated rats were anaesthetized with an overdose of sodium pentobarbital and perused intracardially with 0.9% saline followed by 4% buffered formaline. Their brains were removed from the skull and post-fixed in 4% formaline for 1 h at 4 °C prior to being placed in 30% sucrose solution in 0.1 M phosphate-buffered saline (pH 7.4) for 24 h. The brains were sectioned in the coronal plane using a freezing microtome at 40 �m thickness and stained with Cresyl violet. The atlas of Paxinos and Watson [47] was used to verify the placement and the extent of the lesions. 

## 2.6. _Data analysis_ 

In all experiments no significant differences were revealed between the UNOP and SHAM groups, therefore, their data were collapsed to form a single control group (Ctrl). 

Since the data of the food hoarding experiment were not normally distributed, a nonparametric Mann–Whitney _U_ test analysis was conducted to assess pre- to postsurgery differences in hoarding performance for each lesion group. In all other experiments data were analyzed using analysis of variance (ANOVA). 

In the three-panel runway experiment, separate ANOVAs were used for measures of data from the training trial, which were considered to reflect reference memory performance, and for data from the test trials, which were considered to reflect working memory performance. Data from the training trials were pooled over eight blocks of 3 days and two-way ANOVAs were conducted on the number of errors to reach the goal, with lesion as a between-subjects factor (Ctrl, mPFC) and eight blocks of 3 days as a repeated measurement factor to assess reference memory performance. Data of the test trials (trials 2–6) were pooled over eight blocks of 3 days and four-way ANOVAs were conducted on the number of errors to reach the goal, with lesion as a between-subjects factor and the repeated measurements of blocks (8), days (3) and trials (5). 

In the open field experiment assessing locomotor activity, two-way ANOVAs were conducted over the 2 testing days on the distance traveled, the number of entries into the center, and the time spent in the center of the open field, with lesion as a between-subjects factor and 5 min bins as repeated measurement factor. 

In the Morris water maze experiment, two-way ANOVAs were conducted on the latency to find the platform for spatial and reversal training, with lesion as a between-subjects factor and days as a repeated measurement factor (spatial training: 6 days; reversal training: 3 days). Two-way ANOVAs were conducted on the percentage of time spent in the four quadrants of the pool during the probe tests, with lesion as a between-subjects factor and the four quadrants of the pool as repeated measurement factor. Since the fourth quadrant data point is never independent of the other three, the _P_ values were adjusted to reflect a reduction in degrees of freedom in the effect of quadrant and in the lesion x quadrant interaction. A two-way ANOVA was conducted on the visual-cued test, with lesion as a between-subjects factor and the four platform positions as a repeated measurement factor.

## **4. Discussion** 

NMDA mPFC lesions impaired food hoarding behavior and increased spontaneous exploratory locomotion. In the Morris water maze and the three-panel runway mPFC-lesioned rats showed relatively little effect. In the Morris water maze both control and mPFC lesioned animals learned to locate a hidden platform equally quickly, supporting the conclusion that the damage inflicted to the mPFC had no consequence whatsoever for the processing of spatial information. Similarly, in the three-panel runway, experimental and control animals showed identical learning performance in a delayed matching to position task during the trial tests. Interestingly, however, mPFC lesioned animals showed slower acquisition during both the training trial in the three-panel runway 

and the reversal training in the Morris water maze, suggesting that mPFC plays a role in behavioral flexibility. 

The markedly decreased food hoarding behavior following mPFC lesions, in line with previous reports [9,27,34,54,61], attests to the behavioral efficacy of the lesions. The mPFC lesions did not affect consumption (data not shown) indicating that the decrease in food hoarding was not influenced by actual consumption in this experiment. Similarly, the effects of mPFC lesions on spontaneous locomotion in the open field are in line with previous observations of increased locomotion in a novel environment [4,23,34,35]. In addition, the increased number of entries into the center and the increased time spent in the center of the open field also indicate an increased exploratory behavior, possibly related to a decreased innate fear of anything new, as reported in animal models of anxiety such as the elevated plus maze [35,39]. Results in both the food hoarding and the open field tests confirmed the potency of the lesions of the mPFC in this study.

In addition, when lesioned animals were given a postsurgery recovery period before testing, neither lesions of the mPFC nor mPFC DA depletion prevented the acquisition of the standard radial maze task [7,11]. Several lines of evidence indicate that the deficits observed after mPFC lesions may be independent of mnemonic deficits. For example, in an experiment conducted using an eightarm radial maze with different procedural demands, mPFC-lesioned animals showed a transient deficit in a standard eight-arm task, but were impaired in a delayed non-matching-to-sample task (DNMTS) in which the 

same two alternatives were repeated in every trial [48]. Therefore, in view of the intact working memory ability in the radial arm maze and the three-panel runway following mPFC lesions, it is questionable whether the impairments observed in two-choice tasks such as delayed alternation and delayed matching to sample are to be interpreted as mnemonic deficits. 

In view of the spared ability to acquire and memorize spatially defined information in both the three-panel runway and the Morris water maze, the difficulty observed in mPFC lesioned animals during the training trial in the three-panel runway and the reversal training in the Morris water maze cannot be interpreted as memory deficits. Thus, the deficits may reflect nonmnemonic processes that interfere with learning and depend on the integrity of the mPFC. Considering the results for the training trial in the three-panel runway, it could be argued that the slower acquisition resulted from deficits in the discrimination between white (open) and black (closed) panels. However, such an interpretation can be excluded in view of the good performance

of mPFC-lesioned animals by the end of the experiment and the lack of deficits in the visual training in the Morris water maze. Another possible explanation would be that mPFC lesions induced an inability in learning the tasks. But because the performance of mPFC-lesioned rats improved in the course of the experiment, this consideration is not tenable. However, in the light of results suggesting a crucial role of the rat PFC in tasks that require high attentional control [46], the retarded acquisition observed following mPFC lesions could be considered to result from interference in the process of learning the task, possibly due to attentional deficits. The mPFC has been implicated in sustained, divided and selective attention [44,46]. However, whereas mPFC lesions were shown to affect selective attention in a five-choice serial reaction time task [44], no effects of mPFC manipulations were observed on selective attention in latent inhibition paradigms [6,15,25,33,34,36]. Nevertheless, such attentional deficits may reflect an increased susceptibility or overreaction to irrelevant environmental stimuli. Regarding the slower acquisition during the reversal training in the water maze, another consideration should be taken into account. mPFC-lesioned animals did not have difficulty in the acquisition of the task but in the reversal training, reflecting a difficulty in shifting from one acquired response to another one. Such effects of impaired ability of the rat to change behavior when such changes are required by circumstances have often been observed and associated with reversal learning deficits following mPFC lesions [3,12,14,49,64]. 

In conclusion, despite the clear effect of NMDA mPFC lesions on food hoarding and motor activity, spatial memory per se did not seem to depend on mPFC integrity in the Morris water maze and the three-panel runway experiments. Furthermore, although it is generally accepted that mPFC is not involved in reference memory, mPFC has often been proposed to be a part of the neural circuit subserving working memory processes [5,20]. However, the results of the present study do not support such a consideration and suggest that neither working nor reference memory was affected following mPFC lesions. mPFC lesions induced slower learning in the three-panel runway and in the Morris water maze during the reversal training. This poor performance can be related to a deficit in attentional processes and/or a reduced behavioral flexibility following environmental changes. Thus, in agreement with our data, the reported deficit in delayed response tasks induced by mPFC lesions cannot be attributed to spatial or memory processing deficits. Instead, according to two related theories of frontal lobe function [18,19,42,53], this impairment could result from a general increase in sensitivity to interference effects associated with repeated presentation of the same two response alternatives [28] or to an 

impairment in the temporal organization of behavior. In any event, supplementary investigations remain necessary to clarify this issue and to determine the precise role of mPFC in learning and memory processes in the rat.

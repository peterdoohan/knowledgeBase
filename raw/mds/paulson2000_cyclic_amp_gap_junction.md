## **SUMMARY** 

**Given the rapid turnover of connexin proteins, gap junction (GJ) assembly represents an important means of regulating the extent of GJ communication between cells. This report describes an increase in the level of GJ assembly within one hour following treatment with cAMPelevating reagents or low density lipoprotein (LDL). Dye transfer methods and freeze-fracture with electron microscopy were used to assay junctional permeability and structure, respectively, subsequent to the dissociation, recovery and reaggregation of Novikoff hepatoma cells. Reaggregating cells in the presence of agents that increase cAMP levels (8-Br-cAMP, forskolin and IBMX) enhanced both dye transfer rates between cells and the extent of GJ formation 2- to 3-fold. These data and studies with the protein kinase A inhibitor, H-89, indicate that cAMP signaling plays a key role in enhanced assembly. The response to LDL parallels that to cAMP and relies on the activity of both adenylyl cyclase and protein kinase A. Immunoblot analysis revealed no change in the level of** 

**connexin43 (Cx43) or its phosphorylation states over a period of 2.5 hours. However, three agents (brefeldin A, monensin and nocodazole), that inhibit intracellular membrane trafficking by different mechanisms, all blocked the enhanced assembly of GJs when triggered by either elevated cAMP or exposure to LDL. Related studies, which employed trafficking inhibitors at different stages in GJ assembly, suggested that Cx43 trafficking during enhanced assembly is regulated, in part, by cell contact. Intracellular sources of Cx43 were characterized by colabeling for several markers of cytoplasmic membrane systems. We conclude that an increase in GJ assembly: (i) occurs rapidly in the presence of elevated cAMP or LDL, (ii) does not require an increase in Cx43 levels or major changes in Cx43 phosphorylation and (iii) is dependent upon the trafficking of Cx43 from intracellular storage sites.** 

Key words: Gap junction, Junction assembly, Trafficking, cAMP, Connexin43 

## **INTRODUCTION** 

A gap junction (GJ) is a collection of cell-to-cell channels that provides direct cytoplasmic connections between apposed cells. The establishment or modulation of GJ communication is required for different physiological processes, including the electrical coupling of cells, the control of cell growth and various aspects of development (Werner, 1998; White and Paul, 1999). The proteins of GJ channels in vertebrates, the ‘connexins’, form a family with conserved structure and function. In comparison to other transmembrane proteins (Earl et al., 1987; Hare and Taylor, 1991), connexins have relatively short half-lives, e.g. 2-3 hours for connexin 43 (Cx43) (Beardslee et al., 1998; Crow et al., 1990; Laird et al., 1991; Lampe, 1994; Musil et al., 1990a). Therefore, connexins must be continually synthesized, transported to the cell surface and assembled into functional plaques. As a result, the trafficking 

and assembly of connexins are likely to be important control points in the regulation of GJ communication. This enables tissues to modulate GJ communication rapidly, both positively and negatively, in response to changes in the environment and to programmed events within cells and tissues. 

In terms of the negative regulation of GJ assembly, protein kinase C (PKC) appears to play an important role with Cx43. Activation of PKC with TPA leads to a dramatic inhibition of GJ assembly (Lampe, 1994). These studies were carried out on Novikoff hepatoma cells, which were dissociated without the use of proteases, allowed to recover and then reaggregated to examine the de novo assembly of GJs. Within minutes following exposure to TPA, changes were detected in the phosphorylation of Cx43 and the levels of GJ assembly dropped to less than 1% of the control values, as measured by both intercellular dye transfer and freeze-fracture analysis at the electron microscope level. Thus, the phosphorylation of

Cx43 by PKC or a process(es) activated by PKC is thought to inhibit the GJ assembly process, including an effect at the level of the plasma membrane. 

A number of reports have indicated that increases in GJ communication can also be triggered by different agents. For example, increases in cAMP can upregulate GJ communication in a variety of cell populations expressing different connexins (Atkinson et al., 1995; Azarnia et al., 1981; Burghardt et al., 1995; Burt and Spray, 1988; Chanson et al., 1996; Garfield et al., 1980; Mehta et al., 1992, 1996; Saez et al., 1986; Schiller et al., 1992; Traub et al., 1987). Reports have noted effects on connexin transcription rates, levels of connexin RNA or protein (Darrow et al., 1996; Schiller et al., 1992; Traub et al., 1987), connexin phosphorylation (Saez et al., 1986; Traub et al., 1987), and GJ conductance (Burt and Spray, 1988; Saez et al., 1986; Spray et al., 1991). Although many increases in communication were reported, in some cases, elevations of cAMP had an inhibitory effect (Cole and Garfield, 1986; Granot and Dekel, 1994; Hampson et al., 1992; Sakai et al., 1992) or no effect (Perez-Armendariz et al., 1991) on intercellular communication. Thus, pleiotropic effects on GJ communication, which may depend on cell type and the connexin(s) expressed, are associated with elevated levels of cAMP. In some reports, there were increases in GJ communication without obvious increases in connexin protein levels (Matesic et al., 1996; Mehta et al., 1996). There were also indications of altered Cx43 trafficking, with possible implications for GJ assembly (Atkinson et al., 1995; Burghardt et al., 1995; Holm et al., 1999). However, it was not clear that GJ assembly per se was immediately altered by cAMP treatment. Therefore, we carried out a specific analysis of GJ assembly in the Novikoff system, which was described above, in order to determine whether cAMP acts to regulate the trafficking of Cx43 from cytoplasmic stores and/or the subsequent assembly of connexins into GJs. 

Treatments with LDL were also investigated in the present study and compared with those affecting cAMP levels, since we had earlier demonstrated short-term effects on GJ assembly with LDL (Meyer et al., 1992), apparently without a change in cellular levels of Cx43. In the previous analysis, the apolipoprotein B component of LDL was found to be sufficient for enhancing GJ assembly, implicating receptor-ligand interactions and subsequent cell signaling events in the stimulation of the assembly process. Thus, we reasoned that the effects of LDL might parallel those with cAMP and that an integrated analysis might benefit our overall understanding of connexin trafficking and GJ assembly. 

A quantitative analysis of GJ assembly between cells reaggregated for 60 minutes revealed a 2- to 3-fold increase in assembly, in the absence of an increase in the cellular levels of Cx43, when cAMP levels were elevated via three different mechanisms. Results following the application of several agents that inhibit intracellular trafficking, combined with biochemical measurements of Cx43 levels in non-junctional membranes, further indicated that intracellular stores of Cx43 provide for the short-term increase in GJ assembly. Doublelabel immunofluorescence for both Cx43 and several membrane markers was then used to characterize intracellular Cx43. Thus, this study demonstrates that treatments with cAMP-elevating conditions and with LDL, which appear to act by identical or closely related mechanisms, serve to stimulate 

both connexin trafficking within the cell and the process of GJ assembly at the cell surface.

## **Reagents** 

LDL, 8-Br-cAMP, brefeldin A and nocodazole were purchased from Sigma (St Louis, MO). Forskolin and monensin were purchased from Calbiochem (San Diego, CA). H-89 (N-[2-( _p_ - bromocinnamylamino)ethyl]-5-isoquinolinesulfonamide), SQ 22536 [9-(tetrahydro-2-furyl)adenine or THFA], and 3-isobutyl-1methylxanthine (IBMX) were purchased from LC Laboratories (Woburn, MA). LDL was used at a concentration of 2.5 µ g protein per ml. Forskolin was prepared as a 1000 × stock in absolute ethanol and used at a concentration of 10 µ M. 8-Br-cAMP was prepared in PBS at 30 mM or 500 mM and used at 1 mM. Monensin was prepared as a 1000 × stock in absolute ethanol and used at 5 µ M. Brefeldin A was prepared in a 1000 × stock in absolute ethanol and used at 36 µ M. H-89 was prepared as a 1000 × stock in DMSO and used at 30 µ M. IBMX was prepared as a 100 × stock in deionized distilled water and used at 100 µ M. THFA was prepared as a 100 × stock in DMSO and used at 1 mM. Nocodazole was prepared as a 1000 × stock in DMSO and used at 5-60 µ M. The appropriate solvent controls were used with each reagent. 

## **Cell culture, dissociation, reaggregation** 

The Novikoff hepatoma cell line (N1S1-67) was maintained in suspension culture as previously reported (Meyer et al., 1992) with Swim’s S-77 Medium (Sigma, St Louis, MO) supplemented with 10 mM L-glutamine (Sigma, St Louis, MO), 10% newborn calf serum (Gibco-BRL, Gaithersburg, MD) and penicillin/streptomyocin (Gibco-BRL, Gaithersburg, MD). The growth medium was further supplemented with the following: 1.8 mM calcium chloride, 60 µ M L-cystine, 9.4 mM D-glucose, 620 µ M sodium succinate, 26 mM sodium bicarbonate, 9.2 mM HEPES, 635 µ M succinic acid, 0.01 g/l phenol red, and 2.12 g/l Pluronic F68 flakes (Poloxamer 188 NF surfactant; Specialty Products, BASF Corp., Parsippany, NJ). The cells were subcultured daily from log-phase cultures growing at 37°C in a gyratory shaker at 200 RPM. The transfection of Novikoff cells with a Cx43 antisense construct and the culture of these cells were described previously (Li et al., 1996). 

Assembly of nascent GJs was assayed by the dissociation of Novikoff cells without proteases, followed by reaggreagation, as previously described (Johnson et al., 1974; Meyer et al., 1991). Briefly, Novikoff cells in logarithmic growth were centrifuged and resuspended in Swim’s S-77 medium with 10 mM EDTA at 6 × 10[5] cells/ml. The cells were placed on a gyratory shaker (200 rpm) at 37°C for 15 minutes. The EDTA treatment was repeated once, resulting in greater than 92% single cells. The cells were allowed to ‘recover’ by resuspension in Swim’s S-77 medium with 5% fatty acid-free bovine serum albumin (BSA) (Sigma, St Louis, MO) at 8 × 10[5] cells/ml and placement on the gyratory shaker (200 rpm) for 90 minutes. During the recovery period remnants of previously existing junctions are eliminated (Preus et al., 1981a). The cells were centrifuged at 30 _**g**_ onto Cell-Tak (Collaborative Biomedical Products, Bedford, MA) coated 35 mm dishes or glass coverslips for dye injections or in 50 ml round-bottomed tubes for electron microscopy and then allowed to ‘reaggregate’ for 60 minutes at 37°C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere to form new, functional GJ plaques. Reagents were added to the cells at the beginning of the recovery period or at the beginning of reaggregation, as indicated in the text.

The possible involvement of cAMP-dependent mechanisms in the LDL-mediated enhancement of GJ assembly was examined by using inhibitors of adenylyl cyclase and protein kinase A. Inhibition of adenylyl cyclase activity by 9- (tetrahydro-2-furyl)adenine (THFA) blocked the LDL effect, as measured by dye transfer (Fig. 2A). Although the dye transfer times for cells treated only with LDL were significantly shorter than control values ( _P_ <0.001), the dye transfer times for cells treated with THFA plus LDL ( _P_ >0.3) or with THFA alone ( _P_ >0.7) were not significantly different from untreated controls. The inhibition of enhanced assembly by THFA suggests that the activation of adenylyl cyclase is required for the LDL effect on junctional communication. 

Protein kinase A (PKA) would also be required to mediate an LDL-induced increase in GJ assembly, if the effect of LDL involved a cAMP mechanism. An inhibitor of PKA, the isoquinolinesulfonamide drug, H-89, was utilized to inhibit PKA in the presence of either 8-Br-cAMP or LDL. H-89 was first found to be effective at blocking a cAMP-induced enhancement of GJ assembly (Fig. 2B; with the difference being significant at the level of _P_ <0.005). H-89 was also found to block thoroughly LDL-stimulated assembly, increasing dye transfer times significantly over those obtained in the presence of LDL alone (Fig. 2B; _P_ <0.001). It should be emphasized that H-89 only affected the enhanced level of assembly. There was not a significant reduction in control levels (i.e. basal assembly) by treatment with the drug alone; dye transfer times for cells treated with LDL plus H-89 were not significantly different from non-treated controls or cells treated with H-89 alone (Fig. 2B; _P_ >0.9). Thus, protein kinase A activity appears to be required for the enhanced assembly generated by LDL. 

## **Immunoblot analysis of Cx43 protein levels and phosphorylation states** 

Cx43 is the only connexin that has been detected in Novikoff cells (Johnson and Meyer, 1993; Lampe, 1994) and was, therefore, thought to be the connexin contributing to increased assembly with LDL. This was tested by evaluating the effect of LDL treatment on a line of Novikoff cells stably transfected with a plasmid containing a Cx43 antisense sequence (Li et al., 1996). Following 60 minutes of reaggregation, only limited dye transfer was detected between these cells, which express about one-third the control levels of Cx43. Treatment of these same cells with LDL did not induce an increase in dye transfer as seen routinely with control cells (data not shown). This supports the interpretation that Cx43, not another connexin, is responsible for the increased assembly induced by LDL. 

Western blot analysis of several experiments was performed to determine whether increased levels of Cx43 protein or alterations in Cx43 phosphorylation states were associated with the increased GJ permeability and increased GJ channel number seen with cAMP-elevating conditions and LDL treatment. Immunoblot analysis of total cellular Cx43, using a rabbit polyclonal antibody directed against the amino terminus of Cx43, demonstrated that there was no discernable increase in the level of Cx43 protein following exposure to cAMP, forskolin or LDL, for the 2.5 hours of recovery and 

reaggregation (Fig. 3). The absence of a change in Cx43 levels indicates that neither increased synthesis nor decreased turnover of Cx43 plays a major role under these conditions. 

An alteration in pre-existing Cx43 could be required for the enhanced GJ assembly, which is observed following the 2.5 hour treatment. This might include a chemical modification, e.g. phosphorylation, or a redistribution of connexin within the cell. Since protein kinase A inhibitor, H89, was able to block enhanced assembly, it was reasonable to ask whether or not Cx43 phosphorylation was increased during enhanced assembly. 

It has been well documented that Cx43 appears as multiple bands on western blots as a result of different degrees of protein phosphorylation (Crow et al., 1990; Laird et al., 1991; Lampe, 1994; Musil et al., 1990a). The upper bands can be converted to the fastest migrating band with alkaline phosphatase treatment (Crow et al., 1990; Meyer et al., 1992; Musil et al., 1990a). In Novikoff hepatoma cells, Cx43 is typically seen as three bands when whole cell lysates are resolved by SDS-PAGE (Fig. 3). A correlation between posttranslational phosphorylation and assembly of connexins into functional plaques has been reported for S180 cells transfected with E-cadherin (Musil et al., 1990b). However, there was no change in the ratio of the intensities of the phosphorylated forms of Cx43 with cAMP, forskolin or LDL treatment in the present study (Fig. 3). This confirms and extends the previous report on LDL (Meyer et al., 1991). Thus, no major change in the phosphorylation levels of Cx43 within these hepatoma cells was observed following a 2.5 hour treatment that led to enhanced GJ assembly.

## **DISCUSSION** 

The elevation of intracellular cAMP has been shown to significantly upregulate Cx43-based GJ communication in a number of different systems, typically with a concurrent increase in Cx43 protein (Atkinson et al., 1995; Burghardt et al., 1995; Darrow et al., 1996; Mehta et al., 1992, 1996; Schiller et al., 1992). In the present study, with an interest in the mechanisms of cAMP-dependent effects on communication, we specifically asked whether cAMP is able to enhance the process of GJ assembly in a rapid manner that is not dependent on the synthesis of new connexin protein. These experiments revealed the following: (a) increased concentrations of cAMP can trigger elevated levels of GJ assembly in a period of 60 minutes, (b) this enhanced assembly does not require an increase in Cx43 protein nor does it result in a change in the phosphorylation pattern of Cx43 within Novikoff cells, (c) the response to LDL parallels the response to elevated cAMP, (d) elevated cAMP does not alter the percentage of cells engaged in GJ assembly and relies on the activity of both adenylyl cyclase and protein kinase A, (e) three different agents known to inhibit membrane trafficking are able to block enhanced GJ assembly that is triggered by cAMP or LDL, (f) cell surface biotinylation studies do not detect significant changes in non-junctional Cx43 in association with enhanced assembly and (g) a cell contact-dependent event upstream of the site of monensin inhibition regulates the trafficking of Cx43 that is triggered by cAMP with enhanced assembly.

## **LDL requires cAMP signaling for enhancement of gap junction assembly** 

Since treatments with LDL or with liposomes containing the LDL protein, apolipoprotein B, were earlier reported to 

enhance GJ assembly (Meyer et al., 1991), experiments were also carried out to determine how closely the responses to LDL were related to those with cAMP. The results were parallel in numerous respects, involving the time course, the effect on GJ growth, but not the initiation of assembly, and the lack of an effect on either Cx43 levels or phosphorylation states within Novikoff cells. In addition, the role of cAMP in LDL-enhanced assembly was supported by the ability of inhibitors of adenylyl cyclase (THFA) and protein kinase A (H-89) to block the LDLmediated increase in assembly. H-89 blocks this stimulated assembly to the same extent that it blocks cAMP-stimulated assembly, indicating that LDL does not act through additional, parallel pathways to stimulate GJ assembly. Thus, it appears that cAMP is sufficient to positively regulate assembly and that LDL exerts its effect through a protein kinase A-dependent pathway. Consistent with this, preliminary measurements of cAMP levels in Novikoff cells illustrate that LDL can induce a transient increase in cAMP that peaks within several minutes (data not shown). This transient increase in cAMP may be sufficient to stimulate de novo GJ assembly seen in the first 60 minutes of reaggregation with Novikoff cells. These results with LDL underscore both the importance of enhanced GJ assembly and the need for a thorough analysis of the cAMP

## **cAMP stimulates connexin trafficking with enhanced gap junction assembly** 

Several different mechanisms appear to be responsible for the upregulation of GJ communication as a result of cAMP signaling in various cell systems. For example, a cAMP response element has been identified in the promoter for Cx43 (van der Heyden et al., 1998), which is relevant to a number of studies that report increases in Cx43 expression in association with increased junctional communication (Darrow et al., 1996; Mehta et al., 1992; Schiller et al., 1992). However, increased expression of Cx43 does not appear to be the mechanism for the early course of cAMP-enhanced assembly in Novikoff cells. In our studies, there was no apparent increase in total Cx43 measured by immunoblots of cells exposed for 2.5 hours to three different cAMP-elevating reagents, even though a 2-3 fold increase in GJ assembly was detected with freeze-fracture methods. The lack of an apparent increase in protein detected by immunoblotting is consistent with two recent reports that describe cAMP-elevated junctional permeability without increases in Cx43 mRNA or Cx43 protein in mouse mammary tumor cells within 30 minutes of exposure (Atkinson et al., 1995) and in prostate epithelial cells over a 12-16 hour time period (Mehta et al., 1996). Our finding of increased immunolabeling with enhanced assembly using certain antibodies could be explained by a dispersion of more tightly localized stores or by a difference in antibody affinity and/or accessibility. Indeed, dispersion of the TGN using nocodazole results in what looks like an increase in TGN immunoreactivity. 

With enhanced assembly in Novikoff cells, there was no detectable change in the phosphorylation status of Cx43, as would be indicated by a mobility shift. In support of this observation, there is no clear evidence for the direct phosphorylation of Cx43 by protein kinase A, e.g. from experiments with[32] P-labeling and Cx43 immunoprecipitation (TenBroek et al., 1998), despite efforts in several different laboratories. This includes a study that failed to detect significant phosphorylation of Cx43 with elevated PKA activity in MDCK cells (Berthoud et al., 1992). Furthermore, in terms of physiological parameters, agonists of PKA were shown not to affect the conductance of single GJ channels in SKHep1 cells transfected with Cx43 (Kwak et al., 1995). Thus, the most straightforward interpretation of all these data, including those in the present report, is that short-term increases in cAMP enhance the recruitment of Cx43 hemichannels to developing GJs, without significant effects on single channel properties. In support of this interpretation, it is noted that in Novikoff cells cAMP triggers an increase in GJ permeability that matches rather closely the increase in GJ assembly observed with EM methods. Clearly, long-term elevations of cAMP may elicit other effects on GJ assembly, which could include increased phosphorylation of Cx43 (indirect?) and increased protein expression, as seen with other studies. 

The data presented here point to a cellular redistribution of Cx43 triggered by cAMP or LDL that is required for the enhanced assembly of GJs, which is supported by recent observations on cells expressing a Cx43-GFP chimera (Holm et al., 1999). The application of trafficking inhibitors, BFA or monensin, was found in the present study to block enhanced assembly thoroughly. This suggests that the source of Cx43 for enhanced assembly is intracellular, requiring transport through the biosynthetic pathway (ER to Golgi, TGN and plasma membrane) in order to support assembly. However, the immediate source(s) of Cx43 within the cell upon cAMP triggering is not known. Other reports have also demonstrated that the application of trafficking inhibitors is effective in blocking the transport of Cx43 to the plasma membrane in support of basal assembly and/or GJ communication (Burghardt et al., 1995; De Sousa et al., 1993; George et al., 1999; Laird et al., 1995; Lampe, 1994; Musil and Goodenough, 1993; Puranam et al., 1993). This likely includes, but is not restricted to, Cx43 hemichannels distributed within the non-junctional plasma membrane. ## **Control points in gap junction assembly** 

The studies with trafficking inhibitors provide an opportunity to explore different aspects of the regulation of Cx43 trafficking during enhanced assembly. The role that cell-to-cell contact may play in Cx43 trafficking is one such aspect. It was earlier demonstrated that GJ assembly requires cell contact (Preus et al., 1981a,b), since single cells maintained in suspension for 90 minutes fail to form GJ plaques. However, assembly begins immediately following reaggregation for periods as short as 5-15 minutes (Johnson et al., 1974). If cells are pre-treated as single cells with enhancing agents (during recovery), such as elevators of cAMP, and then reaggregated in the absence of the enhancer, preliminary experiments reveal 

increased levels of GJ assembly (data not shown). As a result, we suggest that enhanced assembly is ‘triggered’ in the absence of cell contact. This triggering appears to involve at least a limited redistribution of Cx43, since enhanced assembly is blocked by BFA, but only if cells are pre-treated with it before reaggregation. BFA is not effective if simply added at the beginning of reaggregation. That is, the mobilization of Cx43 for enhanced assembly appears to be set in motion with cAMP signaling events prior to cell-cell contact. However, if cells are pre-treated to elevate cAMP and then monensin is added at the beginning of reaggregation, enhanced assembly is not observed. One interpretation of these results is that cell contact regulates cAMP-induced Cx43 trafficking at a step that is downstream from the site of BFA action, but upstream of the monensin site. Alternatively, BFA may penetrate cells more slowly and its effect on function may thus be delayed. However, this is not likely given reports in the literature (Chardin and McCormick, 1999; Klausner et al., 1992) and our observation that BFA rapidly affects the level of plasma membrane hemichannels, with changes being detected within 15 minutes (H. Li and R. G. Johnson, unpublished data). The key point in this analysis is that a cell contact-dependent mechanism exerts an influence over the trafficking of Cx43 and, subsequently, the process of enhanced GJ assembly. 

Another insight into the regulation of gap junction assembly is in part provided by the studies with nocodozole. Both in this study using Novikoff cells, and also in our previous studies using Cx43-transfected knockout fibroblasts (TenBroek et al., 1998), nocodazole has been found to block enhanced but not basal levels of assembly regardless of whether the inhibitor is added immediately prior to gap junction assembly or prior to recovery. These studies are in agreement with earlier studies in which control levels of assembly were not disrupted by microtubule disrupting reagents, and they indicate that a second, microtubule-dependent, trafficking pathway may be utilized during enhanced assembly. 

trafficking of connexins from intracellular pools to the plasma membrane for assembly into GJ plaques is involved in a variety of physiological events in other systems, where the relative numbers of GJ channels are thought to be controlled posttranslationally. For example, the appearance and function of Cx43 in rat uterine myometrium prior to labor is a complex interaction involving the regulation of expression and protein transport (Hendrix et al., 1992). While Cx43 is synthesized several days prior to labor, it is only a few hours before labor when Cx43 appears to be rapidly transported to the plasma membrane and incorporated into junctional plaques. In a similar manner, Cx43 protein appears in the 4-cell stage of the mouse embryo, but is not transported to the cell surface and incorporated into plaques until compaction at the 8-cell stage (De Sousa et al., 1993). The treatment of pre-compaction 8- cell embryos with trafficking inhibitors, brefeldin A or monensin, for 4 hours blocks the assembly of GJs at compaction. Both of these examples involve the maintenance of previously synthesized Cx43 in cytoplasmic stores, with subsequent GJ assembly being triggered at the appropriate time. As we have noted in our studies, the recruitment of Cx43 from the cytoplasmic pools would then depend on intracellular trafficking steps and result in an elevation of GJ assembly within a short period of time, even a matter of minutes.

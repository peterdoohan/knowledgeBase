## SUMMARY 

Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples (SPW-Rs) are critical for memory consolidation and retrieval. The neuronal content of spiking during SPW-Rs is believed to be under the influence of neocortical inputs via the entorhinal cortex (EC). Optogenetic silencing of the medial EC (mEC) reduced the incidence of SPW-Rs with minor impacts on their magnitude or duration, similar to local CA1 silencing. The effect of mEC silencing on CA1 firing and field potentials was comparable to the effect of transient cortex-wide DOWN states of non-REM (NREM) sleep, implying that decreased SPW-R incidence in both cases is due to tonic disfacilitation of hippocampal circuits. The neuronal composition of CA1 pyramidal neurons during SPW-Rs was altered by mEC silencing but was restored immediately after silencing. We suggest that the mEC provides both tonic and transient influences on hippocampal network states by timing the occurrence of SPW-Rs and altering their neuronal content. 

## INTRODUCTION 

The hippocampus is characterized by irregularly occurring sharp-wave ripples (SPW-Rs), most prevalent during non-REM (NREM) sleep.[1] Hippocampal neuronal firing during SPW-Rs, particularly in the CA1 region of the hippocampus, is temporally organized, reflecting sequences that were expressed during past waking experiences.[2–5] Owing to this exquisite feature of reactivating past experiences, SPW-Rs have been implicated with the consolidation of memory, and the transfer of information between the cortex and hippocampus.[2][,][6] In support of this hypothesis, artificially truncating SPW-Rs leads to memory impairments,[7–10] whereas the elongation of SPW-Rs improves memory.[11] Therefore, the study of SPW-Rs and the neural mechanisms that generate such activity is highly relevant for behavior. 

Generation of SPW-Rs is generally considered to be intrinsic to the hippocampus. SPW-R complexes arise from the synchronous discharges of CA2-3 pyramidal cells via their spatially extensive excitation.[12] Spontaneous CA3 population discharge induces a current sink in the apical dendrites of CA1 pyramidal cells, detected in the stratum (str.) radiatum as a local field potential (LFP) SPW.[13–15] In turn, the strong excitation of CA1 pyramidal cells and interneurons induces a short-lived and fast (40–150 ms; 100–150 Hz) oscillation, known as the ripple.[16][,][17] In support of this model, optogenetic silencing of CA3 decreased SPW-R occurrence.[18][,][19] Further, SPW-Rs persist despite deneocortication, entorhinal cortex (EC) lesions, and septal and fimbria-fornix lesions.[13][,][14][,][20–23] The independence of SPW and ripple is supported by optogenetic induction of fast oscillations 

in the CA1 region.[17][,][24] Furthermore, ripples can be induced by either CA3 or CA2 inputs, with different SPW profiles.[25] In addition, when the excitatory CA3 inputs to CA1 are suppressed genetically, the CA1 circuit continues to generate ripples, presumably induced by the altered entorhinal input.[26] 

Inisolated invitro hippocampalpreparations,SPW-Rsoccurina rhythmic fashion at 200 ns to s intervals,[27–29] in contrast to irregularly occurring SPW-Rs with skewed distributions in the intact brain.[13][,][30] In vivo, hippocampal SPW-Rs are coordinated with global brain states and especially with neocortical UP and DOWN states of slow oscillations during NREM sleep.[31–35] SPWRs are more tightly correlated with entorhinal UP states than those observed at other regions of the neocortex.[15][,][32][,][33][,][36][,][37] Furthermore, entorhinal inputs differentially modulate the membrane potential of CA1 and CA3 pyramidal cells.[38] Therefore, a working model for the mechanism of SPW-R generation is that neocortical-entorhinal inputs can affect both the timing and spike content of SPW-Rs. This model is in line with ideas about memory consolidation, recall, planning, prediction, and action selection functions of SPW-Rs.[1][,][39][,][40] An important prediction of this model is that the neocortical inputs should be able to modify the neuronal assembly contentofSPW-Rs,and inreturn,SPW-Rsshouldbeabletoselect the spike content of neocortical events. 

Given the assumed pivotal role of the medial EC (mEC) in controlling hippocampal activity,[33][,][41][,][42] we examined whether and how optogenetic silencing of the mEC affects hippocampal SPW-Rs. We found that mEC silencing reduced SPW-R occurrences, altered their spike content, and suppressed their relationship with neocortical UP-DOWN states. ## RESULTS 

## Transient silencing of mEC afferents or local CA1 pyramidal cells suppress CA1 SPW-Rs 

We examined whether cortical inputs mediated by the mEC can affect the probability of occurrence of hippocampal SPW-Rs. We employed an optogenetic approach to silence cells across all layers of the mEC, and for comparison, we also silenced local CA1 pyramidal cells (using the dataset of Zutshi et al.[43] ). The mEC was injected with AAV5-mDlx-ChR2-mCherry (adenoassociated virus) in one or both hemispheres to express ChR2 in GABAergic interneurons across all layers (Figures 1A and 1B). An optic fiber was implanted over the injection sites for transient silencing. In a subset of mice, the right CA1 was also injected with AAV1-CamKII-stGtACR2 virus to express stGtACR2 in excitatory pyramidal cells, with an optic fiber implanted over CA1. In all mice, a single-shank 64-site probe or two-shank 128-site silicon probes were implanted in the right hippocampus to record single units and LFP. One cohort of mice included ipsilateral (to the recording site), contralateral (to the recording site), and bilateral mEC silencing, while the other included ipsilateral mEC, ipsilateral CA1, and their combined silencing (Figures 1A and 1B). Recordings were performed as mice were in their home cage or performed a memory task. For optogenetic experiments in the home cage, 5-s-long blue light square pulses were delivered for each stimulation condition every 30 s for 15 min (Figure 1C). 

Depth profile recordings across layers of the hippocampus revealed a striking and immediate decrease in LFP power during ipsilateral mEC (Figure 1D), but not contralateral mEC or CA1 silencing (Figures S1A and S1B), consistent with our previous study on theta/gamma oscillations during running.[43][,][44] While the LFP power was altered significantly only by ipsilateral mEC silencing, there was a marked reduction in the incidence of SPW-Rs across all mEC and CA1 silencing configurations (Figures 1E and 1F), suggesting that afferent excitation by mEC facilitates the occurrence of SPW-Rs under physiological conditions. The decrease in SPW-R incidence was largest in the first second after mEC stimulation (Figures S1C–S1F), consistent with mEC silencing exerting a transient effect on the firing rate of CA1 pyramidal cells (<500 ms; Figures S1C–S1F). However, the effect on SPW-Rs cannot be explained by firing rate alone, because SPW-Rs remained suppressed long after CA1 firing rates returned to baseline. Moreover, silencing of the contralateral mEC led to a similar decrease in SPW-R incidence, with no observable effects on CA1 firing rates (Figure S1E). Termination of mEC silencing led to an increase in SPW-Rs within the first second after stimulation ended, potentially due to a rebound excitability in the mEC (Figures 1F and S1I). In 

contrast, CA1 silencing led to a continued decrease in SPW-Rs for the first few seconds after stimulation ended, suggesting a prolonged recovery period from local silencing (Figures 1F and S1J). Together, these results indicate that afferent and local suppression of activity affects SPW-Rs through different circuit mechanisms. Each manipulation also had effects on the amplitude, duration, and frequency of the residual SPW-Rs, with the most pronounced effect being a decrease in SPW-R duration and its amplitude during bilateral mEC or local CA1 silencing (Figures 1G, 1H, and S1K). The proportion of ripples associated with a detectable SPW decreased with ipsilateral mEC silencing (Figures S2J and S2K), supporting that SPW and ripple-generating mechanisms are distinct.[1] Similar effects on SPW-Rs were observed even after restricting the analysis to periods of NREM sleep (Figure S1L). This decrease in SPW-Rs was not observed by direct activation of mEC interneuron axon terminals in CA1 (Figure S1M), suggesting the effect was not mediated by direct inhibition. Furthermore, the decrease in SPW-R incidence was also not observed in control mice implanted with optic fibers but lacking the opsin (Figure S1N), confirming that these were not off-target light effects. While these mice were not injected with a control virus, we expect that the within-animal nature of these experiments would control for long-term virus toxicity concerns.[45] We also confirmed that the observed decrease in SPW-Rs did not emerge from arbitrary detection thresholds by applying a range of thresholds and windows to calculate the standard deviation of the signal. The effect was robust across all detection conditions (Figures S2A and S2B). 

We next asked whether the observed changes in SPW-Rs were also present in the awake animal during consummatory states.[46] Mice were trained to run on a delayed spatial alternation task (10-s delay), and either ipsilateral mEC or CA1 silencing was performed on the side arms (where the reward was provided) in alternating blocks of 10 no-stimulation and stimulation trials (Figure S2C). Stimulation was triggered by the mouse crossing the photobeam in front of the water port and terminated by crossing the photobeam to enter the delay zone. While ipsilateral mEC silencing on the side arms decreased the incidence of SPW-Rs (Figures S2D and S2E), this effect was not consistent across sessions and did not reach statistical significance. Yet, mEC silencing was clearly effective as demonstrated by a rebound increase of SPW-R incidence in the delay zone, where no silencing occurred (Figure S2E). In contrast, CA1 silencing drastically reduced the incidence of SPW-Rs on the side arms (Figures S2D and S2F). The residual SPW-Rs during CA1 silencing were too few to perform further analysis, but ipsilateral mEC silencing led to an unexpected increase in SPW-R amplitude (Figures S2G–S2I). Together, these results suggest that hippocampal excitability is affected by mEC inputs somewhat differently during sleep and waking behaviors.

## mEC-driven currents across hippocampal layers during SPW-Rs 

To examine the target effects of mEC perturbation, we calculated the peri-ripple current source density (CSD) maps for SPW-Rs occurring during the 5 s preceding stimulation, and during ipsilateral mEC silencing, contralateral mEC silencing, and bilateral mEC silencing. CSD magnitude decreased in the slm and ml layers, the target layers of the mEC (Figures 2A and 2C), demonstrating that both layer 2 and layer 3 inputs were silenced by the optogenetic manipulation, whereas the sink in str. radiatum brought about by the surviving SPW-Rs was not affected. In comparison, local CA1 silencing led to even less changes in the CSD magnitude or form of the surviving SPWRs (Figures 2B and 2C). These results further confirm that afferent excitation, as reflected by the SPW sink, is dissociable from local CA1 mechanisms responsible for the induction of ripple oscillation. 

Because mEC input also affects granule cells, we next asked whether coordinated population activity, reflected by LFP 

silencing, and the amplitude of the surviving events decreased, confirming that mEC inputs are critical to synchronize activity not just within CA1 but across the hippocampus, including the dentate gyrus (Figures 2A, 2C, S2M, and S2N).

## CA1 cell assembly reactivations during SPW-Rs are biased by mEC inputs 

Despite the reduction in the incidence of CA1 SPW-Rs, SPW-Rs persisted without mEC inputs. We asked whether neuronal activity patterns of CA1 pyramidal cells during these residual SPW-Rs were altered during each manipulation. Only bilateral mEC manipulations led to a moderate decrease in the firing rate of pyramidal cells during SPW-Rs, while, as expected, local CA1 silencing led to a marked reduction (Figures 4A and 4B). Similar effects were observed for CA1 putative interneurons, with the largest effects being observed for combined manipulations (Figure S4A). Thus, while mEC optogenetic silencing transiently reduced the overall firing rates of CA1 pyramidal cells and interneurons during the 5-s silencing pulses, during SPW-Rs this difference was practically unnoticeable. 

To determine whether silencing mEC during sleep altered the neural content of SPW-Rs, we employed two complementary methods. First, we quantified the expression of CA1 pyramidal cell spike assemblies during NREM sleep.[48] Assemblies were 

defined in 20-ms bins across all detected NREM sleep epochs. The expression of these assemblies detected over the entire session was quantified in 5-s time blocks preceding, during, and after each optogenenetic pulse (Figures S4C and S4D). While the change in expression strength remained similar when comparing epochs preceding and following stimulation (control 1) or across successive pre-stimulation epochs (control 2), ipsilateral mEC silencing led to several assemblies increasing and mainly decreasing their expression strength (Figure S4C). The absolute change in assembly expression was significantly higher during ipsilateral mEC, and bilateral mEC silencing compared with both control comparisons (Figure S4D). One possible explanation for these observed effects is that mEC stimulation altered firing rates of neurons that were contributing to these assemblies. To address this potential confound, we identified cells that were significantly participating in an assembly (i.e., >2 standard deviations of their contribution to the weights) and compared the impact of mEC silencing on the rates of both assembly participating and non-participating neurons. The effect of mEC silencing was comparable across both groups of neurons, although there was a pronounced suppression of rates during the first 1 s following stimulation (Figure S4B). To exclude the first second as a potential major contributor, we examined the absolute change in assembly expression by limiting our analysis to 4-s windows, excluding the first second following stimulation. Assemblies did not significantly increase or decrease their expression (Figures 4C and 4D), yet the effect on the absolute change in expression persisted (Figure 4E), suggesting that the effect on assemblies was not a confound of rate changes. These results suggest that even minor perturbation of mEC can significantly alter the composition of neuronal assemblies during SPW-Rs. Unexpectedly, the results also demonstrate that despite the rebound increase in SPW-R incidence upon stimulation termination, the spike content of SPW-Rs in the post period remained similar to baseline. 

Our second approach measured the sequential activation of neurons during SPW-Rs using a rank-order correlation method.[17][,][49] In each session, the temporal position of each neuron during every SPW-Rs was determined. This ranked sequence of every SPW-R was then correlated with two target template sequences. One template sequence was generated using only those SPW-Rs that occurred during mEC silencing epochs, and for an equal comparison, the second template used only SPW-Rs during the preceding 5-s (control) epochs. All types of mEC perturbations significantly decreased the average correlation compared with the time-matched preceding period, suggesting that even unilateral silencing of mEC can alter spike sequences of neurons during SPW-Rs (Figure 4F). To exclude the possibility that the lower correlations arose due to fewer SPW-Rs during mEC silencing, we repeated the analysis by generating another baseline template by subsampling SPW-Rs during the 5 s preceding stimulation to match the number during mEC perturbation periods. ## DISCUSSION 

Manipulating mEC afferents optogenetically, we demonstrated that excitation from mEC plays an important role in triggering hippocampal SPW-Rs. Unilateral mEC silencing, even contralateral to the recording site, significantly decreased the incidence of SPW-Rs. mEC silencing impaired the bidirectional communication between hippocampal SPW-Rs and neocortical UP-DOWN states. The composition and sequences of CA1 unit firing during SPW-Rs were also altered. Together, these results establish a critical role of mEC in timing hippocampal SPW-Rs and suggest that mEC inputs can select the content of SPW-Rs. 

## Incidence of SPW-Rs is affected by mEC inputs 

SPW-Rs are intrinsic population events of the hippocampus, which persist after both subcortical and neocortical/entorhinal deafferentation.[13][,][20] They are present in the fetal hippocampal tissue transplanted in a cortical cavity in rats[50] and emerge in hippocampal slice preparations in vitro in various forms.[27][,][28][,][51][,][52] Their maximal occurrences correlate with the minima of acetylcholine release in the hippocampus.[53] The population burst underlying the SPW event is due to the fast-spreading excitation in the recurrent collaterals of CA2-CA3 pyramidal cells, which depolarize CA1 neurons and are reflected extracellularly as a large sink in str. radiatum or occasionally in str. oriens.[25] In turn, the transient strong excitation of perisomatic interneurons and pyramidal cells gives rise to a short-lived fast oscillation 

(ripple).[16][,][24][,][52] SPWs are most often coupled to ripples, but ripples can be induced by local excitation of the CA1 circuit optogenetically[24] or potentially by entorhinal excitation under conditions where CA3 synaptic transmission is blocked.[26] Conversely, CA1 chemogenetic silencing abolished ripples but had little effect on the incidence of SPWs in str. radiatum.[44] In vitro, SPWRs occur regularly and their dynamic features correspond to those of relaxation oscillators, whose post-duty cycle phase is sensitive to external perturbations.[1] In contrast, SPW-Rs in the intact brain are aperiodic with irregular inter-SPW-R intervals that follow a lognormal distribution.[11][,][30] A potential source of this variability in vivo is the irregular cortical inputs impinging upon the hippocampal circuits, which can bias the time of occurrence of SPW-Rs. 

mEC can exert an influence by at least two different mechanisms: tonic and phasic excitation. In terms of tonic excitation, mEC provides the strongest extrahippocampal innervation to the dentate gyrus and subregions of the hippocampus. Layer 2 inputs of mEC strongly and effectively excite the mid-apical dendrites of dentate granule cells.[54][,][55] Silencing mEC results in the decreased firing of granule cells, CA3 pyramidal neurons, and the power of gamma oscillations,[43][,][56] and, as shown here, virtually abolishes LFP dentate spikes. In contrast, suppressing the mEC had a relatively small impact on sustained CA1 firing rates. This minor effect is likely due to the balanced effect of mEC on the CA1 network. mEC provides strong feedforward excitation to CA1 interneurons[57] with str. lacunosum-moleculare-bounded axons,[58–60] which can balance mEC excitation to pyramidal cells. The mEC also sends direct long-range inhibitory projections to the hippocampus.[61][,][62] Therefore, in our experiments, a potential mechanism could be through direct activation of these interneurons. However, a close examination of the immediate effects of stimulation as well as terminal excitation of putative long-range interneurons in the hippocampus argue against this possibility (Figures S1F, S1G, and S1M). 

Silencing the mEC, either ipsilateral or contralateral to the recording electrode, reduced the incidence of SPW-Rs. This is in contrast to the effect on hippocampal theta oscillations, where ipsilateral silencing virtually abolished theta waves, whereas contralateral silencing had a negligible effect.[43] This may arise due to the differential effect of mEC on CA3 and CA1 populations. While mEC silencing had a negligible effect on the tonic firing rate of CA1 pyramidal cells, it reduced the firing rate of CA3 neurons.[43] The CA3 afferents to CA1 contribute very little to theta currents,[43] whereas the CA3 population burst is the main driver of SPW-Rs. In addition, there is a strong commissural communication between the CA3 regions, which is absent between the CA1 regions.[55][,][63]

In addition to the probability of occurrence, both the duration and amplitude of SPW-Rs were modestly reduced by mEC silencing. Further, the ratio of ripples (detected in CA1 pyramidal layer) and SPW sinks (in str. radiatum), similar to CA1 silencing, also decreased during mEC suppression. These findings indicate that the tonic effect of mEC also contributes to ripple features. In previous work, SPW-R-related replay of waking place cell sequences were shortened by specific layer 3 silencing of EC, and EC3 spike bursts often preceded long-duration CA1 ripples even in the waking mouse.[18] The reduced firing of interneurons during SPW-Rs that we observed in the course of optogenetic suppression of mEC may also contribute to reducing ripple length. Overall, these findings suggest that tonic mEC input to CA1 can shape ripple events. 

One interesting observation was the lack of a robust effect of mEC silencing on the incidence of awake SPW-R. One possibility is that during awake states the hippocampus receives sufficient tonic excitation from subcortical neuromodulators, and the impact of disfacilitation of mEC afferents is therefore less drastic. Nonetheless, analysis of assembly content revealed that mEC inhibition also impacted assembly expression in waking SPW-Rs similar to sleep. 

## Coordination of SPW-Rs with neocortical slow oscillations 

A second effect of mEC may be its phasic influence. During slow oscillations of NREM sleep, neurons in the neocortex and EC alternate between sustained spiking (UP state) and population silence (DOWN state).[33][,][36][,][37][,][47] Granule cells also display UPDOWN-state modulations.[33][,][38] The probability of occurrence of SPW-Rs is several-fold higher during UP states, as compared with DOWN states.[31–35] 

The effect of the naturally occurring DOWN states of NREM sleep can be compared with the optogenetic silencing of mEC on the hippocampus. The magnitude of firing-rate reduction in CA1 during the first 200 ms after ipsilateral mEC stimulation was comparable to that observed during the DOWN state, although the firing rate returned rapidly to control levels, despite continued suppression of mEC output. The similar magnitudes of CA1 spike reduction during DOWN state and immediately after mEC silencing suggest that mEC is the major extrahippocampal contributor of CA1 excitation. In contrast to transient rate changes, the reduction of SPW-R incidence was present throughout mEC silencing, implying that CA1 rate change alone cannot explain the observed effects on SPW-Rs. Relatedly, contralateral mEC silencing did not have a detectable change on CA1 firing rates yet significantly decreased SPW-R occurrence. A likely explanation of the discrepancy of SPW-R probability and CA1 firing rate is that mEC silencing also affected granule cells and CA3 pyramidal cells.[43][,][56] 

In addition to a direct effect, mEC silencing can have an indirect delayed effect on hippocampal networks. We observed a rebound increase of SPW-Rs upon release from suppression of mEC, during both sleep and waking exploration on the maze. This post-suppression effect is reminiscent of the transient rebound spiking of mEC neurons and increased incidence of SPW-R after DOWN-UP shifts in the mEC.[15] These phasic effects, mediated by mEC, can explain the non-rhythmic occurrence of SPW-Rs. Because the durations of both UP and DOWN 

states follow a lognormal distribution,[11][,][15][,][30] we hypothesize that the main source of the irregular occurrence of SPW-Rs is their stochastic drive by the entorhinal input. The phasic increase in firing during the DOWN-UP transition of mEC neurons can provide sufficient boosting to the CA3 recurrent circuits to advance the expected occurrence of spontaneously occurring SPW-Rs. 

Besides mEC, the lateral EC may also contribute to the timing of SPW-Rs. In addition to these cortical inputs, it is possible that hitherto undisclosed thalamic and other inputs can coordinate the timing of both UP and DOWN states and hippocampal SPW-Rs. The present results cannot exclude these possibilities. 

## Medial entorhinal input selects SPW-R-active cell assemblies 

Although mEC silencing altered some of the macroscopic features of hippocampal SPW-Rs, a major effect we identified was its impact on the neuronal assembly content of SPW-Rs and the within-assembly sequence of spiking. These effects were prominent during ipsilateral silencing of mEC, even though single hemispheric manipulations did not affect the spike probability of occurrence of pyramidal neurons during SPW-Rs. An unexpected finding was that prolonged mEC inactivation (5 s) did not induce a permanent or long-lasting perturbation on SPW-R assemblies, since at the cessation of optogenetic stimulation the cell assemblies returned immediately to their pre-stimulation patterns. 

While a direct examination of replay (or reactivation) of awake sequences was not possible in our dataset, we hypothesize that the main contribution of mEC is to maintain a dynamic state in the hippocampus and bias which neuronal trajectories are selected during SPW-Rs. State changes in the mEC can affect the initial conditions that give rise to sequential activation of neurons in the CA3 recurrent system, thus affecting which trajectories (i.e., neuronal sequences) are replayed. Similar initial conditions in the same brain state give rise repeatedly to the same trajectories.[64] mEC silencing can effectively change the hippocampal state and consequently the emergence of specific neuronal trajectories. Our analysis of awake SPW-Rs even suggests the possibility that these trajectories become more ‘‘rigid’’ without mEC input.[23] Self-restoration of the mEC state after optogenetic silencing allows restoration of a similar state in the hippocampus, therefore the same neuronal trajectories are launched. This situation is similar to the selection role of the mEC in the theta state. mEC silencing did not affect the fraction of place cells on the track but induced a new sequence of place fields (‘‘remapping’’).[43][,][56][,][65][,][66] Following mEC silencing, the original place field sequences were restored. These findings indicate that sequential organization of neuronal firing and place fields is not so much a result of plastic synaptic changes but rather a selection of sequences from a preexisting large repertoire of possible trajectories.[67–70]

## EXPERIMENTAL MODEL AND SUBJECT DETAILS 

The experimental mice used in this paper were mostly the same as in our published experiments on theta oscillations.[43] However, the current data were obtained during home cage recordings which were mostly not analyzed in the previous manuscript. We also used sessions when several sites of the linear probe recorded neuronal activity in the overlying neocortex, corresponding to the posterior parietal and anterior visual areas. Behavior epochs (spatial alternation targeting stimulation to the side arms) used for this manuscript are also different from those used in the previous manuscript. Two additional control mice were added to confirm that our effects were not confounded by light stimulation. Experimental procedures, which are identical between the two manuscripts, including viral injections, probe implantations, perfusion, and tissue processing, can be found in the original manuscript. 

## Subjects and surgical procedures 

The Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at New York University Langone Medical Center approved all experiments. Briefly, heterozygous male C57Bl/6 transgenic mice (n = 18 mice, Tg(Grik4-cre)G32-4Stl/J, Jax Stock No. 006474, n = 2 mice, wildtype C57BL/6J, Jax Stock No. 000664) were housed 2-3 per cage in the vivarium on a 12-h light/ dark cycle before surgery. After surgery, the mice were housed individually and exposed to a 12-h reverse light cycle (lights on/off at 7 pm/am). Before behavior training, the mice had ad libitum access to food and water, but water intake was restricted to maintain 85% of their weight during and after behavioral training. Of the 18 Grik4-cre mice, 6 were unilaterally injected with AAV5-mDlx-ChR2-mCherry (titer, 1.4x10[13] ) in the mEC right hemisphere, 3 were bilaterally injected with AAV5-mDlx-ChR2-mCherry in the mEC, 6 were injected with AAV5-mDlx-ChR2-mCherry in the mEC right hemisphere along with AAV1-CamKIIa-stGtACR2-FusionRed in the right dorsal CA1 (titer:1.3x10[11] ), and 6 were injected with AAV5-Dlx-ChR2-mCherry in the mEC right hemisphere along with AAV5-Syn-DIO-PSAM4-GlyR-IRES-GFP (titer: 2x10[12] ) in bilateral CA3. The CA3 silencing sessions were not included in the analysis for this manuscript. Two additional wildtype control mice were implanted with a probe in CA1 and optic fibers over the mEC but not injected with any viruses. 

## Recordings and behavior 

Behavior sessions included mice running 40-60 laps on a delayed spatial alternation task. After each behavior session, hippocampal activity was recorded from mice while they were in their home cage for 3–4 h. During this time, the mice were randomly stimulated with up to three different manipulations for at least 30 trials. The manipulations, which could be individual or combined, involved 5 secs of stimulation followed by 25 s of rest for 15 min, after which there was no further stimulation for the next 15 min. Home cage stimulation was not targeted to a particular brain state, but most analyses in this manuscript were restricted to periods when mice were in NREM sleep. On most days, the animals completed a second spatial alternation task with a different type of stimulation. Before the start of the experiments, the mice were trained for 7-10 days to achieve a minimum performance of 80% in the delayed version of the alternation task. The maze used in the experiment was custom-built in the lab and had dimensions of 70cm x 40 cm, with walls that were 3.7 cm high. Water was delivered by solenoids and activated by the animal crossing infrared beams at the reward and delay zones.[72] 

An Intan RHD2000 system (Intan Technologies) was used to acquire electrophysiological data, which were digitized at 30-kHz. The wideband signal was downsampled to 1250 Hz and used for the LFP. A pulley system was developed to counteract the weight of the tether and headstage. In the home cage, optogenetic stimulation was administered by 5-s square pulses, which were repeated every 30 s for 30-50 trials using PulsePal2 (Sanworks, v2). On the maze, square pulse stimulation was triggered by TTLs from the IR sensors, using PulsePal2. To track the animal’s position, a Basler camera (acA1300-60 gmNIR, Graftek Imaging) was used, which sampled at 30Hz and detected a head-mounted red LED. Position data was synchronized with neural data using TTLs that signaled the shutter position.

## SPW-R detection 

SPW-Rs were detected as described in Tingley and Buzsa´ ki[75] and the code is available at the Buzsaki lab github repository (bz_FindRipples). The channel corresponding to the pyramidal cell layer was defined manually. LFP from this channel was filtered (120220 Hz; Butterworth; order=3). The resulting signal was transformed into a normalized squared signal (NSS) and peaks that exceeded 5 standard deviations above the mean NSS were identified. We confirmed that separately calculating the NSS during baseline and stimulation sessions did not alter our observed effects on SPW-Rs. The start and end points of the SPW-Rs were determined based on a threshold of 2 standard deviations above the mean NSS, and SPW-Rs were limited to a duration of between 30ms and 100ms. This double peak threshold was systematically varied to confirm that our effects did not emerge from detection confounds. Any EMG-related artifact was excluded from the LFP data using techniques described in Schomburg et al.[76] The peak of the SPW-R (max power value > 5 standard deviations) was defined as time 0. SPW-Rs were identified across the entire day, including both the periods spent in the maze and the home cage, but analyses were restricted to specific periods as described. 

## Detection of sharp-waves 

The channel corresponding to stratum radiatum was defined manually for each session. The LFP trace was filtered (2-10 Hz, Butterworth; order = 3) and z-scored. The sharp-wave peak was detected as the time when the z-scored signal exceeded 2 standard deviations below, during the occurrence of a SPW-R. If a SPW-R was not associated with a detected sharp-wave, it was assigned a nan for the sharp-wave. Onset and offset of the sharp-wave were computed as the time when the radiatum filtered z-score signal crossed a threshold of -0.5 (0.5 standard deviations below) before and after the SPW-R peak. 

## SPW-R triggered CSD analysis 

Timestamps corresponding to SPW-R peaks were restricted to 5-s windows of each stimulation period, and for equal comparison, the 5 s preceding each stimulation (baseline). 400 ms long segments of the LFP signal on each channel along the depth of the shank were aligned with reference to the SPW-R peak, and the differential between successive average aligned channels was used to generate the CSD magnitude. Because this analysis was affected by very few SPW-Rs remaining during stimulation periods, multiple sessions within a mouse were pooled to detect a robust CSD. The difference in CSD magnitude was calculated by subtracting the CSD magnitude in stimulation periods from baseline. All analyses were restricted to periods within the home cage.

## Brain state scoring 

State scoring was performed as described in Levenstein et al.[30] and Watson et al.[78] with the custom code SleepScoreMaster. Briefly, spectrograms were generated for 10-s windows (1-s sliding window) by a fast Fourier transform (FFT) of the LFP (1-100 Hz). Three types of signals were used to differentiate brain states – (1) EMG was extracted from the intracranially recorded signals as described in Schomburg et al.[76] We detected zero time-lag correlation coefficients between 300-600 Hz filtered signals (Butterworth filter) across different sites along the shank. The mean of all pairwise correlations measures in 0.5-s bins was recorded as EMG score. (2) Narrowband theta frequency: Theta power was calculated from a hippocampal channel as the ratio of 5-10Hz/2-16Hz from the spectrogram. (3) Broadband LFP: Spectral power in the FFT was log-transformed and z-scored over time for each frequency. Principal component analysis was performed on this z-scored signal, and the first principal component was based on power in the low (<20-Hz) frequency range and had oppositely weighted power in the higher (>32-Hz) frequencies. All states were reviewed by the experimenter, and corrections were made when discrepancies between automated scoring and user assessment occurred.

Spectograms (50-250 Hz) were generated using a morlet wavelet (number of cycles = 5) from whitened LFP signal from the CA1 pyramidal cell layer. UP-DOWN transitions or mEC stimulation periods were used as time 0 to align across all occurrences and the average spectrogram power was plotted. 

## Assembly analysis 

Cell assemblies were defined using an unsupervised statistical framework based on a hybrid PCA followed by ICA.[10][,][48] Spike trains of each neuron were binned in 20-ms intervals by concatenating spikes during sleep NREM periods and z-scored rates were calculated for each bin. Only putative pyramidal cells were included in this analysis. Assemblies were defined by combining all periods of NREM sleep (including stimulation periods). Spike trains were convolved with a Gaussian kernel (SD = 10 ms), and the matrix of firing correlation coefficients for all pairs of neurons was constructed. Principal components with eigenvalues that exceeded the threshold for random firing correlations (using the Marcenko-Pastur law) were used to determine the number of assemblies.� Next, using the fast-ICA algorithm,[79] we determined the vector of weights (contribution of each neuron) for each assembly (component). 

The strength of each assembly’s activation as a function of time was determined by multiplying the convolved z-scored firing rate of a given neuron at a given time by the weight of that neuron’s contribution to the assembly. The product of these weighted spike counts was then summed for all nonidentical pairs of neurons to generate the instantaneous activation strength. For each assembly the average activation strength during baseline (5 or 4s preceding stimulation) and stimulation conditions was compared, and a measure of change in activation strength (D assembly activity) was defined as the absolute value of (Stim-baseline)/(stim+baseline). The fraction of assemblies increasing or decreasing in expression during each manipulation was defined by calculating a threshold of mean±1*std. dev of the stability during baseline sessions and measuring the fraction of assemblies whose expression changed greater than, or less than this threshold during stimulation (Figure 4D). 

Awake assemblies were defined in a similar manner, restricting the spike matrix to either the side arms of the maze, separating baseline and stimulation trials, or by defining assemblies using the entire behavior session on the maze. Expression during awake SPW-Rs was examined by matching the number of SPW-Rs between baseline and stimulation and calculating the D assembly activity across baseline and mEC silencing conditions, as well as within both baseline and mEC silencing conditions. 

## Rank order correlation 

Rank-order tests were used to identify sequences of units repeating consistently using a modified version of the methods described in Stark et al.,[17] Navas-Olive et al.,[49] and Valero et al.[80] The timing of multiple units recorded simultaneously was transformed to a normalized sequence order from 0 to 1 within a SPW-R event. Only events with at least five participating units were included, and only the first spike from each unit in a cycle was considered for the rank order. A template was generated by averaging the ranks of all units during SPW-R events occurring within 5-s stimulation windows. For an equal comparison, similar templates were constructed for the 5-s baseline period preceding stimulation. Next, the rank of units within each SPW-R event during the entire home cage session was correlated to the above-described templates (Pearson correlation). The average correlation of all SPW-R events against the defined templates was calculated per session. In a second analysis, templates of baseline windows were generated by subsampling SPW-Rs to match the number of SPW-Rs persisting during stimulation. 

## QUANTIFICATION AND STATISTICAL ANALYSIS 

## Statistical analysis 

All stimulation conditions were performed randomly, and animals from different experiments were run in parallel cohorts. All statistical tests were conducted using MATLAB R2021a, and the details of the tests used are described with the results. Unless otherwise noted, all tests used non-parametric comparisons of means and variance (Wilcoxon paired signed rank tests, Wilcoxon rank-sum test, Kruskal-Wallis one-way analysis of variance, and Friedman tests). When parametric tests were used, the data satisfied the criteria for normality (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test) and equality of variance (Bartlett test for equal variance). All posthoc tests were performed using Tukey honest significant differences and correcting for multiple comparisons. Boxplots without paired lines represent the median and 25[th] and 75[th] percentiles, and the whiskers represent the data range. Boxplots with connecting data lines represent mean ± sem. In box plots without datapoints, outliers were excluded from the plots, but always included in the statistical analysis.

# Intelligence and the Frontal Lobe: The Organization of Goal-Directed Behavior*

Basic to the study of individual differences is the concept of ‘‘general intelligence’’ or Spearman’s _g._ In this article we suggest that _g_ is largely a reflection of the control functions of the frontal lobe. A series of experiments investigates a phenomenon we call _goal neglect:_ disregard of a task requirement even though it has been understood and remembered. Subjectively it is as though the neglected requirement ‘‘slips the subject’s mind.’’ Previously described in frontal patients, we show that goal neglect can also be seen in some members of the normal population. In line with conventional distinctions between controlled and automatic processing, eliciting conditions for goal neglect include novelty, weak error feedback, and multiple concurrent task requirements. Under these conditions neglect is linked closely to _g_ and extremely common after frontal lesions. Following many other models, we suggest that behavior in any task is structured by a set of action constraints or requirements, derived in part from verbal instructions and specified at multiple levels of abstraction. As originally proposed by Spearman, one possible explanation is that some ‘‘general’’ or _g_ factor makes at least some contribution to success in all manner of diverse tests. Assuming the _g_ factor interpretation, it is easy to show which tests best measure (are most highly correlated with) _g._ This is the basis for design of standard ‘‘intelligence’’ or IQ tests such as the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (or WAIS; Wechsler, 1955) and Raven’s Progressive Matrices (Raven, Court, & Raven, 1988). 

In the terms of Spearman’s (1927) theory, the of _g_ is abstract. Whatever its nature or explanation, _g_ is defined simply as the (hypothetical) factor responsible for broad positive correlations. Given this definition, its measurement is reasonably exact. Providing test batteries are large and diverse, different batteries produce much the same _g_ factor, a result termed by Spearman (1927) ‘‘indifference of the indicator.’’ A further question is the elucidation of this abstract factor in cognitive terms. What functions or processes are reflected in _g?_ A reasonable hypothesis is that _g_ may reflect the function of some particular information processing system, contributing in part to the organization of many different activities and hence producing some positive correlation between them. In this paper we suggest a specific hypothesis of this sort. 

Our hypothesis is that, in large part at least, _g_ the action control functions of the brain’s frontal lobes. Following frontal lobe damage, deficits are commonly seen in the organization of many different kinds of behavior. Coherent, goal-directed behavior is distorted by irrelevant or ill-judged intrusions or by an apparent neglect of task demands; active search for a path toward a task’s goals—so characteristic of normal human behavior—is replaced by performance that seems passive, inert, stereotyped, or fragmented (Fuster, 1980; Luria, 1966). Such deficits can be revealed in almost any behavioral domain, from stimulus categorization (Milner, 1963) to maze learning (Milner, 1965), picture description to solving puzzles, story recall to regard for social conventions (Luria, 1966). In different contexts the patient may appear perseverative or distractable, rigid or inappropriate, passive or impulsive and disinhibited. 

Standard views of the relationship between frontal functions and Spearman’s _g_ reveal an immediate paradox. On the one hand, both are often taken to concern the highest-level ‘‘executive’’ or ‘‘supervisory’’ functions of cognition (e.g., Luria, 1966; Norman & Shallice, 1980; Snow, 1981). Frontal patients are supposed to have difficulties with functions such as ‘‘planning’’ and ‘‘problem-solving’’ which seem basic to intelligence. Working memory—especially its ‘‘executive’’ component (Baddeley, 1986)—is popularly implicated in both _g_ (Kyllonen & Christal, 1990) and frontal lobe function (Goldman-Rakic, 1988). At the same time, it is conventional belief in neuropsychology that frontal functions are rather unrelated to psychometric ‘‘intelligence’’ (e.g., Teuber, 1972).

## THE CONVENTIONAL VIEW 

## _Standard Tests of IQ_ 

Empirically, the view that _g_ is rather unrelated to frontal functions derives mainly from the effects of frontal lesions on IQ. Though tests like the WAIS are not totally insensitive to frontal lesions (e.g., Warrington, James, & Maciejewski, 1986), they seem not to reflect frontal difficulties with any great fidelity. In particular, patients with major frontal lesions and manifest cognitive impairments may nevertheless show WAIS IQs in the superior range (e.g., Eslinger & Damasio, 1985; Shallice & Burgess, 1991). On this basis it has often been suggested that such tests are particularly _unsuitable_ for measuring frontal functions (Teuber, 1972) and that frontal functions accordingly have little to do with conventional ‘‘intelligence.’’ 

Recently, however, we have found reason to question this conclusion (Duncan, Burgess, & Emslie, 1995). Despite its clinical popularity, a test like the WAIS may not be the best way to obtain a measure of _g_ in a clinical population. The concept of _g,_ as an attempt to explain universal positive correlations between different tests, refers to each person’s tendency to do relatively well or relatively badly on all manner of tasks. It may be measured in two different ways. The first is to obtain average performance on a diverse range of tests. This is the method adopted in the WAIS. As we have said, providing test batteries are reasonably large and diverse, they give much the same estimate of _g_ no matter what particular tests they contain. An alternative approach is to find individual tests whose _g_ correlations are high even without averaging over diverse subtests. A test of this sort is likely to be especially dependent on whatever functions _g_ measures. In general, the tests with the highest _g_ correlations are so-called tests of ‘‘fluid intelligence’’ (e.g., Marshalek, Lohman, & Snow, 1983). Typically such tests involve novel problem-solving, with spatial, verbal, or other materials. A good example is Progressive Matrices. Large-scale factor analyses on many different data sets have led to the conclusion that these are the tests most closely related to _g_ in Spearman’s sense (Carroll, 1993). For this reason they may be the best tests to use in measuring changes in _g_ after brain damage. 

An additional important consideration may be a test’s dependence on knowledge. In tests like the WAIS, many subtests (vocabulary, etc.) are simple measures of knowledge. In all probability, such tests of ‘‘crystallized intelligence’’ measure not so much current _g_ as _g_ at the time of learning

Of course one must be cautious in assuming that poor performance on a test after brain damage reflects an impairment in the same function that the test usually measures in the normal population. For example, tests like the Culture Fair and the Progressive Matrices must depend in part on the integrity of the visual system (Basso, DeRenzi, Faglioni, Scotti, & Spinnler, 1973; Risberg, Maximilian, & Prohovnik, 1977), but this is not to say that the normal source of individual variation on the test is its visual requirement. In the same spirit, frontal patients may do poorly on the Culture Fair for reasons unrelated to its strong _g_ correlation in the normal population. To go further one must establish additional parallels between frontal deficits and individual differences in _g,_ as in the experiments we shall report. Already, however, the data are sufficient to question the standard view that _g_ is largely unrelated to frontal functions. 

_Modularity of Frontal Functions_ 

A second concern over any attempt to relate frontal impairments to _g_ derives from strong evidence for modularity in frontal functions. In the macaque, for example, the cortex of the principal sulcus is much more heavily involved in spatial than nonspatial operations (Goldman-Rakic, 1988; Mishkin & Manning, 1978), while orbitofrontal lesions are associated specifically with deficits in recognition memory (Kowalska, Bachevalier, & Mishkin, 1991). Though human data are less clear, certainly several higher-order motor impairments may be specifically associated with posterior frontal regions (e.g., Paus, Kalina, Patockova, Angerova, Cerny, Mecir, Bauer, & Krabec, 1991). At a more abstract level, it is sometimes suggested that such functions as inhibition of inappropriate activity, cognitive switching, and establishment of new goals may be controlled by separate frontal systems. How can such a modular view of frontal function be reconciled with the undifferentiated psychometric concept of _g?_ 

Two approaches seem promising. First, there is certainly evidence that

some frontal regions have rather general functions. In the macaque, very widespread deficits are produced by lesions in the inferior frontal convexity (Mishkin & Manning, 1978; Passingham, 1975). In the human, positron emission tomography shows activation of dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and the anterior cingulate in a wide variety of relatively complex tasks, including generating appropriate verbs in response to nouns (Petersen, Fox, Posner, Mintun, & Raichle, 1988), generating a random sequence of finger movements (Frith, Friston, Liddle, & Frackowiak, 1991), divided visual attention (Corbetta, Miezin, Dobmeyer, Shulman, & Petersen, 1991), and various recall tasks (Grasby, Frith, Friston, Bench, Frackowiak, & Dolan, 1993). In line with these observations, one possibility is that the functions reflected in _g_ are associated with specific frontal regions. 

A second possibility is that the behavioral functions in _g_ arise from joint or cooperative activity in a variety of distinct frontal systems. An analogy with recent views of visual selective attention may be useful (Desimone & Duncan, 1995; Duncan, in press). Lesions in many brain systems, sensory and motor, cortical and subcortical, can produce a bias of attention toward one or the other side of space. The coordinated selection of one object rather than another for the control of current behavior must involve integration of these multiple competitive systems; there is no simple one-toone mapping between a unified behavioral function (the direction of visual attention) and underlying brain systems. Many accounts of frontal function involve selection not of perceptual objects but of action plans, strategies, and so on. This is a possibility we shall expand upon shortly. As in the visual case, selection of actions, strategies, etc., may typically involve cooperative activity in a number of distinct frontal systems. Again there may be no simple, one-to-one mapping between a general (action) selection function—reflected in _g_ —and underlying brain systems. 

On either view it would probably be wrong to think of frontal impairment as simply a reduction in _g._ The bizarre quality of many frontal errors (Eslinger & Damasio, 1985), for example, discourages such a simple view. At the same time, a major part of the effect of frontal lesions may be impairment of those functions reflected in _g._ In the remainder of the article we present a hypothesis of this sort. 

## THE ORGANIZATION OF GOAL-DIRECTED BEHAVIOR 

As we have said, ‘‘executive control’’ has often been implicated in both Spearman’s _g_ and frontal lobe function. By ‘‘executive’’ we refer loosely to some kind of high-level control of processing or action. Many detailed accounts of action selection have been described, including recent accounts dealing specifically with frontal functions (e.g., Cohen & Servan-Schreiber, 1992; Kimberg & Farah, 1993). To provide context for the present work, however, we need only two very general ideas: the idea that action is structured by selection of successive requirements or constraints and the distinction between ‘‘active’’ or ‘‘voluntary’’ and ‘‘passive’’ or ‘‘automatic’’ control.

## _Organization by Successive Constraints_ 

One example of successive constraint selection is provided by the hierarchical structure of much human activity (Miller, Galanter, & Pribram, 1960). An example is shown in Fig. 1. As a result of either internal operations or external prompts, the action control system sets some goal or target state. In Fig. 1, for example, the actor may receive an external instruction ‘‘make soup.’’ This goal may be seen as an abstract requirement on behavior: by whatever means, soup is to be produced. Long-term knowledge of the world indicates that this target state depends on others—for example, availability of boiling water and onions. Availability of chopped onions may accordingly be set as a further requirement or subgoal—especially in the presence of short-term knowledge indicating that onions can currently be obtained (e.g., Hayes-Roth and Hayes-Roth, 1979). This process of unfolding a progressively more detailed set of requirements or constraints continues until one reaches a level of actual motor commands, or in other words, a specification sufficiently detailed to allow actual execution (Sacerdoti, 1974). Hierarchical structure emerges naturally from working on successive small subproblems, using each new requirement on behavior as a guide to adding others which would help achieve it.

early on as requirements if soup is required. There is no consistent association, on the other hand, between producing soup and such low-level considerations as what angle to set one’s elbow joint. A great deal more must be known, beyond a requirement for soup, before the appropriate angle can be determined. Each successive requirement added to the plan acts as a guide to more detailed selections. Proceeding immediately from the most abstract to the most detailed level renders planning chaotic (Sacerdoti, 1974). 

In the context of this account, it has often been noted that new requirements or goals must arise in two different ways (e.g., Anderson, 1983; Duncker, 1945; Reitman, 1965). First, as outlined above, new candidate goals arise by working backward from currently active supergoals. These are candidates bringing the supergoal closer. Thus a requirement for soup suggests a requirement for chopped onions. Equally important, however, must be a process of working forward from the current state, or input from the environment. Chopped onions are more likely to be selected than packaged soup if onions are easily available. More broadly, current goals can be totally overturned by novel input; for example, making soup is abandoned if the baby tips over its high-chair. Candidate goals suggested both by working backward and by working forward compete for control of behavior. 

Though our main concern here is with action, or _controlling_ some state of the world, similar considerations apply also to _establishing_ some variable’s value. In many cases of problem-solving, one wishes simply to establish what is true in the world—whether triangles are congruent, what will happen when two wires are touched together, and so on. Again, however, this is a matter of using the known implications of one fact (that one wire is positive and one negative) to set a value on others (there will be a bang): Just as an action plan is built up by addition of successive constraints, so an internal model of the world is built up by addition of successive facts based on known implications. Models of such problem-solving have much in common with models of action planning; for example, a similar combination of working forward from known facts and working backward from facts one wishes to establish. 

The general point is that behavior is shaped by successive selection of requirements or constraints at multiple levels of abstraction, using knowledge concerning the implications of one fact to establish target values for others. Candidate goals are suggested both by currently active supergoals and by the state of the environment. The control of action is a matter of competition between these candidates to set effective requirements on behavior. 

## _Active vs Passive Control_ 

A distinction between behavior which is ‘‘active,’’ ‘‘controlled,’’ or ‘‘voluntary’’ and that which is ‘‘passive,’’ ‘‘automatic,’’ or ‘‘stimulus-driven’’ has been applied separately to both _g_ (Ackerman, 1988; Snow, 1981) and

frontal (Baddeley, 1986; Bianchi, 1922; Frith et al., 1991; Fuster, 1980; Norman & Shallice, 1980). Both frontal patients and people from the lower part of the _g_ distribution have been thought to have difficulties with ‘‘initial orientation’’ to a problem (Luria, 1966), with ‘‘active’’ or ‘‘selfgenerated’’ search for appropriate strategies or lines of attack (Fuster, 1980; Snow, 1981). As one patient put it, having lost her job as a waitress after frontal surgery, ‘‘You have to have a ‘push’ to wait on several tables at once, and I just didn’t have it any more’’ (Malmo, 1948, p. 542). Following Norman and Shallice (1980), we may ask how such views relate to the general account of action selection sketched above. 

The most obvious factor differentiating subjectively active or controlled from passive or automatic behavior is novelty (Bryan & Harter, 1899; James, 1890; Schneider & Shiffrin, 1977). Novelty in two different senses is implicated in the difficulties of frontal patients. First there is perseveration, or difficulty changing away from some line of action once it becomes inappropriate. Many different examples have been described, from difficulty in abandoning inappropriate motor activity (Bianchi, 1922; Diamond & Goldman-Rakic, 1989; Luria, 1966) to perseveration of higher-order mental sets (Milner, 1963; Owen, Roberts, Hodges, Summers, Polkey, & Robbins, 1993). Second there is the effect of long-term practice or familiarity. It has often been suggested that familiar behavior is relatively immune to the effects of frontal lesions (e.g., Walsh, 1978). If forced to practice a suitable sequence of operations, frontal patients may gradually learn to solve even complex problems (Luria & Tsvetkova, 1964). Similarly, consistent practice in a simple stimulus-response task may gradually reduce _g_ correlations in the normal population (Ackerman, 1988), though this may not be the result in more complex tasks (Rabbitt, 1993; Woodrow, 1939). 

A second important consideration may be the strength of the environmental prompt to behavior, sometimes taken to distinguish ‘‘voluntary’’ or ‘‘selfdirected’’ from ‘‘stimulus-driven’’ action. One form of environmental prompt is a direct verbal instruction. It has often been noted that frontal patients may need explicit verbal prompts either to continue with a task or to satisfy each of its separate requirements (Hecaen & Albert, 1978; Luria & Tsvetkova, 1964). For example, the patient may fail to make progress on some task such as preparing a meal, but be perfectly capable of carrying out each component when individually instructed to do so (Penfield & Evans, 1935). In much the same way, explicit instructions detailing a precise strategy or manner of procedure may reduce _g_ correlations in a variety of complex tasks (Snow, 1981). The distinction between situations requiring a greater or lesser degree of ‘‘spontaneous organization’’ in behavior seems largely to reflect the availability or strength of verbal or other prompts. 

In the context of competition between momentary candidate goals or requirements on behavior, it is easy to see familiarity and prompts as strong sources of bias (Norman & Shallice, 1980). Frequent experience of selecting

a particular set of requirements in the current behavioral context may make them strong candidates for selection again. So also may an explicit verbal command. Under these circumstances, behavior may be experienced as ‘‘passive,’’ ‘‘automatic,’’ or ‘‘stimulus-driven’’ and may be rather independent of the efficiency of the goal selection process. When strong bias toward the correct action is missing, however, reduced efficiency may produce poor choices of behavior. Under these circumstances behavior is experienced as ‘‘active,’’ ‘‘controlled,’’ or ‘‘voluntary.’’ 

Our hypothesis may now be stated as follows. The process of shaping behavior by competition between candidate goals or action requirements is basic to both Spearman’s _g_ and executive deficit following frontal lesions. In line with the standard distinction between controlled and automatic behavior, the shaping process is most likely to fail under conditions of novelty and weak environmental support. In the remainder of the paper, this hypothesis is investigated using a simple behavioral model of the use of environmental prompts to set effective requirements on behavior. As in the examples described above, the prompt we use is a direct verbal instruction. We expect that explicit failure to activate a task’s requirements should be strongly correlated with _g_ and a conspicuous feature of frontal impairment. 

## THE MODEL TASK 

As we have said, a single verbal command may be to prompt appropriate behavior in a frontal patient. The command must be repeated or expanded. At the same time, the patient may show verbally that the command was understood; there is a mismatch between what is _known_ of task requirements and what is attempted in behavior. For example, the patient may have been asked to squeeze her hand when a light comes on; on receipt of the signal, the patient may say, ‘‘I must squeeze!’’—yet make no attempt to do so (Luria, 1966). Asked to draw a square, a patient described by Konow and Pribram (1970) began instead to draw an A (repeated from a previous trial). Her comment was, ‘‘That’s not a square—I guess I’ll draw you an A.’’ In the Wisconsin card-sorting task, cards must be sorted first by one attribute (e.g., color), then by another. Again incorrect sorting strategies may be continued even though the patient is able to state that they should be abandoned (Milner, 1963). More widely, frontal patients may show ‘‘lack of concern’’; though problems are appreciated they seem not to become the focus of either appropriate emotional concern or relevant behavior (Walsh, 1978). Here we shall use the term _goal neglect_ to describe disregard of a task requirement, even though it has been understood. 

Several years ago, a chance observation suggested that a similar goal neglect might sometimes be seen even in the normal population. This observation arose during an experiment on individual differences in bus drivers (McKenna, Duncan, & Brown, 1986). Among the various tests administered, one was a complex dichotic listening task, originally developed by Gopher &

The instruction was to listen for specified targets (digits) on one side or the other; a tone cue at the start of the trial indicated which side to attend, and sometimes, a second tone near the end of the message called for a switch to the opposite side. We discovered that, on occasion, people would show verbally that they had understood the instructions, but then complete the whole task incorrectly (see also Kahneman, Ben-Ishai, & Lotan, 1973). In particular they were likely to neglect the second tone when it called for a switch of sides. Sometimes subjects seemed perfectly able to describe what tones they had heard and what they should have done; the impression, subjectively rather striking, was that knowledge of their failure produced no active _attempt_ to correct it. Significantly, the phenomenon was largely limited to those doing poorly on the Culture Fair test of _g._ 

In the following experiments, we demonstrate goal neglect in an analog of Gopher and Kahneman’s (1971) test. We consider the circumstances under which it occurs, its relationship to Spearman’s _g,_ and the effects of frontal lesions. 

## DEFINITION OF _g_ 

As we described earlier, factor analytic studies show intelligence to lie close to the core of _g_ (Carroll, 1993). With their relative independence from prior knowledge, fluid intelligence tests are also most suitable for investigating the effects of brain lesions. In our experiments, Scale 2, Form A of the Culture Fair test (Institute for Personality and Ability Testing, 1973) is used as our standard fluid intelligence measure. 

The reliability of a test the proportion of non-error variance (see Winer, 1970, p. 126). According to the manual, reliability for Form A of the Culture Fair is 0.76 ( _N_ Å 3999). The squared _g_ correlation reflects the proportion of variance attributable to _g._ Based on a number of factor analyses summarized in the manual, the squared _g_ correlation for Form A of the Culture Fair is .66. The difference between these two values (0.10) reflects reliable test variance _not_ related to _g,_ associated for example with the specific use of spatial materials. Such a small proportion of variance, however, could be responsible for only trivial correlations with other scores. In accordance with standard use of single ‘‘intelligence tests,’’ it seems safe to attribute large correlations with the Culture Fair to its _g_ component. 

## EXPERIMENT 1 

In Experiment 1 we used a visual analog of the dichotic listening test. As in the original test there were two streams of stimuli, which this time were sequences of letters and digits presented to left and right of a fixation point, and again the task was to watch for targets (letters) on one side or the other. In developing and piloting the task we had several concerns in mind. 

First, we wished to replicate conditions under which some subjects would neglect a task requirement even when it could be shown that this requirement

had not been forgotten. Since in the dichotic listening test the part most commonly neglected was a switch of sides in response to a symbolic cue near the end of the trial, we decided to focus on this. We again used a symbolic cue, and tried to ensure that other aspects of the task—repeating targets and ignoring nontargets, beginning the trial on the correct side— would be carried out correctly. 

Second, we wished to produce a task that subjectively called for close attention but was actually possible for all subjects. In the dichotic listening task, stimuli were presented fairly rapidly (2 words/second), but after sufficient prompting, no subject was incapable of obeying the instructions. After pilot work we settled on a rate of 400 ms/item for the visual task. 

## _Method_ 

The experiment was carried out as part of a wider study dealing with a variety of different skills. Each subject attended for two sessions of about 2 h each. Only the letter monitoring (15 min) and Culture Fair (20 min) tests are considered here; for details of other tests see Duncan, Williams, Nimmo-Smith, and Brown (1993). 

_Letter monitoring._ For this task stimuli were presented on a black and white video monitor under the control of an APPLE II microcomputer fitted with special-purpose interrupt and clock boards. Letters—all uppercase—and numbers were presented in the standard font, each character measuring about 0.5 1 0.3 cm. The monitor was placed at a comfortable viewing distance on a desk in a normally lighted room. 

Stimuli from a typical trial are illustrated in Fig. A-1 (Appendix 1). Each trial began with the word READY presented in the middle of the screen. When the experimenter pressed a key, this word disappeared and, after a blank interval of 500 ms, was replaced by the instruction WATCH RIGHT or WATCH LEFT, also written in the middle of the screen. This remained for 1 s, and after a further blank interval of 1 s the stimulus sequence began. This sequence was a series of 200-ms frames, each separated from the next by a 200-ms blank interval. Each frame consisted of a pair of letters or digits presented side by side in the middle of the screen, one to the left and one to the right of center. Separation between the two was about 0.85 cm center-to-center. Ten frames appeared in turn, and then, after the tenth, a / or 0 symbol of similar size to the characters was presented on its own, midway between left and right character positions, i.e., in the exact center of the display. It too was presented for 200 ms and separated by 200-ms blank intervals from preceding and following frames, so that the rhythm of the presentation was undisturbed. Following the symbol came three more character-pair frames to complete the trial. 

Of the 10 pairs of characters, a randomly selected 5 were letter pairs while the rest were digit pairs. The subject was asked to watch for letters on the side indicated by the initial instruction, reading them aloud as they occurred. Letters on the incorrect side were to be ignored. The / or 0 symbol indicated which side to watch for the last three frames, / indicating right and 0 left. We call this symbol the second side instruction or SSI. On half the trials (WATCH RIGHT followed by / and WATCH LEFT followed by 0) the subject was to stay with the initial side for the last part of the trial, while on half (WATCH RIGHT followed by 0 and WATCH LEFT followed by /) he or she was to switch sides. Of the last three frames, the first always contained digits and the other two letters.

## _Discussion_ 

The phenomenon of goal neglect, suggested by our original observations in the dichotic listening task, was replicated in the letter monitoring task in Experiment 1. Though all subjects understood and remembered how they should use the SSI, 15/90 neglected this aspect of the task throughout block 1 and sometimes for longer. Indeed, the evidence suggests that, without some form of verbal prompt, neglect would have continued indefinitely; by subblock 1c there was scarcely any spontaneous remission. On the face of it, such findings are strongly reminiscent of the goal neglect sometimes shown by frontal patients; though understood, a task requirement exerted no apparent control over behavior. 

It is important that, for many subjects at least, neglect did not indicate that the task was impossible. As soon as a verbal prompt was given—in some cases, even just one request to repeat the rules—complete neglect was often replaced by almost immediate and complete success. In Experiments 2 and 3, indeed, we show that some forms of prompting are 100% successful. The effectiveness of prompts shows that, for neglecting subjects, the difficulty lay not in actually responding to the SSI, but in using initial verbal instructions to set up this aspect of task performance. As a result, either the SSI was not noticed at all or it was noticed but disregarded. Later we show that both can happen. 

As we have said, when a subject is given a set of task instructions, those parts which specify task requirements must be turned into active goals. Subjectively, this may correspond to the distinction between simply understanding a set of instructions and actively bearing in mind each task requirement as something to be achieved. The data suggest that, under the conditions of our task, such a process of goal activation fails in some subjects, even though the verbal description of task requirements has been understood. The relevant task requirement ‘‘slips the subject’s mind,’’ but can be activated by a further verbal prompt. 

It is worth noting that, even though most and perhaps all subjects were actually capable of carrying out the task, the presentation rate of 400 ms/item did make the task subjectively demanding, at least on the first few trials. Quite possibly this element of subjective demand is an important condition for encouraging goal neglect; for example, concentrating closely on the stimulus

stream might encourage the SSI to be ‘‘forgotten.’’ Though this is an important question, we shall not pursue it further in these experiments. 

A second important was that goal neglect in our task was to novel behavior, and specifically, behavior before the first passed sub-block. For each subject, performance took the form of 0 . . . _n_ failed sub-blocks followed by an immediate transition to almost perfect success. Once the appropriate task requirement became active it remained in force for the rest of the experiment. 

Both the effects of verbal prompts and the importance of novelty link the goal neglect shown here to standard distinctions between active/controlled/ voluntary and passive/automatic/stimulus-driven behavior. The results fit well with a model in which a requirement on behavior is likely to be lost only if environmental inputs and/or prior experience provide insufficient bias in its favor. 

Beyond establishing goal neglect in our task, Experiment 1 provided some evidence on its relationship to _g._ Neglect was very common in people scoring more than 1 SD below the mean on the Culture Fair test and absent in people scoring more than 1 SD above the mean. Since this conclusion depends on the results of rather few subjects, however, it is only preliminary. A replication was attempted in Experiment 2. 

## EXPERIMENT 2 

Experiment 2 was motivated by four concerns. First, we wished to replicate the relationship between goal neglect and _g_ that was suggested in Experiment 1. In particular, this relationship depended strongly on the performance of just a few subjects whose Culture Fair IQs were more than 1 SD below the mean of the published norms (Fig. 3). Thus more data were needed from people with low Culture Fair IQs. Since scores on fluid intelligence tests decline substantially in old age (Cattell, 1971), we tested a sample of people ages 60–70. 

Second, we were concerned that results in Experiment 1 might have depended partly on the wide range of subjects’ ages. Though partialing out age left the correlation between goal neglect and _g_ virtually unchanged, it seemed wise to test a group whose age range was more restricted. 

Third, we wished to obtain more information on the sensitivity of goal neglect to different kinds of verbal and nonverbal prompt. For subjects who showed neglect of the SSI in block 1, three different kinds of subsequent prompts were used. Half the subjects, as before, simply repeated the //0 rule at the end of block 1. The other half did not repeat the rule at this point; instead in block 2 large arrows appeared simultaneously with the SSI, pointing in toward it. Finally, if neglect was still apparent by block 3, subjects were given explicit verbal feedback whenever they reported letters from the wrong side. We hoped to show that, when prompting was sufficient, _all_ subjects were actually capable of doing the task correctly.

Fourth, in the normal, young population there is of course a substantial correlation between tests like the Culture Fair and tests of verbal rather than nonverbal IQ. As the problem we are investigating concerns use of verbal instructions, it seems possible that Culture Fair score predicts goal neglect only because it also predicts verbal ability. This hypothesis is easily tested with an elderly sample, since with advancing age, declines in fluid intelligence are accompanied by little change in tests of knowledge or crystallized intelligence, including verbal knowledge tests (Cattell, 1971). Thus in the elderly group fluid and crystallized abilities are dissociated; to take advantage of this, we administered standard tests of vocabulary and verbal comprehension in addition to the Culture Fair. 

As a matter of fact, the effects of advancing age themselves add support to the hypothesis of a link between _g_ and frontal lobe function. For many tasks there is little effect of age once changes in fluid intelligence have been partialed out (e.g., Rabbitt, 1993). Similarly, it has often been suggested that frontal dysfunction contributes substantially to the cognitive deficits of the elderly, on both neuropsychological and neuroanatomical grounds (e.g., Mittenberg, Seidenberg, O’Leary, & DiGiulio, 1989). For present purposes, however, we tested an elderly sample simply to obtain a group whose Culture Fair IQs were low but whose verbal IQs were not.

## _Discussion_ 

Though the results from Experiment 1 suggested a close relationship between _g_ and goal neglect, this conclusion rested strongly on the performance of a few subjects with low _g_ scores. Most importantly, therefore, Experiment 2 confirmed the results with a sample biased toward this lower range of _g_ and restricted, furthermore, in age range. It seems safe to conclude that goal neglect in the letter monitoring task is strongly characteristic of people from the lower part of the _g_ distribution. 

It is also important that, in an elderly sample, goal neglect in letter monitoring was predicted by a measure of current problem-solving ability, not verbal knowledge. As abilities change with advancing age, it is the current level of fluid intelligence—not knowledge acquired earlier in life—that predicts the efficiency of goal activation. 

While both arrow prompts and a request to repeat the rules were only marginally effective in this experiment, explicit, trial-by-trial verbal feedback always eliminated neglect of the SSI. Evidently even these elderly subjects were all actually capable of detecting and using the SSI in the time available. As we should expect, the results confirm that repeated, explicit verbal instructions are a potent cue to goal activation. 

Once again, performance took the form of a series of 0 . . . _n_ trials on which the SSI was entirely neglected, followed by a rapid transition to almost perfect success. Again it seems that, at least in this task, neglect is a phenomenon largely limited to trials before a requirement has first been satisfied.

## EXPERIMENT 3 

As we have said, our results are consistent with the common view that both frontal deficits and individual differences in _g_ are especially concerned with behavior that is subjectively active, controlled or voluntary. One of the cardinal features of such behavior is sensitivity to dual task interference (James, 1890; Fitts & Posner, 1967; Schneider & Shiffrin, 1977). To investigate this in the next experiment, we added a further stimulus and requirement to the letter monitoring task. Either once or twice during each trial a dot was briefly presented above or below the stream of letters and digits, and the subject was asked to respond by pressing one of two alternative keys as quickly as possible. Subjects were normal people in early middle age. 

First, we hoped to increase neglect of the SSI, so that it would not be entirely limited to people toward the bottom of the range of _g._ Increasing the frequency of neglect might also increase its _g_ correlation, even in a nonelderly sample. 

Second and more important, we hoped to observe neglect not only of the SSI, but perhaps of the dot as well. In other words we hoped to see a series of 0 . . . _n_ trials on which the dot was ignored, followed by a rapid resolution to consistent success. Repeating our findings with a different task requirement would be important for several reasons. For example, it would show that neglect is not limited to cases in which a symbolic stimulus-response mapping must be retrieved from memory or to cases in which there is a strict time window for successful performance. 

It will be remembered that, in the letter monitoring task, the three basic requirements—to repeat letters and ignore digits, to begin on the correct side, and to respond to the SSI—were described in turn, and that it was the third that some subjects neglected. ## _Discussion_ 

Experiment 3 dealt with a form of dual task interference: the number of goals or task requirements borne concurrently in mind. To this end we added the new dot task and manipulated order of instructions. 

In regard to letter monitoring, the effect of the added dot task was to increase both neglect of the SSI and its correlation with Culture Fair IQ. To give some scale to the obtained correlation (.55), one should remember restriction of IQ range in our sample, unreliability in the Culture Fair test itself, and the very different distributions of the two variables. Taken together, the results from Experiments 1–3 show that neglect in the letter monitoring task is very closely related to scores on a standard problem-solving test of _g._ 

Neglect of the dot task was also observed. Again there was a series of 0 . . . _n_ trials in which this aspect of task requirements was ignored, followed by a rapid resolution to good performance, and again neglect that continued to the end of block 1 was rapidly eliminated by verbal prompting in block 2. These results suggest that, under the right conditions, goal neglect may be a fairly general phenomenon. In particular they show that it is not limited to use of symbolic cues or to cases in which information must be used very rapidly. As we have said, responses outside the formal 3-s latency window were in fact almost absent. This—along with subjects’ spontaneous comments and answers to questions—shows that we are not dealing with any simple inability to respond sufficiently quickly. 

For the dot task, order of instructions was a potent variable. This task requirement was rarely neglected when it was described first. In contrast, neglect was common, and closely related to _g,_ when the dot task was described last. The results show that a major factor in goal neglect is the number of requirements borne concurrently in mind. A new requirement is likely to be neglected only when several others have already been activated. 

In retrospect it is perhaps not surprising that the order of instructions had no effect on neglect of the SSI. This part of the letter monitoring requirements is in any case described after a good deal of prior material (see Appendix 1); the situation is really little different when the brief dot instructions are also added at the beginning. At one level of abstraction we have said that there are three requirements in the letter monitoring task (repeat letters and ignore

For the dot task, the manipulation of order was much stronger—this task was described either first or after a good deal of prior material. 

## EXPERIMENT 4 

Experiments 1 to 3 establish the letter monitoring task as a useful behavioral model of goal neglect in the normal population. Though a task requirement has been understood, it exerts no control over behavior. In line with our general account of action control by competition between momentary candidate goals, neglect is restricted to novel behavior, rapidly resolved by explicit prompts drawing attention to the neglected requirement, and sensitive to the number of competing requirements. As we predicted, it is largely restricted to those in the lower part of the _g_ distribution. The first part of our hypothesis is thus supported: A simple failure to activate an appropriate task requirement, even when that requirement is explicitly described in verbal instructions, is closely related to _g._ 

Experiment 4 dealt with the effects of frontal lesions. On the face of it, neglect in the letter monitoring task resembles the bizarre mismatches between knowledge and behavior that have occasionally been reported for frontal patients. Indeed, this was the basis for our initial interest in the problem. Differences between our task and the usual clinical reports should also be considered, however. In particular there is the question of separation in time. When a patient says, ‘‘I must squeeze my hand,’’ yet fails to do so, the mismatching behavior and verbal description occur almost simultaneously. In our task, in contrast, verbal account and neglect in behavior are produced at quite different times and in quite different contexts. 

Quite possibly, a temporal separation between verbal account and behavior simply increases a task’s sensitivity to any goal activation deficit. In this case, the letter monitoring task should be extremely sensitive to frontal lesions. Experiment 4 was a test of this prediction. 

Two groups of frontal patients were tested. First, we tested three patients with restricted frontal lesions. These patients are useful because of the relative purity of their lesions. As a backup, we added seven more patients with manifest ‘‘executive’’ difficulties following closed head injuries. Though the lesions produced by closed head injury are sometimes diffuse, they often have a major frontal component, and we selected patients whose frontal lesions were substantial. The head injured group are useful also because their cognitive deficits often have much in common with the executive deficits of purer frontal cases (e.g., Vilkki, Holst, Ohman, Servo, & Heiskanen, 1992). Indeed, they are often used as the most common model of such deficits (e.g., Baddeley & Wilson, 1988; Shallice & Burgess, 1991). In the event, we found little difference between the two frontal patient groups.

## _Discussion_ 

Experiment 4 showed that, under the conditions of the letter monitoring task, goal neglect is strongly characteristic of frontal patients. Indeed, the consistency of impairment is extremely striking, given the notorious variability of deficits in this group (e.g., Shallice, 1982). Admittedly we chose patients thought on clinical grounds to suffer from an executive deficit. The results suggest, nevertheless, that the goal neglect problem we have related to _g_ is strongly characteristic of frontal patients. 

As our hypothesis predicts, the neglect of frontal patients was closely similar to that of normal people from the lower part of the _g_ distribution. Again it was largely confined to behavior before the first correct sub-block and sensitive to verbal prompts drawing attention to the neglected task requirement. The data also directly confirmed a deficit in Culture Fair IQ in these patients. 

293 

INTELLIGENCE AND FRONTAL LOBE 

As in other studies, patients whose executive from relatively pure frontal lesions were very similar to those with frontal head injuries. For both groups, neglect of the SSI was predominant. 

In all probability, the neglect obtained here is closely related to other reports of mismatch between knowledge and behavior in frontal patients. The conditions of our task may simply be very favorable to such a mismatch. In all such cases it seems that, unsupported by further prompting, verbal knowledge can be insufficient to bring the requirement it specifies into active control of behavior. 

Finally, the data showed neither goal neglect nor Culture Fair in patients with posterior lesions. Both deficits are specifically associated with frontal lesions and an associated executive disorder. 

## GENERAL DISCUSSION 

These experiments were motivated by a hypothesis linking Spearman’s _g_ to the functions of the frontal lobes. In both cases we suggested that the key function is shaping of behavior by activation of action requirements or goals specified at multiple levels of abstraction. As a simple behavioral model of this function, we investigated disregard of a task requirement even though it has been understood. 

The results gave clear support to our hypothesis. Under the conditions of our experiments, a simple failure to satisfy an explicitly stated task requirement was closely related to _g_ in the normal population. In frontal patients, it was the predominant result. 

Both of these results are fairly remarkable. Though problem-solving is often thought to lie at the heart of ‘‘intelligence,’’ there was no strong element of problem-solving in our tasks. Subjects had simply to remember requirements they had been told explicitly to satisfy and to bring them into control of behavior on receipt of a simple cue. Viewed as a single item, however, goal neglect in our tasks was more closely related to _g_ than comparable items from the Culture Fair test itself. Such results suggest that the simple goal activation function involved in our experiments is a central element of Spearman’s _g._ 

Similarly, it is notoriously to any single test that will reliably be failed by frontal patients (Shallice, 1982). Goal neglect, however, seems extremely sensitive to their deficits. Again, the results imply that the simple goal activation function is a central element of frontal impairment. Along with the results of Duncan et al. (1995), the findings show a close link between frontal lobe function and Spearman’s _g._ 

A further purpose of these studies was to some of the conditions under which goal activation runs into difficulties. We suggested it may be useful to consider goal selection as a competition between many momentarily activated candidates. Our experiments suggest that such a competition is strongly influenced by the novelty of required behavior, by environmental 

294 

DUNCAN ET AL. 

cues or prompts, and by the number of concurrent concerns (cf. Norman & Shallice, 1980). The following sections consider each factor in turn. 

## _Novelty_ 

It has often been suggested that both _g_ and frontal functions are especially important in novel behavior. Our findings were in general agreement with this view. Under the conditions of our tasks, goal neglect was restricted to behavior before the first correct trial. The data suggest that, once a requirement first assumes control of behavior, it remains in force for the remainder of the task. 

The speed of the transition between total neglect and almost perfect performance was extremely striking. The data seem closely related to the concept of perseveration in frontal patients: once an action has been carried out, patients can have trouble inhibiting it (e.g., Luria, 1966). As pointed out by Norman and Shallice (1980), the data suggest a strong tendency for any activated action or action requirement to persist. 

An important consideration may be the following.[4] Consider again that an instruction specifies only an abstract _requirement_ on behavior—that certain letters be reported, certain keys be depressed, etc. To be satisfied, this requirement must be the impetus for unpacking into a detailed structure of more specific requirements or subgoals—leading ultimately to the specification of detailed motor commands. Very likely, this unpacking is achieved only when, for the first time, the abstract requirement specified in the instructions is actually satisfied—in short, on the first successful trial. Actual satisfaction of the requirement specified in instructions may be the impetus that drives construction of a complete structure of subgoals; perhaps it is this whole structure which, once constructed, is so stable. 

It does seem likely that, under other conditions, an activated task requirement could subsequently lose control over behavior. This might be the result, for example, of longer delays between trials, or interspersed irrelevant activity. Under these circumstances it might be possible to observe goal neglect even after initial success. With brief trials that are all broadly similar, however, a requirement that has once been satisfied is not subsequently neglected. 

## _Prompts_ 

As we should expect, activation of a task requirement was strongly dependent on the strength of environmental prompts. In particular, trial-by-trial feedback drawing attention to a neglected requirement almost always caused neglect to resolve. Again this is consistent with the general view that both frontal patients and people from the lower part of the _g_ distribution have

Though we have emphasized the verbal instruction, it is interesting to ask how this instruction combines with stimulus events themselves to produce an overall impetus to action. In this regard a contrast between neglect in letter monitoring and dot tasks may be informative. In letter monitoring, initial instructions seemed to be the key prompt to use of the SSI. If this task requirement was not satisfied by the second sub-block, neglect was likely to continue indefinitely, or at least until further prompts were introduced. Even subjects showing complete neglect often admitted that they had seen /s and 0s, but somehow ignored them. For the dot task, however, the picture was different. Spontaneous remission of neglect occurred throughout block 1, and subjects still neglecting at the end of the block almost always denied that dots had been seen. In this case, becoming aware of dots was apparently a sufficient cue in itself to ensure activation of the corresponding requirement. In both cases, it was the low _g_ subjects who showed neglect, suggesting that we are dealing with two different versions of a similar problem. It would be interesting to know when the imperative stimulus itself is a strong cue to the associated task requirement, as apparently it is in the dot task. One obvious consideration is that the stimulus-response mapping was very natural in this task. 

## _Number of Concurrent Concerns_ 

Dual task interference is often taken to be a hallmark of active/controlled/ voluntary behavior; in Experiment 3, we found neglect to depend on the number of currently active requirements. Our observations are closely related to the general idea of task ‘‘complexity.’’ The difficulties of both frontal patients and low _g_ normals might be expected to increase with task complexity (e.g., Marshalek et al., 1983; Walsh, 1978), in large part perhaps reflecting the number of separate requirements to be satisfied. 

Any system in which candidate requirements compete for control of behavior is likely to be sensitive to the number of winners that the situation demands. For example, if net activation in the system is fixed, activating one set of task requirements will leave less activation available for others. Our results suggest a form of primacy at least in responding to verbal instructions: requirements specified first are the most likely to be satisfied. 

## _Other Factors_ 

Quite possibly there are other factors which also the probability of goal neglect. As we said earlier, for example, time pressure in the letter monitoring task may have increased neglect; on the other hand, keeping short the delay between initial instructions and performance may have decreased it. 

The latter point brings up the relationship between goal neglect as demonstrated here and the problem of ‘‘prospective memory’’ (e.g., Kvavilashvili,

DUNCAN ET AL. 

1987). In the typical prospective memory experiment, subjects again ‘‘forget’’ to carry out an intention established earlier in the experiment. Usually, however, there is a delay of at least several minutes between the original instruction and the cue indicating that the intention should be satisfied. Compared to our tasks, furthermore, there is perhaps less emphasis on ability to keep multiple task requirements active simultaneously. It would be interesting to know how such factors affect the overall problem of ‘‘forgetting’’ appropriate behavior. 

## _The Paradox of Frontal Dysfunction_ 

A familiar paradox of frontal dysfunction is the patient who performs well in many laboratory tests, yet whose disorganization is crippling in the activities of daily life (e.g., Eslinger & Damasio, 1985; Shallice & Burgess, 1991). It is as if the simplified setting of a laboratory test somehow diminishes demands on frontal systems, concealing the deficit that actually exists. By appropriately constraining the situation the experimenter in a sense ‘‘acts as’’ the patient’s frontal lobes, as indeed is evident when a patient who is quite unable to carry out some complex activity succeeds when verbally led through it (Luria & Tsvetkova, 1964). 

In the laboratory it is often the rule to give strong verbal prompts to task requirements, to repeat these until performance is correct, to gather data over a long series of stereotyped trials, and to have only a modest set of concurrent task requirements. In all these respects, the activities of daily life may often be different: there are no explicit verbal prompts, no stereotyped repetition of closely similar ‘‘trials,’’ and sometimes multiple, concurrent concerns. That all three factors are important in making failures conspicuous helps explain how frontal patients often appear so normal in formal testing. Complementarily, the emphasis on novel behavior in our task, the number of concurrent requirements, and the avoidance of verbal feedback may all help to explain why this task is so sensitive to frontal dysfunction. 

## _Generality_ 

Almost all tasks show some positive correlation with _g,_ suggesting some contribution from the _g_ factor to successful performance (Spearman, 1927). Similarly, the disorganization of behavior following frontal lobe damage affects the widest possible variety of tasks (Luria, 1966), and indeed there may be no task that is completely unaffected by frontal lesions. In the domain of memory, for example, it has often been noted that frontal decrements are much less marked for the relatively passive task of recognition than for the more self-organized task of free recall; yet even recognition can show some frontal impairment (Shimamura, 1995; Warrington, 1984). In this respect conventional attempts to distinguish ‘‘frontal’’ from ‘‘non-

Of course, the general idea of action control by selection of successively more specific constraints or requirements could be applied to control of any task. Especially close to our concerns is the element of active search for appropriate strategies or lines of attack that is characteristic of first learning new tasks. Verbal instructions (e.g., to recall a list of words) specify task requirements at one level of abstraction; a ‘‘self-generated’’ strategy (e.g., to use semantic clustering or visual imagery) is a set of additional constraints on action, not specifically mentioned by the verbal input but helping to shape appropriate behavior. Activation of such unprompted constraints or subgoals may be one strong source of universal positive _g_ correlations and frontal impairments. 

A particularly interesting case is the kind of ‘‘problem-solving’’ task conventionally used as the purest measure of fluid intelligence. For the problem in Fig. 8, the solution must respect variations in shape, size, and angle of the enclosed diameter, making item 4 the correct choice (Carpenter, Just, & Shell, 1990). Very plausibly, all three of the factors we have shown to be important in goal neglect operate here too. First there is novelty: In problem-solving tasks of this sort, one typically does not have item after item of much the same sort, but rather a series of rather different items for each of which a substantially new set of requirements must be activated. Second there is prompting: In typical problem-solving, one does not have an explicit verbal description of detailed requirements or strategy; instead, these requirements must be worked out from the task materials. Third there is the issue of concurrent requirements. In part, at least, the problem shown in Fig. 8 is difficult because variations in three different attributes—shape, size, and angle—must be dealt with simultaneously (Carpenter et al., 1990). A problem with only one varying attribute (e.g., two small, empty circles at top left and right; one small, empty

## _Conclusion_ 

It has always been paradoxical that, despite impairments in ‘‘planning,’’ ‘‘problem-solving,’’ etc., frontal patients have supposedly intact ‘‘intelligence.’’ We have suggested that this conventional view is misleading; in particular, it is based on imperfect understanding of the psychometric concept of _g_ and the central role of fluid intelligence. 

In contrast to this conventional view, the simple task we have introduced suggests a close link between Spearman’s _g_ and frontal lobe function. Though it contains no strong element of problem-solving, the tendency to neglect a task requirement described explicitly in verbal instructions relates closely to _g._ In patients with severe frontal difficulties, this tendency is extreme. Key factors are novelty, weak feedback, and multiple concurrent concerns. Despite its simplicity, our task may capture a central function in both Spearman’s _g_ and frontal impairment.

## Taking the next step: cortical contributions to the control of locomotion Trevor Drew[1] and Daniel S Marigold[2] 

**==> picture [23 x 22] intentionally omitted <==**

The planning and execution of both discrete voluntary movements and visually guided locomotion depends on the contribution of multiple cortical areas. In this review, we discuss recent experiments that address the contribution of the posterior parietal cortex (PPC) and the motor cortex to the control of locomotion. The results from these experiments show that the PPC contributes to the planning of locomotion by providing an estimate of the position of an animal with respect to objects in its path. In contrast, the motor cortex contributes primarily to the execution of gait modifications by modulating the activity of groups of synergistic muscles active at different times during the gait cycle.

In more complex situations, for example hunting prey, the planning must take into account navigation through the environment as well as the relative movement of the hunter and the hunted to allow interception. 

The planning and execution of such precise locomotor movements undoubtedly involves the participation of several cortical and subcortical structures, in much the same way as has been illustrated in the literature available for the control of discrete, voluntary movements [1,2]. However, while there are multiple studies on the major cortical areas involved in discrete voluntary movements, only a very few studies have examined the cortical contributions to the planning and execution of locomotion at the single cell level, and these are restricted to the posterior parietal cortex (PPC) and the motor cortex. In the following sections we consider the contribution of these two cortical structures to locomotion with an emphasis on visually guided precision walking.

## Introduction 

Supraspinal structures are essential for adapting gait to the behavioural goals of an animal. As with other voluntary movements, gait modifications require both appropriate planning of the movement to be made as well as precise control over the execution of that movement. During locomotion over uneven or cluttered terrain an animal continually scans its path for possible hazards or obstacles (Figure 1a1). When a potential obstruction is found, the animal must then evaluate how it will negotiate that obstacle. It must determine its size, shape, and location. It must then determine how to modify foot or paw placement (Figure 1a2) to allow it to step over that obstacle while maintaining smooth forward progress and postural stability (Figure 1a3, 4). ## Contributions of PPC to planning of gait 

Studies by Beloozerova and Sirota [3] and from our laboratory [4] have shown that many cells in the PPC show substantial changes in discharge before and during gait modifications requiring visual guidance. These changes in activity are suggested to reflect the neural processes that are required to plan the gait modification, including the visual characteristics of the task environment [3,5]. In the experiments from our laboratory, in which cats modified their gait to step over an obstacle attached to a treadmill belt, we found a population of cells in the lateral bank of the caudal ansate sulcus (area 5b of the PPC) that altered their discharge activity 2–3 steps before the cat stepped over the obstacle [4]. This discharge could potentially signal both the spatial characteristics of the obstacle (size, shape and location) and, in our task, temporal information concerning its relative speed of advance. 

In many of these cells (72%), the increase in cell discharge was best related to the first (lead) limb to step over the obstacle, regardless of whether that limb was contralateral or ipsilateral to the recording site (Figure 1b). This limbindependence raises the important question of whether this signal represents a motor plan, i.e., the intention to step over the obstacle, or whether it is a sensory signal that encodes the characteristics of the approaching obstacle.

More specifically, the PPC may use the information concerning the distance of the obstacle from the body, or the time to contact, to plan paw placement and initiate the gait modification (Figure 1d). 

Further support for the view that cells in the PPC are involved in planning movement, based on an estimation of the relative location of the body and limbs with that of an object, comes from an analysis of cells that discharged in the period between the step over the obstacle with the forelimb and that of the hindlimb (Figure 2a) [10]. Such cells are particularly interesting as they discharged despite the fact that the obstacle was no longer visible to the cat as it passed under the body. When the passage of the hindlimb over the obstacle was delayed, by decreasing 

the speed of the obstacle with respect to the speed of the cat, the duration of the neuronal discharge was equally increased, maintaining its relationship to the hindlimb muscle during the step over the obstacle. In many cells, this relationship to the lead hindlimb was maintained even when changes in the relative speed of the obstacle forced the cat to inverse the order in which the hindlimbs stepped over the obstacle [6,10]. These results indicate that the PPC contributes to the flexible coordination of the forelimb and hindlimb when their activity is not obligatorily coupled as it is during unobstructed treadmill locomotion. Cells with similar properties might equally control the visually guided coordination of forelimbs and hindlimbs required by non-human primates as they climb and move about in trees (i.e., arboreal locomotion).

McVea and Pearson [11] studied a similar issue of forelimb–hindlimb coordination in a task in which the forward progress of a cat stepping over an obstacle was temporarily interrupted so that the forelimbs and hindlimbs straddled the obstacle (Figure 2b). The obstacle was then removed without the cat’s knowledge. When the cat was allowed to continue its progress, it produced the appropriate modification of hindlimb trajectory required to step over the now absent obstacle. This step was adjusted to the height of the absent obstacle, indicating that it was not simply a default movement. Thus, the cat maintained a representation of the obstacle’s size and shape that allowed it to guide the movement required to step over it. This representation was maintained for periods up to 10 min. However, after lesions of the PPC cats were only able to store the height of the obstacle for periods of �5 s and the trajectory of the subsequent step was not significantly different from unobstructed steps (Figure 2b) [12]. Neuronal recordings have provided further evidence that the PPC might integrate obstacle representations with a state estimate of the body and limbs. For instance, cells in the PPC that discharged between the passage of the forelimbs and the hindlimbs over the obstacle during treadmill locomotion demonstrated sustained discharge for periods of 1–2 min (maximum examined) when forward progress was prevented after the cats had straddled an obstacle (Figure 2c) [10]. As this discharge ended at the same time as the hindlimb stepped over the obstacle it might equally contribute to the initiation of the subsequent hindlimb movement. The possible contribution of the PPC to integrating and storing obstacle properties when visual information is no longer available is summarized in Figure 2d. 

The available data suggest that, at least in general terms, the results obtained in cats pertain equally to locomotor control in humans. An early study using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), for example, showed activation of the PPC during imagined walking with obstacles [13]. Measuring brain activity during real locomotion is impossible with fMRI, but recent advances in more mobile imaging techniques now provide the potential to address specific questions about the cortical control of locomotion. For example, Gwin et al. [14] used highdensity EEG recordings to demonstrate spectral power fluctuations in the PPC that were modulated with the step cycle. In addition, a recent study in subjects performing a virtual reality walking task revealed modified spectral power in the PPC when visual guidance was important to the task [15[�] ]. The finding that visually guided foot movements were impaired in a subject with a cortical infarct that affected large portions of the parietal cortex [16] is also compatible with a contribution of the PPC to visually guided locomotion. Future studies in human subjects need to take advantage of the high resolution of modern techniques to better study issues such as effector independence in the PPC, for example, arm 

versus leg representations [17,18[�] ] as well as the temporal relationships of PPC activity to visually guided locomotor behaviours. 

## Contribution of the motor cortex to execution of gait modifications 

While the early onset of the PPC discharge prior to the step over the obstacle speaks strongly to a contribution to planning, most cells in the motor cortex discharge primarily during the step over the obstacle, suggesting instead a contribution targeted more to the execution of the modified step. Studies in a variety of locomotor tasks have shown that motor cortical neurones, including those neurones that project towards the spinal cord via the pyramidal tract (pyramidal tract neurons, PTNs) significantly change the phase and/or magnitude of their discharge activity when gait has to be modified [19–23]. Furthermore, inactivation of the motor cortex leads to changes in limb trajectory and paw placement during gait modifications [22,24,25]. 

Whether the motor cortex specifically controls high-level parameters such as end-point and limb trajectory or whether instead it specifies the muscle activity responsible for those changes is an open question, as it is for more discrete voluntary movements [1]. Arguments in favour of a high-level control of accuracy, corresponding largely to end-point control, come from experiments by Beloozerova’s group. They have argued that the large changes in motor cortical discharge during tasks requiring precise control over paw placement [20,26,27] are incompatible with the small changes in kinematic and electromyographic (EMG) activity observed in these tasks as compared to control walking on a flat surface. 

In contrast, evidence from our laboratory suggests a direct motor cortical contribution to the changes in muscle activity required both to produce the necessary limb trajectory to step over obstacles attached to a moving treadmill belt and to control the subsequent paw placement. Recent work proposes that populations of PTNs control groups of synergistic muscles that are simultaneously active at different phases of the step cycle (Figure 3a) [28,29,30[�] ]. ## Contributions of the PPC and the motor cortex to locomotion: a larger perspective 

The data reviewed above illustrate the essential contributions of the PPC and the motor cortex to the planning and execution, respectively, of visually guided locomotion. The PPC determines the location of an object with respect to the estimated state of the body and as a result, 

determines how to modify gait and paw placement to ensure that the obstacle is safely negotiated, or avoided. The motor cortex, on the other hand, is involved in specifying the necessary pattern of muscle activity to make the appropriate changes in limb trajectory required to step over an obstacle.

The premotor cortex, basal ganglia and cerebellum, all contribute to the control of voluntary movement and are strongly interconnected with both the PPC and the motor cortex. While we have some information on the contribution of the cerebellum [46,47] to the planning and control of locomotion, the contribution of the basal ganglia and the premotor cortex to the control of locomotion, remains poorly understood and understudied. In addition to the circuits implicitly involved in planning and organisation, both the PPC and the motor cortex receive inputs from the sensorimotor cortex that would provide feedback signals from cutaneous and proprioceptive receptors in the limbs. In addition, the PPC receives input from visual extrastriate areas that is integrated with the peripheral afferent feedback information as well as with efference copy signals from structures such as the cerebellum. 

While this review has concentrated on the neural mechanisms involved in the planning of immediate motor modifications required to avoid or step over obstacles, animals must also plan how to navigate their future route in complex environments. Studies in both animals and human subjects suggest that the PPC also contributes to this latter function. For instance, cells in the medial PPC increase their activity at specific points along a route that a monkey navigates in a virtual reality building [48]. The discharge of such cells is highly dependent on context [49]. Work in rodents has also suggested a role for the PPC in different aspects of navigation [50,51]. In addition, imaging studies in humans performing navigation tasks have demonstrated increased activation in several PPC areas, including the precuneus (or medial PPC), and superior and inferior parietal lobules [52,53]. In support, damage to the PPC in humans can disrupt route navigation [54] and the ability to localize objects with respect to self, a disorder known as egocentric disorientation [55]. Together, the research on obstacle avoidance during locomotion in primarily the cat, and work on spatial navigation in the rat, monkey, and human, demonstrates converging functions for PPC related to movement in a cluttered environment. Specifically, PPC contributes to the formation of egocentric spatial representations and to motor planning, and thus plays a critical role in relating objects (i.e., obstacles, landmarks, route features) to the state of the subject’s body and limbs. It may do this by integrating allocentric and egocentric reference frame based information from more distant areas, including the medial entorhinal and retrosplenial cortices (Figure 4) [56,57]. 

## Conclusions 

The literature summarized in this review demonstrates the critical contribution of the PPC and the motor cortex 

to the planning and execution of locomotion. An emphasis is placed on the particular importance of the PPC in providing a dynamic estimation of the relative location of the body and limbs with respect to objects in the environment, and to the planning of the gait modifications required to negotiate those obstacles. Equally, we emphasise in Figure 4 the central importance of the PPC in integrating information from a variety of different structures that provide information on diverse aspects of the interaction between an animal (the actor) and its environment. At the same time, we must also underscore the relative lack of information on the contribution of several cortical and subcortical structures to the control of visually guided locomotion despite the presence of such information for the control of discrete voluntary movements. A richer understanding of the functions of other structures in complex and natural locomotor behaviours is essential, particularly when considering the important locomotor deficits observed in patients with cortical or subcortical pathologies. Determining how different cortical and subcortical structures contribute to complex locomotor behaviours in healthy subjects, and how insult to them impairs locomotion, remains an important challenge for the future. In this respect, it is important to emphasise the extent to which the data obtained in single unit recordings from behaving animals complements that obtained with the more indirect studies used in human subjects.

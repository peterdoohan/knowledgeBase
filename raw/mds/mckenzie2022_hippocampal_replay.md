## Once is enough for hippocampal replay 

## Sam McKenzie[1][,] * 

1Department of Neurosciences, University of New Mexico School of Medicine, Albuquerque, NM 87131, USA 

*Correspondence: samckenzie@salud.unm.edu 

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2022.04.029 

In this issue of Neuron, Berners-Lee et al. (2022) reveal how neural dynamics in the hippocampus change after a single experience, offering a candidate mechanism for how hippocampal plasticity supports episodic memory. 

Despite lives immersed in change, we manage to extract and generalize commonalities to make sense of the causal structure of our world. To do so, we must relate memories of moments separated in time, each lived once only. How does the brain accommodate such ‘‘one trial learning,’’ and how can new information be assimilated when the core aspects of an experience are repeated? These problems are not simple, for, as Louis MacNiece wrote in Variation on Heraclitus, ‘‘One cannot live in the same room twice.’’ 

Berners-Lee and colleagues tackle these profound questions in their most recent article on how hippocampal dynamics change after rats explore a new physical space (Berners-Lee et al., 2022). They targeted the hippocampus due to this region’s critical role for the encoding, storage, and retrieval of episodic memories, those that form the record of our lived experience at a past time and place. Electrophysiological studies have shown that, among other world variables, hippocampal firing correlates with the location of the subject, such that different groups of neurons show place field activity in different locations. Moving across a room causes a sequence of neural ensembles to be active, because different neurons in different locations and 

because the traversal of space must unfold over time. These neural sequences are somehow learned and later ‘‘replayed’’ during sharp wave ripples (SPW-Rs; Wilson and McNaughton, 1994), which are oscillatory population bursts generated by the intrinsic circuitry of area CA1 (Buzsaki, 2015). 

One challenge in studying the neural basis for episodic memory is that these are the memories of events that take place only once. Neurons are noisy, and often animals must be trained to do the same behavior with tens and sometimes hundreds of repetitions to extract meaningful neural correlates. Given these constraints, several strategies were needed to study how a single experience could leave its mark on neural dynamics. 

Absent the ability to study trial-averaged neural activity, Berners-Lee et al. adopted the high-density tetrode recordings that have powered much of the work from the Foster lab, allowing for the simultaneous recordings from over 200 CA1 neurons. Ensemble recordings were then analyzed with a sophisticated set of statistical methods that were developed by the same group across several influential reports. These tools are designed to parse whether observed replay sequences differ from stochastic noise and 

to measure how replay structure is entrained to the oscillations in which the events are embedded. Finally, in a testament to the growing open science movement, this manuscript included data from seven prior studies (six from the Foster group, one from the Buzsaki lab). 

The critical observation made by Berners-Lee et al. was that rats needed to traverse a location just once for significant replay to emerge; somehow that single experience was sufficient to change the replay content for the next hour. The authors arrived at this conclusion through Bayesian decoding analysis of population bursts to reconstruct putative replay paths. By shuffling the spike timing, candidate replay events could be compared against random null distributions. Before track exposure, candidate events looked like the shuffled null across many possible definitions of which population bursts should count as a replay; there are many free parameters in this type of analysis, and such parameter sweeps are needed when we lack the ground truth for how a replay event should be defined. After a single track exposure, replay was confirmed across more of the parameter space used to define which spike patterns constitute a candidate event.

Just as re-reading a text can deepen understanding, re-exposure to the track continued changing the nature of replay. Strikingly, the events got longer. The elongation required experience and could not be explained with the mere passage of time: replays for events with little experience stayed short across an hour of rest; replays for events with more experience (>10 exposures) stayed long. Ripple events for which no replay could be detected were not affected by learning: these started long and stayed long. 

Replay events tend to be anchored by goals, and even with little experience, the early replays spanned the full track. Berners-Lee et al. confirmed prior observations (Pfeiffer and Foster, 2015) that replays evolve in a ‘‘hover and jump’’ fashion, with decoded position remaining constant for �12 ms before it transitions to a new position in discrete ‘‘steps.’’ Experience decreased the distance skipped from one hover point to the next and also increased the number of transitions necessary to get from the start to the end of the replay. Throughout learning, the early hover points persisted even as new hover points were added, as though experience afforded a finer-grain recollection of the minutia of the track that was always grounded in the initial impressions. 

How could the world imprint itself on the brain so quickly? The neural basis for this one-shot learning is unknown. Experiments from the Magee lab have shown that a single strong intracellular depolarization can trigger a plateau potential, which, in turn, can immediately and durably cause a neuron to fire in the spatial vicinity of where that current was injected (Bittner et al., 2017). It is possible that these plateau potentials underlie some of the first-time place fields, but intracellular recordings reveal that not all place fields in a new environment require a plateau potential for their birth (Epsztein et al., 2011). 

Another possibility is that much of the neural assembly membership, and even ordering, existed prior to the first-time experience. There are a growing number of reports describing ‘‘preplay,’’ the observation that there are hints of the pattern that will be observed during a first-time experience already present in the sleep before learning (Dragoi and To- 

negawa, 2011). Preplay is consistent with models wherein encoding occurs upon a backdrop of prior knowledge and the related existing networks are those recalled at the time of learning. Such a conclusion is far from consensus, and there is hot debate as to the existence of preplay, with conclusions hinging upon how to construct the right null distributions to capture the likelihood of observing a neural sequence by chance. According to their definitions and nulls, Berners-Lee et al. find no evidence for preplay, though the controversy is unlikely to be settled here or by any single report. 

As replays got longer, Berners-Lee et al. also observed more interneuron activity and speculated that perhaps interneuron plasticity drove some of the changes in the replay structure. Such a conclusion is consistent with a recent report that used optogenetic depolarization in CA1 to cause place field remapping that correlated with changes in interneuron firing and interneuron recruitment into SPW-Rs (McKenzie et al., 2021). From a mechanistic standpoint, interneurons are well positioned to play this potential role, as they are known to pace the ripple oscillation in which replays are embedded. Furthermore, without strong pyramidal-pyramidal connectivity, lateral inhibition is the dominant way in which CA1 pyramidal cells interact with one another. 

Longer replay events were also associated with longer-lasting prefrontal cortex activity. There have been previous reports that learning extends the duration of ripples and that experimentally elongating ripples through optogenetic depolarization improves memory performance (Fernandez-Ruiz et al., 2019). It remains to be seen whether interneuron plasticity underlies either the observed changes in replay structure or ripple duration. There is still debate around the physiological basis for the ripple oscillation, the mechanistic understanding of which may prove essential if we are to understand the synaptic basis for the learning-related changes in neural dynamics. 

Replay events seem important for hippocampal function, though that function is unknown. A broader generational debate is ongoing over the core computational role of the structure: does it repre- 

sent spatial relations, or is it a memory machine that allows for the storage and retrieval of episodic memories? Is the organization of the record of our personal experiences defined primarily (or solely) according to where those events happened, or can episodic memories be organized according to any type of relationship between one moment and the next? Within the complementary memory systems framework (McClelland et al., 1995), replay has been hypothesized to play a critical role as a teaching signal emitted from a fast-learning system (hippocampus) to a slow-learning system (neocortex), thereby allowing new information to be gradually interleaved into the long-term knowledge base. Studies like that of Berners-Lee et al. pave the way for future work that can test for the replay of episodic content of more complex experiences and to measure how unique episodes become related to an existing knowledge base, wherever that long-term storage locus may be.

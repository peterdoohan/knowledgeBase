Decisions can be reached in different ways. Sometimes they involve careful consideration of the expected outcome of our behavior. Other times, behavior is generated more automatically if a particular response has been repeatedly successful in the past. I review animal research into goaldirected and habit learning including common training paradigms and studies investigating the neural substrates of actions and habits. Further, I summarize the wide range of factors (e.g., drugs, stress, diet) that promote habitual control. Since habitual control is prevalent across a range of neuropsychiatric disorders it is important to be able to accurately identify when behavior is habitual and better understanding of the behavioral and neural determinants of habitual control may enable behavior change when needed.

Nonetheless, there has been a renewed interest in S-R learning; not as an all-encompassing explanation of instrumental learning, but as a means for explaining the development of response habits. It is now well established that even for behaviors that may start out as flexible or goaldirected, control can shift to a more automatic and relatively goal-insensitive system, that is, these behaviors become habits. 

The aim of this review is to discuss animal studies investigating the neural circuits that control habit learning and factors that promote habitual control. Understanding how habits and goal-directed actions are coordinated in the brain is essential for understanding decisionmaking in general as well as failures of behavioral control. Better understanding of the neural bases of pathological habits may aid development of better treatments. 

## Common paradigms 

There have been several empirical approaches to the study of habits. These methods have shaped how we think about habits and also how we define them operationally and so they are summarized briefly here. For more in-depth discussion, the interested reader is directed to several excellent reviews ([4,5], see [6[�] ], for human studies). 

## Outcome devaluation 

## Introduction 

Instrumental or operant conditioning involves learning about specific actions and the consequences they produce. This knowledge can be applied flexibly to produce outcomes that are desirable or to avoid those that are harmful or aversive [1,2]. While such a description seems intuitive; essentially that we choose how to respond with our intended goal in mind, instrumental learning has not always been thought of this way. For example, early explanations of instrumental or operant responding argued that an association between a particular situation (or stimulus) and a response is incrementally strengthened each time that response leads to a satisfying event [3]. Importantly, the satisfying event, that we might now refer to as the reward or outcome, is not itself encoded but serves only to strengthen the association between stimulus (S) and response (R). The S-R account of instrumental learning cannot explain numerous demonstrations that 

Goal-directed actions rely on their contingent relationship to, and the value of, their associated outcome. As such, responding is highly sensitive to changes in the value of that outcome. In contrast, habits rely on S-R associations and thus, are elicited by antecedent stimuli. They are not directly controlled by their outcome and so, when responding is habitual, manipulations of outcome value have no immediate effect of performance. By specifically manipulating outcome value and observing consequent effects on performance, the outcome devaluation test has become a standard assessment used to distinguish goal-directed behaviors from habits (see Figure 1).

The outcome devaluation task identifies goal-directed behaviors and distinguishes them from habits. Contemporary theories of instrumental learning identify two critical factors that determine goal-directed action. The first is the encoded contingency between an action and its specific consequences or outcome and the second is the current value of that outcome [1,2]. Integration of this information allows animals to flexibly modify performance when outcome value changes [2]. In contrast, habitual responding is elicited by stimuli that have gradually become associated with reinforced responses. Habits do not involve a representation of the specific outcome of responding. As such, when responding is habitual, changes in the value of the outcome have no immediate effect on the performance of the response [1]. By specifically manipulating outcome value and observing consequent effects on performance, the outcome devaluation test provides a means of identifying goal-directed and habitual behaviors. 1. In this test, subjects (rats or people) are first trained to perform a particular response such as a lever or button press to earn a particular reward (food, money, etc.). 2. Once stable responding is achieved, the value of that reward is manipulated, either by allowing that outcome to be consumed freely to produce outcome-specific satiety or by pairing consumption with illness. 3. Subjects are then given the opportunity to perform the previously trained response under extinction conditions (i.e. no rewards are delivered during the test) to assess whether performance is altered by the devaluation treatment relative to control groups or conditions. If performance is modified to reflect the new outcome value (i.e., reduced following devaluation), the behavior is considered to be goal-directed. Responding that is insensitive to changes in outcome value is interpreted as habitual. Previous research using this task has demonstrated that while behavior is initially goal-directed and highly sensitive to its consequences, this control can be reduced with extended training [7,17] or exposure to potent rewards such as drugs of abuse [36–41,42[�] ]. Thus, the very same behavior can be goal-directed at one point in time, but habitual at another and the type of control is only revealed by tests such as devaluation.

Rats readily learn to navigate mazes to locate rewards. In a plus or T maze, animals start in a particular location (say a south arm) and from there they must enter a different arm, say the west arm to find a food reward. In such a situation, the animals could use one of two strategies to solve the task. They could form a cognitive map and learn that reward is located in the west location and so travel there to find it. Alternatively, they could learn that a particular sequence of motor responses, ultimately turning left, leads to reward. To distinguish between these alternatives, in a probe trial, the animal is placed in a novel location, the north arm, and allowed to choose between arms from there. If they had learned to solve the maze using a so-called place strategy, they should use the available cues from the maze to navigate to the west arm. If they learned based on a so-called response strategy, they should perform the same response that led to reward in the past, that is, turn left delivering them now to the east arm. Early studies found that rats learned more readily about places than about the particular response sequence nonetheless, given sufficient training, rats come to rely on a response strategy [8].

## Balance between parallel corticostriatal circuits 

A strong literature using loss of function methods in animals and fMRI in humans has clearly established that goal-directed actions and response habits rely on distinct, parallel corticostriatal pathways that center on the dorsomedial (DMS) and dorsolateral (DLS) regions of the striatum and their inputs from the medial prefrontal cortex and sensorimotor cortices, respectively [4,8,9,13,14,16,17]. Recent work also points to the infralimbic cortex as an important contributor to habitual control [12,15]. Neural manipulations can uncover behavior that may not be dominant, for example, inactivation of the DLS after habitual responding is established can restore goal-directed responding [17,18] suggesting that some form of competition between activity in DMS and DLS circuits is likely to determine behavioral control. Nonetheless, remarkably little is known about precisely how these circuits interact to determine the nature of behavioral control under normal or pathological conditions. Several lines of research are making progress on this question. 

Electrophysiological recording studies have examined changes in firing patterns within the DLS and DMS 

while habits are acquired. Such studies have generally found that both regions are active during training but develop different patterns of activity over the course of extended training [10,19]. The DLS is most active at the initiation and termination of a trial thus showing a “taskbracketing” or “chunking” pattern of activity [10,19]. Interestingly, the task-bracketing pattern develops relatively early in training while animals are still sensitive to outcome value, remains stable with overtraining, and is undisturbed by devaluation even when animals run to a devalued goal [12]. Thus, this pattern appears to reflect efficient performance. On the other hand, DMS neurons are most active in the middle of a trial when animals have to choose between alternative responses according to instruction cues. This activity increases as the task is acquired, then decreases with overtraining [19]. The decrease in DMS activity may play a permissive role allowing the expression of habits, consistent with lesion or inactivation studies where habits emerge prematurely if DMS activity is removed [9,17]. 

The “chunking” pattern of DLS activity is particularly interesting considering that even outside a maze, a series of behaviors is typically required to ultimately produce a goal and one way to achieve efficiency is to link individual behaviors into a sequence. Once a sequence is formed, actions within the sequence are driven by the previous action rather than the ultimate outcome of the sequence

Extended training, particularly under schedules where each individual response is not reinforced, may promote not only task efficiency, but relative insensitivity to changes in outcome value if individual responses are components of a sequence or chain of responses. Notably, activity in striatal regions so critical for habit learning also corresponds to the formation of action sequences in free-operant responding [23,24]. Thus, sequence learning provides another way of thinking about response control, where responses cue subsequent responses not unlike stimuli trigger responses under the S-R framework. In either case, responding is not directly related to its outcome. 

Habits are adaptive because they promote behavioral efficiency however, to maintain adaptive responding, it is important to inhibit habits when environmental conditions change. Thus, another line of work has examined how behavior might shift back and forth between flexible and habitual control. Cannabinoid receptors may be important in this regard. The cannabinoid 1 (CB1) receptor, expressed broadly through the brain but within the striatum, preferentially in DLS, is involved in multiple forms of striatal plasticity including short-term and longterm depression [25]. While chronic exposure to cannabinoids, sufficient to produce tolerance, enhances habit formation, likely by promoting plasticity in DLS, acute administration of either agonists or antagonists have been shown to impair habit formation (see [26] for a recent review). This is perhaps not surprising considering that CB1 receptors are found on both glutamatergic and GABAergic terminals in DLS and optimal levels may be essential to learning-related plasticity. 

To produce a situation where animals could perform the same response in either a goal-directed or habitual manner, animals were trained to press a lever in two distinct environments or contexts [15,27,28,29[�] ]. In one context, the lever-press response was reinforced according to random ratio schedules and this response remained goal-directed (i.e., sensitive to outcome devaluation). In the other context, the lever-press response was reinforced according to variable interval schedules and by the end of training was habitual (i.e., insensitive to outcome devaluation). To investigate how dorsal striatal (DS) activity was modulated by the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) the OFC-DS projection was chemogenetically inhibited prior to devaluation testing. This impaired the expression of goal-directed responding. Thus, activity of OFC-DS neurons is necessary for goal-directed control. Selective deletion of CB1 receptors in this pathway, which would otherwise dampen a glutamatergic input to the DS, prevents the expression of habits [29[�] ]. Thus, inputs from the OFC contribute to the switching between response strategies. Whether this pathway is altered by overtraining or under the range of conditions that promote 

abnormal habitual control (e.g. following drug exposure) is unknown and it is likely that multiple inputs, as well as changes within the DS itself, contribute to performance. For example, other work points to entrainment to thetaband oscillations [30] and activity of different classes of interneurons [30–32] as being important for coordinating activity of, and control by, DMS and DLS circuits. 

## Pathological habits 

Under stable conditions habit learning is adaptive because it is efficient and frees cognitive resources for other tasks. Nevertheless, habits can be problematic if there is a need for behavioral change [34]; by their very nature habits are triggered by familiar stimuli and are insensitive to their consequences. 

A range of factors promote habitual control. The first is the amount of training; extended or “overtraining” of a particular response can lead to habitual control and this idea is imbedded within theoretical views regarding how habits are formed [1,3]. Another factor is the type of reinforcement schedule used in training. Training with lean variable interval schedules, where the correlation between the response and outcome delivery is relatively weak, more readily produces habits compared to training on ratio schedules [35], although the latter can eventually produce habitual control given sufficient training [17,36]. Finally, a range of experiences including drug or alcohol exposure [17,18,36–41,42[�] ], stress [43,44] or particular diets [45,46] promote habitual control. Such habits are referred to here as pathological because they form abnormally rapidly and often there is an implication that lack of flexible control over the outcome itself (such as drugs) is maladaptive for the individual although it is important to keep in mind that habits themselves can be quite adaptive [see [6[�] ] for a recent review and Box 1 for examples of habits in neuropsychological disorders]. 

Investigation of the ways in which things like drug exposure alter the neural circuits controlling actions and habits may also yield insights into how these circuits interact normally. As an example, alcohol consumption and exposure causes habitual control to emerge more quickly [17,40,41,47]. Electrophysiological studies have shown that acute alcohol exposure decreases inhibitory transmission onto the medium spiny neurons of the DLS effectively disinhibiting them [48]. Similarly, chronic alcohol decreases GABA release onto the medium spiny neuron (MSN; [48,49]), the output neuron of the striatum, and down regulates endocannabinoid-CB1 receptor signaling and CB1-dependent long-term depression [LTD; [50]]. Further, ethanol promotes LTD at MSN and fastspiking interneuron inputs onto MSNs within the DLS [33[�] ]. Collectively, it appears that reduced inhibition of DLS and consequently increased DLS output following alcohol exposure could favor habitual control. ## Outstanding questions 

Multiple examples of habitual control have been identified in recent years but important questions about when and why habits will dominate remain. 

When animals reduce responding following outcome devaluation this provides compelling evidence of goaldirected control. Lack of sensitivity to outcome devaluation, in turn, provides convincing evidence that a behavior is not directly controlled by its consequences. Nonetheless, such failures of goal-directed control do not directly assess the S-R learning that is thought to underlie habits. While a response, such as a lever press, can become habitual, the stimuli that support the S-R association in a free-operant paradigm are usually incidental and thus poorly defined. The stimuli could come from the environment or context in which the animal is trained [53,54,55[�] ,56]. Indeed, there is a decrement to instrumental performance in general if animals are transferred to a different context than where they were trained [56]. This decrement occurs across a range of training parameters (e.g., brief or extended training) some of which should favor goal-directed responding and others that should favor habits. A little-discussed finding in the literature is that outcome devaluation effects are seldom complete, that is, after effective devaluation where animals reject the outcome entirely, while responding is 

typically dramatically reduced, animals often still perform the ‘devalued’ response to some extent. This residual responding may reflect the S-R or habitual component of performance which would be expected to be quite small after limited training, but grow as training continues [57]. Bouton and colleagues have argued that this S-R component may be driven by context (i.e. Context-R; [56]). However, the magnitude of the performance decrement observed following a context shift does not appear to grow with training, as one would expect, and a context shift has only a modest impact on responding demonstrated to be habitual based on insensitivity to devaluation. Furthermore, when devaluation was combined with a context shift, which should suppress the goal-directed and habitual components of responding, respectively, some responding still remained. It is likely that context is only one of multiple stimuli that contribute to the S-R learning that underlies habits. There is some recent evidence of increased stimulus control following overtraining [58], however this area needs further attention. 

As described above, some studies have explicitly manipulated context, and used training parameters to produce goal-directed responding in one context but and habitual responding in another [15,27,28,29[�] ]. Such findings indicate that when context is manipulated during training, animals can use cues from their environment to guide responding. Another intriguing effect is that contexts paired with potent rewards can induce habitual responding in animals that are otherwise goal-directed. In these studies animals undergo contextual conditioning where one context is paired with drug or junk food while another serves as a control (e.g., paired with saline). Animals then undergo instrumental training in a third context. Sensitivity to devaluation is tested in the various contexts. When tested in the drug-paired context, animals are

In sum, contexts contribute in several interesting ways to habitual performance yet better characterization the stimuli that support habit learning, particularly in the free-operant situation where outcome devaluation is typically tested, would strengthen understanding of habits. Further, understanding how stimuli drive activity in striatal circuits to promote habitual responding will be essential. 

The accelerated habit learning following mere exposure to drug or stress suggests these events impact underlying neural circuitry to impact subsequent learning. While some advances have been made, notably in the alcohol field, many questions remain. For example, is there a common mechanism underlying the impact of drugs, stress, diet etc.? Will the system recover when drug, stress etc. is removed? 

A related point is that when premature or pathological habits emerge, it is often not clear whether this is due to direct strengthening of habit learning and/or, impairment in goal-directed learning and consequent compensation by, or unmasking of, the habit system. This question needs further investigation at both behavioral and neural levels. 

Finally, basic questions still remain about how these parallel systems interact normally to allow dynamic shifting between fast and efficient behavior while maintaining the capacity to flexibly adapt responding in an everchanging environment. Addressing these questions will be essential for understanding behavioral flexibility and control.

## CONNEXIN30 IN RODENT, CAT AND HUMAN BRAIN: SELECTIVE EXPRESSION IN GRAY MATTER ASTROCYTES, CO-LOCALIZATION WITH CONNEXIN43 AT GAP JUNCTIONS AND LATE DEVELOPMENTAL APPEARANCE 

## J. I. NAGY,* D. PATEL, P. A. Y. OCHALSKI and G. L. STELMACK 

Department of Physiology, Faculty of Medicine, University of Manitoba, 730 William Avenue, Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada, R3E 3J7 

**Abstract** ––We previously presented evidence [Nagy _et al_ . (1997) _Neuroscience_ **78,** 533–548] that, in addition to their ubiquitous expression of connexin43, astrocytes produce a second connexin suggested to be connexin30, a recently discovered member of the family of gap junction proteins. A connexin30 specific antibody was subsequently developed and utilized here to confirm and extend our earlier observations. On western blots, this antibody detected a 30,000 mol. wt protein in rat, mouse, cat and human brain, and exhibited no cross-reaction with connexin43, connexin26 or any other known connexins expressed in brain. Immunohistochemically, connexin30 was localized in astrocytes, at gap junctions between these cells and on the astrocyte side of gap junctions between astrocytes and oligodendrocytes. Double labelling revealed co-localization of connexin30 and connexin43 at astrocytic gap junctions. Punctate immunolabelling patterns for both connexins were qualitatively similar, but differences were evident. In contrast to regional connexin43 expression, diencephalic and hindbrain areas exhibited considerably greater expression than forebrain areas, subcortical perivascular astrocytic endfeet were more heavily labelled for connexin30, white matter tracts such as corpus callosum, internal capsule and anterior commissure were devoid of connexin30, and appreciable levels of connexin30 during development were not seen until about postnatal day 15. 

These results indicate that connexin30 is expressed by gray, but not white matter astrocytes, its distribution is highly heterogeneous in gray matter, it is co-localized with connexin43 at astrocytic gap junctions where it forms homotypic or heterotypic junctions, and its emergence is delayed until relatively late during brain maturation. Taken together, these results suggest that astrocytic connexin30 expression at both regional and cellular levels is subject to regulation in adult brain as well as during brain development. � 1998 IBRO. It is becoming evident that cells forming gap junctions often express more than one connexin[6,45] and that structural or other constraints govern the combinations of connexins that can form functional junctions.[4,5,13,15,38,44] These points suggest that the presence of several connexins in individual cells allow selective pathways of gap junctional coupling via regulation of connexin expression and/or differential control of channel permeability at apposing sides of junctions composed of more than a single connexin (i.e. heterotypic junctions). junctions between different neural cell types (heterologous junctions) that each produce more than a single connexin. 

Previously, we reported that astrocytes may express a second connexin in addition to connexin43.[27] We found that affinity-purified, sequencespecific polyclonal antibodies (designated 191A and 71-0500) against connexin26 as well as several monoclonal anti-connexin26 antibodies produced immunohistochemical labelling of astrocytic gap junctions. These antibodies exhibited no crossreaction with connexin43, indicating lack of connexin43 detection at gap junctions. These antibodies did, however, recognize a protein in CNS that migrated at about 30,000–32,000 mol. wt on western blots and that was clearly not connexin26. A subsequent report[9] revealed considerable sequence homology between connexin26 and a novel connexin designated connexin30. The sequence of connexin26 against which our antibody 191A was generated (amino acids 104–119; RKFMKGEIKNEFKDIE) closely matched a corresponding region in connexin30 (amino acids 104–119; RKFIRGEKRNE FKDLE). Based on this homology, we indicated in our report[27] that the approximately 30,000–32,000 mol. wt protein detected in CNS by our anticonnexin26 antibodies was likely connexin30 and that the immunoreactivity seen at astrocytic gap junctions with these antibodies likely reflected the presence of connexin30. To substantiate and extend these findings, we report here the distribution, gap junction localization, connexin43 co-localization and developmental expression of connexin30 in rat CNS as determined with a connexin30-specific antibody. We also examined the utility of this antibody for studies of connexin30 in mouse, cat and human brain. 

## **EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES** 

## _Antibodies and animals_ 

A synthetic peptide corresponding to a 20-amino acid sequence of connexin30[9] was used to generate a rabbit polyclonal antibody that was subsequently affinity-purified using the same peptide. The antibody will be made commercially available (Zymed Laboratories, South San Francisco, CA) and the sequence of the peptide against which it was generated is proprietary. An affinity-purified rabbit polyclonal antibody generated against a peptide represented in the cytoplasmic loop region of connexin26 was kindly provided by Zymed Laboratories (designated 71-0500). The characteristics of this antibody have been previously described.[27] A well characterized polyclonal antibody (designated 18A)[48,49] was used for single immunogold labelling for connexin43. A monoclonal anti-connexin43 antibody was used in double labelling studies. This affinity-purified antibody, generated against a peptide sequence of connexin43[3] and supplied by Zymed laboratories (designated 13-8300), has been reported to detect an unphosphorylated form of connexin43 and its specificity has been previously demonstrated by western blotting and antigen preadsorption procedures.[22,28] 

A total of 57 male Sprague–Dawley rats weighing 350– 500 g and six Holtzman mice weighing 25–30 g were used in 

this study. In addition, brain tissue was obtained from four cats that were subsequently used for other studies. Human brain tissue was obtained from the local pathology laboratory at 4 h and 24 h _post mortem_ for western blot and anatomical analyses, respectively. Rats and mice were obtained from the local Central Animal Care Services and utilized according to approved protocols by the Central Animal Care Committee with minimization of stress to, and numbers of, animals used. 

## _Western blots_ 

Rats and mice were killed by decapitation, brains and spinal cords were removed, placed on an ice-cold metal tray and dissected, or transversely sectioned at a thickness of 1 mm with the aid of a brain blocker and dissected for regional analysis. Some areas were taken to exclude leptomeninges as previously described[27] and leptomeningeal tissue was stripped from the surface of the cerebral cortex for separate analysis. Samples of heart and liver were also collected. For developmental studies, rat pups of different ages were decapitated, their brains removed and dissected into two separate samples for analysis. One included all forebrain structures rostral to the thalamus and the other contained thalamus and brainstem (midbrain, pons and medulla). These tissues as well as samples of human and cat cerebral cortex and cat brainstem were frozen at �70�C until further processing. For western blots using sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS– PAGE), tissues were homogenized in 1 mM sodium bicarbonate buffer (pH 7.4), with 1 µg/ml each of leupeptin and pepstatin and 1 mM phenylmethyl-sulfonyl flouride freshly added from a stock solution in absolute ethanol. The total protein concentration of homogenates was determined using a commercially available kit (Bio-Rad protein assay) with bovine serum albumin (BSA) as the standard. For SDS–PAGE, 1.25–40 µg of total protein was resolved on 12.5% gels and transferred to polyvinyl difluoride membranes in transfer buffer (25 mM Tris, 192 mM glycine and 20% v/v methanol) containing 0.05% SDS. Membranes were blocked for 2–3 h at room temperature in Tris-buffered saline containing Tween-20 (TBST; 20 mM Tris, pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl and 0.2% Tween-20) containing 5% skim milk powder and incubated with anti-connexin30 antibody diluted at 1:300 or antibody 71-0500 diluted 1:500 in TBST containing 1% skim milk powder. Incubations were conducted at room temperature for 3 h or overnight at 4�C. After washing in TBST (4�10 min), membranes were incubated for 1 h at room temperature with a horseradish peroxidase-conjugated anti-rabbit IgG (Sigma) diluted 1:5000 in the same solution as used for the primary antibodies. After the final wash in TBST (4�10 min), membranes were processed with an enhanced chemiluminescence kit (Amersham) for visualization of immunoreactive protein bands. Control procedures involving preadsorption of anticonnexin30 antibody with peptide antigen were conducted by incubation of antibody, diluted 1:100 in 50 mM sodium phosphate buffer, pH 7.4, (PB) containing 0.9% saline (PBS), with 20 µg of peptide at room temperature for 2 h. Transmission densitometry of western blots was conducted using a Bio-Rad model 620 video densitometer. Data was analysed with the Bio-Rad 1-D Analyst software.

## _Immunohistochemistry_ 

For light microscopy studies involving use of the peroxidase–antiperoxidase (PAP) method, brain sections (20 µm) obtained on a sliding microtome were collected free-floating in PBS, washed for 1–3 h at 4�C in PBS containing 0.3% Triton X-100 (PBST) and then incubated for 48 h at 4�C with anti-connexin30 antibody diluted 1:500 in PBST containing 2% normal goat serum (NGS). After a 1 h wash in PBST, sections were incubated for 1.5 h at room temperature with goat anti-rabbit IgG (Sternberger Monoclonal Inc.) diluted 1:100 in PBST containing 2% NGS. This was followed by a 1 h wash in PBST, and then by a 1.5 h incubation at room temperature with rabbit PAP (Sternberger Monoclonals Inc.) diluted 1:500 in PBST containing 2% NGS. The sections were then washed sequentially for 30 min in PBST and then 50 mM Tris–HCl buffer, pH7.4, and incubated for 10–15 min in the same Tris–HCl buffer containing 0.02% 3,3�-diaminobenzidine and 0.005% hydrogen peroxide and then washed in Tris–HCl buffer. Sections were mounted onto slides from 0.5% gelatin/30% alcohol, dehydrated in alcohol, cleared in xylene and coverslipped with Lipshaw mounting medium. Some sections were processed with anti-connexin30 antibody preadsorbed with peptide antigen as described above for Western blots. 

For double labelling by immunofluorescence, brain sections were cut at a thickness of 7 µm on a cryostat, collected on gelatinized slides and incubated for 16 h at 4�C simultaneously with anti-connexin30 and anti-connexin43 (138300) each diluted 1:500 in PBST. The sections were washed for 1 h in PBST and then incubated for 1.5 h at room temperature with fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (diluted 1:200 in PBST) for labelling of connexin30, and simultaneously with Cy3conjugated donkey anti-mouse IgG (diluted 1:200 in PBST) for labelling of connexin43. After washing for 20 min in PBST followed by 20 min in Tris–HCl buffer, the sections were air-dried and coverslipped with anti-fade medium.[41] In control procedures, omission of one or the other of the primary antibodies with inclusion of both of the secondary antibodies produced no inappropriate labelling (i.e. Cy3 labelling with rabbit primary or FITC labelling with monoclonal primary) indicating lack of false positive crossreactions with the antibodies used. Fluorescence was examined on a Leitz Dialux 20 fluorescence microscope using a 

L3 Ploemopak filter cube (excitation 450–490 nm; band pass 500–540 nm) for viewing FITC and a Ploemopak filter cube fitted with a XF37 filter set (excitation 541–551 nm; band pass 565–595 nm; Omega Optical, Brattleboro, Vermont) for viewing Cy3 (excitation max 552 nm; emission max 568–574 nm). The L3/XF37 combination gives negligible Cy3 fluorescence with L3 illumination and conversely no FITC fluorescence with XF37 illumination. As Cy3 gives considerable glare due apparently to its intense fluorescence, tests of several different excitation and emission filter sets revealed the XF37 to be optimal both for viewing and photography. 

Immunohistochemistry by electron microscopy was conducted essentially as previously described with some modifications.[27,30] For electron microscopy using the PAP method, tissue sections were cut at a thickness of 20–30 µm on a vibrating microtome (Leica). These sections were washed for at least 1 h in PBS containing 0.1% Photoflo (PBSF) (Kodak), then incubated with rabbit anticonnexin30 antibody diluted 1:500 in PBSF containing 2% NGS and processed by the PAP method as described above, except that 0.1% Photoflo was substituted for Triton X-100 in all incubations and washes. Following the 3,3�diaminobenzidine reaction step, sections were washed in PBS for a minimum of 1 h and processed for electron microscopy as previously described.[30] For single labelling with 5 nm gold particles, sliding microtome sections cut at a thickness of 20 µm were washed in PBS for 1 h, incubated for 72 h with either anti-connexin43 antibody 18A[49] or with anti-connexin30 antibody diluted 1:2000 and 1:500, respectively, in PBS containing 0.5% Photoflo and 2% NGS. Sections were then washed for 1 h in PBS containing 0.5% Photoflo and 1% BSA (PBSF/BSA) and incubated for 16 h at room temperature with 5 nm gold-conjugated goat antirabbit IgG (British BioCell) diluted 1:50 in PBSF/BSA containing 2% NGS. Sections were washed in PBS for a minimum of 1 h, postfixed for 2 h in phosphate-buffered 2% glutaraldehyde/4% paraformaldehyde, rinsed for 1 h in PBS and then postfixed for 1 h in 50 mM PB containing 2% osmium tetroxide. These sections were then dehydrated and embedded for electron microscopy as previously described.[30] For double labelling by electron microscopy, sliding microtome sections were washed as described above and incubated for 72–96 h at 4�C in an antibody mixture consisting of anti-connexin30 antibody and anti-connexin43 antibody 13-8300, each diluted 1:500 in PBS containing 0.5% Photoflo. After a 1 h rinse in PBSF/BSA, sections wereincubated overnight at 4�C in PBSF/BSA containing 2% NGS, 5 nm gold-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG and horse anti-mouse IgG (Vector Laboratories) diluted 1:50 and 1:100, respectively. After washing in PBST/BSA for 1 h, sections were incubated for 2 h at room temperature with mouse clonoPAP (Sternberger Monoclonals Inc.) diluted 1:500 in PBSF/BSA containing 2% normal horse serum. Sections were washed in PBSF, then in 50 mM Tris–HCl and incubated with 3,3�-diaminobenzidine and hydrogen peroxide as described above. Reacted sections were rinsed in 50 mM PB, fixed in 2% glutaraldehyde/4% paraformaldehyde for 2 h at room temperature, rinsed for at least 1 h in 50 mM PB, postfixed for 1–2 h in 2% osmium tetroxide in 50 mM PB, then dehydrated and embedded for electron microscopy as previously described.[30]

## _Light microscopy_ 

The distribution of connexin30-immunoreactivity (connexin30-IR) in rat brain and spinal cord, shown in Figs 4 and 5, corresponds closely to the more limited survey of immunolabelling previously presented with the anti-connexin26 antibodies[27] that cross-react with connexin30 as demonstrated above. Although we show that connexin30 is widely expressed by astrocytes in the CNS, localized to astrocytic gap junctions and co-localized with connexin43 at these junctions, its distribution is highly heterogeneous, much more so than that of connexin43. Moreover, connexin30-IR is similar to that of connexin43 in some regions, but dramatically different in others. Inter-regional immunolabelling densities for connexin30 and those previously described for connexin43 in rat brain[26,48,49] are compared in Table 2. Densities were assigned by visual inspection of corresponding sections. Nearly all gray matter regions of CNS contained connexin30, labelling in all areas was exclusively punctate, and even lightly labelled regions of gray matter had numerous, albeit faintly stained puncta. Further, although not quantitated, increased staining density tended to correlate with the presence of larger puncta. Thus, differential connexin30 staining density, as with connexin43,[49] was somewhat attributable to the size of puncta, but more so to the intensity with which individual puncta were stained, rather than to number of puncta. 

Most rostrally, connexin30 in the olfactory bulb was heavily concentrated in olfactory glomeruli (Fig. 4A) where punctate staining (Fig. 4B) was typical of that in other brain areas. Staining was faint in other layers and absent in the olfactory nerve layer. As shown in a sagittal section near the midline (Fig. 4C) and in transverse sections at various levels (Fig. 4D–G), connexin30-IR was generally low in most forebrain structures including cerebral cortex, striatum and most subdivisions of lateral septal nuclei. Connexin30-IR was moderate in the medial septal nucleus and high in all basal brain structures beginning rostrally with the vertical and horizontal limbs of the diagnal band (Fig. 4D) and extending caudally through the hypothalamus, globus pallidus, ventral pallidum and mammillary body. Staining was also high in the thalamus, although like connexin43, connexin30 was heterogeneously distributed in this structure (Fig. 4C,G). More caudally, staining was light to moderate in most areas, except in the substantia nigra and the ventral subdivision of the lateral geniculate nucleus where it was substantially more dense (Fig. 4H).

## _Cellular and regional distribution of connexin30_ 

The localization of connexin30 in astrocytes is demonstrated here not only by its presence in cells exhibiting intermediate filaments, but also by its co-localization with connexin43, which is now established to be so widely present at astrocytic gap junctions that it may be considered a marker for these junctions.[31,48,49] Although our ultrastructural analyses of connexin30 to date have not been as extensive as those of connexin43, it was clear that immunolabelling for connexin30, as in the case of connexin43, was largely restricted to gap junctions and their vicinity. The rarity of labelling in astrocyte cell bodies may indicate small cytoplasmic pools of the protein, rapid incorporation within junctional plaques following synthesis, or simply lower accessibility of antigenic sites outside intact junctions. 

Connexin30 is widely distributed in gray matter and even brain areas with weak overall staining exhibit some labelling of gap junctions. This deserves emphasis since differential levels of astrocytic connexin30, rather than presence or absence, underlie the heterogeneous immunostaining patterns obtained. The regional distribution of connexin30 was far more heterogeneous than that of connexin43.[26] Where differences in expression of the two connexins occurred, their was no stereotypical relationships between relative levels, thus excluding differential antibody sensitivity of connexin detection as the cause of these differences. The sharp border of differential connexin30 expression located between fore- 

brain and diencephalic/hypothalamic structures may simply be related to the developmental origin of these areas and indicate that astrocytes in phylogenetically older parts of brain are programmed to express high levels of connexin30. In this regard, it is noteworthy that while it is generally accepted that forebrain structures including striatum have an exclusively telencephalic origin, a diencephalic origin for the globus pallidus has been suggested.[8] Thus, connexin30 and connexin43 may fulfil similar functional roles with a greater contribution of one than the other in regions of common developmental origin. Alternatively, connexin30 and connexin43 expression appear to be differentially regulated in adult brain, suggesting specific cellular requirements for each connexin and perhaps different contributions to inter-glial gap junctional communication. 

Examples of regionally contrasting levels of the astrocytic connexins include white matter, the substantia gelatinosa in spinal cord and the rat cerebellar molecular layer, all of which contained connexin43, but little or no connexin30. Equally striking was the previously described honeycomb labelling pattern for connexin43 that was dense around blood vessels in cerebral cortex, but sparse on vessels in subcortical structures, while connexin30-IR was robust on subcortical as well as cortical vessels. This latter example is particularly noteworthy as the honeycomb pattern arises from labelling of gap junctions between astrocytic processes that form endfeet on blood vessels.[48,49] The concentration of connexin30 at these endfeet throughout the brain suggests a special contribution to gap junctionally-mediated inter-endfoot redistribution of ions or metabolites entering or exiting brain via the blood–brain barrier, at least in gray matter. A separate issue concerns the association of connexin30 with blood vessels in areas that otherwise exhibited an overall low level of staining such as the striatum. Since it appears that each astrocyte deploys main processes for endfoot formation on a given vessel,[47] astrocytes in some brain areas must selectively target the bulk of their connexin30 to processes forming perivascular endfeet, and less to those dispersed elsewhere in neuropil. Such polarity with respect to targeting of intramembranous astrocytic proteins has been previously described.[19] Alternatively, synthesis of connexin30 may occur locally in perivascular processes, perhaps regulated separately from synthesis in astrocytic cell bodies. 

The differential distribution of connexin30 in white and gray matter raises questions about the nature of gap junctions between glial cells in heavily myelinated regions of the CNS. This distribution corresponds to the segregation of astrocytes into two subpopulations based on morphology and location, namely fibrous white matter and protoplasmic gray matter astrocytes. ## _Connexin30 during development_ 

The pattern of connexin30 developmental in brain parenchyma contrasts with the spatial and temporal characteristics of early connexin43 expression. For example, connexin43 was detectable in brain as early as postnatal day 1 where we found it to be distributed diffusely along the length of radial astrocytes.[50] In contrast, while connexin30 levels were still low at 

postnatal day 15, and connexin30-IR was already punctate at the time of its emergence. Its late emergence compared with connexin43 provides further reason to consider that the two connexins may have different functional roles. At least three separate developmental events occur around the time that connexin30 appears. The first of these is myelination, which is underway or progressed substantially by postnatal day 15 and might require astrocytic connexin30 if astrocytic-oligodendrocytic gap junctions involving post-myelinating oligodendrocytes in gray matter contribute to the maturation of this process. However, this would be less applicable to forebrain structures where connexin30 is relatively low. 

The second event is formation of blood–brain barrier, which is complete at about postnatal days 10 to 15.[1] Although the barrier is not structurally composed of astrocytic endfeet, its establishment is coincident with the appearance of endfeet on blood vessels[32] and roughly with the appearance of connexin30 at gap junctionally-coupled astrocytic endfeet where it may contribute to ion or metabolite dispersal as noted above. The third event is maturation of brain energy metabolism, which undergoes a transition from a reliance on ketone bodies in addition to glucose during the first two or three postnatal weeks to a primary reliance on aerobic glycolysis.[20] These biochemical processes may require interglial exchange of particular metabolites, such as lactate, which has been suggested to be released by glial cells for utilization by neurons as an energy source.[40] It is possible that lactate redistribution to meet functional demands is mediated in part by gap junctions between glial cells and that connexin30 and connexin43 may have distinct roles at gap junctions mediating this exchange.

## How distributed is the brain-wide network that is recruited for cognition? 

**Matthew C. Rosen[1] & David J. Freedman[1,2]** 

## **Abstract** 

## **Sections** 

Half a century of neurophysiological recordings from single electrodes established a ‘localized’ viewpoint on function in the brain — that complex behaviour results from computations that are carried out and representations that occur across distinct brain areas, each of which has a specialized role. Data generated from new techniques for specific, high-throughput measurement of neuronal activity and behaviour in rodents have prompted an alternative viewpoint, which posits that neural encoding of behavioural variables is distributed across a wide range of areas: ‘everything, everywhere, all at once’. After briefly introducing these paradigms, we evaluate which of them better describes cognition — the manipulation of internal variables that enables flexible behaviour. Measurements of neuronal activity in both rodents and primates suggest that cognitive variables are reflected broadly but not ubiquitously across the brain, including, to a surprising degree, in regions engaged in controlling movement. We close by discussing why cognitive signals may appear in such areas, as well as the factors that affect the breadth of the brain-wide network that is recruited for cognition.

## **Introduction** 

In navigating through the complex and dynamic world around us, the link between incoming sensations and our actions is neither rigid nor constant. Rather, our sensorimotor behaviour requires manipulation of internal variables through the family of processes that collectively comprise our cognition. Cognition provides behavioural flexibility through multiple levels of abstraction. At a sensory level, stimulus features or entire objects can be flexibly assigned to task-relevant groupings or categories, which endow behavioural importance or meaning to incoming stimuli[1][,][2] . At a contextual level, sensorimotor behaviours can be controlled by abstract rules (that is, matching versus non-matching) that govern the appropriate mappings between stimuli and actions[3][–][5] . At a motor level, abstraction can provide flexibility in how decisions are reported — for example, by selecting the appropriate response modality (such as a gesture, speech or an eye movement)[6][,][7] . 

Determining the neural basis of the internal variables that enable these various forms of abstraction remains a substantial challenge. Unlike sensation, which operates on physical inputs that can be straightforwardly defined and controlled, and unlike action, for which the outputs (behaviour) are observable, cognition is fundamentally internal and, thus, operates with variables that are harder to constrain or measure. 

A central challenge for systems and cognitive neuroscience is to identify the brain network, and the neural computations within that network, that enable behavioural flexibility and abstraction during complex behaviour. A traditional ‘localization of function’ view parcellated brain regions into distinct sensory, motor and cognitive regions. Although it has been long recognized that the organization of the brain does not adhere to a strict view of modularity — mixed neuronal encoding of sensory, motor and cognitive functions is observed throughout cortical and subcortical regions — a looser view of modularity is widely held, with abstract cognitive functions ascribed to association cortical areas such as parietal–frontal regions[8][,][9] . Recent work has increasingly questioned this localized view in favour of a more distributed framework in which cognitive computations are mediated by a much wider swath of the network than previously thought. 

In this Perspective, we evaluate the extent to which these viewpoints describe abstract cognition. We begin by briefly introducing the localized and distributed views of functional organization in the brain. After outlining the broad experimental approach that has enabled the brain-wide study of the neural correlates of cognition in rodents and non-human primates, we discuss the primary functional insights that have emerged: that cognitive signals are broadly distributed through regions tied to movement but not primary sensory areas. We close by reviewing the factors that appear to regulate the breadth of the brain network engaged in cognition. 

## **Localization and distribution of sensory and motor functions** 

Emerging technologies have dramatically expanded the throughput of neuronal recordings during behaviour. Among the surprising insights these methods have yielded, notably in recent rodent experiments, is that movements profoundly modulate neural activity across the brain, even in primary sensory areas. This has rekindled a debate about the fundamental principles that account for the functional architecture of the brain, particularly the degree to which functions are localized within versus distributed across different anatomical areas. (A similar debate has also unfolded in the human cognitive neuroimaging literature[10][–][12] , although our focus here is on evidence from non-human animals.) 

In this section, we briefly review the modern origins of both views, focusing on how they have emanated from contemporary methods for perturbing and measuring the cerebral cortex. 

## **Evidence for localized maps of movement and sensation** 

Systematic application of electrical stimulation across the cerebral cortex in the late nineteenth century revolutionized empirical arguments for the localization of function in the brain. German physiologists Gustav Fritsch and Eduard Hitzig used this approach in 1870 to identify a cortical region in dogs that, when stimulated electrically, produced small, repeatable twitches contralateral to the stimulated site[13] . Through lesioning, they also confirmed the functional importance of this area: ablating sites that evoked a particular kind of movement when stimulated impaired the ability of subjects to generate those movements. Ferrier, and eventually Horsley and others, meticulously applied these techniques to identify a region of cortex in the macaque monkey with similar properties[14][,][15] . Penfield and others would do the same decades later to map somatosensory and motor cortex in humans undergoing neurosurgery[16] . 

These findings established that at least some aspects of brain function are localized in the mammalian cortex, which provided a foothold for studying functional properties of individual neurons. Through lesions and stimulation, one could identify the broad functional role of a region. Electrophysiological recordings focused on those regions could then illuminate how the various parameters relevant for that function drive the responses of individual neurons, one electrode penetration at a time. This paradigm led to foundational discoveries of sensory neurophysiology in the past century, including the structure and logic of visual responses in the striate cortex by Hubel and Wiesel[17][,][18] , selectivity for visual motion in the middle temporal (MT) area[19][–][21] , and selectivity for objects and faces in inferotemporal cortex[22][–][24] . A similar approach revealed a network of regions closely linked to specific kinds of movements, including reaching movements in a subregion of the primary motor cortex[25][,][26] and saccadic eye movement vectors in the frontal eye fields[27][,][28] and superior colliculus (SC)[29] . 

These early studies provided strong evidence for localization of various sensory and motor processes in the brains of higher mammals. They also raised the possibility that the same principle may hold true for other functions necessary for cognitively demanding behaviour — not just sensation and action, but also all the internal operations that link them (planning, reasoning, inference and so on). 

## **Evidence for widespread encoding of action** 

Dramatic recent increases in the scale and throughput of neuronal and behavioural measurements, particularly in rodents, have begun to complicate this localized view. Instead of recording through one or a handful of electrodes at a time, electrophysiology experiments can now simultaneously record hundreds of densely arranged channels from a single multielectrode array[30][–][32] . Novel optical approaches, combined with advances in engineering fast, bright, genetically encoded calcium indicators, now also enable imaging of large neuronal populations — from thousands up to a million neurons — nearly simultaneously[33][–][36] . Cameras and other sensors have increased in resolution and decreased in cost, enabling simultaneous monitoring of many aspects of the behaviour of a subject during neuronal measurements. 

These technical advancements have unlocked a newly unbiased approach to studying the relationship between neural activity and behaviour[37] . Conceptually, this approach is like that of a forward genetic screen, but instead of using genotypic variations to discover

Several influential experiments pioneering such large-scale monitoring of neural activity and behaviour in mice have converged on a surprising discovery: around a third of the variation in neural activity across the cortex, including primary sensory areas, is associated with small, uninstructed movements, rather than any particular goal-directed task. Using two-photon calcium imaging of large neuronal populations (~10,000 cells) in mice free to run on a wheel in darkness, a study found that around 30% of the variance in visual cortex activity could be attributed to facial movements[41] . Subsequent electrophysiological recordings using eight Neuropixels probes showed a similar pattern — around 40% of variance explained by facial movements — in multiple other brain areas, both cortical and subcortical[41] . Another study obtained an analogous result from mice performing a delay-based spatial discrimination task[42] . Wide-field calcium imaging across the cortex, combined with careful quantification of behaviour, showed that uninstructed movements explained around 30% of the measured variation in neural activity. Measurements of activity with single-cell resolution revealed similar contributions of uninstructed movements to neuronal responses on single trials. A study also noted a similar phenomenon during a visual discrimination task[43] . The degree to which uninstructed movements covaried with measures of neural activity across the cortex was similar to that observed for internal states, such as hunger, thirst, arousal and fear, which regulate diverse aspects of physiology and behaviour[41][,][44][,][45] . 

In summary, emerging techniques for measuring neuronal activity across the brain in rodents have revealed widespread modulations 

related to small motor behaviours. These modulations associated with action are also strikingly large: even in primary sensory areas, small movements consistently explain a substantially greater proportion of variation in activity than task variables (for example, instructed movements, visual or auditory stimuli). The breadth and prominence of these signals has demonstrated that at least some aspects of behaviour are reflected broadly across the brain (Fig. 1). A strong version of this view holds that the same principle may generally describe the neuronal encoding of many variables that animals use to guide their behaviour — ‘everything, everywhere, all at once’. Several candidate explanations can further reconcile this idea with prior reports of localization, including a ‘streetlamp effect’ (a localized view of function reflects localized measurements) or differences between areas being quantitative and qualitative. As a somewhat provocative corollary to this idea, some have proposed de-emphasizing distinctions between areas in studies of the neural basis of behaviour altogether[46] . (If most variables are encoded almost everywhere, the logic goes, questions of the form ‘what is the role of area Y in behaviour Z’ may miss the point.) 

## **Cognition: localized or distributed?** 

Do cognitive variables — decisions, plans and so on — also modulate activity across the brain? Brain-wide recordings from rodents engaged in a variety of decision-making behaviours find that neuronal activity distributed throughout the brain reflects action selection in the lead-up to response execution[47][–][51] . Further evidence suggests that these signals emanate from a network involving frontal motor and premotor areas and the basal ganglia[52] . However, this network may not be as widespread as that containing neurons that encode movement[53] . For example, a report noted modulations of neuronal activity in primary visual cortex by visual stimuli and action but not by choice[47] . A recently published brain-wide survey of the International Brain Laboratory[48] underscores a similar point (with the addition[54] that the selectivity profiles of neurons for various task variables convey systematic differences by area).

## **Box 1 | Brain-wide systems neuroscience at single-cell resolution: challenges and opportunities** 

Mammalian systems neuroscience today benefits from a wealth of tools for perturbing and measuring the brain, many of which originated in smaller animals or invertebrates. Efforts to develop similar techniques for use in larger animals such as primates, however, face some additional challenges. As a result of the advantages of mouse as a model system for genetics, many tools that enable labelling, perturbation and measurement of distinct genetically defined cell types in mice are now commercially available. The situation in primates and other large mammals is more ‘pre-commercial’: although similar tools are being developed, they have generally not yet been engineered for widespread adoption. The physics of a much larger brain also give rise to various technical hurdles[51] that have hindered the translation of optical approaches for imaging neuronal activity. The degree of cortical folding in some primates also poses problems for imaging, as resolving neuronal cell bodies deep in sulci is more difficult than 

in a stretch of tissue that is naturally flat relative to the _z_ -axis of the microscope objective. 

Despite these issues, there is nonetheless considerable reason for optimism. High-density silicon probes similar to those revolutionizing rodent studies have now been designed and optimized for use in primates, and many of the technical challenges related to inserting them into surface and deep structures have been surmounted[69][,][145][–][154] . Methods for perturbing activity with high spatial and temporal precision are beginning to experience similar improvements[155] . Other model organisms may also help to sidestep some of the physical hurdles to imaging in traditional non-human primate models. Marmosets are an especially promising candidate: they reproduce relatively quickly (shorter gestational time and more offspring per litter than macaques), facilitating the building of transgenic lines, and their brain is lissencephalic (fewer sulci that can pose problems for imaging)[156][–][158] .

In addition to expansion in brain size and number of neurons by several orders of magnitude from mice to macaques, comparative neuroanatomy reveals at least a threefold expansion in the number of distinct cortical areas[55] . This effectively increases the potential number of hierarchical processing stages between sensory input and motor output in primates relative to rodents, which could in principle allow increased separation between the networks of areas mediating sensory, motor and cognitive functions. Quantitative retrograde tracing experiments have also revealed substantial differences in interareal connection density between species, which can influence the propagation or sharing of signals between regions. In mice, almost all (97%) cortical areas are directly connected to one another[56] ; in macaques[57] and marmosets[58] , however, only about 60% of such direct connections are observed. These differences in anatomy and connectivity both predict that individual cortical areas in the ‘flatter’ rodent brain might be recruited for a more diverse range of functions than in the comparatively ‘deeper’ primate brain[59] . However, testing these predictions by directly comparing data across species is a fraught enterprise, as any observed ‘species’ difference tends to also reflect systematic methodological differences between systems. 

A promising alternative would be to repeat the experiments described above with rodents in primates. However, the narrower range of techniques available for interrogating the brain in non-human primates have thus far rendered this approach technically impractical (Box 1). Below, we discuss a strategy that has nonetheless enabled the piecewise assembly of a brain-wide view on cognition in non-human primates, which we term ‘deep task sampling’. After briefly introducing the origin and logic of this approach, we review the evidence it has yielded about the distribution of cognitive variables in the macaque monkey brain. 

## **Deeply sampled task paradigms** 

Neurophysiological studies of decision-making in primates offer a template for circumventing technical limitations on measurement throughput to assemble a brain-wide picture of cognition: ‘deep sampling’ of well-controlled behavioural tasks. In the human neuroimaging 

literature, deep sampling refers to collecting many measurements from the same subject rather than a single measurement from each of many subjects. Here, we use the term to refer to repeatedly studying a single behavioural task paradigm across a range of brain areas and/or subjects. Using the same task across different experiments buys the ability to straightforwardly compare neuronal representations of task variables across a wider array of brain areas than is practical in a single experiment. This enables the pursuit of questions about how cognition unfolds across the brain by aggregating across individual studies focused on only one or a few brain areas at a time (for a related approach, see Box 2). 

Such tasks have revealed how various regions associated with movement reflect the formation of decisions that are directly mapped to specific actions. The now-classic motion discrimination task of Shadlen, Newsome and others offers a particularly illustrative example[60][,][61] (Fig. 2a). During this paradigm, monkeys need to decide on the net direction of a dot-motion stimulus, which requires integration of noisy sensory evidence across time. In the most extensively studied version of this task, subjects indicate their decisions through an operant saccade in a direction congruent with their decision — that is, an eye movement towards a leftward target for a stimulus judged as moving to the left (Fig. 2b). Repeated experiments using this paradigm have identified neuronal representations across the cortex and various subcortical areas that map onto core computational features of decision-making, including integration of noisy evidence, thresholding and choice bias[62][–][69] . In early and mid-stage visual areas MT and middle superior temporal area (MST), neural activity reflects the instantaneous motion evidence that subjects need to integrate to perform the task successfully[70][,][71] . In the lateral intraparietal area (LIP), subpopulations of neurons reflect both instantaneous motion evidence and the integral of this evidence, which is strongly correlated with the eventual choices of subjects[69] . Subsequent experiments have since found correlates of this latter signal across a broad range of areas beyond LIP, including the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (PFC), the SC and the basal ganglia[62][,][63][,][66][,][68][,][72] .

## **Box 2 | A case study for interrogating the brain-wide basis of cognit ion through task standardization** 

Although the myriad demands of complex behaviour have long been expected to engage potentially much of the brain, most laboratory studies have historically focused on one or a handful of regions at a time. Combining insights across areas to build a brain-wide picture, thus, traditionally required inferences drawn from data across multiple groups. Such inferences are fraught for various reasons. For example, independent investigators seldom use identical behavioural tasks or training curricula. They also frequently use a variety of different hardware and software to collect and process data — for example, different recording modalities or different analysis and pre-processing procedures. Any of these factors could lead to systematic differences in neural data between laboratories that would be difficult to distinguish from, for example, a genuine difference between brain areas recorded by separate groups. 

In the main text, we describe a retrospective approach for mitigating these issues by exploiting existing neural data from ‘deeply sampled’ tasks — frequently collected from different areas across a series of experiments from a single group and later combined for analysis. Several groups and institutes are also beginning to address these issues prospectively through standardization[48][,][49][,][159] . The International Brain Laboratory (IBL), which recently published its initial brain-wide activity survey, effectively exemplifies this approach. To minimize the confounds outlined above, all of the constituent groups of IBL (>20) shared recording modalities, software for data processing and analysis, and a common task[141][,][160][–][162] . The latter is illustrated in the figure: on each trial, head-fixed mice viewed an oriented grating presented in one hemifield and needed to indicate the stimulus location (left versus right hemifield) with an opposite movement of a wheel (for example, left wheel turn for a stimulus in the right hemifield). 

Each aspect of the standardization outlined above has further yielded publicly available tools that other groups can now use. However, what scientific insights are emerging from this approach that would otherwise have been inaccessible? As the goal from the outset was to construct a comprehensive brain-wide map of neural activity during cognition, measurements were not targeted on the basis of any kind of prior assumption about the role of an area in the task. This unbiased approach has led to a variety of surprising findings about the degree and manner of engagement of subcortical areas in decision-making, essentially none of which would have been studied in this context otherwise. Common themes between these deeply sampled tasks illuminate two broad principles that describe how abstract cognitive variables are represented across the primate brain. First, early sensory areas (S1 and MT) appear to devote the bulk of their bandwidth to encoding of the present contents of sensation in their primary modality. In this section, we introduced deep task sampling, an approach used to retrospectively evaluate the encoding of cognitive variables across the brain from experiments that used localized measurements of neural activity. In line with analogous findings in rodents, deeply sampled task paradigms that demand abstract cognition consistently implicate areas associated with movement, but not primary sensory areas. The remainder of this Perspective will address why and under what conditions abstract cognition may engage such areas for successful behavioural performance. 

## **What is the importance of cognitive signals in premotor areas?** 

Although the experiments reviewed above demonstrate that areas involved in movement are strongly modulated by cognitive variables — in the case of LIP and SC, reflecting a causal role in behavioural performance — they leave open the exact role of these modulations in behaviour. On the one hand, animals could solve cognitively demanding tasks in part through their motor behaviour. Movements that correlate with task variables, in particular, could help to satisfy demands for maintaining information in short-term memory by ‘externalizing’ different remembered items in different body states — for example, like keeping a running count on one’s fingers. On the other hand, neuronal populations in premotor areas harbour many dimensions of activity that do not drive action[108] . This extensive movement-null space, already shown to allow movement preparation without movement execution, may similarly offer a malleable representational substrate for covert mental operations. This latter possibility would suggest a more central role for 

movement-related brain circuits in cognitive functions than previously appreciated. Emerging evidence is consistent with both scenarios: small motor behaviours across model systems reflect ‘covert’ cognitive variables, but accompanying neural data suggest that the encoding of these variables in premotor areas may predominantly occupy dimensions of activity orthogonal to those linked to movement[109] . 

The recent increased focus on measurement and analysis of small, uninstructed behaviours has revealed a curious trend: even during tasks that prohibit overt movements, subjects make small movements that reflect the current contents of their cognition, like a poker player’s ‘tell’. We observed this curious effect in the same study that compared all data sets recorded from monkeys performing the DMC task[110] . Despite strict controls on the gaze of subjects, we discovered that all 11 subjects nonetheless made category-correlated fixational eye movements (Fig. 3a–d). These movements, although small in amplitude (generally subtending only tenths of a degree of visual angle), were robust and choice-correlated, reflecting the incorrect category on error trials. They were also generally idiosyncratic, occurring at different times and with different relationships to the direction of the motion stimulus across different subjects. This feature of behaviour emerged after the onset of category selectivity in regions engaged in oculomotor control, ruling out the possibility that category encoding in these areas reflects eye movements alone. Subsequent analyses of reversible inactivation studies of SC and LIP linked category-correlated eye movements particularly closely to the SC. During SC but not LIP inactivation, category-correlated eye movements were markedly attenuated. Furthermore, in the lead-up to these eye movements, the dimension of SC population activity that best discriminated between categories became aligned to the dimensions predicting eye movement directions, suggesting that cognitively modulated eye movements may arise from transient alignment of SC category representations with an output-potent gaze control axis. 

Although it is traditionally noted parenthetically (for example, as a factor for which neural analyses need to control), this kind of behavioural effect — small, uninstructed movements modulated by ongoing cognitive function — has been reported across an array of experimental paradigms and model systems. An early report translating a classic memory-guided orienting paradigm from non-human primates to rats found that the head directions of animals during the memory delay were correlated with their upcoming overt orienting responses[111] (Fig. 3e,f). High-speed video recordings of mice performing a memory-guided directional licking task reproducibly reveal a similar trend: uninstructed orofacial movements and postural adjustments predicting where an animal is preparing to lick[109][,][112] . Similar observations during a probabilistic foraging paradigm suggest that this feature of behaviour is not specific to memory-guided orienting[113] . Moreover, mice also make small, uninstructed movements that are not locked to task events but that nonetheless closely reflect a latent internal state (task engagement versus disengagement)[114] . That these movements (1) occur with timing that is idiosyncratic with respect to task events and (2) reflect a variable (task engagement) that is neither instructed nor controlled by the experimenter strengthens the view that small behaviours generally reflect myriad ‘internal’ variables during cognition. 

Importantly, this feature of behaviour generalizes across laboratories and task paradigms in primates. In monkeys, for example, small eye movements have also been reported to reflect complex image stimuli held in short-term memory[115] . What do these cognitively correlated movements suggest about the roles of motor and pre-motor areas in behaviour? At a minimum, they suggest that the activation of these areas during delayed-response tasks may extend beyond explicit movement preparation[122] . Given that the effects reported thus far in primates are almost all at the level of eye movements, it is tempting to propose a privileged role for oculomotor control networks in cognition. This possibility is in line with a diverse literature in monkeys reporting encoding of cognitive task variables in frontal areas linked with gaze control[101][–][104] . However, similar neural modulations have also been reported in other regions associated with movement, both during the VDT and other paradigms, raising the possibility that premotor areas beyond those implicated in eye movements may be engaged in cognition. The lack of evidence for cognitively modulated movements by other effectors (hands, body and face) in primates may accordingly reflect the relative paucity of experiments that track or analyse these movements during cognitive tasks (with some recent exceptions; for example, see refs. 123,124). More broadly, the tendency of subjects to make cognitively correlated movements leaves open but does not confirm the role of cognitive signals in premotor areas as drivers of movement. Future experiments could address this possibility through manipulations that target the ability of subjects to execute or rely on such movements during task performance. 

## **What factors influence how broadly cognitive signals appear in the brain?** 

What determines the breadth of the network engaged for performing a given behavioural task? Do all goal-directed decision-making behaviours evoke modulations of activity across the same spectrum of areas, or is there a logic that describes the distribution of function across the brain? 

Perturbation surveys and electrophysiological recordings both suggest that, to a first approximation, the breadth of the network a task engages reflects the degree of the cognitive demand of that task. By ‘cognitive demand’ we mean the cumulative complexity of computations a subject uses to solve the task — a factor related to the number and difficulty of information-processing steps that lead to a response. This insight arises from comparisons between tasks that involve statistically identical sensory stimuli and instructed movements but different degrees of cognitive demand. In both mice and monkeys, as cognitive demand increases, a progressively broader network of areas appears to be engaged in task performance. 

## **Optogenetic surveys in mice** 

The development of optogenetics has allowed researchers to repurpose light to carry out perturbation surveys during behaviour — similar to what Ferrier did in the nineteenth century with electrical stimuli and what Lashley did with lesions in the twentieth century. The small size of the mouse brain, together with these new tools, makes it well-suited to this approach: whole skulls of mice can be cleared and exposed to 

light, enabling reversible, spatially and temporally precise stimulation across the brain during awake behaviour. 

By the mid-2010s, several groups had begun to perform these surveys in mice to identify regions necessary for the performance of simple decision-making tasks. Initially, these surveys reported only localized effects. One study applied photoinhibition across an array of 55 cortical volumes, spaced by 1 mm, as mice performed a memory-guided tactile discrimination task[125] . Although most of these sites overlapped with regions that contain neurons modulated by choices of mice during the delay[49] , inhibition markedly changed the performance of mice at only 7 of 55 sites, mostly covering a frontal premotor area (the anterolateral motor cortex) implicated in voluntary licking behaviours[126] . Using a similar approach, another study found that inhibition only of visually responsive sensory and premotor areas impaired the choice behaviour of animals during a visual discrimination task[127] . 

Subsequent work has pointed to cognitive demand as a key determinant of how broadly photoinhibition across the cortex affects task performance. In one study, mice were trained to perform three T-maze navigation tasks in virtual reality[128] . These tasks shared similar visual and motor components but differed in their cognitive complexity: in one task, the reward location was continuously signalled by a visual stimulus on the left or right, whereas in the other tasks, the reward location was memory-guided or informed by accumulation of noisy evidence. Photoinhibition across an array of 29 sites revealed that the breadth of causally important areas varied by task and particularly with the complexity of sensorimotor or cognitive demands. During the visually guided task, photoinhibition only at visually responsive premotor and sensory sites impaired behaviour. However, the same inhibition protocol applied during the more demanding memory-guided and evidence-accumulation tasks markedly impaired performance at all 29 sites. A study that adopted a similar approach has demonstrated that this effect reflects training history: a broader set of sites in the dorsal cortex led to behavioural impairments upon photoinhibition in mice trained to perform more demanding navigation tasks versus simpler, visually guided ones[129] . These results suggest that as the cognitive demand of a task increases, the network of brain areas recruited for its performance also increases. 

## **Task-related neuronal encoding in the primate brain** 

Non-human primate experiments point to a similar trend. In LIP, neuronal activity reflects the choices of animals about where to look strongly during highly trained cognitive tasks but weakly during undirected behaviour, even though animals made similar movements towards identical visual stimuli in both contexts[130] . PFC neurons likewise strongly encode visual stimuli when they engage the cognition of subjects but not when they are passively viewed, despite stimuli and motor behaviour (central fixation) being identical in both contexts[131] . This task-dependence is not observed in all higher cortical areas: in inferotemporal cortex, for example, neuronal responses to visual stimuli appear to be more independent of the importance of those stimuli for behaviour[131][,][132] . 

Such differences in recruitment between tasks can reflect both the demands themselves of those tasks and the lingering effects of long-term training history. These relationships have been illuminated through data from subjects performing several closely related behavioural tasks. In one study, monkeys were trained to sequentially perform a delayed discrimination task followed by a categorization task[91] . Both tasks involved the same motion stimuli, event timings

In both cases, this view emerged from comparisons across an array of carefully constructed tasks sharing similar sensory and motor demands but different cognitive demands. In rodents, these cross-task comparisons have been coupled with modern perturbation surveys to reveal that more regions are necessary for performing more cognitively demanding tasks. In primates, these comparisons have been coupled with electrophysiological recordings to reveal selective engagement of the parietal cortex by increasing cognitive demand. Together, these convergent findings across systems suggest that the network of brain regions recruited for behaviour generally grows along with increasing cognitive demand and task complexity. 

## **Conclusions** 

Tools for measuring the activity of neurons across the brain during behaviour have heralded an exciting new era of mammalian systems neuroscience. In mice, these tools show that ongoing actions modulate neuronal activity in almost every brain area. Is the same true of cognitive variables — plans, decisions, working memory and so on? Emerging evidence suggests that the answer may be ‘yes, but’: yes, the activity of neurons distributed across the brain reflects the contents of the cognition of subjects during laboratory tasks, but not everywhere, and not identically across areas. These insights derive from studies in rodents and non-human primates trained to perform a variety of decision-making tasks, including some that dissociate cognitive variables from specific actions and motor reports. Such studies have revealed that cognitive variables are generally reflected in the activity of neurons distributed across a network of higher-order but not primary sensory regions. 

Despite careful experimental design to minimize overt movements and covert shifts of attention, these tasks recruit, to a surprising degree, areas that are associated with controlling movement. Concomitant with this recruitment, behavioural subjects across systems — from rodents to monkeys to humans — make small uninstructed behaviours that reflect their ongoing cognition, like a poker player’s ‘tell’. Future work will be critical to resolve whether these movements themselves have an important role in task performance or whether they reflect leak from a separate process that makes use of the same network. 

What are factors determine how broadly cognitive variables are encoded across the brain? Evidence across species suggests that the breadth of the network recruited for performing a cognitive task reflects task complexity. Such demand-dependent recruitment could occur via the diffuse projections from neuromodulatory systems that signal effort and/or arousal, either of which might be predicted to increase with the cognitive demand and complexity of a task. In mice, for example, perturbations of the locus coeruleus that modulate physiological correlates of arousal (for example, pupil dilation) also broadly increase functional connectivity among a wide range of brain areas[136] . Cognitive demand-dependent recruitment could also reflect top–down feedback from executive areas such as the PFC, which target a wide array of areas and are known to signal task rules or behavioural context[8][,][137][,][138] . 

That the brain-wide footprint of cognition is profoundly task-dependent also highlights the need to resolve the influence of prolonged behavioural training. Most of the neural data we review here were measured in subjects trained to expert-level task performance. This approach yields well-controlled, stereotyped behaviours that generally facilitate interpretation of neuronal activity measurements, but at the cost of an unknown and potentially important effect on neuronal activity across the brain. In other domains, particularly motor control, these effects can even encompass changes to which brain regions are necessary for performing a behaviour when it is newly learned versus well-learned[139][,][140] . Although in principle this influence would be conceptually straightforward to resolve for cognition, too — measure neuronal activity across the brain as subjects learn to perform a cognitive task, and track changes across the learning process — doing so in practice remains technically difficult, especially in non-human primates or humans. 

The title of this Perspective asks the question: how distributed is the brain-wide network that is recruited for cognition? The balance of the evidence across experiments and model systems points towards the beginning of an answer: it is distributed but not completely so. In other words, although neurons encoding cognitive variables are observed in various areas, they seem not to appear everywhere. This beginning of an answer is also the beginning of other questions. What are the cognitive signals that appear across different areas good for? Moreover, what, if anything, distinguishes the encoding in one area from that in any other? 

An intriguing possibility is that each seemingly ‘distributed’ signal is not simply replicated in different structures but instead rendered in a variety of formats. This idea is consistent with the observations of several independent groups from the data set of the International Brain Laboratory, whereby activity from different brain areas was generally different, even for regions that harboured common signals (for example, were both engaged in encoding choice)[48][,][141] . This idea may also normatively explain demand-dependent recruitment. If different areas enact different transformations on their inputs and can, thus, lead to different formats, one way of accessing a broader palette of formats is to recruit more areas for encoding and representation. The key pieces needed for addressing this possibility already exist, including tools for measuring and perturbing neural activity across the brain and a nascent theoretical framework (representational geometry) for precisely defining ‘format’[142][–][144] . The time is now ripe for putting these pieces together to address the nuance and importance of distributed cognitive encoding in the brain.

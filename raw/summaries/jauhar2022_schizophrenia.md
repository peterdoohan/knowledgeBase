### One-line summary

A comprehensive Lancet Seminar reviewing the epidemiology, clinical features, neurobiology, genetics, and treatment of schizophrenia, concluding that the disorder is best understood as a polygenic, neurodevelopmental condition with converging dopaminergic and glutamatergic pathology, for which dopamine-blocking drugs remain the mainstay of treatment.

---

### Background & motivation

Schizophrenia is among the most serious psychiatric disorders, characterised by positive symptoms (delusions, hallucinations, formal thought disorder), negative symptoms, cognitive impairment, and in many cases significant social and occupational decline with reduced life expectancy of 13–15 years. Despite over a century of intensive research, its aetiology remains incompletely understood and treatment options remain limited. This Seminar synthesises the current state of knowledge across epidemiology, clinical presentation, brain structure and function, neurochemistry, genetics, and treatment, and identifies active areas of debate and future directions.

---

### Methods

- Narrative review of the schizophrenia literature, covering studies from the early 20th century through 2021.
- Includes synthesis of large meta-analyses, genome-wide association studies (GWAS), birth cohort studies, structural and functional neuroimaging studies, neurochemical imaging (PET), and randomised controlled trials of pharmacological and psychological treatments.
- No formal inclusion/exclusion criteria are stated; the paper is a Lancet-commissioned Seminar providing an authoritative overview for a clinical audience.

---

### Key findings

- Lifetime morbid risk is approximately 7–12 per 1000; incidence is modestly higher in men (male-to-female ratio ~1.70).
- Symptom structure: factor analyses consistently identify three syndromes — reality distortion, disorganisation, and negative/poverty symptoms.
- Cognitive impairment (executive function, memory, attention) is now recognised as a core clinical feature, present across the illness and largely stable post-onset (though a 10-year follow-up found decline in IQ and verbal measures).
- Structural MRI: lateral ventricular enlargement (~25% by volume) and ~2% whole-brain volume reduction; frontal lobe (Cohen's d −0.49), temporal lobe (−0.43), and hippocampus (−0.52) most affected.
- Functional imaging: hypofrontality (dorsolateral PFC) and failure of medial frontal cortex deactivation during task performance are well-replicated findings; the latter suggests disruption of the default mode network.
- Dopamine hypothesis: increased amphetamine-stimulated dopamine release and increased dopamine synthesis capacity (meta-analytic effect size 0.52, 95% CI 0.21–0.83) in schizophrenia; elevated synthesis capacity also observed in prodromal individuals.
- Glutamate hypothesis: supported primarily by ketamine studies in healthy volunteers producing psychosis-like symptoms; post-mortem evidence for altered NMDA receptor numbers is inconsistent.
- Genetics: schizophrenia is largely polygenic; 2014 GWAS identified 108 loci (since expanded); heritability 64–81%; rare copy number variants and protein-truncating variants confer moderate-to-large effect sizes (OR 2–60-fold).
- Cannabis: meta-analytic OR for psychosis ~2.0 in users; OR 4.8 for daily high-potency cannabis use.
- Treatment: antipsychotics (D2 receptor blockade) reduce positive symptoms; clozapine is superior for treatment-resistant cases (response rate ~40%); CBT has small effect sizes (d ~0.33 for overall symptoms, reduced further in blinded trials).
- Early intervention benefits are clear at 18 months–2 years but diminish at 5-year follow-up.
- Progressive brain volume loss (~twice the rate of healthy controls) has been documented in prospective studies, concentrated in frontal lobes.

### Computational framework

Not applicable in a formal modelling sense. However, the paper engages with several computational ideas at a conceptual level:

- **Reward prediction error (RPE) / aberrant salience**: the review explicitly invokes Schultz's dopamine RPE framework to propose that increased and aberrant striatal dopamine signalling could explain the formation of delusions (and possibly hallucinations) via aberrant assignment of salience to neutral stimuli.
- **Default mode / task-positive network interaction**: the paper frames functional imaging findings in terms of the anticorrelated default mode and task-positive networks, proposing that schizophrenia may reflect disturbed toggling between these networks — a conceptual (not formally computational) framework.
- These frameworks are used to interpret neuroimaging and neurochemical data rather than as formal computational models fit to data.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to the current synthesis, several overlapping models coexisted: (1) a neurodevelopmental model emphasising static early-life brain changes (supported by lateral ventricular enlargement present at onset and in relatives), (2) the dopamine hypothesis implicating presynaptic dopamine excess (initially postsynaptic D2 upregulation, later revised to excess dopamine synthesis/release), and (3) an NMDA-receptor hypofunction hypothesis emerging from phencyclidine/ketamine observations. Structural abnormalities (frontal, temporal, hippocampal volume reductions) and hypofrontality were established findings. The genetic contribution was known to be substantial but had not been mapped to specific loci. Psychosis was traditionally treated as a categorical entity distinct from normal experience. The paper's introduction reflects an ongoing transition from a purely neurodevelopmental, static model towards one incorporating progressive changes, network-level dysfunction, and converging neurochemical pathways.

---

### What this paper contributes

As a review, the paper consolidates and clarifies the state of the field across multiple domains:

- Establishes that brain structural and functional changes in schizophrenia are neither fully static (there is progressive volume loss) nor fully explained by antipsychotic medication.
- Synthesises evidence that schizophrenia is a polygenic disorder with both common and rare variant contributions converging on synaptic biology.
- Frames dopaminergic dysfunction in terms of aberrant RPE signalling as a mechanistic account of psychosis, integrating neurochemical and symptom-level evidence.
- Notes that CBT has only small effects, tempering clinical guidelines that have broadly recommended it.
- Highlights that the continuum model of psychosis (extended phenotype) is influential but contested, and that retrospective childhood adversity data are likely biased.
- Identifies the need to understand how structural and functional brain changes translate into specific symptoms as a key unresolved challenge.

The review does not provide new primary data; its contribution is synthesis, critical appraisal, and framing of open questions.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Lateral ventricles** — enlarged (~25% by volume); landmark structural finding since 1976.
- **Frontal lobe (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex)** — hypofrontality at rest and during executive tasks; locus of hypofrontality meta-analyses; reduced grey matter volume (d −0.49).
- **Medial frontal cortex** — failure of task-induced deactivation; key node of the default mode network.
- **Temporal lobe** — grey matter reduction (d −0.43); superior temporal gyrus thickness correlated with positive symptom severity.
- **Hippocampus** — volume reduction (d −0.52); part of the default mode network.
- **Striatum** — locus of increased dopamine synthesis capacity and excess amphetamine-stimulated dopamine release; central to the dopamine hypothesis.
- **Default mode network** (posterior cingulate, precuneus, medial frontal cortex, hippocampus, angular gyrus) — failure of deactivation; proposed site of disturbed network interaction.
- **Task-positive/executive network** (lateral PFC, anterior insula, dorsal medial frontal cortex) — reduced activation relative to healthy controls; anticorrelated with default mode network.
- **Salience network** (anterior insula, dorsal anterior cingulate, amygdala, VTA, thalamus) — proposed relevance to aberrant self-referential thinking and symptom formation.
- **Paracingulate cortex** — altered morphology associated with hallucinations.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the high bar for mechanistic insight as defined here. While it describes a plausible algorithm (aberrant dopamine RPE signals generate delusions by tagging neutral stimuli as salient), it does not provide primary neural data linking specific model variables to measured neural activity — it synthesises existing PET and fMRI literature rather than offering new recordings, single-unit data, or direct tests of the RPE account against alternatives. The review therefore operates primarily at the computational and, partially, implementational levels (dopamine synthesis in the striatum), but does not provide the joint algorithmic + neural-data evidence required.

The paper proposes: **Computational level** — the brain fails to correctly assign salience, generating anomalous prediction errors that lead to false inference (delusions); **Algorithmic level** — excess presynaptic dopamine release produces aberrant RPE signals; **Implementational level** — localised to striatal dopamine synthesis pathways, with some evidence from PET measures of dopamine synthesis capacity. However, the direct link between RPE signals and delusion formation is inferred rather than empirically tested within the paper.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Causal direction of most aetiological associations (cannabis, urban living, childhood adversity) remains unclear; retrospective childhood adversity data are methodologically problematic.
- Glutamate hypothesis remains supported mainly by indirect (ketamine) evidence; post-mortem NMDA receptor findings are inconsistent.
- Progressive brain volume changes are documented but their functional significance and whether antipsychotics contribute remain debated.
- No glutamatergic drugs have shown benefit in large clinical trials despite ongoing research interest.
- CBT effect sizes are small and diminish in well-blinded trials; the clinical recommendation for universal CBT may need revision.
- Early intervention benefits are not sustained at 5-year follow-up.
- The mechanism by which genetic risk (converging on synaptic biology) translates into the specific symptom clusters of schizophrenia remains unknown.
- Whether schizophrenia is best treated as a categorical entity or as the extreme of a psychosis continuum remains actively debated.
- The relationship between network-level dysconnectivity (default mode/task-positive interaction) and specific symptoms is not established.

### One-line summary

Psychotropic drugs fail to cure mental illness because translational neuroscience cannot fully capture the psychological constructs — shaped by language, personal narrative, and subjective experience — that are central to psychiatric disorders, and treatment efficacy therefore requires integrating biological interventions with psychologically-oriented approaches.

---

### Background & motivation

Psychotropic medications have provided meaningful symptom relief for psychiatric conditions for over half a century, yet symptoms typically recur upon discontinuation and the underlying disorders remain unaltered. The dominant translational research paradigm assumes a direct causal chain from brain biology to psychological phenomena and attempts to map complex behaviours onto discrete neural circuits in animal models. This paper argues that this paradigm has fundamental limitations because many clinically relevant psychological constructs — including confidence, self-efficacy, personal narrative, and subjective experience of illness — cannot be adequately reduced to identified neuronal pathways and are inaccessible to animal or in vitro models that lack human language capacity.

---

### Methods

This is a hypothesis and theory paper (narrative review and conceptual argument). The authors:

- Survey critiques of the translational research model in psychiatry from the broader literature.
- Draw on theoretical work in philosophical neuroscience (LeDoux/Pine two-system model, higher-order theories of consciousness, neuroconstructivism, strong emergence hypothesis).
- Use illustrative case examples (auditory hallucinations in schizophrenia, anxiety disorders, PTSD, depression relapse) to argue for the gap between biological mechanisms and psychological constructs.
- Propose an integrative bio-psycho-social framework that emphasises language, subjective report, and self-efficacy as essential clinical targets.

No primary data are presented.

---

### Key findings

- Psychotropic medications consistently reduce symptoms but do not change the trajectory of psychiatric disorders; symptom return upon discontinuation is the norm, not the exception.
- Translational animal models necessarily exclude human language and subjective verbal report, which are essential to understanding and treating many psychiatric phenomena (e.g., subjective fear vs. defensive behaviour circuits).
- Many clinically important psychological constructs — confidence, psychological denial, contextual understanding, compartmentalisation — cannot plausibly be mapped onto discrete neuronal networks.
- Computational psychiatry and Bayesian/predictive frameworks show promise for linking neural signals to behaviour, but leave unaddressed higher-order psychological constructs such as insight, implied meaning, and self-concept.
- Symptom suppression by pharmacological agents can create conditions in which psychological and cognitive-behavioural interventions become more effective, but the drugs alone cannot alter the ego-congruent mental constructs built up over years of experiencing the disorder.
- Integrated approaches (e.g., combining drugs with CBT, mindfulness, or novel modalities such as ketamine/psychedelics for PTSD) are identified as the most promising path forward.

---

### Computational framework

The paper does not employ a computational framework itself, but discusses several frameworks in the context of their promise and limitations:

- **Bayesian / predictive coding**: framed as potentially useful for bridging neural activation patterns and behaviour via prediction error signals, but argued to fall short of capturing complex psychological constructs.
- **Computational psychiatry**: briefly reviewed as an emerging discipline that can model task behaviour and connect it to neural substrates, but acknowledged to leave higher-order subjective and linguistic phenomena outside its scope.

The paper's own argument is primarily conceptual and philosophical rather than formally computational.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper explicitly critiques the prevailing translational research model of psychiatric disorders, which the authors characterise as:

1. All psychological states are causally determined by underlying brain mechanisms.
2. Complex behaviours can be decomposed into discrete measurable units and mapped onto neural circuits.
3. Animal (and in vitro) models can adequately capture the biology relevant to human psychiatric illness.
4. Better pharmacological targeting of brain systems will ultimately yield cures or disease-modifying treatments.

The introduction signals that this model has driven psychiatric neuroscience for over two decades and has produced a wealth of biological data without corresponding clinical advances.

---

### What this paper contributes

The paper does not present new empirical findings but advances the following theoretical positions:

- The direct-causality assumption (brain → mind) is an overreach; biological brain mechanisms are necessary but not sufficient to explain or treat clinically relevant psychological states.
- Human language is not merely an epiphenomenon but a constitutive element of many psychiatric disorders that cannot be proxied by animal behaviour.
- The translational gap is not primarily a resource or methodological problem but reflects an inherent ontological mismatch between biological and psychological levels of description.
- The clinical role of psychotropic agents should be reconceptualised as symptom-relief enablers that open windows for psychological intervention, rather than as disease-modifying agents per se.
- A coherently integrated bio-psycho-social model that accounts for language, narrative, and self-efficacy is proposed as the necessary framework for progress.

---

### Brain regions & systems

The paper does not report original neuroimaging or recording data. Brain regions are mentioned only in passing as examples drawn from existing literature:

- **Prefrontal cortex** — cited in the context of frontal mechanisms of metacognition, confidence, and mindfulness-related enhancement of executive function.
- **Amygdala** — cited regarding fear circuits, parental buffering of stress responses, and early adversity models.
- **Auditory cortex** — cited as an example of tonotopic abnormalities in schizophrenia, used to illustrate the limits of biological modelling for the full phenomenology of auditory hallucinations.
- **Insular cortex** — mentioned in relation to salience and executive networks activated during mindfulness meditation.

None of these regions constitute a focused analytical target in the paper itself.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight as defined by the criteria. It proposes no algorithm and presents no neural data. It is a conceptual argument about the limits of mechanism-level explanation in psychiatry. The paper explicitly acknowledges that the causal relationships between brain mechanisms and psychological constructs remain unresolved and may be inherently difficult to specify.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- The paper's arguments are primarily conceptual; no empirical evidence is marshalled to directly test the claim that integrated bio-psycho-social approaches outperform pharmacological approaches in altering disease trajectory.
- The boundaries of which psychological constructs are and are not reducible to neural circuits are asserted but not formally characterised.
- The strong emergence hypothesis, which the authors invoke to ground their view of mind-brain interaction, is philosophically contested and not empirically constrained by data in this paper.
- The paper does not specify criteria by which the proposed integrative framework could be falsified.
- Open questions raised: How can computational psychiatry incorporate subjective self-report and language in a principled way? Can portable imaging technologies (fNIRS, EEG) bridge the gap between lab models and real-world clinical assessment? To what degree can self-efficacy be operationalised as a treatment endpoint?

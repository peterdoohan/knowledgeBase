### One-line summary

Elevation of intracellular cAMP or treatment with LDL triggers a rapid 2-3 fold enhancement in gap junction assembly within 60 minutes by stimulating connexin43 trafficking from intracellular stores, without requiring new protein synthesis or changes in Cx43 phosphorylation.

### Background & motivation

Gap junction (GJ) communication is essential for processes including electrical coupling, cell growth control, and development. Connexins have short half-lives (2-3 hours for Cx43), requiring continuous synthesis, transport, and assembly. Previous work showed that protein kinase C activation inhibits GJ assembly, but the mechanisms by which cAMP upregulates GJ communication were unclear—some studies showed increased connexin expression while others showed rapid effects without protein synthesis. This study specifically examined whether cAMP can enhance GJ assembly rapidly through post-translational mechanisms.

### Methods

- **Cell system**: Novikoff hepatoma cells (N1S1-67) maintained in suspension culture
- **Dissociation/reaggregation assay**: Cells dissociated with EDTA (no proteases), allowed to recover 90 minutes, then reaggregated for 60 minutes to assay de novo GJ assembly
- **Treatments**: 
  - cAMP-elevating agents: 8-Br-cAMP (1 mM), forskolin (10 µM), IBMX (100 µM)
  - LDL (2.5 µg/ml)
  - Trafficking inhibitors: brefeldin A (36 µM), monensin (5 µM), nocodazole (5-60 µM)
  - Signaling inhibitors: H-89 (PKA inhibitor, 30 µM), THFA (adenylyl cyclase inhibitor, 1 mM)
- **Assays**:
  - Dye transfer: Lucifer Yellow CH ionophoretic injection into one cell of a pair, measuring time to appearance in adjacent cell
  - Freeze-fracture electron microscopy: quantification of GJ particles per interface
  - Western blotting: Cx43 levels and phosphorylation states
  - Cell surface biotinylation: labeling of non-junctional plasma membrane Cx43
  - Immunofluorescence: Cx43 colocalization with organelle markers (TGN38, Rab5B, ConA-ER)

### Key findings

- **cAMP elevating agents enhance GJ assembly**: Treatment with 8-Br-cAMP, forskolin, or IBMX reduced dye transfer times by approximately half (P<0.001), indicating 2-3 fold increase in GJ communication. Freeze-fracture EM showed forskolin increased GJ particles per interface from 806±107 to 2053±317 (2.5-fold increase).

- **Enhanced assembly is rapid**: Adding 8-Br-cAMP only during the 60-minute reaggregation period was sufficient to significantly increase GJ communication (mean dye transfer time 14.6±1.5 seconds, P<0.001).

- **cAMP affects plaque size, not initiation**: The percentage of cell interfaces displaying GJs remained ~70% in both control and forskolin-treated cells. cAMP modulates the growth/size of GJ plaques but not the recruitment of cells into GJ formation.

- **LDL enhances assembly via cAMP signaling**: LDL reduced dye transfer times similarly to cAMP (Fig. 1). The adenylyl cyclase inhibitor THFA blocked the LDL effect (P>0.3 for LDL+THFA vs control). The PKA inhibitor H-89 blocked both cAMP-stimulated and LDL-stimulated assembly (P<0.005 and P<0.001 respectively). H-89 did not affect basal assembly levels.

- **Freeze-fracture confirms LDL effect**: LDL increased GJ particles per interface to 2842±358 (Table 1), a 3.5-fold increase over control. Nocodazole blocked this increase (701±132 particles, comparable to control 806±107).

- **No change in Cx43 protein levels or phosphorylation**: Western blot analysis showed no increase in total Cx43 protein after 2.5 hours of treatment with cAMP, forskolin, or LDL (Fig. 3). The ratio of phosphorylated Cx43 forms (upper bands) to non-phosphorylated (fastest migrating band) remained unchanged.

- **Cell surface Cx43 pool unchanged**: Biotinylation of plasma membrane Cx43 showed no significant change with LDL treatment (normalized level 0.90±0.20 of control, n=4). Non-junctional plasma membrane Cx43 does not appear to be the source for enhanced assembly.

- **Trafficking inhibitors block enhanced assembly**: Brefeldin A (inhibits ER-to-Golgi transport), monensin (inhibits TGN-to-plasma membrane transport), and nocodazole (microtubule disruptor) all blocked cAMP- and LDL-stimulated GJ assembly (Figs 4, 5). Basal assembly was only minimally affected by these inhibitors.

- **Cell contact regulates trafficking**: BFA added 90 minutes after cAMP/LDL (at reaggregation start) did not block enhanced assembly, while monensin added at the same time did block it. This suggests: (1) cAMP triggers Cx43 redistribution prior to cell contact, moving it past the BFA-sensitive step (ER/Golgi), but (2) cell contact is required for Cx43 to pass the monensin-sensitive step (TGN/plasma membrane transport).

- **Intracellular Cx43 localization**: Immunofluorescence revealed cytoplasmic Cx43 clusters (1-3 µm) that did not colocalize with ER (ConA), TGN (TGN38), or early endosomes (Rab5). Cx43 at cell-cell interfaces increased with cAMP treatment. Monensin treatment caused accumulation of larger Cx43-positive structures near cell surfaces.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to this study, the prevailing understanding was that cAMP upregulates gap junction communication through multiple mechanisms including: (1) increased connexin transcription and protein synthesis, (2) altered connexin phosphorylation states, and (3) direct effects on channel conductance. The negative regulation of gap junction assembly by protein kinase C (via Cx43 phosphorylation) was well established. However, it was unclear whether cAMP could enhance gap junction assembly through rapid post-translational mechanisms independent of new protein synthesis. Additionally, while LDL was known to enhance gap junction assembly, the mechanism linking LDL to this effect was unknown.

### What this paper contributes

This paper establishes that cAMP and LDL trigger rapid enhancement of gap junction assembly (within 60 minutes) through a novel mechanism involving connexin trafficking from intracellular stores, without requiring new protein synthesis or changes in Cx43 phosphorylation. Key contributions include:

1. Demonstration that cAMP elevating agents (8-Br-cAMP, forskolin, IBMX) and LDL produce 2-3 fold increases in GJ assembly within one hour, measured by both dye transfer and freeze-fracture EM.

2. Evidence that this enhanced assembly does not require increased Cx43 protein levels or altered phosphorylation states, as shown by Western blot analysis over 2.5 hours of treatment.

3. Discovery that the LDL effect is mediated through cAMP signaling, requiring both adenylyl cyclase and protein kinase A activity, as demonstrated using specific inhibitors (THFA and H-89).

4. Demonstration that enhanced assembly requires connexin trafficking from intracellular stores, as three different trafficking inhibitors (brefeldin A, monensin, nocodazole) all block the enhanced assembly.

5. Evidence that cell contact regulates Cx43 trafficking at a step downstream of the BFA-sensitive site but upstream of the monensin-sensitive site, indicating a cell contact-dependent control point in the trafficking pathway.

### Mechanistic insight

This paper provides significant mechanistic insight into the regulation of gap junction assembly, though it does not reach the level of Marr's three levels analysis as it focuses on cellular and molecular mechanisms rather than algorithmic or computational-level explanations.

**Cellular mechanism**: The study establishes that cAMP and LDL trigger enhanced gap junction assembly through a specific trafficking pathway:
1. cAMP signaling (via PKA activation) triggers redistribution of Cx43 from intracellular stores
2. This redistribution occurs prior to cell-cell contact but requires microtubules (nocodazole-sensitive)
3. Cell contact then regulates a subsequent trafficking step (between BFA and monensin-sensitive sites)
4. Cx43 is delivered to the plasma membrane and assembled into functional gap junction plaques

**Molecular players**: The mechanism involves adenylyl cyclase, protein kinase A (PKA), microtubules, and the C-terminal tail of Cx43 (which is required for the cAMP response). The signaling may involve pertussis toxin-sensitive G proteins (Gi family), based on unpublished observations and parallels with aquaporin trafficking.

**Physical implementation**: The trafficking pathway involves movement of Cx43 through the ER-Golgi-TGN pathway and/or from post-Golgi vesicular compartments to the plasma membrane. Intracellular Cx43 stores are found in cytoplasmic clusters (1-3 µm) that do not colocalize with ER, TGN, or early endosomes, suggesting novel storage compartments.

### Limitations & open questions

1. **Identity of intracellular Cx43 stores**: The exact cytoplasmic compartments containing Cx43 stores remain unidentified. Immunofluorescence showed Cx43 clusters do not colocalize with ER, TGN, or early endosomes, but the specific identity of these stores requires further investigation.

2. **Mechanism of Cx43 recruitment**: How cAMP signaling triggers the mobilization of Cx43 from intracellular stores is not fully understood. The C-terminal tail of Cx43 is required, but the specific signaling events linking PKA activation to connexin trafficking remain to be elucidated.

3. **Role of G proteins**: While the Discussion suggests pertussis toxin-sensitive G proteins (Gi family) may be involved in cAMP-triggered Cx43 trafficking, based on unpublished data and parallels with other systems, this requires direct experimental validation in the Novikoff cell system.

4. **Physiological relevance**: The study uses Novikoff hepatoma cells, a transformed cell line. While the authors note parallels with physiological contexts (uterine myometrium before labor, mouse embryo compaction), the generalizability to normal tissues requires further investigation.

5. **Mechanism of cell contact sensing**: The study identifies that cell contact regulates Cx43 trafficking at a step between BFA and monensin-sensitive sites, but the molecular mechanism by which cell contact triggers this trafficking step is unknown.

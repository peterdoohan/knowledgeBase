### One-line summary

Three species of predominantly herbivorous voles display innate, species-typical hunting stereotypes when confronted with mobile insect prey, with the Tuva silver vole exhibiting a distinct hunting tactic that likely reflects its phylogenetic distance from the other two species.

---

### Background & motivation

Although invertebrates are found in the diets of nearly all rodent species, it was long unclear whether rodents actively hunt mobile prey or merely collect immobile invertebrates. Active hunting had been studied mainly in carnivorous and omnivorous species, and only recently discovered in granivorous rodents; hunting behaviour in predominantly herbivorous voles had not been examined. This study addresses that gap by experimentally testing three herbivorous vole species and comparing their hunting stereotypes with those of previously studied rodent species of varying dietary specialisation.

---

### Methods

- **Subjects**: 22 East European voles (*Microtus levis*), 46 narrow-headed voles (*Lasiopodomys gregalis*), and 68 Tuva silver voles (*Alticola tuvinicus*); all predominantly herbivorous. Laboratory-born individuals had no prior insect exposure.
- **Task**: Each rodent was placed individually in a 30 × 30 × 35 cm arena and presented with live cockroaches (*Nauphoeta cinerea* and *Shelfordella lateralis*) as mobile prey.
- **Test modes**: Mode 1 (all narrow-headed and East European voles, plus 16 Tuva silver voles) — one session, three insects in succession. Mode 2 (52 Tuva silver voles) — one insect per day for ten days. Total: 1,138 video records (96.5 hours).
- **Behavioural coding**: An alphabet of 19 behavioural elements was used; sequences were coded with Observer XT 12.5. Elements were classified as key (attack), auxiliary (pursuit/handling), or noise.
- **Analysis**: Transition probability matrices used to construct ethograms (hunting stereotype schemes); Fisher's exact test and Kruskal–Wallis H test for between-species comparisons (Bonferroni correction applied).

---

### Key findings

- Purposeful predatory hunting was confirmed in all three herbivorous vole species; the full hunting stereotype appeared without prior experience, indicating its innate nature.
- All species shared a universal stereotype structure: pursuit (walking/running) → sniffing → bite (W) → seize with paws (E) → optional handling/nibbling → consumption.
- Voles initiate attacks with a bite rather than paw-seizing, distinguishing them from predatory/omnivorous hamsters and aligning them more closely with field striped mice and brown rats among muroids.
- Tuva silver voles had a significantly lower proportion of key elements and higher proportion of auxiliary elements compared to the other two species (Fisher's exact test, p < 0.003 for all comparisons).
- Tuva silver voles inflicted fewer bites per stereotype (median bite count: 2 vs. 6–11 in the other species; H-test, p < 0.003) and less leg-nibbling, instead quickly capturing prey alive and consuming it immediately.
- East European and narrow-headed voles showed similar hunting tactics to each other, inflicting more double and multiple bites and more prey handling/immobilisation before eating.
- East European voles uniquely nibbled cockroach wings (K) and displayed spontaneous shaking (O), not observed in other species.
- 29.4% of East European voles and 29.4% of narrow-headed voles abandoned immobilised prey without eating it; this was not reported for Tuva silver voles.
- Species-level differences in hunting tactics mirror known phylogenetic relationships: East European and narrow-headed voles are more closely related to each other than either is to the Tuva silver vole.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to this study, active predatory hunting in rodents was considered a trait associated with carnivorous or omnivorous dietary specialisation. Herbivorous rodents, including voles, were assumed to consume invertebrates opportunistically by collecting immobile prey rather than actively pursuing and subduing live insects. The introduction signals that hunting behaviour had only very recently been demonstrated in granivorous species (bank vole), and that the behaviour of purely herbivorous voles was entirely unstudied. The theoretical framing is relatively thin — the paper's main backdrop is a comparative ethological question rather than a mechanistic neural or computational model.

---

### What this paper contributes

The study demonstrates, for the first time, that three herbivorous vole species exhibit coordinated, innate hunting stereotypes directed at mobile prey. This extends the known taxonomic range of active predatory behaviour in rodents beyond carnivorous and omnivorous species. The finding that naive laboratory-born animals display the full stereotype supports the conclusion that hunting behaviour in rodents is ancestrally inherited rather than learned. Species-level differences in hunting tactics (particularly the distinct profile of the Tuva silver vole) are shown to track phylogenetic relationships, suggesting that hunting behaviour carries a phylogenetic signal useful for comparative analysis.

### Brain regions & systems

Not applicable. This is a behavioural ethology study with no anatomical, neuroimaging, or neurophysiological component. The level of analysis is entirely behavioural/ethological — the unit of description is the observable motor stereotype and its transition probabilities between elements.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight at Marr's levels. It characterises behavioural stereotypes at the level of observable motor elements and transition probabilities but provides no neural data (recordings, imaging, lesions, pharmacology) and does not formalise an algorithm linking neural processes to the hunting behaviour. The authors note that spontaneous shaking in East European voles may indicate a stress reaction, citing pharmacological literature, but no mechanistic evidence is provided in this study.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Sample sizes are unequal across species (especially the very small East European vole group: 18 successful stereotypes), limiting statistical power for some comparisons.
- Only two cockroach species were used as prey; generalisability to other prey types is unknown.
- The two test modes (single-session vs. multi-day) were applied unequally across species; the authors state this did not affect reported results, but the cross-species comparability of experience effects is not fully addressed.
- The phylogenetic interpretation of behavioural differences is suggestive but not formally tested (e.g., no phylogenetic comparative method).
- The neural or hormonal mechanisms underlying the innate hunting stereotype, and the mechanisms producing species differences, are entirely unaddressed.
- Why East European and narrow-headed voles frequently abandon immobilised prey (29.4% each) without eating is unexplained.

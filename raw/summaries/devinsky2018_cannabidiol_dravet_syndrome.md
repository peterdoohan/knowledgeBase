### One-line summary

Long-term add-on cannabidiol (CBD) treatment in patients with treatment-resistant Dravet syndrome maintained sustained reductions in convulsive and total seizure frequency for up to 48 weeks with an acceptable safety profile.

---

### Background & motivation

Dravet syndrome (DS) is a severe, treatment-resistant epileptic encephalopathy caused predominantly by SCN1A mutations, characterised by recurrent convulsive seizures persisting throughout life despite multiple antiepileptic drugs (AEDs). Prior short-term randomised controlled trials (GWPCARE1 Part B, 14 weeks) demonstrated that pharmaceutical CBD significantly reduced convulsive seizure frequency, but the long-term durability, safety, and tolerability of CBD in DS was unknown. This open-label extension trial (GWPCARE5) was designed to address that gap by following patients from the completed placebo-controlled trials for up to three years.

---

### Methods

- **Study design**: Open-label extension (GWPCARE5; NCT02224573) of two placebo-controlled parent trials (GWPCARE1 Parts A and B; GWPCARE2) in patients with DS.
- **Population**: 264 patients with DS enrolled; efficacy analysis restricted to 104 patients from GWPCARE1 Part B (the subgroup with comparable baseline criteria and unblinded data). Age range 2–19 years; mean age ~9.8 years; median 3 concomitant AEDs.
- **Intervention**: Oral pharmaceutical CBD (100 mg/mL), titrated from 2.5 to 20 mg/kg/day over 2 weeks; dose adjustable up to 30 mg/kg/day based on response and tolerability. Added to existing AED regimens.
- **Duration**: Median treatment 274 days (range 1–512 days); up to 1 year (some countries) or 3 years (US, France, Poland).
- **Primary outcome**: Long-term safety and tolerability (treatment-emergent adverse events, vital signs, ECG, laboratory parameters).
- **Secondary outcomes**: Convulsive and total seizure frequency per 28 days in 12-week windows; responder rates (≥25%, ≥50%, ≥75%, 100% reduction); status epilepticus episodes; Subject/Caregiver Global Impression of Change (S/CGIC) scale at weeks 24, 36, and 48.
- **Analysis**: Descriptive; no formal hypothesis testing. Seizure frequency changes expressed as percentage change from pre-randomisation baseline of parent trial. Last-observation-carried-forward (LOCF) sensitivity analyses conducted.

---

### Key findings

- **Safety**: 93.2% of patients experienced at least one adverse event; most were mild (36.7%) or moderate (39.0%). Most common AEs: diarrhoea (34.5%), pyrexia (27.3%), decreased appetite (25.4%), somnolence (24.6%). Serious AEs occurred in 29.2% of patients; 7.2% discontinued due to AEs. Two deaths from SUDEP, considered unrelated to CBD (rate ~11/1000 patient-years, consistent with background DS mortality).
- **Liver enzyme elevations**: 22 of 71 patients (31%) taking concomitant valproic acid from GWPCARE1 had ALT/AST >3× ULN. No patient met Hy's law criteria. No elevations in patients not on valproic acid.
- **Seizure efficacy — convulsive seizures**: Median reduction from baseline of 37.5% in weeks 1–12 (from 12.4 to 7.5 seizures/month), maintained at 42.9%–44.3% across weeks 13–48. More than 40% of patients achieved ≥50% reduction at each time window. 4.8% were convulsive seizure-free in their last 12 weeks.
- **Seizure efficacy — total seizures**: Median reduction of 39.5% in weeks 1–12 (from 32.4 to 14.5 seizures/month), maintained at 39.0%–50.7% through week 48. 2.9% were total seizure-free in their last 12 weeks.
- **Dose stability**: Mean modal dose remained consistent across reporting periods (19.9–22.7 mg/kg/day), indicating no tolerance development.
- **Patient-reported outcomes**: 85% of patients/caregivers reported overall improvement on the S/CGIC after 48 weeks; >80% at all assessed time points.

### Computational framework

Not applicable. This is a clinical trial with descriptive statistical analyses of seizure frequency, adverse events, and patient-reported outcomes. No computational or mechanistic modelling is employed. The results constrain pharmacological models of CBD's antiseizure mechanisms (e.g., TRPV1, GPR55 antagonism, adenosine reuptake modulation) by establishing its sustained clinical efficacy but do not directly test those mechanisms.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's introduction frames DS as a severe, pharmacoresistant epileptic encephalopathy, predominantly driven by loss-of-function mutations in SCN1A (encoding Nav1.1 sodium channel subunits), leading to recurrent convulsive and other seizure types throughout life. Standard of care at the time comprised clobazam and valproic acid as first-line agents, with stiripentol, topiramate, or ketogenic diet as second-line options — all providing incomplete seizure control in most patients. The prevailing model assumed that DS seizures were largely intractable and that adding new agents would produce modest, potentially non-sustained benefits. CBD was understood to have antiseizure properties via novel, non-cannabinoid-receptor mechanisms, but evidence for long-term efficacy beyond 14 weeks was lacking.

---

### What this paper contributes

This trial extends the evidence base from a 14-week RCT to up to 48+ weeks, showing that the seizure frequency reductions observed in the parent trial (approximately 40% for convulsive seizures) are maintained without dose escalation, ruling out early tolerance as an explanation for initial efficacy. The stable mean modal dose across 12-week intervals is a direct empirical counter to a potential tolerance concern. The study establishes the long-term safety profile (notably the valproic acid interaction with liver enzymes) at sufficient scale (264 patients) to characterise risk meaningfully. The S/CGIC data add evidence that seizure reductions translate to perceived clinical benefit. Together, these findings positioned CBD as a viable long-term maintenance treatment for DS — a gap that previously existed despite promising acute efficacy data.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight. It presents clinical outcome data (seizure counts, adverse events, patient-reported outcomes) from an open-label extension trial without any neural recordings, imaging, or mechanistic assays. While the introduction references candidate mechanisms for CBD's antiseizure action (TRPV1, GPR55 antagonism, adenosine reuptake), the trial provides no data linking these mechanisms to the observed efficacy. The contribution is purely clinical-pharmacological: establishing durability and safety rather than explaining the computational or neural mechanism of CBD's action.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- No placebo arm in the extension phase; changes from baseline cannot be cleanly attributed to CBD versus natural history or regression to the mean.
- Patients differed in prior CBD exposure (some from CBD arm, some from placebo arm of parent trials), creating heterogeneity in baseline seizure frequency relative to true pre-treatment state.
- Not all patients had reached the later (36–48 week) time windows at the data cut, reducing sample size and statistical power for later periods.
- Meal composition and fasting state at dosing were not recorded, potentially introducing pharmacokinetic variability.
- The valproic acid–CBD liver enzyme interaction is established as a risk but the mechanism is not elucidated; optimal management strategy is not fully characterised.
- Open question: whether efficacy is maintained beyond 48 weeks and whether any subgroup (by genotype, age, concomitant medications) shows differential long-term response.
- SUDEP rate (~11/1000 patient-years) is consistent with DS background rates, but long-term impact of CBD on SUDEP risk remains unknown.

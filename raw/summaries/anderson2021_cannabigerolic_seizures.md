### One-line summary

Cannabigerolic acid (CBGA) has anticonvulsant effects in some mouse seizure models but also exhibits proconvulsant effects in others, and interacts with multiple epilepsy-relevant molecular targets including GABAA receptors, GPR55, and TRPV1.

---

### Background & motivation

Cannabis has a long history of use in epilepsy treatment, recently validated by regulatory approval of cannabidiol (CBD) for Dravet syndrome. Children with intractable epilepsy are often treated with artisanal, cold-extracted cannabis preparations that contain low CBD but are enriched in other phytocannabinoids, particularly phytocannabinoid acids. This raises the possibility that non-CBD constituents contribute to the anticonvulsant properties of these preparations. The paper aims to screen minor phytocannabinoids — including acidic precursor molecules such as CBGA — for anticonvulsant activity and to characterise the molecular pharmacology of the most promising candidates.

---

### Methods

- **Animal models**: Scn1a+/- mice (F1 hybrid on 129S6/SvEvTac x C57BL/6J background), a model of Dravet syndrome; Swiss outbred mice for conventional seizure threshold tests.
- **Hyperthermia-induced seizure screen**: Seven phytocannabinoids tested at 100 mg/kg i.p. in Scn1a+/- mice (P14–16); seizure threshold temperature for generalised tonic–clonic seizure (GTCS) measured; n = 12–18 per group.
- **Spontaneous seizures and survival**: Scn1a+/- mice primed with a single hyperthermia-induced seizure at P18 then treated subchronically with CBGA via supplemented chow (1400 or 2500 mg/kg chow); spontaneous GTCS counted over 60 h; survival monitored to P30; n = 15–19 per group.
- **Combination treatment**: CBGA + clobazam assessed against both hyperthermia-induced and spontaneous seizures.
- **Conventional seizure models**: Maximal electroshock (MES) threshold and 6-Hz psychomotor seizure threshold tests in Swiss mice; CC50 determined by up-and-down method.
- **Molecular pharmacology**: β-arrestin GPCR screen at 242 GPCRs (Eurofins DiscoverX); GPR55 antagonism assay (ERK phosphorylation in HEK293 cells); TRPV1 inhibition (FLIPR calcium assay); GABAA receptor modulation and desensitisation (two-electrode voltage clamp in Xenopus oocytes expressing concatenated human γ2–β2–α1–β2–α1 GABAA receptor).
- **Plasma pharmacokinetics**: LC-MS/MS quantification of phytocannabinoid plasma levels.

---

### Key findings

- Three phytocannabinoids showed novel anticonvulsant activity against hyperthermia-induced seizures in Scn1a+/- mice: CBGA (effective at 30 mg/kg), CBDVA (100 mg/kg), and CBGVA (100 mg/kg). CBDV was also confirmed anticonvulsant. CBGV and CBG were inactive; Δ9-THCV was proconvulsant.
- CBGA was the most potent phytocannabinoid identified, requiring a lower dose than CBD (which is effective at 100 mg/kg in this model).
- High-dose subchronic CBGA (2500 mg/kg chow) significantly increased spontaneous GTCS frequency in Scn1a+/- mice (proconvulsant effect), but neither dose affected survival.
- CBGA (30 mg/kg) combined with sub-threshold clobazam (1 mg/kg) was significantly more anticonvulsant against hyperthermia-induced seizures than either drug alone; the combination also reduced the proportion of mice experiencing spontaneous GTCS (28% vs 63% in controls).
- CBGA was anticonvulsant in the MES threshold test (30 mg/kg increased CC50) but proconvulsant in the 6-Hz psychomotor seizure threshold test (10 and 30 mg/kg reduced CC50).
- CBGA showed no agonist or antagonist activity at 242 GPCRs (including CB1 and CB2).
- CBGA inhibited GPR55 activation (IC50 ~5 μM for both ML-186 and LPI) as a non-competitive antagonist.
- CBGA inhibited TRPV1 channel function (IC50 22 μM).
- CBGA was a positive allosteric modulator (PAM) of GABAA receptors (EC50 910 nM; Emax 271%), but prolonged preincubation (180 s) caused significant GABAA receptor desensitisation.
- Estimated brain CBGA concentrations in vivo (720 nM–4 μM) fall within the range relevant for GPR55 inhibition and GABAA PAM activity, but not TRPV1 inhibition.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The introduction signals that the field's working model is that CBD is the primary therapeutic phytocannabinoid in cannabis, with its anticonvulsant efficacy attributed to activity at multiple targets (GPR55, TRPV1, GABAA receptors, and others). Artisanal cannabis preparations used for childhood epilepsy are known to have diverse phytocannabinoid profiles and are often enriched in acidic precursor compounds, but these minor cannabinoids were poorly characterised. The baseline assumption was therefore that CBD is the lead anticonvulsant compound and that other phytocannabinoids — especially the acid forms — might contribute to efficacy but had not been systematically investigated. Dravet syndrome is understood to arise from loss-of-function SCN1A mutations that impair Nav1.1 function in inhibitory interneurons, making GABAergic signalling particularly relevant.

---

### What this paper contributes

The study establishes for the first time that three phytocannabinoid acids (CBGA, CBDVA, CBGVA) have anticonvulsant activity, suggesting that cold-extracted artisanal cannabis preparations may owe part of their efficacy to these compounds rather than CBD alone. However, CBGA's profile is more nuanced than CBD's: it is proconvulsant in some contexts (spontaneous seizures at high dose; 6-Hz model) and has a narrower therapeutic window. The finding that CBGA acts as a GABAA PAM with an EC50 within the in vivo therapeutic range provides a plausible mechanism for both its anticonvulsant and proconvulsant effects (the latter via desensitisation with prolonged exposure). The study also confirms that a pharmacokinetic interaction with clobazam elevates CBGA plasma levels, analogous to what has been reported for CBD and clobazam. Overall, the results indicate that CBGA, CBDVA, and CBGVA warrant further investigation but that CBGA does not share the broad-spectrum anticonvulsant versatility of CBD.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar. It provides in vitro pharmacological data on CBGA's molecular targets (GABAA PAM activity, GPR55 antagonism, TRPV1 inhibition) and in vivo seizure data, but it does not provide neural recordings, imaging, or other direct neural measurements linking specific molecular actions to the observed seizure phenotypes. The mechanistic discussion is inferential: the authors reason that GABAA PAM activity at the EC50 of 910 nM is within in vivo brain concentration estimates and could account for both anticonvulsant effects (enhanced GABAergic tone) and proconvulsant effects (desensitisation with prolonged exposure), but this connection is not empirically validated at the circuit or neural activity level.

### Limitations & open questions

- The metabolic pathways of CBGA are unknown; proconvulsant or anticonvulsant effects could be mediated by metabolites rather than CBGA itself.
- Brain penetration of CBGA is low (~2% brain:plasma ratio), limiting direct CNS bioavailability.
- The proconvulsant effects in some models make CBGA unsuitable as a monotherapy without further optimisation or analogue development.
- The bioisosteric replacement of the labile acid moiety is proposed but not demonstrated.
- The specific molecular target(s) mediating anticonvulsant and proconvulsant effects in vivo remain unresolved; the GABAA desensitisation hypothesis for the proconvulsant effects is mechanistically plausible but not tested in vivo.
- Pharmacokinetic interactions with clobazam (elevation of CBGA levels acutely; reduction of N-desmethylclobazam chronically) complicate interpretation of combination effects.
- Survival was not improved by any CBGA treatment, and untreated control survival (78%) was higher than typical for this model, introducing uncertainty about effect size estimates.
- CBDVA and CBGVA received only limited characterisation; their mechanisms, dose–response profiles in other models, and longer-term effects remain unknown.

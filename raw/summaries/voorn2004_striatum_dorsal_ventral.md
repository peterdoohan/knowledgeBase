### One-line summary

The dorsal-ventral distinction of the striatum should be reoriented to a dorsolateral-to-ventromedial functional gradient that better captures both anatomical connectivity patterns and behavioral functions.

---

### Background & motivation

The classic dorsal-ventral divide of the striatum (dorsal sensorimotor striatum vs. ventral limbic striatum) has guided research for three decades but may not offer the best view of striatal function. Recent anatomical, neurophysiological, and behavioral findings suggest that sharp boundaries between dorsal and ventral striatum are absent, and a mediolateral functional organization may be more appropriate. This review synthesizes evidence to propose a revised framework.

---

### Methods

- Comprehensive review of anatomical, neurophysiological, and behavioral literature on striatal organization
- Analysis of cortical, thalamic, amygdaloid, and dopaminergic input patterns to the striatum
- Review of behavioral studies examining dorsal vs. ventral striatal function in reward, locomotion, learning, and cognitive control
- Examination of neurophysiological and neurochemical differences between striatal regions

---

### Key findings

- **Behavioral overlap between dorsal and ventral striatum**: Psychostimulant effects on reward, locomotion, and conditioned reinforcement, while strongest in ventral striatum, can also be elicited from dorsal sites; opioid-induced feeding can be triggered from both ventral and dorsal striatal sites
- **Mediolateral functional distinction**: Dorsolateral striatum mediates procedural/stimulus-response learning while dorsomedial striatum is involved in spatial learning—a function also shared with ventral striatum; inhibitory control and cognitive flexibility show differential involvement of dorsomedial vs. ventral regions
- **Anatomical continuity**: No clear cytoarchitectonic, myeloarchitectonic, or chemoarchitectonic boundary exists between dorsal and ventral striatum; the same neuronal cell types (medium spiny neurons and interneurons) are present throughout with similar densities
- **Dorsolateral-to-ventromedial gradient**: Cortical, thalamic, amygdaloid, and dopaminergic inputs all follow a dorsomedial-to-ventrolateral zonal organization; dorsolateral striatum receives sensorimotor information, ventromedial parts collect viscerolimbic inputs, and intermediate areas receive associational information
- **Shell as the ventromedial extreme**: The nucleus accumbens shell exhibits unique characteristics including direct projections to hypothalamic and mesencephalic areas that enable direct behavioral control; the shell can invigorate behaviors coordinated through the core but does not orchestrate dorsal striatal functions

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The field has operated under a bipartite dorsal-ventral division of the striatum for three decades: the dorsal striatum (caudate-putamen) processes sensorimotor information for habit formation and motor control, while the ventral striatum (nucleus accumbens) processes limbic information for motivation, reward, and affective behavior. This division was based on differential cortical, thalamic, and dopaminergic inputs, with the boundary between nucleus accumbens and caudate-putamen serving as the demarcation line. Within the ventral striatum, core and shell subdivisions were recognized as functionally distinct.

---

### What this paper contributes

This review challenges the classic dorsal-ventral dichotomy and proposes reorienting the framework to a dorsolateral-to-ventromedial functional gradient. The key contributions include:

- Demonstrating that behavioral functions classically attributed to ventral striatum (reward, motivation) also engage dorsal regions, particularly dorsomedial striatum
- Showing that anatomical inputs (cortical, thalamic, amygdaloid, dopaminergic) follow a continuous dorsolateral-to-ventromedial gradient rather than segregating at a dorsal-ventral boundary
- Establishing that no clear cytoarchitectonic or chemoarchitectonic boundary exists between dorsal and ventral striatum
- Proposing that the nucleus accumbens shell represents the ventromedial extreme of this gradient, with unique efferent projections enabling direct behavioral control via hypothalamic and mesencephalic targets
- Arguing for a heterarchical rather than hierarchical organization of striatal function, with parallel processing across regions and interactions mediated by corticostriatothalamic loops and intrastriatal connections

### Brain regions & systems

- **Striatum (dorsal)** — traditionally viewed as sensorimotor; caudate-putamen complex; receives sensorimotor cortical inputs; involved in procedural learning and motor control
- **Striatum (ventral)** — traditionally viewed as limbic; nucleus accumbens (core and shell); receives limbic cortical, hippocampal, and amygdaloid inputs; involved in motivation and reward
- **Nucleus accumbens core** — part of ventral striatum with more "classical" striatal output patterns; coordinates behavior through pallidal and nigral projections; involved in instrumental conditioning and response control
- **Nucleus accumbens shell** — most ventromedial part of striatum; unique projections to lateral hypothalamus and dopaminergic cell groups; involved in unconditioned behaviors (maternal behavior, feeding, defensive behavior); can invigorate behaviors coordinated by core
- **Substantia nigra** — target of striatal outputs; receives topographically organized projections from striatum
- **Ventral pallidum** — target of ventral striatal outputs
- **Lateral hypothalamus** — unique target of shell projections; mediates affective and motivated behaviors
- **Ventral tegmental area (VTA)** — source of mesolimbic dopamine; receives projections from caudomedial shell

### Limitations & open questions

- The dorsolateral-to-ventromedial gradient hypothesis requires further systematic comparisons between dorsal-ventral and medial-lateral coordinates; such studies have not been carried out as yet
- The exact nature of interactions between striatal subregions remains unclear; while the paper argues against a strict hierarchical organization, the precise mechanisms of "heterarchical" interactions need further investigation
- The role of intrastriatal connections in mediating between-region communication is poorly understood
- How the shell's unique projection to dopaminergic cell groups enables modulation of processes throughout the striatum requires further behavioral and physiological investigation
- The relationship between striatal topography and the spiraling striatonigrostriatal pathways proposed in primates needs clarification in rodent models

### One-line summary

Astrocytic connexin-43 (Cx43) hemichannels and gap junctions play critical roles in the pathophysiology of CNS disorders including Alzheimer's disease, glioma, brain ischemia, and chronic pain through regulation of gliotransmitter release and inflammatory signaling.

---

### Background & motivation

Astrocytes, the most abundant cells in the CNS, form interconnected networks via gap junctions composed primarily of connexin-43 (Cx43). Unpaired connexins can function as hemichannels, releasing gliotransmitters (ATP, glutamate, NAD+, D-serine) into the extracellular space. While gap junctions mediate intercellular communication critical for homeostasis, hemichannels provide a route for neuron-glial exchange. Dysregulation of Cx43 has been implicated in multiple neurological disorders, yet the specific mechanisms and therapeutic potential remain to be synthesized.

### Methods

- **Scope**: Comprehensive narrative review of literature on astrocytic Cx43 in CNS disorders
- **Literature coverage**: Primary research articles from 1995-2018 on connexin biology, neurodegenerative diseases, glioma, ischemia, and pain
- **Disease models reviewed**: 
  - Alzheimer's disease (AD): APP/PS1 mice, patient brain tissue
  - Parkinson's disease (PD): rotenone-treated rats
  - ALS: SOD1 and SOD1G93A mice
  - Glioma: Cx43 KO mice, human GBM cell lines (LN229), BALB/c nude mice
  - Ischemia: Wistar rats, neonatal SD rats, preterm fetal sheep
  - Pain models: spinal cord injury, chronic constriction injury (CCI), spinal nerve ligation (SNL), partial sciatic nerve ligation (PSNL), bone cancer, chemotherapy-induced neuropathy
- **Synthesis method**: Narrative review organized by disease category with mechanistic summaries

### Key findings

**Alzheimer's Disease (AD)**
- Cx43 expression is upregulated in cortical regions with amyloid-β (Aβ) plaques in AD patients and APP/PS1 mice
- Cx43 hemichannels (not gap junctions) are activated during AD pathogenesis, releasing ATP and glutamate that cause neuronal Ca2+ overload and synaptic depression
- Cx43 hemichannel blockers (boldine, cannabinoids) or genetic deletion of astroglial Cx43 reduce neuronal damage and improve cognitive function
- Astroglial Cx43 deletion reduces astrogliosis and increases synapse numbers without affecting plaque formation

**Parkinson's Disease (PD)**
- Total and phosphorylated Cx43 are elevated in rotenone-treated PD models
- Gastrodin (Chinese herbal medicine) ameliorates PD by downregulating Cx43

**Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)**
- Cx43 is upregulated in ALS patients and models, leading to elevated hemichannel activity, enhanced gap junction coupling, and increased intracellular Ca2+
- Cx43 blockers protect against motor neuron toxicity

**Glioma**
- Cx43 expression varies with tumor grade, stage, and location; generally lower in high-grade glioma cores compared to low-grade
- Cx43 confers temozolomide (TMZ) resistance via EGFR-JNK-ERK1/2-AP-1 axis activation
- Cx43 C-terminal interacts with actin cytoskeleton to promote tumor cell migration and invasion
- Cx43 hemichannels and gap junctions both contribute to glioma progression
- Cx43 peptidomimetics (αCT1, Gap19) as adjuvants restore TMZ sensitivity

**Brain Ischemia**
- Astrocytic Cx43 expression increases after hypoxia/ischemia injury
- Ischemia/reperfusion and inflammation activate astrocytic hemichannels via increased extracellular Ca2+ and cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α)
- p38 kinase activation by pro-inflammatory cytokines triggers Cx43 hemichannel opening
- Abnormally opened hemichannels cause uncontrolled ATP/glutamate release and Ca2+ overload, leading to excitotoxicity and inflammation amplification
- Cx43 mimetic peptides (Gap26) reduce infarct volume and improve neurological function

**Chronic Pain**
- Astrocytic Cx43 is upregulated in multiple chronic pain models (spinal cord injury, nerve ligation, inflammation, opioid exposure, bone cancer)
- Cx43 hemichannels release ATP, glutamate, and chemokines (CCL2, CXCL1, CXCL12) that modulate nociceptive neurons and activate microglia
- Cx43/Cx30 double knockout attenuates neuropathic pain following spinal cord injury
- Cx43 blockers (carbenoxolone, Gap26, Gap27, Peptide5) reduce mechanical allodynia and hyperalgesia across multiple pain models
- Cx43 expression is regulated by TNF-α, IL-1β, bFGF, and sigma-1 receptors in pain states
- MAPKs (ERK, JNK) phosphorylate Cx43, leading to gap junction closure and hemichannel opening

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The review synthesizes evidence supporting the following established understanding:

**Astrocyte connectivity and hemichannel function**: Astrocytes form gap junction networks primarily via Cx43, enabling rapid intercellular exchange of ions and metabolites essential for K+ and glutamate buffering, calcium wave propagation, and synaptic plasticity. Unpaired Cx43 hemichannels serve as an alternative route for gliotransmitter release (ATP, glutamate, NAD+, D-serine) into the extracellular space.

**Hemichannel regulation**: Hemichannel opening is dynamic and controlled by multiple factors. Lower or higher intracellular Ca2+ increases opening probability. Pathological conditions (oxidative stress, low pH, mechanical stimulation, inflammation) enhance hemichannel opening. MAPK phosphorylation regulates channel gating, trafficking, and assembly. Uncontrolled opening leads to cell damage through excessive ATP/glutamate release, Ca2+ overload, and osmotic/ionic imbalance.

**Astrocytes in CNS disorders**: Astrocyte dysfunction, including altered connexin expression and hemichannel activity, is associated with neurodegenerative diseases (AD, PD, ALS), glioma, ischemia, and chronic pain. Reactive astrogliosis represents a common pathological feature across these conditions.

---

### What this paper contributes

This review synthesizes and updates understanding of astrocytic Cx43 hemichannels and gap junctions across multiple CNS disorders, establishing several key advances:

**Disease-specific Cx43 mechanisms**: The review identifies distinct patterns of Cx43 dysregulation across disorders—upregulation in AD plaques, TMZ-resistance in glioma via EGFR-JNK-ERK1/2-AP-1 axis, hemichannel-mediated chemokine release in chronic pain, and inflammation-triggered opening in ischemia.

**Therapeutic target validation**: The review compiles evidence that Cx43 blockade (genetic deletion, hemichannel-specific peptides like Gap26/Gap27/Peptide5/Gap19, or non-selective inhibitors like carbenoxolone) provides protective effects across AD, glioma, ischemia, and chronic pain models, establishing Cx43 as a promising therapeutic target.

**Gap junction vs. hemichannel dissociation**: The review clarifies that different Cx43 functions (gap junction coupling vs. hemichannel-mediated gliotransmission) can have distinct or even opposing roles in pathology, with hemichannels often mediating deleterious effects while gap junctions may be protective.

**Regulatory mechanisms**: The review synthesizes upstream regulators of Cx43 including inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β), growth factors (bFGF), MAPK signaling (ERK, JNK, p38), and sigma-1 receptors, providing a framework for understanding context-dependent Cx43 modulation.

**Future research directions**: The review identifies unresolved questions including the need for specific Cx43-targeting drugs that penetrate the blood-brain barrier, clarification of time-dependent Cx43 changes in disease progression, and investigation of Cx43-pannexin interactions.

### Brain regions & systems

**Astrocytes (CNS-wide)**: The predominant cell type studied, with Cx43 expression throughout the brain and spinal cord. Astrocytic networks via gap junctions enable K+ and glutamate buffering, calcium wave propagation, and metabolic support across the CNS.

**Prefrontal cortex / Hippocampus**: Regions showing altered Cx43 expression in Alzheimer's disease models, with elevated Cx43 immunoreactivity at amyloid-β plaques correlating with astrogliosis.

**Substantia nigra**: Region showing reactive astrocytes with elevated Cx43 in Parkinson's disease models.

**Spinal cord**: Critical region for chronic pain mechanisms, with Cx43 upregulation in astrocytes following spinal cord injury, nerve ligation, and inflammation. Cx43 hemichannels in spinal astrocytes release chemokines (CCL2, CXCL1, CXCL12) that maintain neuropathic pain.

**Trigeminal system**: Ectopic orofacial pain following inferior alveolar nerve injury involves Cx43 upregulation in trigeminal ganglion astrocytes.

**Retina**: Retinal ischemia-reperfusion injury involves Cx43 hemichannel-mediated vascular leak and retinal ganglion cell death.

**Glioma tumors**: Cx43 expression varies by tumor grade, stage, and location—generally lower in high-grade glioma cores. Cx43 contributes to temozolomide resistance and tumor cell migration/invasion.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight as defined (no new algorithm formalized with supporting neural data). This is a review paper synthesizing existing evidence rather than presenting original experimental or modeling work.

However, the review synthesizes evidence that constrains models of astrocyte-neuron interaction:

- The review establishes that Cx43 hemichannels serve as a conduit for gliotransmitter release (ATP, glutamate) that modulates neuronal excitability and synaptic transmission
- Evidence shows hemichannel opening is regulated by intracellular Ca2+, pH, oxidative stress, and inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β)
- MAPK signaling (ERK, JNK, p38) phosphorylates Cx43, leading to gap junction closure and hemichannel opening
- The review identifies that chemokine release (CCL2, CXCL1, CXCL12) from astrocytes is mediated by Cx43 hemichannels, though the exact mechanism remains unclear since chemokines are too large to pass directly through hemichannels

### Limitations & open questions

**Acknowledged limitations of the reviewed literature**:

- Lack of specific Cx43-targeting drugs that penetrate the blood-brain barrier; current tools (carbenoxolone, Gap26, Gap27, Peptide5) are either non-selective or have limited CNS penetration
- Discrepancy in Cx43 expression changes across different pain models and time points—some studies show upregulation while others show downregulation, suggesting time-dependent or context-dependent regulation that is not fully characterized
- Difficulty distinguishing hemichannel vs. gap junction contributions in many studies; most pharmacological blockers affect both channel types
- Mechanism by which Cx43 hemichannels mediate chemokine release remains unclear since chemokines are too large to pass directly through hemichannels

**Open questions identified by the review**:

- How do different phosphorylation sites on Cx43 (21 sites reported) specifically contribute to different CNS disorders?
- What is the role of pannexin-1 (also expressed in astrocytes and inhibited by carbenoxolone) in pain and other CNS disorders, and how do connexins and pannexins interact?
- How does Cx43 function change in a time-dependent manner during disease progression (e.g., early vs. late stages of AD plaque formation)?
- Can specific Cx43-targeting therapeutics be developed for different CNS conditions based on the dynamic changes in Cx43 expression and function?
- What is the precise signaling mechanism linking Cx43 hemichannels to chemokine synthesis and secretion?

### One-line summary

Acute oral CBD (300 mg) appears to alter physiological and psychological responses to aerobic exercise, including VO2, pleasure ratings during exercise, and inflammatory markers, without impairing performance.

### Background & motivation

CBD has demonstrated anti-inflammatory, analgesic, anxiolytic and neuroprotective effects with potential benefits for athletes. Despite growing popularity among athletes (26% of British professional rugby players report using CBD), only two prior interventional studies had investigated CBD effects on athletic performance outcomes. This pilot study aimed to gain preliminary understanding of CBD's effects on exercise physiology and bioenergetics to determine whether larger, fully powered trials are warranted.

### Methods

- **Design**: Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover pilot trial with ≥7-day washout between sessions
- **Participants**: 9 endurance-trained males (mean ± SD VO2max: 57.4 ± 4.0 mL·min⁻¹·kg⁻¹; running 63 ± 21 km/week)
- **Treatment**: 300 mg oral CBD or placebo (MCT oil) administered 1.5 h before exercise
- **Exercise protocol**: 
  - RUN 1: 60 min submaximal treadmill running at 70% VO2max (fixed speed 10 km/h, individualised gradient)
  - RUN 2: Incremental treadmill test to exhaustion (gradient increased 2% every 3 min)
- **Measurements**: Respiratory gases (VO2, VCO2, RER), heart rate, blood glucose, blood lactate, RPE, pleasure-displeasure ratings (Feelings Scale), serum inflammatory markers (IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α, LPS, myoglobin), plasma anandamide (AEA), sleep quality, muscle soreness
- **Analysis**: Cohen's dz effect sizes with 85% CIs; effects "possibly affected" (worthy of further investigation) if 85% CI included ±0.5 but not zero

### Key findings

- **Submaximal exercise (RUN 1)**: CBD appeared to increase VO2 at 37 min (+38 ± 48 mL·min⁻¹, dz: 0.25–1.35) and ratings of pleasure at 20 min (+0.7 ± 0.9, dz: 0.22–1.32) and 40 min (+0.8 ± 1.1, dz: 0.17–1.25); blood lactate also appeared elevated at 40 min (dz: >0.00–1.03). No differences in HR, RPE, blood glucose, or RER were observed.

- **Maximal exercise (RUN 2)**: CBD appeared to increase VO2max (+119 ± 206 mL·min⁻¹, dz: 0.06–1.10) and RERmax (+0.04 ± 0.05, dz: 0.24–1.34); no differences in time to exhaustion, HRmax, or other measures.

- **Inflammatory markers**: Exercise increased IL-6, TNF-α, myoglobin, creatine kinase, and LPS. CBD appeared to decrease IL-1β Post-RUN 2 and 1-h Post-RUN 2, but appeared to increase myoglobin 1-h Post-RUN 2 (possibly due to slightly longer exercise time). Effects on other inflammatory markers were uncertain.

- **Endocannabinoids**: Plasma anandamide (AEA) increased with exercise; CBD appeared to reduce AEA concentrations Post-RUN 2 (dz: −2.19 to −0.79) compared to placebo.

- **Psychological outcomes**: No clear anxiolytic effects observed (participants had low baseline anxiety). "Fatigue" appeared possibly reduced Post-RUN 1 but possibly elevated 1-h Post-RUN 2 with CBD. Sleep quality and next-day muscle soreness were not affected.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The introduction establishes that CBD has demonstrated anti-inflammatory, analgesic, anxiolytic and neuroprotective effects in clinical populations, with the working assumption that these properties could benefit athletes. Prior to this study, only two interventional studies had investigated CBD effects on athletic performance outcomes, with mixed results. The prevailing expectation was that CBD might reduce inflammation, muscle damage, and anxiety during exercise without impairing performance, but the specific effects on exercise physiology (oxygen consumption, substrate metabolism, cardiovascular responses) were largely unknown in athletic populations.

### What this paper contributes

This pilot study provides the first systematic investigation of acute oral CBD effects on comprehensive exercise physiology and bioenergetics in endurance-trained athletes. Key contributions include:

1. **Preliminary evidence for altered oxygen kinetics**: CBD may increase both submaximal VO2 and VO2max, suggesting potential effects on tissue oxygen extraction or utilization, though the mechanism remains unclear (vasodilation hypothesized).

2. **Enhanced affect during exercise**: CBD appeared to increase pleasure ratings during the first 40 minutes of submaximal exercise, suggesting potential benefits for exercise experience without increasing perceived exertion.

3. **Modulation of inflammatory responses**: CBD appeared to reduce IL-1β (a pro-inflammatory cytokine) following exercise, consistent with anti-inflammatory effects observed in preclinical studies, though effects on other markers (IL-6, TNF-α) were uncertain.

4. **Endocannabinoid modulation**: CBD appeared to reduce exercise-induced increases in plasma anandamide (AEA), contrary to previous findings in schizophrenia patients, suggesting complex interactions with the endocannabinoid system during exercise.

5. **Safety/performance profile**: CBD did not appear to impair aerobic exercise performance (no detrimental effects on time to exhaustion, RPE, or HR responses), suggesting potential utility in sporting contexts.

The study establishes that CBD's effects on exercise physiology warrant investigation in larger, fully powered trials with broader populations and varied dosing regimens.

### Mechanistic insight

This paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight. While the study presents empirical findings on CBD's effects on exercise physiology, it does not:

1. Present or formalize an algorithm that could explain the phenomenon — the mechanisms by which CBD might increase VO2, improve affect, or modulate inflammation remain speculative (vasodilation hypothesized but not tested).

2. Provide neural data supporting any specific algorithm — the study measures peripheral physiological variables only (respiratory gases, blood markers, heart rate) with no neural recordings or imaging.

The paper proposes vasodilation as a possible mechanism for increased VO2 based on preclinical findings, but this is not tested directly in the study. The effects on endocannabinoid tone (reduced anandamide) suggest CBD may modulate the endocannabinoid system, but the functional implications for exercise physiology remain unclear. This is appropriately positioned as an exploratory pilot study to inform future mechanistic investigations.

### Limitations & open questions

**Acknowledged limitations:**

1. **Pilot study not formally powered**: The study was not designed nor formally powered to assess "effect"; analytical approach susceptible to Type I error (false positives).

2. **Male-only sample**: Only male participants recruited to minimize variability; findings may not generalize to female athletes given menstrual cycle influences on substrate metabolism.

3. **Carryover effects**: 5 of 9 participants had detectable CBD metabolites at placebo session Baseline (from prior CBD treatment ≥7 days earlier), though concentrations were low and unlikely to meaningfully affect results.

4. **Suboptimal timing**: Plasma CBD concentrations were relatively low during initial stages of submaximal exercise; exercise may have started before reaching Tmax (206 ± 25 min).

5. **High dose**: 300 mg dose selected for clinical effects; may not reflect doses in over-the-counter products (~5-150 mg) used by general athletic population.

**Open questions:**

- What is the mechanism by which CBD increases VO2 and VO2max? Is vasodilation the primary mechanism?
- Why does CBD reduce plasma anandamide during exercise (opposite to chronic CBD effects in clinical populations)?
- Do effects persist or differ with chronic CBD administration vs. acute dosing?
- Are effects consistent across sexes, fitness levels, and exercise modalities?
- Do lower doses (5-150 mg) produce similar effects to the 300 mg dose used here?
- Can CBD's anti-inflammatory effects protect against exercise-induced muscle damage in more demanding protocols (heat stress, eccentric loading)?

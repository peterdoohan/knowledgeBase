### One-line summary

A neuroimaging-focused review synthesising how acute sleep deprivation disrupts the human brain across five functional domains — attention, working memory, reward/incentive processing, negative emotion, and hippocampal memory encoding — and explores the mechanistic and clinical implications of those disruptions.

---

### Background & motivation

Sleep deprivation (SD) is near-ubiquitous in industrialised societies and co-occurs with every major neurological and psychiatric disorder, yet frameworks explaining how the brain is specifically altered by SD are comparatively underdeveloped relative to frameworks explaining sleep's benefits. The effects of SD are not simply the mirror-image of sleep's benefits; extended wakefulness introduces additional pathological factors (e.g. adenosine accumulation, dopaminergic changes) that are independent of absent sleep per se. A mechanistic account grounded in neuroimaging is needed to understand which brain networks are selectively vulnerable, to explain observed behavioural impairments, and to identify therapeutic targets in clinical conditions associated with sleep disruption.

---

### Methods

- Scope: neuroimaging studies (primarily fMRI, with some PET) of healthy adults undergoing acute total SD (24–48 hours) or partial sleep restriction, covering five functional domains.
- Synthesis method: narrative review; no formal meta-analytic inclusion criteria are stated, but key papers are highlighted for their specific contributions.
- Domains covered: sustained and selective attention; working memory; positive/reward-related affect and incentive processing; negative/aversive emotional processing; hippocampus-dependent memory encoding.
- Additional topics: resting-state functional connectivity changes (Box 2); dopaminergic mechanisms (including PET ligand-binding data on D2/D3 receptors); noradrenergic mechanisms (REM sleep model); cholinergic pharmacological rescue; clinical insights in PTSD, addiction, obesity, and Alzheimer disease (Box 3); acute versus chronic SD and inter-individual variability (Box 1).

---

### Key findings

**Attention and working memory**
- SD reliably reduces fMRI signal in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) and intraparietal sulcus during attentional and working-memory tasks.
- The thalamus shows variable activity: elevated thalamic activity correlates with preserved performance; reduced thalamic activity coincides with attentional lapses, suggesting it gates cortical arousal under SD.
- The default mode network (DMN) fails to disengage during task performance under SD; the degree of aberrant on-task DMN activity predicts severity of both attention and working-memory impairment.
- TMS applied to extrastriate cortex can partially rescue visual working-memory performance in sleep-deprived individuals; repeated application every 6 hours maintained performance for up to 3 days without sleep.
- Resting-state connectivity is broadly reduced by SD — within-DMN connectivity falls, and adaptive DMN–attentional-network decoupling is impaired.

**Reward and incentive processing**
- One night of SD increases ventral striatum fMRI activity during monetary reward anticipation and receipt; frontal valuation regions (mPFC, OFC, insula) show amplified responses to rewarding stimuli but fail to accurately discriminate between reward magnitudes.
- PET shows that total SD downregulates dopamine D2/D3 receptor (D2/3R) availability in dorsal and ventral striatum; degree of D2/3R downregulation predicts subjective sleepiness and reduced thalamic activity.
- A proposed mechanism: adenosine accumulation with extended wakefulness drives D2/3R internalisation and reduces D2R affinity for dopamine via A2A–D2R allosteric interaction, indirectly elevating D1R-mediated approach behaviour.
- SD impairs accurate updating of reward value over time (Iowa Gambling Task) and increases effort discounting (favouring low-effort rewards).
- SD-associated increases in striatal reward responses are sensitive to individual dopamine-transporter genotype.

**Aversive emotion processing**
- One night of SD produces ~60% increase in amygdala fMRI reactivity to negative images; replicated after partial sleep restriction (4–5 hours/night) and in habitually poor sleepers.
- SD reduces functional connectivity between mPFC and amygdala, consistent with loss of top-down prefrontal regulatory control.
- SD elevates cue-evoked anticipatory activity in amygdala, anterior insula, and anterior cingulate cortex; trait anxiety amplifies the SD-related increase in anticipatory insula activity.
- The salience-detection network (amygdala, anterior insula, anterior cingulate) loses its graded sensitivity under SD, producing over-generalised emotional reactivity and impaired discrimination of emotional intensity and valence.
- SD disrupts central–peripheral autonomic coupling, impairing outward emotional expression and empathetic accuracy.
- Proposed mechanism: SD prevents the normal overnight REM-sleep-dependent reduction in noradrenergic tone from locus coeruleus, resulting in hypernoradrenergic state and over-generalised affective sensitivity.

**Hippocampal memory encoding**
- SD impairs learning and reduces hippocampal encoding-related fMRI activity; selective NREM slow-wave sleep deprivation (with total sleep time preserved) similarly reduces hippocampal encoding.
- SD decreases hippocampal functional coupling with encoding-relevant cortical regions (intraparietal sulcus, posterior cingulate, primary visual cortex) but increases connectivity with subcortical arousal regions (thalamus, brainstem), interpreted as compensatory but insufficient.
- CA3–dentate gyrus hippocampal subfield volume predicts vulnerability to SD-induced encoding impairment and magnitude of recovery after sleep.
- Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (donepezil) partially restores frontoparietal and fusiform cortical activity in sleep-deprived individuals, with greatest benefit in those most vulnerable to SD.
- Anterior cingulate and precuneus DMN activity discriminated sleep-deprived from rested participants during encoding with 93% sensitivity and 92% specificity.

### Computational framework

Not applicable in a formal sense — the paper does not develop or test a computational model. However, it implicitly invokes a signal-to-noise (SNR) or gain-control framework at several points: SD is argued to reduce the dynamic range of neural sensitivity in valuation (PFC reward coding) and affective salience (amygdala/insula) circuits, producing over-generalised responses that fail to track incremental stimulus differences. This SNR framing is informal and descriptive rather than formalised.

The paper also references adenosine-mediated homeostatic sleep pressure as a key biochemical variable linking time-awake to neural dysfunction, consistent with the two-process model of sleep regulation (Borbély 1982).

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's introduction frames the field as having well-characterised accounts of what sleep does (consolidation, emotional regulation, hippocampal encoding, etc.) and some characterisation of the behavioural consequences of SD, but with an underdeveloped understanding of the specific neural mechanisms by which SD impairs brain function. The dominant assumption prior to the neuroimaging era was that SD effects could be largely inferred by reversing the benefits of sleep. The paper argues this reverse-inference approach is insufficient because SD is a composite state (absent sleep plus extended wakefulness plus adenosine accumulation plus neurochemical changes) rather than simply "no sleep." The existing neuroimaging literature at the time of writing had established that frontoparietal networks, thalamus, amygdala, and hippocampus are affected, but a coherent mechanistic account — especially linking neurochemical changes (dopamine, noradrenaline, adenosine, acetylcholine) to network-level dysfunction — was lacking.

---

### What this paper contributes

This review synthesises neuroimaging evidence to establish a multi-domain account of how SD alters brain function:

1. It proposes that network instability — not merely reduced activity — is the core neural phenotype of SD, particularly the failure of reciprocal DMN–task-network suppression that underlies attention and working-memory impairments.
2. It articulates a dopaminergic mechanism for SD-related reward hypersensitivity: adenosine-driven D2/3R downregulation leads to a D1/D2 receptor imbalance that biases approach behaviour and impairs graded reward coding.
3. It proposes a noradrenergic (REM sleep) model of affective dysregulation: failure to reduce noradrenergic tone during REM sleep creates hypernoradrenergic tone that over-sensitises the affective salience network and impairs emotional discrimination.
4. It identifies the CA3–dentate gyrus subfield volume as a novel biomarker of vulnerability to SD-induced memory encoding deficits.
5. It integrates resting-state and task-based evidence to argue that SD degrades intrinsic network integrity before task demands are imposed.
6. It identifies chronic sleep loss (rather than acute total SD) and individual differences (trait vulnerability, genetics, anatomy) as major gaps in the field.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)** — reliably deactivated by SD during attention, working memory, and episodic memory encoding tasks; site of top-down attentional control and goal-directed behaviour.
- **Intraparietal sulcus (IPS)** — reduced activity under SD during attention and working-memory tasks; part of the frontoparietal attention network.
- **Thalamus** — pivotal gating hub; variable activity under SD that tracks performance; mediates ascending arousal and cortical attentional state.
- **Default mode network (DMN)** — fails to disengage from task states under SD; disrupted within-network connectivity; impaired decoupling from attentional networks.
- **Medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC)** — reduced connectivity with amygdala (loss of regulatory control); impaired reward valuation and emotion discrimination coding.
- **Amygdala** — hyper-reactive to negative and even neutral stimuli after SD; reduced top-down mPFC regulation; part of the affective salience network.
- **Anterior insula** — amplified anticipatory and salience responses under SD; impaired graded sensitivity to emotional intensity; implicated in interoceptive processing.
- **Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** — part of salience-detection network; elevated under SD during anticipation of aversive stimuli; reduced discrimination of emotional valence.
- **Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** — impaired valuation signalling under SD; fails to accurately code reward vs. punishment.
- **Striatum (ventral and dorsal)** — amplified reward-anticipation fMRI signal under SD; D2/3R downregulation demonstrated by PET; affected by adenosine-dopamine interaction.
- **Hippocampus (particularly CA3–dentate gyrus)** — reduced encoding-related activity after SD; decreased connectivity with sensory cortex; increased connectivity with subcortical arousal regions; subfield volume predicts vulnerability.
- **Extrastriate (visual) cortex** — reduced activity during visual working memory under SD; site of TMS rescue interventions.
- **Locus coeruleus** — proposed source of hypernoradrenergic tone under SD as REM sleep is curtailed.
- **Right frontoinsula / salience network** — proposed controller of DMN–task-network switching; shows reduced activity under SD.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper approaches but does not fully meet the bar for mechanistic insight. It presents proposed algorithms (e.g. adenosine–D2/3R–D1R imbalance for reward hypersensitivity; REM sleep–noradrenaline model for affective dysregulation) and cites neural imaging data (fMRI, PET ligand binding) consistent with those proposals. However:

- For the dopaminergic reward model: PET evidence of D2/3R downregulation (Volkow et al., 2012) is cited as supporting the D1/D2 imbalance hypothesis, and fMRI striatal hyperactivation is noted. But the specific prediction that D1R-mediated signalling drives the observed striatal fMRI amplification is based on a limited set of studies (e.g. Knutson & Gibbs, 2007) and is described as a "tenable hypothesis" requiring experimental testing.
- For the noradrenergic model: the proposal that SD prevents normal REM-sleep-mediated noradrenaline reduction is supported by indirect evidence (REM-sleep deprivation studies, noradrenaline measurement in PTSD), but no direct human neuroimaging study measuring noradrenergic activity alongside SD-induced amygdala hyper-reactivity is presented.

Mapped to Marr's levels where the evidence is strongest (attention/working memory):
- **Computational**: the brain must selectively allocate attention to task-relevant stimuli and suppress irrelevant processing (DMN) to support goal-directed behaviour.
- **Algorithmic**: ascending arousal (thalamic gating) maintains reciprocal suppression between frontoparietal task networks and the DMN; SD destabilises this reciprocal inhibition, producing erratic on/off-task switching.
- **Implementational**: thalamocortical circuits mediate arousal gating; DLPFC and IPS provide top-down attentional control; adenosine build-up may locally depress both thalamic gating and prefrontal function. The specific cell types and biophysical mechanisms are not addressed.

Overall, the paper synthesises convergent evidence at the computational and algorithmic levels, with partial but not definitive implementational grounding.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Most reviewed studies use acute total SD (24–48 hours); the neural effects of chronic partial sleep restriction (weeks to years, more ecologically common) are largely uncharacterised.
- Brain recovery from SD is poorly understood: the mechanisms, time course, and sleep-stage requirements for neural restoration are unknown.
- Significant inter-individual variability in SD vulnerability (with heritable components) is documented but the neural correlates of resilience vs. vulnerability are not fully explained.
- Within-subject, between-domain differences in vulnerability are raised as a hypothesis but have not been tested: a person resilient to SD in attention may be vulnerable in emotion, suggesting domain-specific network susceptibility.
- Inconsistencies across studies in reward processing (not all studies show amplified striatal responses) are attributed to mixed-gamble designs, sex differences, and dopamine-transporter genetics, but no unifying resolution is proposed.
- Microsleeps during fMRI scanning confound resting-state and even task-based results; objective monitoring of sleep–wake state inside the scanner is highlighted as an unmet need.
- The adenosine–D1/D2 imbalance model and the REM–noradrenaline model are both explicitly framed as hypotheses awaiting direct experimental test.
- The translational gap between basic SD neuroscience and clinical practice remains wide; sleep disruption is not yet routinely targeted in the diagnosis or treatment of neurological and psychiatric disorders.

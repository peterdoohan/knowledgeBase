### One-line summary

This review synthesises the molecular machinery — phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and scaffolding-protein binding — that controls the full life cycle of connexin-based gap junctions from biosynthesis through assembly, internalization, and degradation.

---

### Background & motivation

Gap junctions (GJs) are the only cellular structures that allow direct cytoplasmic communication between neighbouring cells, playing essential roles in development, tissue homeostasis, and electrical coupling. Despite extensive study of connexin (Cx) structure, the regulatory mechanisms that govern when and how GJ plaques are assembled and turned over were incompletely understood as of 2013. GJ channels have a surprisingly short half-life of 1–5 hours, indicating tight and dynamic regulation. This review addresses the gap in understanding of how posttranslational modifications and protein–protein interactions orchestrate the Cx43 life cycle, with implications for cardiac, hepatic, and neurological physiology and disease.

---

### Methods

This is a narrative review focused primarily on the best-characterised connexin isoform, Cx43 (GJA1), with selected reference to other isoforms. The review covers:

- Structural analysis: integration of published X-ray crystal (Cx26 GJ channel at 3.5 Å) and NMR (Cx43 COOH-terminal tail) structures to contextualise regulatory sites.
- Phosphorylation: survey of kinases (Akt, PKA, CK1, CDC2, MAPKs, PKC, Src) and their phosphorylation sites on the Cx43 COOH terminus, drawing on in vitro kinase assays, cell culture overexpression, mutational analyses, and electrophoretic mobility shift studies.
- Ubiquitination: review of proteasomal (Lys48-linked), endolysosomal (Lys63-linked/HRS/Tsg101), and autophagic (p62/SQSTM1) degradation pathways.
- Endocytosis machinery: synthesis of evidence for clathrin-mediated GJ internalization via AP-2, Dab2, dynamin2, and myosin VI, and clathrin-independent pathways via Nedd4/Eps15.
- ZO-1 interaction: review of structural and functional studies linking ZO-1's PDZ2 domain to the Cx43 COOH terminus.
- Scope: primarily mammalian cell culture and in vivo cardiac/hepatic systems; invertebrate innexins and pannexins are noted but not extensively covered.

---

### Key findings

- The Cx43 COOH-terminal tail is the primary regulatory hub: at least 15 phosphorylation sites have been identified, with CK1 (S325/328/330), PKA (S364/365), and Akt (S373) promoting GJ assembly and stability, while CDC2 (S255), MAPKs (S255/279/282), PKC (S368), and Src (Y247/265) drive channel closure and internalization.
- PKA phosphorylation of S365 acts as a "gate-keeper" that prevents PKC phosphorylation of S368, thereby stabilising GJ plaques.
- Phosphorylation events cause long-range conformational changes in the Cx43 COOH terminus that allosterically regulate distal binding sites (e.g., Src binding at P274–P283 disrupts ZO-1 binding at D379–I382).
- GJ internalization is primarily clathrin-mediated, requiring AP-2, Dab2, dynamin2, and myosin VI; knockdown of any component reduces annular GJ (AGJ) vesicle formation and increases plaque size.
- Two AP-2–binding tyrosine-sorting signals (Y265AYF and Y286KLV) in Cx43 mediate internalization; mutation of Y286 increases Cx43 half-life ~3.5-fold.
- Internalized AGJ vesicles are degraded via autophagy (colocalization with LC3, p62, Beclin1) under basal and starvation conditions; inhibition of autophagy causes accumulation of cytoplasmic AGJ vesicles.
- Ubiquitination of Cx43 serves dual roles: Lys48-linked chains target unassembled polypeptides to the proteasome; Lys63-linked chains (via Nedd4, Smurf2, HRS/Tsg101) signal GJ internalization and lysosomal/autophagic degradation.
- ZO-1 binds the extreme COOH terminus of Cx43 (DLEI motif; I382 essential) via a domain-swapped PDZ2 dimer; ZO-1 localises to the outer rim ("perinexus") of GJ plaques and regulates plaque size and connexon accretion, but does not control GJ internalization rate per se.
- Only ~0.1–10% of GJ channels in a plaque are electrically active at any time; spatial segregation of new (peripheral, ZO-1-bound, active) and old (central, phosphorylated, inactive) channels suggests a regulated assembly/disassembly cycle within plaques.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

As framed in the introduction, GJ channels were understood structurally — six connexin subunits per hemichannel, head-on docking across apposing plasma membranes, dense packing into plaques — and were known to turn over rapidly (~1–5 h half-life). A handful of binding partners (ZO-1, Src, caveolin-1, catenins, tubulin) were known, and phosphorylation had been linked to GJIC regulation. However, the field lacked a coherent mechanistic account of how specific posttranslational modifications and binding partners coordinate the sequential steps of GJ assembly, lateral accretion into plaques, channel closure, endocytic removal as intact double-membrane AGJ vesicles, and degradation. The prevailing view treated GJ regulation as largely governed by channel gating; the idea that internalization and degradation are as tightly regulated as assembly was not fully developed.

---

### What this paper contributes

The review synthesises converging evidence into a coherent model of the Cx43 life cycle governed by sequential posttranslational modifications: Akt/PKA/CK1 phosphorylation promotes forward trafficking and plaque assembly; Src/MAPK/PKC/CDC2 phosphorylation drives channel closure; ubiquitination (Nedd4) at GJ plaques signals clathrin-mediated or Eps15-dependent internalization; AGJ vesicles are then routed to autophagic or endo-/lysosomal degradation via p62/SQSTM1 or HRS/Tsg101. Critically, it establishes that these events are spatially organised within individual plaques (new channels at the rim, old at the centre) and that ZO-1 participates in controlling connexon accretion rather than internalization per se. The review identifies that many regulatory mechanisms described for Cx43 cannot be simply extrapolated to other isoforms, and flags several unresolved controversies (e.g., Cx32/ZO-1 interaction, clathrin-independent pathways, which specific ubiquitin-linkage types predominate in vivo).

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for this section. It is a review of biochemical and cell-biological data (kinase assays, co-immunoprecipitation, live-cell fluorescence imaging, dominant-negative overexpression) and does not present or formalise an algorithm in the computational sense, nor does it provide neural recordings or imaging data linking specific molecular mechanisms to neural function. It describes the biochemical machinery implementing GJ turnover at Marr's implementational level for non-neural cells, but does not address algorithmic or computational levels as applied to neural computation.

### Limitations & open questions

- The majority of mechanistic data derives from Cx43 in cell culture (HeLa, rat fibroblasts, cardiomyocytes); generalisability to other Cx isoforms and in vivo contexts is largely untested.
- The specific ubiquitin linkage types (Lys48 vs. Lys63) and chain lengths on assembled GJ plaques in situ have not been directly measured.
- The Cx32/ZO-1 (and Cx30/ZO-1) interaction remains disputed; structural basis unclear.
- Which endocytic pathway (CME vs. Eps15/Nedd4-dependent) predominates under different physiological conditions is unknown.
- Spatial resolution of phosphorylation states within individual plaques (rim vs. centre distribution) has not been directly demonstrated.
- COOH-terminal GFP tags on Cx43 eliminate ZO-1 binding and render channels non-functional, creating artefacts in many published live-imaging studies; development of alternative tools is needed.
- The role of SUMOylation (Cx43-K144, K237) and its interplay with ubiquitination is not yet understood.
- Autophagic degradation pathway details (which autophagy pathway, which receptors) remain to be elucidated.

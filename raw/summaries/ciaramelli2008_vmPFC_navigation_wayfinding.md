### One-line summary

A single patient with bilateral ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) damage showed severe wayfinding impairment despite intact landmark knowledge and spatial memory, attributable to a failure to maintain goal destinations in working memory, which was substantially remediated by periodic goal rehearsal.

---

### Background & motivation

Spatial navigation is known to depend on a distributed network of brain regions, but the functional contribution of ventromedial and ventrolateral prefrontal cortex — which is activated during navigation in fMRI studies — was unclear prior to this paper. Specifically, it was not known whether vmPFC activity during navigation reflects encoding of goal destinations into long-term memory, or an online working memory maintenance function. Neuropsychological case studies of patients with vmPFC lesions and navigation impairment were lacking, leaving the necessity of this region unestablished. This paper fills that gap by presenting the first documented case of a vmPFC-lesioned patient with specific topographical disorientation in a familiar environment.

---

### Methods

- **Design**: Single case neuropsychological study (patient LG) with laboratory and ecological components.
- **Patient**: LG, a 56-year-old male with bilateral vmPFC lesion (BAs 10, 11, 12, 32; right > left; plus BA 24 on left medial wall) following anterior communicating artery aneurysm rupture and clipping.
- **Neuropsychological profiling**: Comprehensive battery covering general intelligence, short- and long-term memory, attention, working memory (2-back task, PASAT, Visual Elevator), executive function (WCST, verbal fluency), and spatial memory (Corsi block).
- **Laboratory wayfinding task**: 10 location pairs within LG's home town. LG verbally described routes under four conditions: Standard (no cues), Remind (goal name flashed every 15 s), Rehearse (prompted to mentally rehearse goal every 15 s), Warning (neutral alerting signal every 15 s). Each pair tested twice; two age-matched controls included. Performance compared using Wilcoxon and Sign tests.
- **Error analysis**: Route familiarity, location familiarity (self-rated and spouse-rated), route length, number of turns, and number of landmarks were examined as predictors of navigation accuracy.
- **Ecological study**: Real-world navigation between landmarks under the same four conditions (n = 10 trials each); prompts delivered by a human operator or via mobile phone.
- **Landmark recognition memory test**: Recognition of 15 familiar local landmarks vs. 15 unfamiliar foils; directional knowledge probed for correctly identified landmarks.

---

### Key findings

- **Laboratory task**: LG succeeded on 7/20 (35%) trials in the Standard condition, 16/20 (80%) in the Remind condition, 13/20 (65%) in the Rehearse condition, and 9/20 (45%) in the Warning condition. Both Remind (Z = 2.4, p < 0.05) and Rehearse (Z = 2.2, p < 0.05) significantly outperformed Standard; Warning did not (p = 0.4).
- **Ecological study**: Success rates mirrored the lab findings — Standard 3/10 (30%), Remind 7/10 (70%), Rehearse 7/10 (70%), Warning 2/10 (20%).
- **Error analysis**: LG's errors reflected "attraction" to highly familiar locations (mean familiarity rating 9.00) rather than the goal locations (mean rating 6.15; t(25) = 2.35, p < 0.05). Routes containing familiar lure locations were harder than routes without (familiarity ratings 7.14 vs. 6.0, p = 0.07). Route length, number of turns, and landmark density did not predict errors.
- **Goal knowledge preserved**: On incorrect trials, LG could recall the goal destination 70% of the time across conditions, confirming that long-term memory for goals was largely intact.
- **Landmark recognition**: LG's recognition of familiar local landmarks was within the normal range (12/15 vs. controls' 14–15), and directional knowledge around landmarks was nearly flawless (1 error vs. 0 for controls, non-significant).
- **Rehabilitation**: After training to rehearse his goal mentally while navigating, LG was able to travel to work independently, and generalised the strategy to other daily activities.

### Computational framework

Not applicable in a formal sense. The paper is purely empirical with no explicit computational model. However, the discussion frames LG's deficits in terms of **attractor network dynamics**: the authors (citing Treves, 2005) propose that vmPFC damage leaves cortical attractor representations of familiar places intact while disrupting the "grammar" governing transitions between attractor states, causing the system to fall into strong, familiar attractors. Additionally, the discussion invokes **reversal learning** as a complementary account: vmPFC may assign and update the reward/goal value of locations, and its damage impairs the suppression of previously rewarded spatial responses. The results most directly constrain models of **goal maintenance in working memory** during action sequences.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to this paper, the field understood topographical disorientation primarily through a taxonomy based on posterior cortical lesions — landmark agnosia, egocentric disorientation, heading disorientation — with vmPFC implicated in navigation only via fMRI correlations (Spiers & Maguire, 2006). The dominant view was that navigation deficits arise from failures in perceptual or representational processes (landmark recognition, route knowledge, allocentric/egocentric transformation) supported by posterior parietal, occipital, and medial temporal regions. The prefrontal cortex was acknowledged to support planning and goal encoding, but its necessity for online navigation in a familiar environment was unestablished. The paper's introduction signals that vmPFC was a candidate region without confirmed causal necessity.

---

### What this paper contributes

This paper establishes that vmPFC is causally necessary for successful navigation in a familiar environment — not for encoding or retrieving spatial knowledge, but for maintaining goal destinations in an active state throughout navigation. It dissociates long-term spatial memory (intact in LG) from online goal maintenance (impaired), and shows that the specific failure mode is interference from high-familiarity "attractor" locations. The finding that Rehearse cues (which provided no external goal information) were as effective as Remind cues (which re-presented the goal) argues that the core deficit is in sustaining active goal representation, not in short-term forgetting. This constitutes a new functional role for vmPFC — prospective goal maintenance during extended action sequences — that is distinct from its previously described roles in reversal learning, long-term memory encoding, and confabulation. The rehabilitation finding demonstrates clinical translational value: externally supported goal rehearsal can compensate for this deficit.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC; BAs 10, 11, 12, 32)** — primary lesion site in LG; proposed to be necessary for maintaining goal destinations in working memory during navigation; implicated by the paper as the neural substrate preventing capture by familiar attractor locations.
- **Rostral anterior cingulate cortex (BA 32)** — part of LG's lesion; co-implicated in goal maintenance and action monitoring.
- **Left medial prefrontal wall (BA 24)** — smaller additional lesion in LG.
- **Posterior parietal cortex** — mentioned as supporting egocentric navigation (not directly investigated).
- **Lingual gyrus** — mentioned as supporting landmark identification (not directly investigated).
- **Medial prefrontal / orbitofrontal cortex** — cited from rodent and human literature as tracking current navigational goals and spatial reward value.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the full bar for mechanistic insight at Marr's algorithmic and implementational levels. It provides behavioural dissociation evidence (condition comparisons) and lesion localisation, but does not present neural recording, imaging, or pharmacological data from LG that would link a specific computational process (e.g., a goal maintenance signal) to measured neural activity. The lesion specifies where, but not how the mechanism operates.

What the paper does establish is:

- **Computational**: The brain must maintain a representation of the current goal destination in a state accessible to action selection throughout an extended navigation sequence, in the face of interference from competing, high-familiarity representations.
- **Algorithmic**: vmPFC supports active rehearsal/refreshing of goal representations; without this, the system is captured by the strongest available attractor (most familiar location). The algorithm requires more than initial goal encoding — it requires continuous or periodic reactivation.
- **Implementational**: vmPFC (BAs 10, 11, 12, 32) is a necessary node, but the cellular or circuit-level implementation is not addressed.

The paper does not provide neural data linking the proposed goal-maintenance process to specific vmPFC activity patterns, which is why it falls short of the full mechanistic bar.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Single case study: conclusions rest on one patient (LG); replication in additional vmPFC-lesioned patients is needed.
- LG has executive function and working memory deficits beyond spatial navigation (impaired WCST, 2-back, PASAT), making it difficult to isolate the specific contribution of vmPFC to navigational goal maintenance from more general WM or inhibitory control deficits.
- Reversal learning was not formally tested in LG; the reversal-learning interpretation is inferential.
- The relative contributions of goal maintenance vs. interference suppression (reversal learning) cannot be fully dissociated in this study.
- Controls were only 2 individuals; statistical comparison power is low.
- The distance between attractor locations and goal locations was not correlated with navigation success (as predicted by a goal-proximity reward-updating account), but the analysis may have been underpowered.
- The paper does not specify whether the vmPFC's role is specific to spatial navigation or extends to all extended action sequences requiring prospective goal maintenance (though the discussion raises this parallel).

### One-line summary

Optogenetic silencing of the medial entorhinal cortex reduces sharp-wave ripple incidence, mimics cortical DOWN states, and alters the neuronal assembly content of ripples while preserving the ability to rapidly restore baseline patterns upon stimulation cessation.

### Background & motivation

Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples (SPW-Rs) are critical for memory consolidation and are believed to be primarily intrinsic to the hippocampus, persisting even after cortical deafferentation. However, SPW-Rs in vivo are irregularly occurring and coordinated with neocortical UP-DOWN states, suggesting that extrahippocampal inputs may modulate their timing and content. The specific role of the medial entorhinal cortex (mEC), the major cortical gateway to the hippocampus, in regulating SPW-Rs remains unclear.

### Methods

- **Subjects**: Male C57Bl/6 transgenic mice (Grik4-cre) and wildtype mice
- **Viral injections**: AAV5-mDlx-ChR2-mCherry in mEC to express ChR2 in GABAergic interneurons; AAV1-CamKIIa-stGtACR2-FusionRed in CA1 for pyramidal cell silencing
- **Recordings**: Silicon probes (64-128 channels) implanted in hippocampus; simultaneous neocortical recordings in subset of mice
- **Stimulation**: 5-s square light pulses every 30 s during home cage NREM sleep; stimulation during delayed spatial alternation task for awake recordings
- **Manipulations**: Ipsilateral mEC, contralateral mEC, bilateral mEC, CA1, and combined mEC+CA1 silencing
- **Analysis**: SPW-R detection (120-220 Hz filtered LFP), current source density (CSD) mapping, cell assembly analysis (PCA-ICA), rank-order correlation for sequence analysis, UP-DOWN state detection from neocortex

### Key findings

- **SPW-R incidence**: All mEC and CA1 silencing configurations significantly reduced SPW-R incidence; ipsilateral mEC silencing showed ~50% reduction; effect was largest in first second after stimulation
- **Rebound effect**: mEC silencing led to rebound increase in SPW-Rs after stimulation cessation (unlike CA1 silencing which showed continued decrease)
- **CSD analysis**: mEC silencing reduced SPW-R-related currents in stratum lacunosum-moleculare (slm) and dentate molecular layer (ml), the target layers of mEC; CA1 silencing did not affect these layers
- **Ripple features**: Bilateral mEC and local CA1 silencing decreased ripple amplitude and duration; ipsilateral mEC silencing decreased proportion of ripples associated with detectable SPW
- **Neocortical coordination**: mEC silencing impaired bidirectional communication between hippocampal SPW-Rs and neocortical UP-DOWN states; DOWN state duration increased during mEC silencing
- **Assembly content**: mEC silencing altered CA1 cell assembly expression during SPW-Rs; absolute change in assembly expression was significantly higher during ipsilateral and bilateral mEC silencing
- **Sequence reactivation**: Rank-order correlation analysis showed that mEC silencing significantly decreased the consistency of spike sequences during SPW-Rs, suggesting scrambled sequences
- **Rapid restoration**: Neuronal assembly patterns returned immediately to pre-stimulation baseline upon cessation of mEC silencing
- **Awake SPW-Rs**: mEC silencing effects on awake SPW-Rs during behavior were less consistent than during sleep, though assembly content was still altered

### Computational framework

Not applicable. This is an empirical optogenetic manipulation study. However, the results are interpreted within a dynamical systems framework where SPW-R occurrence is conceptualized as a stochastic process driven by neocortical-entorhinal inputs, with the mEC providing "tonic" (sustained excitability) and "phasic" (transient UP-state) influences on hippocampal network states.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The prevailing model holds that SPW-Rs are intrinsic to the hippocampus, generated by the recurrent collateral system of CA2-3 pyramidal cells that depolarize CA1 neurons, creating the sharp-wave current sink in stratum radiatum. The ripple oscillation arises from transient strong excitation of perisomatic interneurons and pyramidal cells. This intrinsic model is supported by the persistence of SPW-Rs after neocortical/entorhinal lesions, subcortical deafferentation, and even in vitro in hippocampal slice preparations. The field also recognized that in vivo SPW-Rs are irregularly occurring and coordinated with neocortical UP-DOWN states, but the specific role of mEC in regulating SPW-R timing and content was not well established.

### What this paper contributes

This paper establishes that the mEC plays a critical role in both timing and content of hippocampal SPW-Rs, updating the prevailing intrinsic model:

1. **Incidence regulation**: mEC provides tonic excitatory drive necessary for SPW-R occurrence; silencing mEC reduces SPW-R incidence by ~50%, mimicking the effect of cortical DOWN states
2. **Content selection**: mEC inputs bias which neuronal assemblies are recruited during SPW-Rs and shape the sequential organization of spikes; silencing mEC alters assembly expression and "scrambles" spike sequences
3. **Rapid reversibility**: The effects on assembly content are immediately reversible upon cessation of mEC silencing, suggesting that mEC inputs dynamically select from preexisting hippocampal trajectory repertoires rather than permanently reorganizing circuits
4. **Mechanism**: The mEC provides both tonic (sustained excitability) and phasic (UP-state coordinated) influences on hippocampal networks, with the phasic component helping explain the irregular, lognormal distribution of SPW-R intervals in vivo

These findings suggest that SPW-Rs are not purely intrinsic events but are modulated by cortical inputs that determine both when they occur and what they contain.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Medial entorhinal cortex (mEC)**: Source of cortical input to hippocampus; silencing here reduces SPW-R incidence and alters content; provides both tonic and phasic excitation to hippocampus; target layers are stratum lacunosum-moleculare (CA1) and dentate molecular layer
- **Hippocampal CA1**: Recording site for SPW-Rs; receives mEC input in stratum lacunosum-moleculare; contains the pyramidal cells whose assemblies are modified during mEC silencing; local silencing reduces SPW-Rs but with different recovery dynamics than mEC silencing
- **Hippocampal CA3**: Source of recurrent collateral input that drives SPW generation; mEC silencing reduces CA3 firing rates; strong commissural connections between hemispheres explain contralateral mEC effects
- **Dentate gyrus**: Receives strong mEC layer 2 input; dentate spikes virtually abolished by mEC silencing; granule cell firing suppressed by mEC silencing
- **Neocortex (overlying posterior parietal and anterior visual areas)**: Shows UP-DOWN states during NREM sleep that coordinate with SPW-Rs; mEC silencing impairs bidirectional communication between cortex and hippocampus during SPW-Rs; DOWN states lengthen during mEC silencing

### Mechanistic insight

This paper provides mechanistic insight meeting the high bar: it identifies the mEC as a critical regulator of hippocampal SPW-Rs through both algorithmic and implementational levels, supported by neural data from optogenetic manipulations.

**Computational level**: The brain must solve the problem of when to trigger memory consolidation events (SPW-Rs) and which memories to reactivate. The irregular, lognormal distribution of SPW-R intervals in vivo suggests a stochastic process rather than an intrinsic oscillator.

**Algorithmic level**: The mEC implements a two-component mechanism: (1) tonic excitation that maintains hippocampal excitability and gates SPW-R occurrence, and (2) phasic excitation coordinated with neocortical UP states that biases the timing and content of SPW-Rs. The mEC selects which neuronal assemblies are recruited during SPW-Rs from a preexisting repertoire of possible trajectories, effectively "scrambling" sequences when silenced. The lognormal distribution of SPW-R intervals is explained by the stochastic drive from mEC UP-DOWN transitions.

**Implementational level**: The mechanism is implemented through specific anatomical projections: mEC layer 2/3 cells project to stratum lacunosum-moleculare (CA1) and dentate molecular layer, providing direct excitation to CA1 pyramidal cells and interneurons. The balance of excitation to pyramidal cells and interneurons explains the modest effect on CA1 firing rates. Optogenetic silencing of mEC GABAergic interneurons (via ChR2) suppresses mEC output, effectively mimicking natural DOWN states. The effect propagates through the hippocampal circuit: reduced granule cell and CA3 pyramidal cell firing, diminished dentate spikes, and altered CA1 assembly dynamics.

### Limitations & open questions

- The study did not directly examine replay of waking place cell sequences due to dataset limitations; the effects on memory consolidation were inferred from assembly dynamics rather than directly tested
- The effects of mEC silencing on awake SPW-Rs during behavior were less consistent than during sleep, suggesting state-dependent modulation that was not fully characterized
- The study used transient (5-s) silencing periods; longer-term chronic silencing effects on SPW-Rs and their content remain unexplored
- The relative contributions of mEC layer 2 vs. layer 3 inputs to SPW-R regulation were not dissociated; both project to hippocampal targets
- The specific cell types within mEC (stellate cells, pyramidal cells, interneurons) that are responsible for the observed effects were not identified
- Whether lateral entorhinal cortex contributes similarly or differently to SPW-R regulation was not examined
- The mechanism by which mEC inputs "select" specific neuronal trajectories from the preexisting repertoire remains unclear at the synaptic/circuit level

### One-line summary

The human hippocampal–entorhinal system implicitly encodes a map-like, metric representation of discrete non-spatial relationships between objects, best described by a successor representation (predictive map) rather than Euclidean distance.

---

### Background & motivation

The hippocampal–entorhinal system supports spatial navigation through continuous metric maps (place cells, grid cells), but many real-world relationships are discrete, non-spatial, and learned without conscious awareness. Prior work showed hippocampal encoding of pairwise associations and temporal community structure, but it was unclear whether map- or graph-like knowledge structures — with a proper metric over states — could be encoded implicitly for non-spatial, discrete object relationships. This paper asks whether the same organisational principles that govern spatial cognitive maps extend to abstract relational graphs, and what metric best characterises such representations.

---

### Methods

- **Design**: Two-session, two-group human experiment. Day 1 (implicit learning): participants observed sequences of object images whose transitions were generated by a random walk on a hidden 12-node graph; no mention of any underlying structure; cover task was button-press to object orientation.
- **Neuroimaging cohort (n = 23)**: On day 2, scanned with fMRI (3T) while viewing a reduced 7-node subgraph with randomised (non-structured) transitions. fMRI adaptation (repetition suppression) was used as an index of representational similarity between objects.
- **fMRI analysis**: Event-related GLMs with parametric regressors for (a) link distance, (b) communicability (matrix exponential of adjacency matrix), (c) successor representation, and (d) Euclidean distance. ROIs defined via independent contrasts within the hippocampal–entorhinal system and from an independent study (Chadwick et al., 2015). Multidimensional scaling (MDS) applied to the neural adaptation matrix to visualise reconstructed graph structure.
- **Behavioural cohort (n = 26)**: Performed same two-day paradigm but on day 2 measured response times (rather than fMRI) to the same object transitions. Communicability, link distance, and Euclidean distance were regressed onto log-transformed response times.
- **Controls**: Temporal proximity during training, object familiarity, and graph centrality (number of neighbours) were all tested as alternative predictors and ruled out.

---

### Key findings

- fMRI adaptation in bilateral entorhinal cortex (peak t22 = 4.75 right, t22 = 4.42 left, FWE-corrected within entorhinal/subicular ROI) decreased monotonically with link distance on the graph, recovering the graph structure even though participants had no explicit knowledge of it.
- The distance effect was non-directional (symmetrised shortest path, not directional path, predicted the signal; t22 = 2.78, p = 0.01 vs. t22 = −1.64, p = 0.11), consistent with a map-like rather than sequence-based representation.
- MDS of the neural distance matrix recovered the actual graph topology (r = 0.65 with link distances, p = 0.003 by permutation test; no link crossings in 86.8% of null-distribution graphs had crossings, whereas the recovered map had none).
- When communicability (matrix exponential; a parameter-free instantiation of the successor representation) competed with Euclidean distance, communicability alone predicted entorhinal activity (left hippocampal-entorhinal: FWE p = 0.006 when both included), indicating a non-Euclidean, predictive metric.
- Activity also scaled with the successor representation when gamma was set to the standard value (Figure 4 supplement).
- In the behavioural cohort, response times were longer for object pairs with lower communicability (t25 = 2.77, p = 0.01), while link distance (p = 0.69) and Euclidean distance (p = 0.40) were not significant, confirming implicit behavioural knowledge shaped by the predictive metric.
- After debriefing, no subject reported awareness of the graph structure.

### Computational framework

**Successor representation / predictive map** (Dayan, 1993; Stachenfeld et al., 2016).

The successor representation (SR) defines the value of a state as the discounted expected future occupancy of all states: M = (I − γA)^{−1}, where A is the adjacency matrix and γ < 1 is a discount factor. Entries of M encode how often state j is expected to be visited in the future when starting from state i, giving a predictive distance metric over the graph.

The paper also uses **communicability** (matrix exponential: e^A), which is the parameter-free counterpart from graph theory. Both measures compute a weighted sum over paths of all lengths, giving more weight to shorter paths. Critically, this metric distorts the graph non-uniformly: nodes that participate in many short paths (high "communicability centrality") appear closer together than raw link distance would imply, in contrast to Euclidean distance which would preserve link structure uniformly.

The key modelling assumption is that the hippocampal formation does not represent current state per se, but rather the distribution of likely future states — consistent with proposals that place cells encode predictive rather than current-location information.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper operates against the backdrop of the hippocampal "cognitive map" theory (O'Keefe and Nadel, 1978; O'Keefe and Dostrovsky, 1971). The prevailing view holds that the hippocampal–entorhinal system encodes a continuous metric map of physical space via place cells (hippocampus) and grid cells (entorhinal cortex), supporting flexible spatial navigation. Extensions to non-spatial domains were acknowledged — the hippocampus had been shown to encode temporal proximity, social rank, and two-dimensional abstract spaces defined by continuous dimensions (Constantinescu et al., 2016; Tavares et al., 2015) — but whether the system could form a proper metric map of *discrete*, *non-spatial*, *implicitly learned* associative graphs was unknown. The dominant assumption was that non-spatial hippocampal representations might reflect pairwise associative strength or temporal community structure, not a globally coherent map with a well-defined metric.

---

### What this paper contributes

The results establish that the hippocampal–entorhinal system automatically organises implicit, discrete, non-spatial relational knowledge into a map-like structure with a metric. Key advances over prior knowledge:

1. **Metric vs. association**: Prior work showed hippocampal sensitivity to pairwise associations or community membership. This paper reconstructs a full graph topology from neural data, showing the system encodes globally consistent inter-node distances, not just local links.
2. **Predictive metric**: The neural signal (and behaviour) is best described by the successor representation / communicability, not Euclidean or link distance. This implies the hippocampus does not faithfully reproduce the physical graph but rather warps it according to expected future visitation — the same principle proposed for spatial place and grid cells.
3. **Implicit and non-spatial**: The map forms without explicit awareness, without a continuous organising dimension (e.g. time or space), and for discrete objects. This substantially broadens the domain of map-like hippocampal computation.
4. **Dual validation**: Neural (fMRI adaptation) and behavioural (response times) results converge on the same metric, strengthening causal claims about implicit knowledge guiding behaviour.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Entorhinal cortex (bilateral)** — primary locus of the fMRI adaptation effect; encodes the predictive metric over the discrete object graph. Left peak: [−18, −19, −22]; Right peak: [24, −25, −22] (FWE-corrected within entorhinal/subicular mask). Also shows communicability effect surviving Euclidean-distance competition.
- **Hippocampal formation (including subiculum)** — broader ROI within which effects were also significant via small-volume correction; implicated as the system encoding the successor representation of non-spatial state space.
- **Orbitofrontal cortex** — mentioned in Discussion as representing cognitive maps of decision spaces in other work (Schuck et al., 2016), but not a focus of this paper; no significant effects found there, possibly because learning was implicit and no active use of structure was required.
- **Parahippocampal cortex** — included in the broader anatomical ROI for SVC but not reported as a site of specific effects.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper meets both criteria for mechanistic insight.

1. It formalises an algorithm (the successor representation / communicability) as the computational process underlying the distance metric.
2. It provides fMRI data (adaptation signal in the hippocampal–entorhinal system) that specifically supports the predictive metric over two alternatives (Euclidean distance and raw link distance).

**Computational level**: The brain is solving the problem of inferring the latent relational structure of the environment in order to support flexible, generalising behaviour — including novel inferences about pairs of items never experienced together. The optimal representation for such flexible computation is one that encodes the expected future state distribution, not just current associations.

**Algorithmic level**: The hippocampal–entorhinal system represents each object by a weighted sum of its expected future successors (successor representation), instantiated as communicability in the matrix-exponential formulation. Representational similarity (indexed by fMRI adaptation) between two objects is proportional to their communicability, which distorts the graph non-uniformly relative to Euclidean distance. The representation is non-directional (symmetric) and globally coherent enough to support MDS recovery of the graph topology.

**Implementational level**: The paper does not directly address cellular mechanisms. It identifies entorhinal cortex as the primary neural substrate (consistent with its known role via grid cells in spatial metric computation), but does not identify specific cell types, connectivity patterns, or neuromodulators. The authors note that theoretical work (Stachenfeld et al., 2016) proposes grid-cell firing could arise as a principal component decomposition of the SR transition matrix, suggesting the same circuits supporting spatial grid codes may implement the abstract SR — but this remains theoretical here.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- **fMRI adaptation interpretation**: The adaptation (repetition suppression) signal is an indirect proxy for representational similarity; alternative interpretations (e.g. prediction error rather than representational distance) cannot be fully excluded, though control analyses rule out several confounds.
- **No explicit task use of structure**: Because learning was implicit and subjects never had to use the structure for decisions, it is unclear whether the observed map would be updated or expressed differently during active goal-directed use. Orbitofrontal and parietal areas that show state-prediction errors during active behaviour were not engaged here.
- **Graph size and topology**: The graph had 7 nodes (reduced from 12) and a specific topology. Generalisability to larger, higher-dimensional, or more complex graph structures is untested.
- **Temporal confound**: Temporal proximity and graph distance are correlated in this design; the multiple-regression control suggests link distance dominates, but in other designs with different statistics they might not dissociate cleanly.
- **Mechanism of SR computation**: How the brain actually computes and stores the successor representation remains unclear; no cellular or circuit-level data are provided.
- **Sleep / consolidation**: Whether overnight consolidation (day 1 training, day 2 scanning) is necessary for map formation, or whether maps could form within a single session, is not tested.
- **Individual differences**: No analysis of variability across subjects in map quality or behavioural effect strength.

### One-line summary

The dorsolateral band of the entorhinal cortex is necessary for recall of spatial memory, while the ventromedial band mediates defensive behavior but not spatial memory, revealing a modular functional organization of the entorhinal cortex that mirrors the dorsal-ventral specialization of the hippocampus.

### Background & motivation

The entorhinal cortex serves as the primary interface between the hippocampus and neocortex, yet previous studies using selective excitotoxic lesions of the entorhinal cortex failed to produce the pronounced spatial learning impairments typically observed after hippocampal lesions. This paradox raised questions about whether alternative cortical inputs (perirhinal, postrhinal, presubiculum, parasubiculum) could compensate for entorhinal damage. The present study tested whether this discrepancy arose because previous lesion studies spared the dorsolateral band of the entorhinal cortex, which provides the primary visuospatial input to the dorsal hippocampus.

### Methods

- **Subjects**: 124 naive male Long-Evans rats
- **Behavioral task**: Morris water maze (diameter 198 cm) with hidden platform (reference memory task)
- **Preoperative training**: 6 days of training (2 blocks of 4 trials per day)
- **Lesion groups**:
  - **Dorsolateral band lesions** (n=14 analyzed): NMDA-induced excitotoxic lesions targeting the dorsolateral band of entorhinal cortex using 9 injection sites per hemisphere
  - **Ventromedial band lesions** (n=8 analyzed): NMDA lesions using angled injection approach to avoid hippocampal damage
  - **Perirhinal/postrhinal control lesions** (n=6 analyzed): Control lesions to assess damage to adjacent cortical areas
  - **Mechanical control lesions** (n=10 analyzed): Needle tracks without NMDA to control for mechanical damage
  - **Sham-operated controls** (n=28 for dorsolateral replication, n=8 for ventromedial)
- **Postoperative testing**: Probe trial 7 days after surgery, followed by reversal training with platform in opposite quadrant
- **Elevated plus maze**: Additional testing for defensive behavior (6 rats with ventromedial lesions)

### Key findings

- **Dorsolateral lesions abolished spatial memory retention**: Rats with dorsolateral band lesions spent only 8.7% ± 2.9% of search time in the platform zone (vs. 38.0% ± 3.0% for sham controls), with 13 of 14 rats performing at chance level (0%–16.1% in target zone; chance = 12%)
- **Lesion size correlation**: No significant correlation between lesion size and time in target zone within the dorsolateral group (r = -0.43; p = 0.13)
- **Adjacent damage not responsible**: Perirhinal/postrhinal lesions did not impair spatial memory, and performance in the dorsolateral group was unrelated to the amount of damage in adjacent structures
- **Mechanical damage not responsible**: Mechanical control lesions (needle tracks only) did not impair spatial memory
- **New learning was partially spared**: During reversal training, escape latencies were longer in the dorsolateral group (group effect: F(1,44) = 8.1, p < 0.01), but the final probe trial showed no significant group × quadrant interaction (F = 2.1, p = 0.10)
- **Ventromedial lesions spared spatial memory**: Rats with ventromedial band lesions showed intact retention (32.7% time in target zone vs. 35.5% for sham controls), with no significant group × quadrant interaction (F = 1)
- **Ventromedial lesions affected defensive behavior**: On the elevated plus maze, rats with ventromedial lesions made more entries into open arms (group difference: t(8) = 2.6, p < 0.05), though total time in open arms was not significantly enhanced (t(8) = 1.3, p = 0.2)

### Computational framework

Not applicable. This is a lesion-based behavioral study without explicit computational modeling. However, the findings constrain models of hippocampal-entorhinal information flow, supporting the view that the entorhinal cortex serves as a necessary interface for transmitting spatial information between neocortical visual areas and the hippocampus. The modular organization (dorsolateral vs. ventromedial bands) aligns with functional differentiation models of the hippocampal longitudinal axis.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to this study, the prevailing understanding was that the entorhinal cortex serves as the major interface between the hippocampus and neocortex, with the hippocampus being essential for spatial memory. However, selective excitotoxic lesions of the entorhinal cortex in previous studies had generally failed to produce the pronounced spatial learning impairments observed after hippocampal lesions. This led to alternative hypotheses: (1) that cortical inputs to the hippocampus via sparser routes (perirhinal/postrhinal cortices, pre/parasubiculum) might be sufficient for spatial memory, or (2) that previous entorhinal lesions had been incomplete, particularly sparing the dorsolateral/dorsocaudal regions that provide the strongest visuospatial input to the dorsal hippocampus.

### What this paper contributes

This paper establishes that the dorsolateral band of the entorhinal cortex is necessary for recall of spatial memory, resolving the paradox of spared spatial learning after entorhinal lesions in previous studies. The critical insight is that the entorhinal cortex has a modular functional organization:

1. **Dorsolateral band**: Essential for spatial memory; lesions here completely disrupt retention of preoperatively learned spatial information. This region receives visuospatial input from the postrhinal cortex and projects primarily to the dorsal hippocampus, which is critical for spatial processing.

2. **Ventromedial band**: Not required for spatial memory but mediates defensive behavior (reduced open arm avoidance on elevated plus maze). This region connects primarily with the ventral hippocampus, which is involved in emotion-related behaviors.

The findings demonstrate that the functional differentiation along the dorsal-ventral axis of the hippocampus reflects its connectivity with distinct modules of the entorhinal cortex. This provides a circuit-level explanation for why previous studies with lesions targeting more ventral entorhinal regions spared spatial memory.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Dorsolateral band of entorhinal cortex**: Critical for spatial memory; receives visuospatial input from postrhinal cortex and projects to dorsal hippocampus; contains neurons with sharp spatial tuning (place cells); lesions abolish retention of spatial memory
- **Ventromedial band of entorhinal cortex**: Not required for spatial memory; connects primarily to ventral hippocampus; lesions affect defensive behavior (elevated plus maze performance) but spare spatial navigation
- **Intermediate band of entorhinal cortex**: Anatomically between dorsolateral and ventromedial; partially lesioned in both lesion groups but not specifically targeted
- **Dorsal hippocampus**: Receives primary input from dorsolateral entorhinal band; critical for spatial memory encoding and retrieval
- **Ventral hippocampus**: Receives primary input from ventromedial entorhinal band; involved in defensive behavior and emotion-related processing
- **Perirhinal cortex**: Adjacent to dorsolateral entorhinal band; damage in some animals but control lesions showed no effect on spatial memory
- **Postrhinal cortex**: Adjacent to dorsolateral entorhinal band; provides visuospatial input to dorsolateral band; damage in some animals but control lesions showed no effect on spatial memory
- **Parasubiculum**: Adjacent to ventromedial band; minor damage in some animals
- **Presubiculum**: Adjacent to ventromedial band; minor damage in some animals

### Mechanistic insight

This paper meets the high bar for mechanistic insight by combining lesion-based behavioral analysis with neuroanatomical specificity to map functions onto distinct circuit modules. The core algorithmic insight is that the entorhinal cortex is functionally organized into parallel bands that route different types of information to distinct hippocampal subregions:

- **Computational level**: The brain solves the problem of coordinating multiple memory systems by segregating spatial and emotional information into distinct processing streams. The dorsal hippocampus specializes in allocentric spatial representation, while the ventral hippocampus handles emotion-related behaviors like defensive avoidance. The entorhinal cortex implements this segregation through its modular organization.

- **Algorithmic level**: The dorsolateral band of the entorhinal cortex serves as a necessary gateway for visuospatial information flowing from the postrhinal cortex to the dorsal hippocampus. Lesions here interrupt this information flow, preventing recall of spatial memories. The ventromedial band serves a parallel gateway function for information reaching the ventral hippocampus, supporting defensive behavior but not spatial navigation. The bands are partially insulated from each other, allowing selective disruption of one pathway while sparing the other.

- **Implementational level**: The anatomical implementation relies on the topographic organization of entorhinal-hippocampal projections. The dorsolateral band projects primarily to the dorsal hippocampus, while the ventromedial band projects to the ventral hippocampus. The dorsolateral band receives dense visuospatial input from the postrhinal cortex, whereas the ventromedial band connects with structures involved in emotional processing. The NMDA-induced excitotoxic lesions selectively destroy neurons in the target band while sparing fibers of passage, allowing precise functional mapping.

### Limitations & open questions

- **Incomplete lesions**: The dorsolateral lesions were not complete, leaving some tissue in the dorsolateral band and intermediate/ventromedial bands intact. This may explain why new learning (reversal training) was less impaired than retention.
- **Encoding vs. storage vs. retrieval**: The study did not dissociate whether the dorsolateral band is involved in encoding, storage, or retrieval of spatial memories. The weaker effect on new learning suggests possible differential involvement in these processes.
- **Perceptual vs. memory functions**: The study could not determine whether the dorsolateral band participates in computing allocentric location (perception) versus storing memories of location. The partial sparing of new learning suggests it may not be critical for spatial perception.
- **Complete vs. partial lesions**: It remains unclear whether spatial memory would be disrupted by larger ventromedial lesions, though the complete abolition of retention by dorsolateral lesions smaller than the largest ventromedial lesions suggests functional specificity.
- **Cellular mechanisms**: The exact contribution of the entorhinal cortex in spatial computation and information storage at the cellular/synaptic level remains unresolved.

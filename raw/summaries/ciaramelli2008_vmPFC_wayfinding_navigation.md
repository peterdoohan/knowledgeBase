### One-line summary

A single-patient lesion study demonstrates that the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) is necessary for maintaining spatial goals in working memory during navigation, with the patient characteristically veering toward highly familiar "attractor" locations when goal maintenance failed.

---

### Background & motivation

Functional neuroimaging studies had identified activity in ventromedial and ventrolateral prefrontal cortex during navigation tasks (particularly during goal-destination processing and route planning), but it was unclear whether these regions are causally necessary for navigation or what their specific functional contribution is. The existing taxonomy of topographical disorientation (egocentric disorientation, landmark agnosia, etc.) did not include a category for goal-maintenance failure during navigation. This paper fills that gap by describing and experimentally characterising a new form of navigation impairment following vmPFC damage.

---

### Methods

- **Patient**: LG, a 56-year-old male with bilateral vmPFC lesion (BAs 10, 11, 12, 24, 32, larger on right) following subarachnoid haemorrhage from a ruptured anterior communicating artery aneurysm. Neuropsychological profile: impaired working memory and executive function, intact long-term memory, intact basic attention, intact landmark recognition.
- **Laboratory study**: 10 pairs of familiar town locations; patient verbally described routes under four conditions — Standard (no aid), Remind (goal name flashed every 15 s), Rehearse (prompted to mentally rehearse goal every 15 s), Warning (neutral alerting signal every 15 s). 20 trials per condition. Compared to 2 age- and gender-matched healthy controls.
- **Ecological study**: Real-world navigation between town landmarks under the same four conditions (Remind, Rehearse, Warning, Standard), with 10 trials per condition. An operator provided cues by phone.
- **Landmark recognition test**: 15 familiar and 15 unfamiliar landmark photographs; recognition accuracy and route-direction knowledge tested.
- **Error analysis**: Route characteristics (length, turn options, landmark count, familiarity) related to navigation accuracy; familiarity ratings obtained for both goal and mistakenly-visited locations.

---

### Key findings

- **Standard condition**: LG succeeded on 7/20 laboratory trials and 3/10 ecological trials, confirming severe wayfinding impairment.
- **Remind condition**: Performance improved to 16/20 (lab; Z = 2.4, p < 0.05) and 7/10 (ecological), not significantly different from controls.
- **Rehearse condition**: Performance improved to 13/20 (lab; Z = 2.2, p < 0.05) and 7/10 (ecological), significantly better than Standard. This rules out a simple short-term memory decay account — internally generated rehearsal was sufficient.
- **Warning condition**: No significant improvement (9/20 lab, 2/10 ecological), ruling out a generic alertness effect.
- On error trials, LG could recall the correct goal destination in 70% of cases, confirming that long-term memory for the goal was intact.
- LG's errors involved heading to locations with significantly higher personal familiarity than actual goals (mean familiarity 9.00 vs. 6.15; t(25) = 2.35, p < 0.05). Seven recurring "attractor" locations were identified — all personally and occupationally significant.
- Route length, number of turns, and landmark count did not predict error rates; familiarity with locations within a route predicted errors negatively (p = 0.07).
- Landmark recognition and route-direction knowledge were intact (no significant difference from controls).
- Rehabilitation: LG was trained to self-rehearse his destination and successfully began travelling to work independently; he generalised the strategy to other daily activities.

### Computational framework

Not applicable in the strict sense; the paper does not deploy a formal computational model. However, the discussion explicitly invokes the language of attractor networks: the patient's behaviour is interpreted as a failure of goal-maintenance that allows strongly weighted "attractor states" (familiar, personally relevant locations represented in cortical networks) to dominate behaviour. The authors cite Treves (2005) on frontal latching networks as a candidate mechanism for prescribing transitions between attractor states.

The paper also engages the reversal-learning framework: vmPFC damage is proposed to impair the ability to confer current "goal" status to a target location, overriding previously rewarded but currently irrelevant associations. This parallels formal accounts of prefrontal cortex in context-dependent suppression of learned stimulus–response associations (Norman & Shallice, 1986; Cohen et al., 1999).

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to this paper, the role of vmPFC in navigation was suggested but unconfirmed by fMRI evidence (Spiers & Maguire, 2006; 2007), which showed vmPFC activation during initial goal encoding, route planning, and distraction management by taxi drivers. The broader framework held that topographical disorientation could arise from several distinct mechanisms (egocentric disorientation, landmark agnosia, heading disorientation, anterograde disorientation), all understood primarily in terms of perception, long-term memory, or spatial representation — not working memory maintenance of goals. The posterior parietal cortex and hippocampus/parahippocampal region were considered the principal navigation-relevant structures, with prefrontal cortex implicated only tentatively and in ancillary roles such as initial goal encoding.

---

### What this paper contributes

The paper establishes vmPFC as causally necessary for a specific component of navigation: the active maintenance of the goal destination in working memory throughout a route. This is dissociable from long-term memory for landmarks, routes, and goal destinations (all preserved in LG), and from basic attentional or spatial representation capacities. The deficit is characterised as one of working memory maintenance and/or reversal learning — a failure to keep current goal representations dominant over competing, over-learned attractor locations. The paper thus adds a new category to the taxonomy of topographical disorientation. It also provides a clinically actionable rehabilitation strategy (periodic self-rehearsal of goals) that was demonstrated effective in both laboratory and real-world settings.

---

### Brain regions & systems

- **Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC; BAs 10, 11, 12)** — primary lesion site; proposed to maintain spatial goal destinations in working memory and to suppress interference from familiar competing locations during navigation.
- **Rostral anterior cingulate cortex (BA 32) and medial prefrontal wall (BA 24)** — also lesioned in LG; implicated by authors in working memory maintenance and goal-directed behaviour, though not dissociated from vmPFC proper in this single-patient study.
- **Posterior parietal cortex** — referenced as involved in egocentric navigation (Rosenbaum et al., 2004; Spiers & Maguire, 2006); intact in LG.
- **Lingual gyrus** — referenced as involved in landmark identification; intact in LG.
- **Medial prefrontal cortex / orbitofrontal cortex (rodent homologues)** — cited as coding for spatial goals and contributing to reversal learning.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper partially meets the bar. It proposes a specific algorithm (active goal maintenance in working memory via vmPFC, preventing attractor capture by familiar locations) and provides lesion evidence and behavioural dissociation (Rehearse > Standard despite no external goal information; Warning = Standard) that constrains the account. However, it does not provide neural recordings or imaging data directly linking the proposed mechanism to specific neural activity patterns in vmPFC.

- **Computational**: The brain must maintain a currently relevant spatial goal in an active state throughout navigation, counteracting the pull of over-learned location representations.
- **Algorithmic**: vmPFC keeps the goal destination active in working memory; periodic re-encoding or rehearsal can substitute for this maintenance. Familiar "attractor" locations compete as dominant representations, and vmPFC normally enables suppression of these task-irrelevant associations.
- **Implementational**: Lesion localised to BAs 10, 11, 12, 24, 32; no cell-type or connectivity-level evidence provided. Authors reference Treves (2005) on frontal latching networks as a candidate implementation but do not test this directly.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Single-patient study: findings cannot be generalised without converging evidence from additional vmPFC patients or imaging studies.
- Lesion encompasses multiple prefrontal subregions (BAs 10, 11, 12, 24, 32); it is unclear which subregion(s) are critical for goal maintenance vs. reversal learning vs. other executive functions.
- Reversal learning was not directly tested in LG; the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test impairment is used as a proxy.
- Controls were only two healthy individuals; statistical comparisons are accordingly limited in power.
- The distance between attractor location and spatial goal did not predict navigation success (contrary to a goal-proximity value-updating hypothesis), but the sample was small and this requires systematic study.
- It is unclear whether the deficit is best characterised as a working memory maintenance failure or a reversal-learning/interference-suppression failure — the authors treat these as related but do not fully dissociate them.
- The rehabilitation effect, while promising, was demonstrated informally; no systematic follow-up or controlled rehabilitation trial was conducted.

### One-line summary

A cross-species review arguing that understanding foraging niches is essential for translating frontal cortex function between rodents and primates, with macaques specializing in prediction (distant foraging) and rats in evaluation (local foraging).

### Background & motivation

The relationship between rodent and primate frontal cortex is intensely debated, with implications for translating neuroscience findings across species. Rather than focusing solely on anatomical differences (e.g., granular vs. agranular cortex), this review advocates for a neuroethological approach that considers how each species' foraging niche shaped their frontal cortex organization. The divergence in foraging strategies—macaques foraging over large ranges using vision vs. rats foraging locally using olfaction—provides a framework for understanding functional similarities and differences.

### Methods

- Comprehensive literature review of anatomical, behavioral, and neurophysiological studies in rats, macaques, marmosets, and humans
- Comparative analysis of frontal cortex cytoarchitecture and connectivity across species
- Functional comparison using three core foraging domains: Prediction, Evaluation, and Action
- Focus on two key frontal regions: orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)

### Key findings

- **Anatomical differences**: Macaque frontal cortex contains granular, dysgranular, and agranular regions, while rat frontal cortex is entirely agranular. VLPFC (area 12) is unique to primates and emerged during anthropoid evolution.

- **Anatomical similarities**: Agranular OFC and parts of ACC show conserved connectivity patterns across rats and macaques, particularly regarding striatal projections and amygdala connections.

- **Prediction function**: VLPFC in macaques is specialized for probabilistic learning and predicting reward availability at distant locations. In rats, lateral OFC supports both stimulus-based probabilistic learning and evaluation functions.

- **Evaluation function**: OFC in both species is essential for reinforcer devaluation tasks, enabling animals to update and use current food values. However, in rats, PL (prelimbic cortex) also contributes, whereas in macaques, area 32 is not involved.

- **Action function**: ACC in both species encodes reward prediction errors and maintains action strategies (Win-Stay). It integrates stimulus and action values to drive choice strategy, with functional correspondence between rat and macaque ACC.

- **Functional divergence**: While prediction, evaluation, and action in macaques rely on distinct subregions, these processes are more integrated across frontal areas in rats.

### Computational framework

The review employs a functional decomposition of foraging into three computational components:

1. **Prediction**: Assessing probability, risk, and availability of rewards; generating expectations about food location and timing. This involves probabilistic inference and model-based learning about environmental dynamics.

2. **Evaluation**: Assessing the quality and current value of potential rewards through sensory inspection; updating value representations based on internal state (hunger/satiety). This involves value-based decision-making and state-dependent utility computation.

3. **Action**: Planning and selecting foraging strategies; integrating multiple decision parameters; maintaining behavioral strategies based on feedback. This involves action selection, evidence accumulation, and policy updating based on reward prediction errors.

The framework assumes that frontal cortex implements these functions through specialized circuits that evolved to match species-specific foraging demands.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to this review, the field was divided on cross-species frontal cortex comparisons. One perspective, following Preuss and Wise, emphasized anatomical differences—particularly the presence of granular prefrontal cortex in primates and its absence in rodents—arguing that rats lack a primate-like prefrontal cortex. This view suggested limited translational validity from rodent to primate frontal cortex studies. An alternative perspective, based on connectivity patterns (particularly mediodorsal thalamic projections), proposed that rats do possess prefrontal homologs. The prevailing debate focused heavily on cytoarchitectonic differences, with functional comparisons often overlooking the ecological context of foraging behavior.

### What this paper contributes

This review advances the field by proposing a neuroethological framework that integrates anatomical, functional, and ecological perspectives. Key contributions include:

1. **Foraging niche as organizing principle**: The paper establishes that species-specific foraging demands—range size, time horizon, sensory modality—shaped frontal cortex specializations. Macaques' large-range visual foraging favored prediction circuits (VLPFC), while rats' local olfactory foraging favored evaluation circuits (OFC/PL).

2. **Functional correspondence despite anatomical divergence**: The review identifies conserved functions in agranular OFC and ACC across species while acknowledging primate-specific innovations (granular VLPFC for probabilistic learning).

3. **Integration vs. specialization**: A key insight is that frontal functions are more distributed/integrated in rats but more localized/specialized in macaques, reflecting different evolutionary solutions to similar computational problems.

4. **Translation guidance**: The framework provides concrete guidance for when rodent-primate translation is likely valid (e.g., reinforcer devaluation, ACC-mediated action strategies) versus when caution is warranted (e.g., probabilistic learning involving VLPFC).

### Brain regions & systems

- **Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** — agranular in rats; agranular, dysgranular, and granular in macaques. Central to reward evaluation and stimulus-based value learning. In rats, lateral OFC supports both probabilistic learning and evaluation; in macaques, evaluation is OFC-dependent while probabilistic learning depends on VLPFC.

- **Ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (VLPFC)** — area 12; present only in primates (granular cortex). Specialized for probabilistic reward learning and predicting food availability at distant locations. Emerged during anthropoid evolution as foraging ranges expanded.

- **Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** — agranular in both rats and macaques. Key integrator of reward, cognition, and action planning. Encodes reward prediction errors, maintains action strategies (Win-Stay), and drives behavioral adjustment based on feedback. Functional correspondence across species despite some connectivity differences.

- **Prelimbic cortex (PL)** — rat area 32; contributes to reinforcer devaluation in instrumental settings, unlike macaque area 32. Shows species differences in evaluation function.

- **Infralimbic cortex (IL)** — rat area 25; agranular; involved in threat memory extinction (distinct from primate area 25 function in defensive responses).

- **Amygdala** — interacts with OFC for reinforcer devaluation across species; projections from lateral OFC to basolateral amygdala support value updating in rats; amygdala terminals in OFC crucial for adaptive responding.

- **Striatum** — receives topographic projections from ACC and OFC; corticostriatal connectivity patterns show species correspondence for some (areas 32, 25) but not all (area 24) frontal regions.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the high bar for mechanistic insight as defined in the criteria. While it provides extensive discussion of computational functions (prediction, evaluation, action) and their neural correlates, it does not present a formal algorithm that could mechanistically explain the phenomena, nor does it provide new neural data specifically supporting such an algorithm over alternatives. The paper is a review that synthesizes existing findings rather than presenting novel mechanistic models or new empirical data. The discussion of OFC and ACC functions (e.g., reward prediction error encoding, value updating, action strategy maintenance) describes neural correlates and causal involvements but does not formalize these as implementable algorithms or provide the specific neural data that would distinguish between algorithmic alternatives.

### Limitations & open questions

- **Paradigm mismatch**: Direct comparison between rat and macaque studies is hampered by differences in behavioral paradigms. For example, rodent probabilistic reversal learning often employs action/spatial methods while primate studies use stimulus-based approaches.

- **Limited direct comparison**: Few studies have directly compared the same frontal regions across species using matched tasks. Most comparisons are inferential across different experimental designs.

- **VLPFC function in rodents**: The review notes that no rodent homolog exists for primate VLPFC (granular cortex), yet lateral OFC in rats appears to support similar probabilistic learning functions. Whether this represents true functional homology or convergent evolution remains unclear.

- **Anterior vs. posterior OFC**: While macaque studies have dissociated anterior (using stored value) vs. posterior (updating value) OFC functions, similar dissociations in rodents are less clear and require further investigation.

- **Spatial cognition integration**: The review identifies a gap in understanding how spatial navigation systems (hippocampus) interact with frontal cortex during foraging, particularly in primates.

- **Temporal horizons**: The paper highlights that differences in foraging time horizons (seasonal planning in macaques vs. short-term in rats) have received insufficient attention in laboratory studies.

### One-line summary

Excitotoxic NMDA lesions of rat medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) leave spatial learning and working memory intact in the Morris water maze and three-panel runway, while impairing food hoarding, elevating locomotion, and slowing reversal learning — suggesting mPFC deficits in spatial tasks reflect attentional and flexibility impairments rather than mnemonic ones.

---

### Background & motivation

The rat mPFC is widely considered the homologue of the primate dorsolateral PFC and has been implicated in working memory, spatial learning, and delayed-response performance. However, the evidence is inconsistent: some lesion studies report severe spatial memory deficits resembling hippocampal damage, while others find intact or only transiently impaired performance, leading to debate about whether mPFC disruption genuinely impairs memory or instead disrupts non-mnemonic processes such as attention and behavioural flexibility. This study sought to clarify the contribution of mPFC to spatial reference and working memory using two complementary paradigms (Morris water maze and three-panel runway delayed matching-to-position), alongside behavioural controls (food hoarding, open-field locomotion) known to be sensitive to mPFC damage.

---

### Methods

- **Subjects**: 20 adult male Wistar rats; groups: mPFC lesion (N = 10, reduced to N = 7 after histological exclusions), sham lesion (N = 5), and unoperated controls (N = 5); sham and unoperated groups collapsed into a single control group (N = 10) after confirming no differences.
- **Lesion**: Bilateral excitotoxic lesions via stereotaxic NMDA injection (10 mg/ml, 0.2 µl per site) targeting anterior cingulate (ACd), prelimbic (PL), and infralimbic (IL) areas; verified by Cresyl-violet histology using the Paxinos & Watson atlas.
- **Food hoarding paradigm**: Rats given access to a hoarding cage for 60 min; pellets transported counted pre- and post-surgery (non-parametric Mann–Whitney U test).
- **Three-panel runway**: Delayed matching-to-position task; daily sessions of one cued training trial + five uncued test trials across 24 days (8 blocks × 3 days); errors at four choice points scored; inter-trial interval 2 min.
- **Open-field locomotion**: Two consecutive days, 30 min sessions; distance, centre entries, and time in centre measured in 5-min bins.
- **Morris water maze**: Six days of spatial training (4 trials/day, hidden platform), followed by a probe test, 3 days of reversal training (platform moved to opposite quadrant), a reversal probe test, and a visual-cued control test.
- **Analysis**: ANOVAs with lesion as between-subjects factor; repeated-measures factors varied by task (days, blocks, trials, quadrants); swimming speed and visual-cued performance assessed as motor/visual control measures.

---

### Key findings

- **Food hoarding**: mPFC lesions significantly reduced hoarding post-surgery (Mann–Whitney P < 0.01), confirming lesion efficacy; controls were unaffected (P = 0.18).
- **Open-field locomotion**: mPFC-lesioned rats travelled significantly greater distances (F(1,15) = 8.10, P < 0.02) and made more centre entries (F(1,15) = 7.41, P < 0.02), replicating known hyperactivity effects.
- **Three-panel runway — training trial (reference memory)**: Both groups showed equivalent error reduction across days (significant block effect, F(7,105) = 22.27, P < 0.01); mPFC group showed a near-significant trend towards slower acquisition (F(1,15) = 3.42, P = 0.08), but not a clear impairment.
- **Three-panel runway — test trials (working memory)**: No significant lesion effect or lesion × block interaction; both groups showed equivalent error reduction across blocks and across within-session trials, indicating intact working memory.
- **Morris water maze — spatial training**: No difference in escape latency or distance between groups; both improved equivalently (days effect: F(5,75) = 18.82, P < 0.001).
- **Morris water maze — spatial probe test**: Both groups spent equivalent and above-chance time in the platform quadrant (~40%), indicating intact spatial memory consolidation.
- **Morris water maze — reversal training**: mPFC rats were significantly slower than controls on day 2 of reversal (F(1,15) = 13.17, P < 0.01), producing a near-significant lesion × days interaction (F(2,30) = 3.18, P = 0.06); by day 3 performance converged.
- **Morris water maze — reversal probe test**: No group difference; both groups preferentially searched the new platform quadrant (~39%).
- **Visual-cued test**: No group differences in latency, distance, or speed, ruling out visual or motor confounds.

### Computational framework

Not applicable. The paper is purely empirical with no explicit computational model. The results constrain accounts of mPFC function that invoke working memory buffers or spatial map representations as the primary computational contribution of the region. Instead, the findings support frameworks emphasising mPFC's role in flexible control, interference suppression, or attentional gating — such as Fuster's temporal organisation account and Goldman-Rakic's representational memory framework, both cited as alternatives to interpret the null memory findings.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

At the time of writing, the dominant view was that rat mPFC (particularly prelimbic and anterior cingulate areas) is a critical node in working memory circuits, considered the rodent homologue of primate dorsolateral PFC. This view was supported by evidence of delay-period firing in mPFC neurons, impaired performance on spatial delayed alternation and other delayed-response tasks following mPFC lesions, and parallels with dlPFC lesion effects in primates. Extensive mPFC lesions were known from some studies (e.g., Sutherland et al. 1982; Kolb & Whishaw 1983) to produce spatial navigation deficits resembling those after hippocampal lesions, lending further support to the idea that mPFC is necessary for spatial and working memory. The alternative — that mPFC deficits in memory tasks are secondary to disrupted attentional or flexibility processes — was emerging but contested.

---

### What this paper contributes

The results challenge the view that mPFC is necessary for spatial or working memory per se. Both reference and working memory were intact in two independent spatial tasks, and probe tests confirmed normal retention of platform locations. The observed impairments — slower initial training-trial acquisition and slowed reversal learning in the water maze — occurred against a backdrop of intact ultimate memory performance, and were not attributable to motor or visual deficits. The paper argues these transient acquisition slowing effects reflect deficits in attentional processes and/or behavioural flexibility (shifting from an acquired response to a new one), rather than mnemonic failure. This repositions the reported deficit in delayed-response tasks as interference-sensitivity or temporal organisation impairment, not a working memory storage or retrieval deficit.

---

### Brain regions & systems

- **Medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC)** — the primary target; includes dorsal anterior cingulate (ACd), prelimbic (PL), and infralimbic (IL) areas; proposed locus of attentional control and behavioural flexibility rather than spatial memory storage.
- **Frontal area 2 (Fr2)** — variably damaged across lesioned animals; noted but not the primary focus.
- **Hippocampus** — mentioned as reference point; prior work implicates it in spatial memory and some mPFC lesion effects were described as resembling hippocampal damage, but hippocampus is not directly manipulated or recorded here.
- **Mediodorsal thalamus** — cited in the introduction as the defining thalamic connection of PFC across species; not directly studied.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight. It provides lesion data and behavioural measures but no neural recordings, pharmacological dissections, or imaging data linking mPFC activity to specific computational steps in spatial learning. The lesion approach can rule out necessary contributions but cannot identify the algorithm by which mPFC supports the processes (attentional gating, interference suppression, reversal) that appear to depend on it. No cell-type, connectivity, or biophysical evidence is presented.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Small sample sizes (N = 7 mPFC, N = 10 controls), reducing power to detect modest effects; some near-significant trends (P = 0.06–0.08) may reflect insufficient power rather than true absence of effects.
- Lesions were made using fixed coordinates; variable extent (histologically verified) may introduce heterogeneity, and Fr2 was inconsistently damaged across animals.
- The study used a 2-min inter-trial interval in the three-panel runway; longer delays might reveal delay-dependent mPFC contributions to working memory not captured here.
- The Morris water maze protocol (animals placed on platform if they failed to locate it) differs from some earlier studies showing impairment — the authors note that procedural differences (reinforcement contingencies) may account for discrepant findings across labs.
- The paper does not resolve whether the reversal impairment reflects attentional capture by previously reinforced cues, reduced sensitivity to error feedback, or impaired response inhibition — these remain open mechanistic questions.
- No within-session delay manipulation was performed, leaving open the possibility that mPFC does contribute to working memory specifically at longer delays.

### One-line summary

This review synthesises evidence that pharmacological blockers of gap junctions (including carbenoxolone, quinine, mefloquine, quinidine, anandamide, oleamide, and several others) produce anticonvulsant effects across a wide range of in vitro and in vivo animal seizure models, implicating enhanced gap junctional communication as a mechanism contributing to seizure generation.

---

### Background & motivation

Gap junctions (GJ) formed by connexin (Cx) proteins mediate electrical synaptic transmission between neurons and glia, enabling rapid bidirectional ionic coupling. Because hypersynchronous neuronal activity is the hallmark of convulsive seizures, it has been hypothesised that enhanced gap junctional intercellular communication is an underlying mechanism involved in seizure generation and maintenance. Several studies have documented upregulation of connexin expression in animal seizure models and in human epileptic tissue, strengthening this hypothesis. Despite this mechanistic interest, the pharmacological evidence for GJ blockers as anticonvulsants had not been comprehensively reviewed prior to this work.

---

### Methods

This is a narrative review. The authors:

- Surveyed the available in vitro and in vivo literature on the effects of GJ blockers in animal seizure models.
- Covered the following compounds: carbenoxolone (CBX), quinine (QUIN), mefloquine (MFQ), quinidine (QND), anandamide (ANA), oleamide (OLE), meclofenamic acid, niflumic acid, flufenamic acid, heptanol, octanol, glycyrrhetinic acid, retinoic acid, and Cx-mimetic peptides (Gap26, Gap27).
- Organised findings by compound and by route of administration (in vitro brain slices; systemic in vivo; direct intracerebral or intracerebroventricular injection).
- Included original unpublished data from their own laboratory on CBX, QUIN, MFQ, and QND in the maximal electroshock and pentylenetetrazole (PTZ) rat models.
- Summarised target selectivity and off-target actions for each compound in a reference table.

---

### Key findings

- **Carbenoxolone (CBX)** is the most studied GJ blocker in seizure models. It consistently reduces epileptiform discharge amplitude and frequency in hippocampal and thalamocortical slices; systemically (i.p. 5–300 mg/kg) it delays seizure onset and reduces clonic and tonic seizure duration across PTZ, maximal electroshock, audiogenic, and pilocarpine models. Direct microinjection into inferior colliculus, substantia nigra, basolateral amygdala, hippocampus, reticular thalamic nucleus, and reticular formation all reduce seizure severity.
- **Quinine (QUIN)** at 25–240 mg/kg (i.p.) reduces seizure incidence and duration in PTZ models; i.c.v. injection reduces epileptiform activity induced by penicillin and PTZ. Direct cortical application reduces seizure propagation from an aminopyridine-induced epileptic focus.
- **Mefloquine (MFQ)** at 40 mg/kg (i.p.) significantly reduces seizure incidence and spectral power of epileptiform activity in PTZ models and reduces tonic hindlimb extension duration in maximal electroshock. Higher doses (~187 mg/kg) are neurotoxic.
- **Quinidine (QND)** shows anticonvulsant effects at 4–30 mg/kg in electroshock and penicillin models; the authors' own data show only modest protection at 120 mg/kg in maximal electroshock.
- **Anandamide (ANA)** shows dose-dependent bidirectional effects on PTZ seizures: anticonvulsant at low i.c.v. doses (10–40 µg), proconvulsant at high doses (80–160 µg); inhibitors of ANA metabolism potentiate anticonvulsant effects.
- **Oleamide (OLE)** inhibits PTZ-induced seizures but not picrotoxin- or strychnine-induced seizures.
- **Fenamates** (meclofenamic, flufenamic acids) increase seizure latency to PTZ and reversibly abolish GABA-A-blocker-evoked seizure-like events in neocortical slices.
- **Heptanol and octanol** suppress epileptiform bursting in hippocampal slices and reduce seizure discharges when applied to the epileptic focus in vivo.
- **Glycyrrhetinic acid** eliminates interictal spikes in a focal epileptogenesis model and reduces atypical absence seizure duration when injected into the reticular thalamic nucleus.
- All compounds tested in vitro share a common profile: reduction of both amplitude and frequency of epileptiform activity in brain slices.
- The authors propose two main anticonvulsant mechanisms: (a) blockade of assembled GJ containing neuronal Cx36, reducing neuronal synchronisation; and (b) blockade of astrocytic Cx43 hemichannels, reducing calcium-driven gliotransmitter release.

### Computational framework

Not applicable. The paper is a pharmacological review with no formal computational or mathematical modelling. The results constrain mechanistic models of seizure generation that incorporate electrical coupling between neurons as a synchronising mechanism — in particular, network models in which gap-junction-mediated coupling strength is a parameter governing the transition to hypersynchronous dynamics.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's introduction frames the field as holding two complementary views: (1) the classical model of seizures as a phenomenon of chemical-synapse dysregulation (excess excitation or reduced inhibition); and (2) a growing hypothesis, based on connexin expression studies and knockout animal models, that electrical synaptic coupling via GJ constitutes an additional, parallel mechanism for generating and sustaining hypersynchronous neuronal activity. The review positions itself against a backdrop where the GJ hypothesis is plausible but where systematic pharmacological evidence — especially in vivo — is limited and scattered. The field was also aware that GJ blockers are pharmacologically impure (they have multiple off-target actions on ion channels and neurotransmitter receptors), complicating mechanistic interpretation.

---

### What this paper contributes

By collating the scattered in vitro and in vivo literature alongside the authors' own unpublished data, the review establishes that anticonvulsant effects of GJ blockers are a robust and reproducible phenomenon across multiple compound classes, multiple seizure models, and both systemic and focal administration routes. This strengthens confidence that GJ blockade — rather than off-target channel effects alone — contributes to the observed efficacy. The review also identifies MFQ as an underexplored compound with a promising dose-response profile, proposes dual neuronal (Cx36) and astrocytic (Cx43 hemichannel) mechanisms of action, and highlights that several GJ blockers (glycyrrhetinic acid, niflumic acid, retinoic acid, Cx-mimetic peptides) remain largely untested in seizure models. Key unresolved questions include: whether anticonvulsant effects are truly GJ-mediated or dominated by off-target ion channel actions; which brain regions and which Cx isoforms are the critical therapeutic targets; and whether GJ blockers could be developed into clinically viable antiepileptic drugs.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Hippocampus (CA3, dentate gyrus)** — primary locus for in vitro slice experiments; site of epileptiform activity induction and CBX/QUIN/heptanol/octanol application; Cx36 upregulation demonstrated here in kindling models.
- **Neocortex / somatosensory cortex** — epileptic focus site for 4-aminopyridine and tetanus toxin models; direct application of CBX, QUIN, meclofenamic acid shown to reduce seizures.
- **Entorhinal cortex** — QUIN and octanol microinjection shown to modify hippocampal epileptiform activity.
- **Thalamus (reticular nucleus, ventroposteromedial nucleus)** — CBX and glycyrrhetinic acid in the reticular nucleus reduce absence seizure duration; ventroposteromedial nucleus injection had no effect.
- **Thalamocortical system** — used in slice experiments for absence seizure models; CBX and QND reduce seizure-like activity.
- **Inferior colliculus** — CBX microinjection reduces seizure duration in audiogenic model.
- **Substantia nigra** — CBX microinjection reduces seizure severity.
- **Basolateral amygdala** — CBX and retinoic acid reduce afterdischarge duration and kindling-generated seizures.
- **Reticular formation** — CBX microinjection shown by the authors to reduce incidence of generalised tonic-clonic seizures induced by PTZ.
- **Striatal astrocytes** — ANA shown to block calcium wave propagation via gap junctional mechanisms.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not fully meet the bar for mechanistic insight at Marr's three levels. While it proposes specific algorithms (Cx36-mediated neuronal synchronisation; Cx43 hemichannel-mediated gliotransmitter release) and cites supporting pharmacological and genetic data, the review does not provide original neural recordings that directly test these mechanisms against alternatives. The evidence linking GJ blocker effects to specific connexin isoforms is largely indirect (based on selectivity profiles and Cx36-KO mouse comparisons). Furthermore, a fundamental confound acknowledged in the paper is that all known GJ blockers also affect multiple ion channels and neurotransmitter receptors, preventing clean attribution of anticonvulsant effects to GJ blockade specifically.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- All GJ blockers reviewed have significant off-target actions (ion channels, neurotransmitter receptors), making it difficult to attribute anticonvulsant effects specifically to GJ blockade.
- Most in vitro evidence is stronger than in vivo evidence; many compounds have never been tested systemically or in focal in vivo models.
- The relationship between GJ blockers and seizures shows model-dependence and dose-dependence (e.g., ANA is bidirectional; MFQ is anticonvulsant at low doses but neurotoxic at high doses).
- Connexin expression changes following seizures are inconsistent across models, brain regions, and seizure durations — sometimes upregulated, sometimes downregulated.
- The specific Cx isoforms and brain regions that are the critical therapeutic targets remain unclear.
- Cx-mimetic peptides offer potential selectivity but have slow onset of action and may also block pannexins.
- Clinical translation is uncertain: CBX causes mineralocorticoid-like side effects; QUIN and MFQ are no longer first-line antimalarials partly due to adverse effects.
- The role of astrocytic versus neuronal gap junctions in seizure generation is not resolved.

### One-line summary

The hippocampus and parahippocampal region show conserved anatomy and function across mammals, and this conservation supports a species-general computational account in which the hippocampus binds item identity (via perirhinal cortex / lateral entorhinal area) with spatial context (via postrhinal/parahippocampal cortex / medial entorhinal area) to support declarative memory.

---

### Background & motivation

Early comparative research left unresolved whether the hippocampus performs the same function in rodents and humans, given that rodent work emphasised spatial memory (place cells, Morris water maze) while human work implicated the hippocampus in episodic and semantic declarative memory. Meanwhile, the enormous diversity of mammalian neocortex raised the possibility that hippocampal function might have diverged across species. The paper addresses this by synthesising anatomical, physiological, and behavioural evidence to determine whether hippocampal anatomy and function are conserved, and if so, to derive a species-general mechanistic account.

---

### Methods

This is a narrative review covering:

- Comparative neuroanatomy of the hippocampus and parahippocampal region across mammalian taxa (rat, macaque, human, bat, mouse, cat, hedgehog tenrec, tree shrew, and others).
- Cross-species behavioural paradigms adapted for multiple species: trace eyeblink conditioning, delayed nonmatch-to-sample (DNMS), transitive inference, and retrograde amnesia.
- Single-unit electrophysiology from rats, monkeys, and humans examining the spatial and nonspatial tuning of hippocampal neurons.
- Review of computational models of hippocampal memory (McClelland et al. 1995, Alvarez & Squire 1994, O'Reilly & Rudy 2000, Treves & Rolls 1994, and others).
- Synthesis of lesion, pharmacological (NMDA receptor blockade), immediate-early gene, and functional neuroimaging studies dissociating subregional contributions (perirhinal vs. postrhinal cortex; CA3 vs. CA1).

---

### Key findings

- The internal circuitry of the hippocampus (DG → CA3 → CA1 → subiculum) and the topography of entorhinal inputs to hippocampal subfields are strikingly conserved across all mammalian species examined.
- The parahippocampal region shows intermediate conservation: entorhinal-hippocampal projections are highly conserved, but neocortical inputs to perirhinal and postrhinal/parahippocampal cortices differ between species in content (not organisation).
- Functional homology is supported across four behavioural domains: (1) hippocampal damage impairs trace eyeblink conditioning in rabbits, mice, rats, and humans; (2) delay-dependent recognition memory impairment (DNMS) is observed in rats, monkeys, and humans; (3) hippocampal damage disrupts transitive inference in rats and monkeys, with medial temporal lobe activation during transitive inference in humans; (4) temporally graded retrograde amnesia is observed in both humans and experimental animals.
- Hippocampal single neurons in rats and humans are equally likely to be modulated by spatial, nonspatial, or conjunctive (spatial + nonspatial) features, arguing against a species-specific spatial specialisation.
- Hippocampal neurons rapidly acquire representations of new spatial and nonspatial information (e.g., fear conditioning in one session, scene–location associations in monkeys and humans) and these representations are context-dependent.
- Perirhinal cortex and lateral entorhinal area (LEA) preferentially process nonspatial stimulus identity; postrhinal/parahippocampal cortex and medial entorhinal area (MEA) preferentially process spatial context — a dissociation supported by electrophysiology, lesion, immediate-early gene (c-fos), and neuroimaging data.
- CA3 appears specialised for conjunctive/item-context associations and pattern completion (supported by selective NMDA-KO mouse data); CA1 appears specialised for temporal order memory and bridging time within episodes.

### Computational framework

The paper synthesises existing computational models and proposes a new schematic model grounded in conserved cross-species anatomy.

**Core framework: dual-stream convergence in the hippocampus**

- Two parallel input streams reach the hippocampus through the parahippocampal region:
  - *Nonspatial stream*: perirhinal cortex → lateral entorhinal area (LEA) → hippocampus
  - *Spatial/contextual stream*: postrhinal (parahippocampal in primates) cortex → medial entorhinal area (MEA) → hippocampus
- These streams converge (are combined) in DG and CA3, but remain segregated in CA1 and subiculum.
- CA3 is proposed to form item-in-context (conjunctive) associations by binding the two streams; its recurrent collaterals and high LTP support rapid pattern storage and pattern completion.
- CA1 is proposed to represent the temporal order of events and to compare/combine conjunctive associations with a veridical sensory record.
- The framework explains why apparent species differences (spatial memory in rats, episodic memory in humans) are downstream consequences of divergent neocortical input to a conserved hippocampal computation: different species present different content to the same machinery.

Key assumptions: rapid one-trial synaptic change in hippocampus; limited synaptic changes sufficient for storage; neocortical differences determine input content but not the intrinsic computation.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The review opens against a backdrop of two partially incompatible accounts of hippocampal function. The human literature (anchored by H.M.; Scoville & Milner, 1957) held that the hippocampus supports declarative memory — conscious recall of facts and events, including both spatial and nonspatial content. The rodent literature, by contrast, emphasised spatial memory: O'Keefe and Nadel's (1978) cognitive map theory argued the hippocampus is a spatial map-building device, supported by the discovery of place cells. The prevailing comparative assumption, therefore, was either that hippocampal function had diverged between rodents and primates, or that the two literatures could not easily be reconciled. A further background assumption was that the hippocampus sits at the apex of a strictly hierarchical neocortical processing stream. The paper argues against the divergence assumption and refines the hierarchical-only picture with parallel streams.

---

### What this paper contributes

The review establishes three main contributions:

1. **Anatomical conservation**: Detailed cross-species anatomical evidence demonstrates that hippocampal internal circuitry and the topographic organisation of entorhinal inputs are remarkably conserved, even as neocortical inputs differ substantially.

2. **Functional conservation**: By marshalling evidence from tasks adapted for multiple species (trace conditioning, DNMS, transitive inference, retrograde amnesia) and from single-unit recordings in rats, monkeys, and humans, the review makes a strong case that the fundamental hippocampal computation is the same across mammals. Apparent species differences (spatial vs. episodic emphasis) are reinterpreted as downstream translations through divergent neocortex.

3. **A species-general mechanistic model**: The dual-stream convergence framework (nonspatial via perirhinal/LEA; spatial via postrhinal/MEA; combination in DG/CA3; temporal ordering in CA1/subiculum) provides a parsimonious account grounded in conserved anatomy. This unifies previously disparate findings and generates testable predictions (e.g., auditory spatial vs. nonspatial convergence in echolocating bats).

Key unresolved questions identified: the exact extent of spatial vs. general contextual processing in postrhinal cortex/MEA; whether hippocampal involvement in episodic memory in humans reflects a uniquely human specialisation or a conserved function expressed differently through a richer neocortex; the precise contribution of hippocampus vs. parahippocampal cortex to recognition memory.

---

### Brain regions & systems

- **Hippocampus (CA1, CA3, DG, subiculum)** — primary focus; proposed locus of conserved declarative memory computation; CA3 for conjunctive/item-context associations and pattern completion; CA1 for temporal order and episodic binding.
- **Dentate gyrus (DG)** — combined with CA3 in the model as a site of convergence of spatial and nonspatial streams; associated with pattern separation.
- **Entorhinal cortex, lateral area (LEA)** — relay for nonspatial stimulus identity information from perirhinal cortex to hippocampus.
- **Entorhinal cortex, medial area (MEA)** — relay for spatial/contextual information from postrhinal cortex to hippocampus.
- **Perirhinal cortex** — processing and representation of individual stimulus identity (nonspatial); impaired in DNMS and object recognition following lesion.
- **Postrhinal cortex (parahippocampal cortex in primates)** — processing of spatial context; receives dorsal-stream/parietal inputs; damaged animals show object-location but not object-identity deficits.
- **Prefrontal cortex** — mentioned as interacting with hippocampus to maintain CS representation during the trace interval in eyeblink conditioning.
- **Cerebellum / hindbrain** — supports delay eyeblink conditioning; requires hippocampal input when a trace interval is inserted.
- **Neocortex (broadly)** — the locus of species divergence; its differences across mammals are argued to explain apparent species-specific hippocampal functions.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper partially meets the bar. It proposes a well-specified algorithm (dual-stream convergence, CA3 pattern completion and item-context binding, CA1 temporal ordering) and cites substantial neural data supporting the functional dissociation of perirhinal/LEA (nonspatial) vs. postrhinal/MEA (spatial) streams, and CA3 vs. CA1 subregional roles. However, this is a review rather than a primary empirical study, and the proposed model is described by the authors themselves as a "schematic" rather than a true computational model. The evidence spans multiple studies and species but is not systematically combined to test the specific algorithm against alternatives in a single unified analysis. No new neural recordings or lesion data are reported.

- **Computational**: The brain solves the problem of rapidly binding items (objects, odours, stimuli) with their spatial context and temporal order to enable flexible, relational declarative memory.
- **Algorithmic**: Two parallel input streams (item identity via perirhinal/LEA; spatial context via postrhinal/MEA) converge in DG/CA3, enabling conjunctive item-context representations. CA3 recurrent connectivity supports pattern completion. CA1, receiving segregated LEA/MEA inputs, encodes temporal order of events and links associative memories into episodes.
- **Implementational**: Conserved hippocampal internal circuitry (DG→CA3→CA1→subiculum) with NMDA receptor-dependent LTP in CA3 (for rapid conjunctive storage) and CA1 (for temporal coordination). The topographic separation of LEA and MEA projections to CA1/subiculum (proximal vs. distal targets) provides the anatomical substrate for maintaining stream identity into CA1. The review does not address specific cell types, neuromodulators, or biophysical mechanisms.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- The proposed dual-stream model is explicitly a schematic, not a formal computational model; its predictions are largely qualitative.
- The boundary between "spatial" and "general contextual" processing in postrhinal cortex and MEA is unresolved; conflicting lesion results for perirhinal and postrhinal contributions to contextual fear conditioning are noted but not resolved.
- Whether episodic memory (in the full Tulvingian sense) is unique to the human hippocampus or shared with other mammals remains debated; operational definitions used in animal studies (what-where-when) may not capture the full construct.
- The degree of hippocampal involvement in recollection vs. familiarity is actively debated, with conflicting results across studies attributed to methodological differences, damage extent, or genuine species differences — the review does not resolve this.
- Whether some memories (particularly those with rich spatial detail) permanently require the hippocampus, or whether all declarative memories consolidate to cortex, is left open.
- The review covers primarily rats, macaques, and humans; the framework's generality to other mammalian orders (e.g., cetaceans, marsupials, monotremes) remains largely untested.

### One-line summary

A comprehensive perspective review mapping the definitions, epidemiology, neurobiology, and therapeutic landscape of treatment resistance across schizophrenia, major depressive disorder, bipolar affective disorder, and OCD, identifying heterogeneity in criteria and limited treatment options as the field's central problems.

---

### Background & motivation

Treatment resistance affects 20–60% of patients with major psychiatric disorders and is associated with disproportionately high healthcare costs (up to ten-fold higher than for treatment-responsive patients), yet receives less than 1% of psychiatric research output. Despite this burden, only one drug — clozapine — is licensed as a monotherapy for a treatment-resistant condition in psychiatry. The field lacks harmonised definitions of resistance across and within disorders, creating heterogeneity in trial populations and limiting the validity of comparative research. This paper addresses the conceptual, epidemiological, neurobiological, and therapeutic state of the field, proposing a reporting checklist and research agenda.

---

### Methods

- Systematic review of 23 national and international consensus guidelines defining treatment resistance for schizophrenia (9), depression (10), bipolar affective disorder (1), and OCD (3).
- Analysis of scientific publications on treatment resistance in psychiatry from 2000–2019 to quantify research investment trends (regression analysis).
- Search of ClinicalTrials.gov for Phase 2 and 3 studies on novel agents for treatment-resistant schizophrenia, depression, bipolar affective disorder, OCD, panic disorder, PTSD, and substance dependence; 15 actively recruiting studies identified.
- Narrative synthesis across sections on definition, pseudo-resistance, epidemiology, neurobiology, and therapeutics.

---

### Key findings

- Three core components are shared across all 23 guidelines: (1) correct diagnosis confirmed, (2) adequate treatment delivered (dose and duration), (3) inadequate symptomatic response.
- Major heterogeneity exists within these core components: number of required treatment trials ranges from undefined to three for depression; treatment duration per drug ranges from 2 to 12 weeks; response thresholds range from "no effect" to quantified scale cut-offs; adherence is rarely formally assessed, and plasma level monitoring is recommended in only one guideline.
- No operationalised definitions of treatment resistance exist for mania, panic disorder, PTSD, or substance dependence.
- Over one-third of patients identified as treatment-resistant schizophrenia show evidence of poor adherence, suggesting a substantial pseudo-resistance problem.
- Annual direct medical costs for treatment-resistant schizophrenia in the USA exceed $34 billion; hospitalisation costs are up to ten-fold higher than for non-resistant schizophrenia.
- Three broad neurobiological models are identified: (A) same pathophysiology as responsive illness but more severe; (B) neurobiologically distinct pathophysiology; (C) hybrid of both, with additional neurobiological alteration.
- Evidence aligns: clozapine's efficacy (low D2 occupancy, multi-receptor profile) fits Model B; esketamine/olanzapine-fluoxetine combination fits Model C; high-dose strategies fit Model A.
- Fifteen novel interventions were in active clinical trials at time of writing, covering immune, glutamatergic, serotonergic, dopaminergic, and neurostimulatory targets.
- Research output on treatment resistance grew significantly (R² = 0.71, p < 0.0001) from 2000–2019 but remained at approximately 0.5% of total psychiatric publications in 2019.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's introduction frames treatment resistance as a recognised but poorly defined clinical phenomenon — one that has existed since the earliest use of antipsychotics and antidepressants. The prevailing working assumption in the field, as laid out by the authors, is that treatment resistance is a unitary phenomenon within each disorder, sharing the same neurobiology as treatment-responsive illness but to a greater degree. This assumption underpins standard dose-escalation strategies and has shaped existing guideline criteria. The paper also signals an emerging challenge to this view from neurobiological evidence (especially glutamatergic changes in treatment-resistant schizophrenia and immune dysregulation in treatment-resistant depression) suggesting that resistant cases may represent neurobiologically distinct subtypes. The introduction is primarily clinical and epidemiological rather than theoretically deep; it foregrounds definitional heterogeneity and burden, not mechanistic debate.

---

### What this paper contributes

This review establishes, through systematic analysis of 23 guidelines, that the field does not have a consistent, operationalised concept of treatment resistance — even for the best-studied conditions. This is the review's key empirical contribution: quantifying the extent of heterogeneity in definitions and its implications for clinical trial validity. On the neurobiology side, the paper synthesises evidence for three mechanistic models of resistance and maps approved or developing treatments onto each model — a useful heuristic for understanding why different treatment strategies are appropriate for different patients. The paper also contributes a practical reporting checklist (Box 1) to standardise how treatment resistance is documented in future clinical trials. Finally, it identifies major gaps: no consensus definitions for mania, PTSD, or panic; near absence of monotherapy trials; and gross under-investment relative to prevalence. These constitute an explicit research agenda for the field.

### Brain regions & systems

The paper is primarily clinical and neurobiological rather than systems-neuroscience focused. Relevant systems noted:

- **Dopaminergic system (nigrostriatal/mesolimbic)** — central to antipsychotic mechanism of action via D2 receptor blockade; D2/3 receptor upregulation proposed as a mechanism of secondary treatment resistance in schizophrenia.
- **Glutamatergic system / anterior cingulate cortex** — elevated glutamatergic metabolites in the anterior cingulate cortex observed in treatment-resistant schizophrenia versus responsive patients; implicated in Model B resistance.
- **Serotonergic system** — implicated in antidepressant mechanism and in treatment-resistant depression; esketamine's additional serotonergic effects noted.
- **Noradrenergic system** — high-dose venlafaxine's efficacy in treatment-resistant depression linked to enhanced noradrenergic engagement above 150 mg/day.
- **Immune/neuroimmune system** — C-reactive protein elevated in treatment-resistant depression; cytokine dysregulation (e.g., TNF) implicated in a subgroup of resistant cases.
- **Blood-brain barrier** — discussed as a factor in pseudo-resistance via P-glycoprotein polymorphisms affecting drug penetrance.

No single brain region is foregrounded as a primary focus; the paper operates at the neurochemical systems level.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight as defined here. It does not present or formalise a specific algorithm for treatment resistance, and it does not provide original neural data linking a proposed computational mechanism to measured brain activity. It synthesises existing evidence for three disease models but does so at the level of neurotransmitter systems, without mapping these onto Marr's algorithmic or implementational levels. The neurobiological section is informative as a taxonomy of hypotheses but does not adjudicate between them with direct neural evidence.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- No operationalised consensus definitions of treatment resistance for mania, PTSD, panic disorder, or substance dependence.
- Marked heterogeneity in existing definitions makes comparisons across trials unreliable; the paper acknowledges this but cannot resolve it.
- The three neurobiological models are not empirically tested against each other; their relative prevalence in patient populations is unknown.
- Pseudo-resistance is acknowledged as a major clinical problem but remains systematically under-assessed; prospective algorithms for ruling it out are not validated in the paper.
- The review was conducted before trial results for many of the 15 identified interventions were published; their efficacy remains to be established.
- Functional impairment is included as a criterion in only one of 23 guidelines, despite its clinical relevance.
- Transdiagnostic approaches to treatment resistance (e.g., across psychotic symptoms in schizophrenia and bipolar disorder) are proposed but not developed.
- The review focuses on pharmacological treatment resistance; definitions and criteria for psychological therapy resistance are similarly underdeveloped.

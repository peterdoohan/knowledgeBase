### One-line summary

Single-channel permeability measurements reveal that mCx30.2 gap junctions are ~40- to 170-fold more adapted for electrical than metabolic communication compared to Cx40, Cx43, and Cx45, with permeability determined by the more restrictive hemichannel in heterotypic configurations.

---

### Background & motivation

Gap junction channels formed by connexins enable both electrical signaling and metabolic communication between cells. Cardiac tissue expresses multiple connexin isoforms (mCx30.2, Cx40, Cx43, Cx45) with distinct expression patterns, but the permeability properties of homotypic and heterotypic channels formed by these cardiac connexins were not systematically quantified at the single-channel level. Understanding these permeability differences is crucial for understanding how different connexin compositions affect cell-cell communication in cardiac tissue.

---

### Methods

- **Cell system**: HeLa cells stably transfected with wild-type mCx30.2, Cx40, Cx43, or Cx45, or fusion forms with EGFP/CFP
- **Electrophysiology**: Dual whole-cell patch clamp to measure junctional conductance (gj) in homotypic and heterotypic cell pairs
- **Dye transfer assays**: Fluorescent dyes introduced via patch pipette into donor cell; fluorescence imaging to monitor transfer to recipient cell
- **Dyes used**: Alexa Fluor-350 (AF350, 326 Da, -1 charge), Lucifer yellow (LY, 443 Da, -2 charge), ethidium bromide (314 Da, +1), DAPI (279 Da, +2), propidium iodide (415 Da, +2)
- **Single-channel permeability calculation**: Pγ = vol2 × ΔFI2/[Δt(FI1,n − FI2,n)(gj/γ)], where γ is single-channel conductance
- **Heterotypic junctions**: Created by co-culturing cells expressing different connexins; identified via differential GFP fluorescence or nuclear/cell membrane dyes

---

### Key findings

- **Homotypic channel permeabilities for AF350**: Cx43 (86 × 10⁻¹⁵ cm³/s) > Cx40 (33 × 10⁻¹⁵ cm³/s) > Cx45 (5.5 × 10⁻¹⁵ cm³/s) >> mCx30.2 (0.04 × 10⁻¹⁵ cm³/s); ~2,000-fold difference between Cx43 and mCx30.2
- **Homotypic channel permeabilities for LY**: Cx43 (24.6 × 10⁻¹⁵ cm³/s) > Cx40 (6.9 × 10⁻¹⁵ cm³/s) > Cx45 (1.1 × 10⁻¹⁵ cm³/s); mCx30.2 showed no detectable LY transfer (non-permeable)
- **Permeability ratios (γ/Pγ for AF350)**: mCx30.2 (225 × 10⁻³ S·cm⁻³·s) >> Cx45 (5.8) > Cx40 (4.5) > Cx43 (1.3), indicating mCx30.2 is ~40- to 170-fold more adapted for electrical than metabolic communication
- **Heterotypic channel permeability**: Values were intermediate to corresponding homotypic channels; hemichannels retain their permeability characteristics upon docking
- **Restrictive hemichannel dominance**: When one hemichannel was substantially less permeable (e.g., mCx30.2), it largely determined the overall channel permeability
- **Size vs. charge selectivity in mCx30.2**: Discrimination based on molecular size (>400 Da) rather than charge; permeable to DAPI (279 Da, +2) but not to propidium iodide (415 Da, +2) or LY (443 Da, -2)
- **Cx40/Cx43 incompatibility**: Heterotypic junctions between Cx40 and Cx43 did not form functional channels due to inability to dock

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The prevailing understanding was that gap junction channels formed by different connexin isoforms have distinct permeability properties, but quantitative single-channel permeability data for cardiac connexins was limited. Previous studies had established that Cx40, Cx43, and Cx45 are the major cardiac connexins with distinct expression patterns affecting cardiac conduction. mCx30.2 had been recently identified as a cardiac connexin expressed in the conduction system, with very small single-channel conductance (~9 pS). It was known that heterotypic channels can form between compatible connexins, but the permeability properties of these heterotypic cardiac connexin channels had not been systematically quantified.

---

### What this paper contributes

This paper provides the first quantitative single-channel permeability measurements for homotypic and heterotypic gap junction channels formed by cardiac connexins mCx30.2, Cx40, Cx43, and Cx45. Key contributions include:

1. **mCx30.2 is specialized for electrical coupling**: The ~40- to 170-fold higher ratio of conductance to permeability for mCx30.2 compared to other cardiac connexins demonstrates that mCx30.2 gap junctions are notably more adapted for electrical than metabolic communication.

2. **Hemichannel permeability is preserved upon docking**: Heterotypic channels exhibit permeability values intermediate to their corresponding homotypic channels, indicating that hemichannels retain their permeability characteristics when docked.

3. **The restrictive hemichannel dominates**: When one hemichannel in a heterotypic pair is substantially less permeable, it largely determines the overall channel permeability.

4. **Size-based discrimination in mCx30.2**: The inability of mCx30.2 to pass molecules >400 Da (LY, propidium iodide) while passing smaller molecules (DAPI, AF350) indicates size-based rather than charge-based selectivity.

### Brain regions & systems

Not applicable. This study uses a heterologous expression system (HeLa cells) to study cardiac connexins. However, the findings are relevant to:

- **Cardiac conduction system**: mCx30.2 is expressed most abundantly in the AV node and other conduction tissue
- **Working myocardium**: Cx43 is the primary connexin in ventricular and atrial working muscle
- **Atrial tissue and Purkinje fibers**: Cx40 is prominently expressed in these regions
- **Cardiac development and disease**: Cx45 expression is developmentally regulated and present in various cardiac regions

The study also notes that mCx30.2 is expressed in neurons of hippocampus, cerebellum, thalamus, and brainstem, suggesting relevance to central nervous system function as well.

### Mechanistic insight

This paper does not fully meet the high bar for mechanistic insight as defined in the guidelines. While it provides detailed biophysical characterization of gap junction permeability, it does not present a formal algorithm or computational model of the process, nor does it provide neural data in the sense of recordings from native tissue. However, the study does provide important biophysical constraints that inform understanding of how connexin composition affects cell-cell communication:

The study demonstrates that:
1. **The pore structure determines permeability**: The ~2,000-fold difference in AF350 permeability between Cx43 and mCx30.2 channels (despite only ~12-fold difference in conductance) indicates that pore geometry and size exclusion, not just overall conductance, determine molecular permeability.

2. **Hemichannels retain individual properties upon docking**: The observation that heterotypic channels exhibit intermediate permeabilities demonstrates that hemichannel properties are preserved upon docking, with the more restrictive hemichannel largely determining overall permeability.

These findings constrain models of gap junction function and suggest that mCx30.2 may have evolved to provide electrical coupling in the AV node without allowing passage of larger signaling molecules that might disrupt the precise timing required for proper cardiac conduction.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- **Cationic dye quantification**: Permeabilities for positively charged dyes (ethidium bromide, DAPI, propidium iodide) could only be assessed qualitatively due to their binding to nucleic acids, preventing accurate quantification
- **Heterotypic junction coverage**: Only 5 of 6 possible heterotypic junctions were examined; Cx40/Cx43 heterotypic junctions could not be studied due to their inability to dock
- **Cell system limitations**: HeLa cells are a heterologous expression system; results may not fully recapitulate native cardiac myocyte environment
- **Limited metabolic substrates**: Only fluorescent dyes were tested, not actual metabolic signaling molecules (e.g., ATP, cAMP, IP3) that may have different permeability properties
- **Molecular mechanism**: The structural basis for the size selectivity in mCx30.2 remains unresolved; suggests a pore constriction but does not identify specific structural features

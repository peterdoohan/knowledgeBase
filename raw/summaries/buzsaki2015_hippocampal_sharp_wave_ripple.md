### One-line summary

Sharp wave-ripples (SPW-Rs) are the most synchronous population events in the mammalian brain, arising from CA3 recurrent circuitry during offline states, and serve as the neural substrate for memory consolidation, sequence replay, prospective planning, and potentially creative thought — while their pathological variants are biomarkers of epileptogenicity and cognitive disease.

---

### Background & motivation

SPW-Rs are large-amplitude (40–100 ms negative deflection) LFP events in CA1 stratum radiatum, coupled with fast oscillatory ripples (110–200 Hz) in the CA1 pyramidal layer, that occur during consummatory behaviors and non-REM sleep — i.e., the "offline" brain states complementary to the theta-dominated exploratory states. By 2015, SPW-Rs had been identified as the first definitive neural biomarker for cognitive operations, but their full functional scope and mechanisms of generation remained incompletely understood. The review synthesises three decades of in vivo, in vitro, pharmacological, optogenetic, and computational work to unify the physiology, cognitive functions (retrospective and prospective), and disease relevance of SPW-Rs in a single framework.

---

### Methods

This is a comprehensive narrative review covering:

- Electrophysiological studies (in vivo extracellular, intracellular, juxtacellular, optogenetic) in rodents, primates, and humans characterising SPW-R generation, propagation, and interneuron-specific firing patterns
- In vitro slice and whole-hippocampus preparations examining pharmacological and cellular mechanisms of SPW-R and ripple generation
- Population-level spike sequence analyses (template matching, Bayesian decoding, explained variance measures) during sleep replay and awake replay in behaving rodents
- Closed-loop optogenetic and electrical disruption/enhancement experiments testing the causal role of SPW-Rs in memory consolidation
- Computational models of population burst generation (Traub-type recurrent excitation), ripple oscillation (PYR-INT, INT-INT, gap junction models; Brunel-Wang, Taxidis), and LFP current sources (Schomburg)
- Human EEG/depth electrode data linking SPW-R incidence to memory performance and identifying pathological ripples (p-ripples) in epilepsy
- Rodent models of epilepsy (kainic acid, pilocarpine), schizophrenia (calcineurin KO, MAM model), and Alzheimer's Disease (rTg4510 tau transgenic, hAPP mice)

### Key findings

**Generation and physiology:**
- SPW-Rs are intrinsic, self-organised events of the CA3-CA1 system: they persist after neocortical, entorhinal, and septal deafferentation but are suppressed by subcortical neuromodulators (acetylcholine, norepinephrine, serotonin, histamine, cannabinoids).
- The sharp wave is generated by synchronous CA3 recurrent collateral bursting, conveyed to CA1 apical dendrites via Schaffer collaterals; the ripple (110–200 Hz) is a local CA1 phenomenon.
- Neuronal participation probability in SPW-Rs follows a strongly skewed lognormal distribution: ~1.5% of CA1 pyramidal cells participate in ~50% of events; up to 50% of pyramidal cells can be recruited in large events.
- PV-expressing basket cells are the primary pacemakers of ripple frequency, demonstrated by optogenetic experiments (Stark et al., 2014; Schlingloff et al., 2014): PV activation induces ripple-frequency entrainment of pyramidal cells and GABAA receptor blockade abolishes ripples.
- The SPW is terminated by a GABAB-mediated post-ripple hyperpolarisation that scales with ripple magnitude; this creates a refractory period between events.
- Gap junctions are not essential for physiological ripples (Connexin36/45 double knockout mice show normal SPW-Rs).
- SPW-Rs travel along the septotemporal CA1 axis at ~0.35 m/s; propagation is smooth in septal-intermediate segments but the temporal segment is often functionally isolated.
- Ripple coherence is spatially local (confined by interneuron-mediated inhibition), even when the underlying SPW sweeps a larger volume.

**Memory and replay:**
- Waking place cell sequences are replayed in time-compressed form (~10–20x compression) during SPW-Rs of post-experience non-REM sleep (Wilson & McNaughton 1994; Lee & Wilson 2002; Nadasdy et al. 1999).
- Both forward (prospective) and reverse (retrospective) replay occur during waking SPW-Rs; forward replay predominates before traversal and reverse replay after (Diba & Buzsaki 2007).
- Selective closed-loop disruption of SPW-Rs during post-learning sleep or waking impairs hippocampus-dependent spatial memory without affecting other sleep parameters (Girardeau et al. 2009; Ego-Stengel & Wilson 2010; Jadhav et al. 2012).
- Conversely, closed-loop coupling of SPW-R detection to prefrontal slow-oscillation/spindle reset enhances memory consolidation (Maingret et al. 2013).
- SPW-R incidence increases following novel learning and correlates with subsequent memory performance; NMDA receptor blockade during encoding abolishes the post-learning elevation of SPW-Rs (Girardeau et al. 2014).
- Hippocampal cell assemblies reactivated during SPW-Rs predict prefrontal cortex spindle activity ~40 ms later (Peyrache et al. 2009; Wierzynski et al. 2009).

**Prospective and constructive roles:**
- Forward SPW-R sequences encode not just recently traversed trajectories but future paths and novel shortcuts never directly experienced (Pfeiffer & Foster 2013; Gupta et al. 2010).
- "Preplay" of future place cell sequences on unvisited tracks occurs during pre-exploration rest/sleep, suggesting a large repertoire of self-organised sequences preconfigures hippocampal dynamics (Dragoi & Tonegawa 2011, 2013).
- SPW-R clusters can "stitch together" arm-specific sequences to represent joined routes and predict upcoming choices (Wu & Foster 2014).
- A two-population model is proposed: a small fast-firing "rigid" minority of neurons forms the preexisting sequence vocabulary; a larger slow-firing plastic majority is refined by novel experience.

**Disease:**
- Physiological ripples are converted to pathological ripples (p-ripples, >200 Hz or abnormally synchronous at <200 Hz) in epileptic tissue; p-ripples are a reliable biomarker of the epileptogenic zone and their surgical removal correlates with seizure freedom (Bragin et al. 1999; Jacobs et al. 2012).
- In a calcineurin KO model of schizophrenia, SPW-R occurrence is elevated but sequential reactivation of place cells during SPW-Rs is abolished (Suh et al. 2013); in a MAM model, hippocampal-prefrontal SPW-R/spindle coordination is disrupted (Phillips et al. 2012).
- In rTg4510 tau transgenic mice, SPW-Rs have reduced amplitude and altered temporal structure, with interneuron phase-locking significantly decreased (Witton et al. 2014).

### Computational framework

The paper integrates two overlapping frameworks:

**1. Dynamical systems / population burst dynamics (SPW generation):**
SPW-Rs are treated as emergent events from a recurrent excitatory attractor network (CA3). The CA3 recurrent collateral system operates as a stochastic relaxation oscillator: when subcortical neuromodulator suppression is lifted, population bursts spontaneously emerge from random fluctuations in synchrony exceeding a recruitment threshold. The magnitude distribution of events is lognormal, consistent with a skewed distribution of synaptic weights and firing rates. Key variables are the fraction of co-active pyramidal cells, the magnitude of the SPW sink (excitatory drive), and the ripple frequency (set by PV interneuron GABAA kinetics).

**2. Sequence-based memory / vocabulary model (cognitive functions):**
Hippocampal computation is modelled as a two-stage memory system (Buzsaki 1989; Marr 1971). During theta-associated waking exploration, transient synaptic weights encode episodes. During subsequent SPW-Rs, time-compressed replays of sequential cell assemblies (neural "words") consolidate hippocampal content to neocortex. The "vocabulary" model extends this: a preconfigured repertoire of self-organised assembly sequences pre-exists in the hippocampus (arising from lognormal dynamics), and learning is primarily a process of matching novel experiences to preexisting sequence templates, with plastic slow-firing neurons refining those matches. SPW-R clusters chain sequences into "sentences" representing extended spatial trajectories or event sequences.

The Brunel-Wang (2003) integrate-and-fire model is highlighted as the most systematic formalisation, showing that fast (ripple-frequency) oscillations emerge from interneuron-interneuron interactions at high excitation levels, while slower gamma oscillations emerge when recurrent excitation is stronger.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

At the time of the review's writing, the dominant understanding was that SPW-Rs are the hippocampal event mediating memory consolidation through a "two-stage" model (Buzsaki 1989; McClelland et al. 1995): theta state encodes new information via long-term synaptic potentiation in CA3, and subsequent SPW-R states during non-REM sleep replay those patterns to the neocortex, gradually consolidating memories from labile hippocampal traces to stable neocortical representations. The CA3 recurrent network was accepted as the SPW generator and CA1 as the ripple generator. The inhibitory interneuron role was partially characterised (PV basket cells fire at ripple frequency, axo-axonic cells are suppressed) but the specific mechanism of ripple-frequency pacing was actively debated between gap junction (axonal coupling), PYR-INT loop, and INT-INT loop hypotheses. The retrospective replay function was well-supported, but the prospective/planning role and the "vocabulary" / preplay concept were newer and contested at the time of review.

---

### What this paper contributes

The review makes the following substantive contributions:

1. **Definitively resolves the ripple generation debate** (as of 2015): through synthesis of optogenetic data (Stark et al. 2014; Schlingloff et al. 2014), the review establishes that PV basket cell INT-INT interactions, acting via GABAA receptors, are necessary and sufficient for ripple frequency pacing — and that axonal gap junctions are not essential.

2. **Expands the functional model from retrospective to prospective and constructive**: the review reframes SPW-Rs as not merely replaying the past but generating novel trajectories (preplay, shortcuts), thereby establishing their role in planning, decision-making, and potentially creative thought.

3. **Introduces the neuronal vocabulary model**: proposes that the hippocampus generates a large self-organised repertoire of sequences that preconfigures learning, and that experience is largely a matching/refinement process rather than de novo construction — a significant theoretical departure from pure Hebbian tabula rasa accounts.

4. **Establishes disease relevance**: systematically connects p-ripple physiology to epilepsy, schizophrenia, depression, and Alzheimer's Disease, unifying them under disrupted SPW-R dynamics, and argues that anti-epileptic drugs' broader therapeutic efficacy may reflect shared mechanisms.

5. **Identifies SPW-Rs as the first definitive cognitive biomarker**: summarises causal evidence (closed-loop disruption) that SPW-Rs are not correlates but necessary mechanisms for hippocampus-dependent memory consolidation and working memory.

### Brain regions & systems

- **CA3 hippocampus** — primary generator of sharp waves via synchronous population bursts in the strongly recurrent collateral system; source of the Schaffer collateral drive that initiates ripples in CA1
- **CA1 hippocampus** — site of ripple generation; receives CA3 Schaffer collateral input; output to subiculum, entorhinal cortex, prefrontal cortex; deep vs. superficial pyramidal cell sublayers have distinct SPW-R firing properties
- **CA2 hippocampus** — proposed alternative SPW trigger via basal dendritic excitation of CA1; understudied at review time
- **Dentate gyrus** — source of dentate spikes (DS1, DS2) that modulate SPW-R probability via CA3; shows stochastic resonance relationship with SPW-R incidence
- **Subiculum** — receives CA1 output; shows slower ripples than CA1; subicular bursting propensity makes it vulnerable to p-ripple generation in epilepsy
- **Entorhinal cortex (deep layers, layer 5)** — receives CA1 SPW-R output with decreasing excitatory gain; superficial layers suppressed during SPW-Rs; UP-DOWN state transitions trigger SPW-Rs via CA3
- **Medial prefrontal cortex** — monosynaptic target of CA1/subicular output; cell assemblies reactivated ~40 ms after hippocampal SPW-Rs; disrupted hippocampal-PFC coordination in schizophrenia models
- **Thalamus / thalamocortical system** — sleep spindles loosely entrain SPW-Rs via neocortical-entorhinal pathway; negative BOLD response in thalamus during SPW-Rs (Logothetis et al. 2012)
- **Medial septum** — cholinergic neurons suppress SPW-Rs when active; optogenetic activation abolishes SPW-Rs; GABAergic septal neurons (targeting CA3 axo-axonic cells) proposed to trigger SPW bursts via disinhibition
- **Ventral striatum / lateral septum / hypothalamus** — subcortical targets of hippocampal output during SPW-Rs; hypothalamus potentially involved in endocrine function via SPW-R-driven activation
- **Olfactory piriform cortex / amygdala** — show SPW-like population events that are largely independent of hippocampal SPW-Rs but may share consolidation functions

### Mechanistic insight

This paper meets the high bar for mechanistic insight.

**Phenomenon:** Hippocampal sharp wave-ripples are the most synchronous physiological population events in the mammalian brain, arising during offline states, and carry temporally compressed sequential neural activity that supports memory consolidation and planning.

**Computational level:** The hippocampal system solves the problem of consolidating rapidly encoded episodic memories from a high-capacity but labile temporary store (CA3/CA1) to distributed long-term neocortical representations. Additionally, it must generate a rich combinatorial repertoire of sequential representations for prospective planning without requiring direct experience of every future event.

**Algorithmic level:**
- SPW-Rs implement time-compressed, sequential replay of cell assembly sequences (neural "words") at ~10–20x waking speed.
- Replay can be forward (prospective, planning) or reverse (retrospective, reinforcement).
- SPW-R clusters chain sequences into extended representations of spatial trajectories or episode structures ("sentences").
- A large vocabulary of self-organised sequences is preconfigured by the lognormal dynamics of synaptic weights and firing rates; learning primarily matches experiences to pre-existing templates and refines slow-firing plastic neurons.
- Ripple frequency (110–200 Hz) is set by the time constant of GABAA-receptor-mediated inhibition among interconnected PV basket cells (INT-INT model), not by gap junctions or simple PYR-INT loops.

**Implementational level:**
- The sharp wave is generated by synchronous bursting in the CA3 recurrent axon collateral system (~60–100 km of axons in the rat, contacting 5–10 billion potential synapses).
- The ripple is generated locally in CA1 by PV basket cells, whose mutually inhibitory connections (via fast GABAA kinetics, ~IPSP decay 5–10 ms) pace pyramidal cells at ripple frequency.
- The LFP ripple has two main current contributors: superimposed action potentials of synchronously firing pyramidal cells (negative troughs), and somatic IPSCs from PV basket cells (positive peaks).
- Spatiotemporal coordination of multiple local ripple oscillators is achieved by the long-range axon arbours of PV interneurons interconnected through fast inhibition.
- Distinct interneuron types play specific roles: PV basket cells (maximally active, pacemakers), bistratified cells (active, suppress pyramidal bursting via dendritic inhibition), axo-axonic cells (suppressed or variably active, may facilitate pyramidal excitability during SPW-Rs), O-LM cells (variably active, suppress entorhinal input), CCK basket cells (suppressed, auto-disinhibition via CB1 receptors).
- Post-ripple silence is caused by an active (likely GABAB-mediated K+ conductance) hyperpolarisation whose magnitude scales with ripple size.

**Neural data supporting the algorithm over alternatives:**
- Connexin36/45 double knockout mice show normal SPW-Rs, refuting the gap junction hypothesis.
- Optogenetic activation of pyramidal cells alone (CaMKII::ChR2) induces genuine ripples, confirming that interneuron activation is not required to initiate but only to pace them.
- Local GABAA receptor blockade (picrotoxin/gabazine) abolishes ripples in vivo and in vitro; PV activation alone can induce ripple-frequency pyramidal entrainment even with NMDA/AMPA receptors blocked.
- Closed-loop disruption of SPW-Rs causally impairs memory consolidation, proving behavioural relevance of the algorithm.

### Limitations & open questions

- **Directionality of replay** (forward vs. reverse) is not mechanistically explained; the place-field tail hypothesis is insufficient as a universal account.
- **Preplay** is surprising from a Hebbian tabula rasa view: it is unclear whether preconfigured sequences reflect entirely internal dynamics or residual associations from prior experience in different environments.
- **Distinction between PRE and POST sleep** comparisons is complicated by lognormal firing rate distributions, homeostatic firing rate changes across sleep, and short recording epochs.
- **Causal link between SPW-R spike sequences and neocortical plasticity** has not been directly demonstrated; it is unknown whether the ripple oscillation itself (vs. the SPW envelope) is functionally critical.
- **Role of SPW-Rs in synaptic plasticity** is poorly constrained: it is unclear whether SPW-Rs strengthen, depress, or decorrelate specific synapses, and whether STDP or alternative mechanisms are operative.
- **Interneuron choreography** is incompletely mapped in drug-free, freely behaving animals: most detailed interneuron classification uses anesthesia, which changes SPW-R properties.
- **Septotemporal topography**: conditions determining propagation direction and the differential contribution of dorsal vs. ventral SPW-Rs to distinct neocortical targets remain unknown.
- **Translation to primates/humans**: ripple frequency is lower (~80–160 Hz) and the mechanistic data are sparse; whether the same PV-based pacing mechanism applies is assumed but not demonstrated.
- **P-ripples and disease**: the mechanistic link between p-ripples and specific cognitive symptoms in non-epileptic diseases (schizophrenia, Alzheimer's) is largely inferential from animal models.
- **Conscious vs. subconscious role**: whether SPW-R sequences enter conscious processing or are strictly subconscious precursors remains entirely unresolved.

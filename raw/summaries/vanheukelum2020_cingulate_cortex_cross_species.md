### One-line summary

The standard Cg1/Cg2 nomenclature for rodent cingulate cortex is anatomically non-homologous to the ACC/MCC partitioning used in other mammals, and adopting the homologous ACC/MCC definition better aligns rodent findings with cross-species results and reveals clearer functional organization within rodent cingulate cortex itself.

### Background & motivation

Cross-species homologies are essential for translating neuroscience findings, yet rodent cingulate cortex is typically parcellated into Cg1 and Cg2, drawing borders perpendicular to the ACC/MCC boundary used in humans, primates, and rabbits. This discrepancy creates a fundamental obstacle for cross-species comparison because Cg1 and Cg2 each span parts of both ACC and MCC, while excluding A25 and A32 (which are part of ACC in other mammals). The authors argue that a homologous nomenclature proposed by Vogt et al. (2005) has been underutilized and that re-evaluating rodent literature through this lens can reconcile apparent inconsistencies.

### Methods

The authors conducted a systematic literature review and meta-analysis of cingulate cortex studies across species:

- **Literature search strategy**: Systematic PubMed searches for studies investigating ACC and MCC function in humans (from 2000 onwards) using keywords: autonomic function/control, negative emotion, positive emotion, pain, fear, attention, and decision-making combined with ACC and MCC search terms
- **Cross-species anatomical comparison**: Reviewed connectivity studies in humans (diffusion-weighted MRI), monkeys (axonal fibre-tracing), and rodents (tract-tracing studies [14,71,72–78])
- **Functional mapping**: Mapped functional categories from human studies onto rodent cingulate cortex based on anatomical coordinates (bregma coordinates) provided by each study
- **Connectivity reinterpretation**: Reanalyzed published rodent connectivity data comparing Cg1/Cg2 vs. ACC/MCC parcellations using data from Fillinger et al. (2016) [14] and Hoover & Vertes (2007) [71]

### Key findings

- **Structural homology**: The ACC/MCC nomenclature draws borders along the rostrocaudal axis (A24/A24′ boundary), consistent with humans, primates, and rabbits, while the Cg1/Cg2 nomenclature draws borders perpendicular to this axis, creating non-homologous parcellation

- **Connectivity differences**: When using the ACC/MCC nomenclature, rodent ACC shows strong connections to amygdala, OFC, hypothalamus, and autonomic brainstem nuclei (matching human ACC connectivity), while rodent MCC connects to parietal cortex, motor cortices, and pontine nuclei (matching human MCC connectivity). The Cg1/Cg2 nomenclature produces mixed, less specific connectivity patterns

- **Functional segregation**: Studies using the ACC/MCC nomenclature show clearer functional segregation:
  - Rodent ACC (A24, A25, A32): involved in reward, negative affect, pain, autonomic regulation, and depressive-like behaviors
  - Rodent MCC (A24′): involved in attention, decision-making, pain gating, and aggression regulation (complementary to ACC)
  
- **Reinterpretation benefits**: Reanalyzing existing Cg1/Cg2 studies by their anatomical coordinates revealed that behavior-related volumetric differences between aggressive and non-aggressive mice vanished when using Cg1/Cg2 definitions but became clear when separating ACC and MCC

- **Parvalbumin interneuron gradient**: The concentration of parvalbumin interneurons in MCC (but not ACC) predicted differences in aggressive behavior, demonstrating structural-functional relationships that are obscured by the Cg1/Cg2 nomenclature

### Computational framework

Not applicable. This is a review and anatomical comparative analysis without explicit computational modeling. However, the paper implicitly assumes a framework of functional segregation and specialized information processing where distinct anatomical regions (ACC vs. MCC) serve different computational purposes: ACC for emotional/motivational evaluation and autonomic regulation, MCC for action control, conflict monitoring, and decision-making under uncertainty.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The prevailing model in rodent neuroscience has been that cingulate cortex is divided into Cg1 (dorsal) and Cg2 (ventral), with these regions often implicitly or explicitly treated as corresponding to human MCC and ACC respectively. This model treats A25 (infralimbic cortex) and A32 (prelimbic cortex) as separate from cingulate cortex proper. The field has operated under the assumption that findings from Cg1/Cg2 studies can be directly compared to human ACC/MCC studies, despite the fundamental anatomical misalignment. The prevailing model assumes functional continuity across species while using anatomical definitions that are themselves discontinuous.

### What this paper contributes

This paper fundamentally challenges the prevailing Cg1/Cg2 model and provides a comprehensive argument for adopting the homologous ACC/MCC nomenclature in rodent research:

1. **Anatomical clarity**: Demonstrates that the Cg1/Cg2 border is perpendicular to the ACC/MCC border, meaning Cg1 and Cg2 each contain parts of both ACC and MCC, while excluding A25 and A32 which are ACC components in other mammals

2. **Connectivity alignment**: Shows that the ACC/MCC nomenclature produces connectivity patterns in rodents that closely match those in humans (ACC: amygdala, OFC, hypothalamus, autonomic nuclei; MCC: parietal, motor, pontine nuclei), whereas Cg1/Cg2 shows mixed, less specific patterns

3. **Functional segregation**: Demonstrates that when studies are reinterpreted using the ACC/MCC nomenclature, clearer functional dissociations emerge between ACC (emotion, reward, autonomic regulation) and MCC (attention, decision-making, pain gating, aggression)

4. **Empirical demonstrations**: Provides concrete examples where the ACC/MCC nomenclature reveals findings obscured by Cg1/Cg2, such as aggression-related volumetric differences and parvalbumin interneuron gradients

5. **Practical recommendations**: Advocates for explicit reporting of anatomical definitions, Brodmann areas, and stereotaxic coordinates in future studies, and for review articles to verify whether cited rodent studies actually investigated ACC/MCC or Cg1/Cg2

### Brain regions & systems

- **Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** — emotional and motivational processing hub; strong connections to amygdala, OFC, hypothalamus, and autonomic brainstem nuclei; involved in reward processing, negative affect, pain, and autonomic regulation
- **Midcingulate cortex (MCC)** — action control and decision-making hub; strong connections to parietal cortex, motor cortices, and pontine nuclei; involved in attention, conflict monitoring, error detection, and reward-based decision-making
- **Subgenual ACC (sACC)** — autonomic regulation and emotional processing; connections to autonomic brainstem nuclei
- **Pregenual ACC (pACC)** — emotional processing; connections to amygdala and vmPFC
- **Anterior MCC (aMCC)** — moderate amygdalar input; cognitive control functions
- **Posterior MCC (pMCC)** — no amygdalar input; sensorimotor and pain processing
- **Cingulate area 1 (Cg1)** — non-homologous rodent nomenclature; encompasses A24b and A24b′ (parts of both ACC and MCC in homologous nomenclature)
- **Cingulate area 2 (Cg2)** — non-homologous rodent nomenclature; encompasses A24a and A24a′ (parts of both ACC and MCC in homologous nomenclature)
- **Infralimbic cortex (IL)** — equivalent to A25; part of ACC in homologous nomenclature but treated as separate in Cg1/Cg2 nomenclature; involved in autonomic regulation and fear extinction
- **Prelimbic cortex (PL)** — equivalent to A32; part of ACC in homologous nomenclature but treated as separate in Cg1/Cg2 nomenclature; involved in fear expression and decision-making
- **Retrosplenial cortex (RSC)** — connected to MCC; involved in visuospatial orientation
- **Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** — connected to ACC; involved in reward and emotional processing
- **Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** — connected to ACC; involved in emotional processing
- **Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC)** — connected to both ACC and MCC; involved in cognitive control
- **Amygdala** — strongly connected to ACC; involved in emotional processing and fear
- **Hypothalamus** — connected to ACC; involved in autonomic regulation
- **Thalamus** — differentially connected to ACC and MCC subregions
- **Parietal cortex** — strongly connected to MCC; involved in attention and sensorimotor processing
- **Motor cortex** — connected to MCC; involved in action execution
- **Periaqueductal grey (PAG)** — connected to both ACC and MCC; involved in pain modulation
- **Nucleus accumbens (NAc)** — connected to ACC; involved in reward processing
- **Pontine nuclei** — connected to MCC; involved in motor control
- **Autonomic brainstem nuclei** — connected to ACC; involved in autonomic regulation

### Mechanistic insight

This paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight as defined in the instructions. It is a review and opinion piece that synthesizes existing anatomical and functional literature to argue for a nomenclature change. While it discusses anatomical connectivity patterns and functional roles of cingulate subregions, it does not present or formalize a specific algorithmic model, nor does it provide neural data that specifically supports one computational algorithm over alternatives. The paper's contribution is anatomical-taxonomic rather than mechanistic-computational.

### Limitations & open questions

- **Adoption barriers**: Researchers prioritize consistency across studies and may continue using established Cg1/Cg2 definitions; many laboratories still use brain atlases featuring the Cg1/Cg2 definition
- **Functional differences between species**: Not all features of rodent cingulate cortex are directly homologous to humans; rodent MCC may serve certain functions different from human MCC
- **Sulcal differences**: Sulcal areas of cingulate cortex present in primates but not rodents may contribute unique functions to primate prefrontal cortex beyond size constraints
- **Limited rodent MCC research**: The Cg1/Cg2 definition has largely prevented rodent MCC from being studied separately from ACC; few studies have explicitly investigated rodent A24′ (MCC) independently
- **Cognitive functions in rodents**: It remains unclear whether rodents have cognitive functions analogous to primate MCC functions (attention, decision-making) and where these are implemented if not in MCC
- **Subcircuits in rodent MCC**: Unlike human/primate MCC, rodent MCC currently appears uniform; future research may reveal subcircuits similar to those in human/primate MCC
- **Visual connections**: Rodent MCC has strong reciprocal connections with visual cortex; the functional purpose of these connections in less vision-driven rodents is unclear
- **Task development**: Better behavioral tasks are needed to dissect cognitive functions precisely in rodents and test whether similar functional mappings exist across species
- **Integration of markers**: Current homology definitions rely on either structural or functional markers in isolation; future work could merge anatomical and functional indicators (microcircuit structure, long-range connectivity, functional activation) to pinpoint homologous areas more consistently

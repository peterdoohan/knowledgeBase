### One-line summary

This review synthesises evidence that protein phosphatases — particularly PP2A — regulate cardiac gap junction (connexin 43) phosphorylation state, and that dysregulated dephosphorylation underlies reduced intercellular coupling, slow conduction, and arrhythmia susceptibility in heart failure and ischaemia.

---

### Background & motivation

Gap junctional communication between cardiomyocytes is essential for coordinated electrical propagation and contraction. Connexin 43 (Cx43), the dominant ventricular connexin, must be appropriately phosphorylated to maintain coupling; dephosphorylation reduces conductance and promotes arrhythmias. Research into cardiac phosphorylation has historically focused on protein kinases, but protein phosphatases (PPs) — the opposing regulators — have received comparatively little attention. This review addresses that gap by cataloguing the phosphatases known to interact with Cx43 or its upstream regulators, and by evaluating them as potential therapeutic targets.

---

### Methods

- Narrative review of published literature on cardiac connexin phosphorylation and dephosphorylation.
- Scope covers in vitro cell studies, ex vivo heart preparations (rat, rabbit, dog, guinea pig), and transgenic mouse and knock-in models.
- Includes authors' own prior experimental work (HF rabbit model, PAK-1 overexpression studies, PP2A co-localisation experiments).
- No formal systematic search or inclusion criteria stated; breadth is thematic rather than meta-analytic.

---

### Key findings

- Cx43 is predominantly phosphorylated under normal conditions; a 64% increase in non-phosphorylated Cx43 was found in HF rabbits relative to controls.
- PP2A co-localises with Cx43 in cardiac tissue, and PP2A co-localisation with Cx43 increased 2.5-fold in HF; PP1 levels at Cx43 were unchanged.
- Phosphatase-resistant Cx43 (S3E knock-in mice) showed resistance to conduction slowing and reduced arrhythmia inducibility; non-phosphorylatable Cx43 (S3A knock-in) showed the opposite.
- PP2A inhibition with okadaic acid (10 nmol/L) in vitro enhanced Cx43 phosphorylation and improved intercellular coupling in HF myocytes.
- PAK-1 (p21-activated kinase 1) was elevated at Cx43 in HF rabbit and human left ventricle; overexpression of active PAK-1 increased PP2A activation and Cx43 dephosphorylation, reducing coupling.
- Calcineurin (PP2B) can indirectly activate PP1 via dephosphorylation of Inhibitor 1 (I1); cyclosporine A prevented ischaemia-induced Cx43 dephosphorylation in rats.
- The tyrosine phosphatase TC-PTP can reverse v-Src-mediated Cx43 tyrosine phosphorylation but cannot fully restore gap junctional communication because MAPK-mediated serine phosphorylation is unaffected.
- Findings on specific Cx43 phosphorylation sites (Ser279, Ser282, Ser297, Ser306, Ser365, Ser368, Tyr265) are often contradictory across studies, emphasising the complexity of site-specific regulation.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The field's working model, as represented in the paper's introduction and discussion, holds that cardiac gap junctions composed of Cx43 are critical for cell-to-cell electrical propagation, and that Cx43 expression and phosphorylation state are the primary determinants of coupling strength. Kinase activity (PKA, PKC, CaMKII, MAPK, Src) was understood to be the dominant modulator of Cx43 phosphorylation, with dephosphorylation by phosphatases largely left uncharacterised. Heart failure and ischaemia were known to reduce Cx43 abundance and shift its phosphorylation toward hypophosphorylated forms, but the specific phosphatase mechanisms driving this remained poorly defined. PP1 and PP2A were known to be the predominant serine/threonine phosphatases in the heart, and PP2A had been implicated in Cx43 dephosphorylation, but upstream regulatory pathways (e.g. PAK-1, B56α, ankyrin-B) had not been systematically reviewed in this context.

---

### What this paper contributes

The review consolidates a previously fragmented literature to establish that phosphatase activity — not only kinase dysregulation — is a mechanistically important driver of Cx43 dephosphorylation, conduction slowing, and arrhythmogenesis in cardiac disease. It makes the case that PP2A is the primary culprit (elevated specifically at Cx43 in HF), identifies PAK-1 as an upstream activator of PP2A at Cx43, and maps additional regulatory nodes (B56α/ankyrin-B interaction, calcineurin/PP1/I1 axis, Src/TC-PTP tyrosine pathway) as potential intervention points. The paper also highlights unresolved contradictions in site-specific phosphorylation data as a key outstanding problem.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not fully meet the two-criteria bar for this section. It provides mechanistic hypotheses supported by a combination of the authors' own experimental data (PP2A co-localisation, PAK-1 overexpression, okadaic acid rescue) and reviewed literature, but it does not present a formal algorithm mapping computation to neural (or cardiac) implementation, nor does it systematically test one mechanistic model against alternatives. The closest it comes is the PAK-1 → PP2A → Cx43 dephosphorylation pathway, which is grounded in biochemical co-immunoprecipitation and functional coupling data from rabbit and human HF tissue. However, the causal chain is assembled from multiple separate studies rather than a single integrated test.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Findings on the functional consequences of phosphorylation at specific Cx43 serine and tyrosine residues are frequently contradictory across studies and model systems; the review does not resolve these contradictions.
- Most PP inhibitor studies use pharmacological tools (okadaic acid, calyculin A, fostriecin) that lack full selectivity among PP1, PP2A, PP4, PP5, and PP6, complicating interpretation.
- The role of Cx40 and Cx45 phosphorylation in gap junctional communication, particularly in atrial arrhythmias, is poorly characterised relative to Cx43.
- The interaction between B56α–PP2A and ankyrin-B at Cx43 is speculative; no direct evidence linking this complex to Cx43 regulation is presented.
- TC-PTP effects on tyrosine-phosphorylated Cx43 are incomplete, and MKP-1 involvement remains speculative.
- Clinical translatability of PP inhibitors (e.g. fostriecin) as antiarrhythmics remains undemonstrated; existing oncology pharmacokinetic data offer only indirect support.

### One-line summary

This editorial presents ten practical rules for structuring scientific manuscripts, organised around the principle that good structure serves the reader's needs at every scale — from sentence to full document.

---

### Background & motivation

Scientific writing is a critical yet often undertrained skill. Poor structure impedes "concept transfer" — the cross-disciplinary communication that drives scientific progress. The authors argue that many writing mistakes arise from authors writing for themselves (as the world's leading expert on their own work) rather than for a naive reader. The paper fills a gap between abstract writing advice and concrete, actionable structural principles applicable across sections, paragraphs, and sentences.

---

### Methods

This is a perspective/editorial piece, not an empirical or computational study. The synthesis draws on the authors' experience as scientists and writing mentors, informed by a wide body of scientific writing literature. The approach is narrative and prescriptive, organised into ten rules grouped into three categories:

- **Principles (Rules 1–4)**: general rules that apply across all communicative forms (papers, grants, posters).
- **Components (Rules 5–8)**: section-specific structural guidance for abstract, introduction, results, and discussion.
- **Process (Rules 9–10)**: heuristics for efficient manuscript construction and iterative improvement through feedback.

---

### Key findings

- **Rule 1 (Focus)**: Each paper should have a single central contribution, communicated in the title. Trying to communicate multiple contributions dilutes each and reduces memorability.
- **Rule 2 (Reader-centred writing)**: Writers are the least qualified to evaluate their own work from a naive reader's perspective; writing should be designed with the reader's cognitive constraints (e.g. working memory) in mind.
- **Rule 3 (C-C-C structure)**: The Context-Content-Conclusion scheme should govern structure at all scales — paper, section, and paragraph. It reduces reader confusion about why material is presented and what it means.
- **Rule 4 (Logical flow)**: Avoid zig-zag (covering the same topic in scattered locations) and use parallelism (parallel ideas expressed in parallel syntax) to reduce reader cognitive load.
- **Rule 5 (Abstract)**: The abstract must be a complete, self-contained story using C-C-C; the most common mistake is presenting results before adequate context.
- **Rule 6 (Introduction)**: The introduction should narrow from broad field gap to specific gap, culminating in a clear statement of what the paper does to fill it; it should not be a broad literature review.
- **Rule 7 (Results)**: Results should be presented as a logical sequence of declarative statements (ideally as subsection headers) supported by figures; each paragraph should set up, answer, and close one logical question.
- **Rule 8 (Discussion)**: The discussion should explain how the gap was filled, acknowledge limitations, and describe how the contribution catalyses future work.
- **Rule 9 (Time allocation)**: Writers should invest disproportionately in title, abstract, and figures (highest readership) and use outlining (one sentence per planned paragraph) to avoid wasted effort on text that does not serve the story.
- **Rule 10 (Iterate)**: Writing is an optimisation problem; feedback from test readers and reviewers is essential. Non-specific or unenthusiastic reviews usually indicate the big-picture story was not communicated; highly specific feedback usually points to local logic failures.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's implicit baseline is that scientific writing is primarily a matter of content quality and personal style, with structural considerations treated as secondary or idiosyncratic. Authors are typically untrained in formal writing principles and tend to structure manuscripts chronologically (reflecting how the research was conducted) rather than logically (reflecting what the reader needs to understand the claim). The introduction signals awareness of a broad existing literature on scientific writing but characterises it as insufficiently actionable for practising scientists.

---

### What this paper contributes

The paper establishes a concrete, hierarchically organised framework for manuscript structure that can be applied at multiple scales (whole paper, section, paragraph, sentence). Its core contribution is the articulation of the C-C-C (Context-Content-Conclusion) scheme as a universal default structural unit, and the mapping of this unit onto each section of a paper with section-specific guidance. The paper also makes explicit the reader-centred design philosophy that should govern all writing decisions, grounding this in cognitive psychology (working memory constraints, primacy/recency effects). The practical rule-violation diagnostics (Table 1) give writers a concrete self-assessment tool.

### Limitations & open questions

- The paper explicitly acknowledges glossing over word choice, grammar, the creative process, and collaboration.
- The rules are prescriptive and based on expert opinion and reasoning rather than empirical evidence that following them improves publication or citation outcomes (though one cited paper suggests shorter, result-describing titles receive more citations).
- The C-C-C framework is acknowledged to serve patient readers better than impatient ones, and the trade-off with "content-first" structures is noted but not fully resolved.
- The paper does not address genre variation (e.g. whether all rules apply equally to theory papers, methods papers, and data papers) or disciplinary differences in norms.
- Personal style as a legitimate deviation from structure is acknowledged but not operationalised — when is rule-bending justified?

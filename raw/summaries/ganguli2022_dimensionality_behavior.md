### One-line summary

A commentary advocating for quantitative phenomenological characterization of behavior before mechanistic investigation, centred on an information-theoretic framework for defining and estimating the dimensionality of behavioral time series.

---

### Background & motivation

The convergence of high-speed video, machine learning-based pose estimation, and large-scale neural recording has produced an explosion of richly detailed behavioral data. This raises the question of how to organize scientific efforts: should researchers immediately seek neural or biochemical mechanisms, or first characterize behavior quantitatively at the phenomenological level? Ganguli argues — following and extending Bialek (2022) — that rigorous quantitative phenomenology must precede mechanistic inquiry, and provides a framework for one fundamental quantitative property of behavior: its dimensionality. The dimensionality of behavior constrains how many degrees of freedom an underlying neural or biochemical system may need to generate it, making it a critical quantity for guiding subsequent mechanistic study.

---

### Methods

This is a commentary/theoretical synthesis. The approach involves:

- Reviewing historical examples in biology where quantitative phenomenology preceded and guided mechanistic discovery (DNA error correction, single-photon vision, action potential dynamics, perceptual limits).
- Presenting and elaborating the information-theoretic dimensionality framework introduced in the companion paper (Bialek 2022).
- Defining behavioral dimensionality d* via mutual information: the minimum number of optimal measurements of the behavioral past needed to capture the same predictive information about the future as the full past, as the time horizon T grows large.
- Illustrating the framework with concrete dynamical systems examples (leaky integrator, damped harmonic oscillator) and discussing the role of maximum-entropy (MaxEnt) approximations for practical estimation.
- Identifying a distinct universality class — power-law temporally correlated behavior — that leads to effectively infinite dimensionality.

---

### Key findings

- The dimensionality d* of a behavioral time series can be defined as the minimum number of smooth functions of the behavioral past that, optimally chosen, recover essentially all predictable information about the behavioral future as the time horizon T → ∞.
- For behavior generated by a finite-dimensional stochastic dynamical system with a maximum autocorrelation time τ_max, this information-theoretic measure correctly recovers d* from the behavioral time series alone (e.g., d* = 1 for a leaky integrator, d* = 2 for a damped harmonic oscillator).
- Practical estimation of d* reduces to computing the eigenvalue spectrum of a kernel matrix derived from second-order temporal correlations under a MaxEnt Gaussian approximation, with statistical significance assessed via random matrix theory.
- Behaviors with power-law temporal correlations (long-range correlations) lead to mutual information I_pred(T) that grows logarithmically with T rather than saturating, implying effectively infinite dimensionality — an intrinsically richer universality class than exponentially-decorrelating systems.
- Recent evidence (Alba et al. 2020) suggests that Drosophila behavioral states exhibit such long-range correlations, placing animal behavior in this more complex class.
- Scaling laws in language modeling by large neural networks may be related to the power-law structure of human behavioral time series, though the connection is speculative.

---

### Computational framework

The framework is **information-theoretic**, applied to dynamical systems generating behavioral time series.

- **Key quantity**: predictive mutual information I_pred(T) — the mutual information between the behavioral past (x_{-T...0}) and the behavioral future (x_{0...T}).
- **Dimensionality d***: defined as the minimum d such that the ratio f(d, T) = I_pred(T, d) / I_pred(T) saturates in d at large T, where I_pred(T, d) uses the best d measurements of the past.
- **MaxEnt approximation**: the full mutual information calculation is approximated by fitting a maximum-entropy distribution constrained to match second-order temporal statistics, reducing the problem to eigenvalue decomposition of a temporal correlation kernel.
- **Assumptions**: the framework is justified analytically when the behavioral time series is jointly Gaussian across time; the MaxEnt approximation is exact for linear dynamical systems driven by white noise.
- **Two universality classes**: (1) exponentially decaying temporal correlations → finite d*, saturating I_pred(T); (2) power-law temporal correlations → infinite effective d*, logarithmically growing I_pred(T).

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper is not focused on a specific neural system or brain region. Rather, it engages with a methodological disposition prevailing in modern systems neuroscience: the assumption that because large-scale neural recordings and behavioral measurements can now be made simultaneously, the primary scientific goal should be to directly explain behavior in terms of neural or biochemical mechanisms. Ganguli (following Bialek) characterizes this as a premature skip past the phenomenological stage. The implicit prevailing model is that behavior is complex, its dimensionality is uncharacterized, and that rich mechanistic data (connectomes, population recordings) are sufficient tools for understanding it.

---

### What this paper contributes

The commentary argues that quantitative behavioral phenomenology — specifically the rigorous characterization of behavioral dimensionality — is an essential precursor to mechanistic inquiry, not a distraction from it. It extends and expounds Bialek's framework by: (1) situating it in a broader historical tradition of productive biological phenomenology; (2) explicating the information-theoretic definition of dimensionality and its connection to dynamical systems theory; (3) introducing the MaxEnt/random-matrix estimation pipeline; and (4) highlighting the distinction between finite-dimensional (exponentially decorrelating) and infinite-dimensional (power-law) behavioral universality classes. Together, these contributions provide conceptual grounding and a practical toolkit for quantifying the complexity of behavior before asking how it is mechanistically generated.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight as defined. It proposes and formalises an algorithm (the information-theoretic dimensionality framework) but provides no neural data of any kind — no recordings, imaging, lesion studies, or pharmacology. The paper is purely theoretical and phenomenological, explicitly arguing that mechanistic neural data should be sought *after* such behavioral characterization is in hand.

### Limitations & open questions

- The MaxEnt/Gaussian approximation is exact only for linear dynamical systems; its accuracy for nonlinear or non-Gaussian behavioral processes is not fully characterized.
- Practical estimation of the kernel eigenvalue spectrum with finite data requires careful handling of statistical significance via random matrix theory, which introduces its own assumptions and challenges.
- Whether real animal behaviors genuinely exhibit power-law temporal correlations (and thus infinite dimensionality) remains an open empirical question beyond the Drosophila example.
- The proposed connection between behavioral scaling laws and machine learning scaling laws (e.g., language model neural scaling laws) is explicitly speculative and undeveloped.
- The framework addresses scalar behavioral time series; extension to high-dimensional, multi-joint, or multi-animal behavioral data is not discussed.
- The relationship between behavioral dimensionality d* and the dimensionality of neural population activity (which has its own extensive literature) is noted but not formally bridged.

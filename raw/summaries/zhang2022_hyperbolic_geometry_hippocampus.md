### One-line summary

Neurons in rat dorsal CA1 hippocampus represent space according to a 3D hyperbolic geometry whose radius expands proportionally to the logarithm of exploration time, matching the theoretically optimal representation for the number of CA1 neurons.

---

### Background & motivation

Place cells in the CA1 region of the hippocampus encode spatial position, but the geometry of that representation at the population level was unknown — it was generally assumed to be approximately Euclidean. Hierarchical organisation in networks, including neural networks, can confer advantages for efficient signal routing and maximally informative representations, and such hierarchical organisation implies a latent hyperbolic geometry. The paper asks whether CA1 population activity is organised according to hyperbolic rather than Euclidean geometry, and whether any such organisation is static or dynamically shaped by experience.

---

### Methods

- **Subjects and recording**: Dorsal CA1 putative pyramidal cells from rats (n = 3 animals for novel 48-m linear track, plus publicly available Buzsaki lab datasets for 2.5-m linear tracks and 1.8 × 1.8-m square boxes). Recordings used tetrodes (64-channel system) or shank probes (32–64 channels).
- **Topological analysis (clique topology / Betti curves)**: Pairwise spike-train correlations (cross-correlograms, τmax = 1 s) between simultaneously recorded neurons were computed. Clique topology (CliqueTop MATLAB package) was applied to these correlation matrices to extract Betti curves — counts of 1D, 2D, and 3D cycles across a range of edge-density thresholds. Experimental Betti curves were compared against curves generated by sampling 300 replicate point sets from candidate geometries (Euclidean of various dimensions; hyperbolic of various dimensions and radii). Goodness-of-fit assessed by integrated Betti values and L1 distances.
- **Place field analysis**: Rate maps (1-cm bins for linear track, 2 × 2-cm bins for box) smoothed with Gaussian kernels. Place field sizes were fit with exponential distributions (MLE) to estimate the curvature/radius of the hyperbolic geometry for short time windows where the topological method is unreliable.
- **Temporal dynamics**: Hyperbolic radius was tracked across sessions of increasing exploration time (minutes to days) and across 1-m segments of the novel linear track (seconds). Running speed and entropy of track geometry were used as covariates.
- **Computational modelling**: Fisher information was computed analytically for model neuron populations with Gaussian place fields and Poisson variability, comparing exponential, uniform, and log-normal distributions of place field sizes. Optimal hyperbolic radius as a function of network size was extrapolated to the full CA1 neuron count.
- **Bayesian spatial decoder**: Maximum likelihood decoder applied to square box data to quantify how decoding accuracy (exponent c of error-vs-spikes curve) relates to hyperbolic radius over time.

---

### Key findings

- CA1 population activity is topologically consistent with a 3D hyperbolic geometry, not Euclidean geometry, in both linear track (three animals; radius range 13–15.5) and square box environments (multiple sessions; two-way ANOVA: F = 7,924.61, P < 10⁻⁸; 3D best fit vs. all other dimensions, P < 10⁻⁸).
- The radius of the hyperbolic representation grows proportionally to the logarithm of exploration time (r = 0.85, P = 3 × 10⁻⁶ for square box across days), consistent with the information-theoretic prediction that maximal learnable information scales as log(T).
- On shorter timescales (seconds), the hyperbolic radius on novel track segments increased with temporal familiarity (r = 0.50, P = 0.0003); place field size correlated positively with first-pass speed (r = 0.35, P = 9 × 10⁻²¹), meaning faster (less familiar) traversal produced larger, less resolved place fields.
- The exponent of the Bayesian decoding accuracy curve (c) increased significantly over exploration time (r = 0.86, P = 10⁻⁶) and correlated with hyperbolic radius (r = 0.70, P = 0.0007), demonstrating that larger hyperbolic radius supports better spatial localisation.
- Fisher information analysis showed that exponentially distributed place field sizes (as expected under hyperbolic geometry) provide more positional information than uniform or log-normal distributions (P < 0.001 at all network sizes), and there is an optimal hyperbolic radius for each network size.
- Extrapolating the optimal-radius curve to the full CA1 neuron count (320,000–490,000 active pyramidal cells, 30–40% active per environment) closely matched experimentally observed radii, indicating the observed geometry is near the information-theoretic optimum.
- Control analyses confirmed that shuffled spike trains do not produce Betti curves consistent with hyperbolic geometry, and that neither average running speed, active exploration time, nor time outside the environment explains the radius expansion.

### Computational framework

**Hyperbolic geometry / information theory.**

The paper formalises CA1 spatial representation as a set of points embedded in 3D hyperbolic space. Key variables:
- **Hyperbolic radius R**: the size (extent) of the representation, related to its curvature ζ by R = ζ⁻¹ Rmax.
- **Place field size distribution**: predicted to be exponential with decay constant ζ (the curvature); this follows from the construction of a hierarchical tree where neurons with larger place fields occupy higher levels.
- **Betti curves** (topological invariants): computed from pairwise neural correlations; used as geometry fingerprints invariant to monotonic transformations of the correlation values.
- **Information acquisition**: maximal information receivable from a novel environment scales as log(T/t₀), which predicts R ~ log(T) — the same logarithmic expansion observed empirically.
- **Fisher information**: used to quantify the accuracy with which position can be decoded; provides the normative criterion for optimal R given network size N (optimal R ~ log(N)).

Core assumption: neural pairwise correlations reflect place field overlap, and the topological structure of the correlation matrix encodes the latent geometry of the representation.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The introduction frames CA1 as the essential substrate for spatial representation (O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978; Scoville & Milner, 1957), with place cells firing at specific spatial locations (place fields). The prevailing working model at the time of this paper was that CA1 neurons form a distributed code for position with place fields varying in size across the environment but with no strong framework specifying the global geometric organisation of the population code. It was generally intuited — and implicitly assumed — that spatial representation is approximately linear or Euclidean, consistent with everyday perception of distance. The paper directly challenges this default assumption by asking whether a non-Euclidean (hyperbolic) organisation better accounts for the population-level structure of place cell activity. Prior work on other systems (head direction cells, grid cells) had identified specific manifold structures using persistent homology, but CA1 spatial coding had not been subjected to this geometric analysis.

---

### What this paper contributes

The paper establishes that CA1 spatial representations are not organised according to Euclidean geometry but instead conform to 3D hyperbolic geometry across multiple animals, environments, and recording paradigms. This is a qualitative shift in the prevailing model: the representational geometry of the hippocampal spatial map is negatively curved, not flat.

Beyond the geometric characterisation, the paper shows that this geometry is not static but expands dynamically with exploration, and that the rate of expansion matches a principled information-theoretic prediction (R ~ log(T)). This connects the representational geometry directly to the statistics of information acquisition from novel environments.

The paper further demonstrates that the observed hyperbolic radius is close to the theoretically optimal value given the number of CA1 neurons, suggesting that the geometry is not incidental but reflects an efficient coding principle. Before this paper, the field could note that place fields reorganise with experience; this paper specifies the coordinated, network-level geometric form that reorganisation takes and provides a functional account of why that form is optimal.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Dorsal CA1 hippocampus** — primary region of analysis; proposed locus of hyperbolic spatial representation. Place cells here exhibit exponentially distributed field sizes and pairwise correlations consistent with 3D hyperbolic geometry.
- **Hippocampus / entorhinal cortex (broader system)** — the paper notes its results are complementary to known dorsal-ventral gradients in place field size across the hippocampus and to grid cell representations in entorhinal cortex, but these broader structures are not directly analysed.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper meets both criteria: it presents a formalised algorithm (hyperbolic geometry with logarithmically expanding radius, implemented via addition of small place fields over time), and it provides neural recordings that specifically support this algorithm over the alternative (Euclidean geometry).

**Computational level**: The brain solves the problem of efficient spatial localisation given a finite number of neurons. Hyperbolic geometry maximises Fisher information per neuron compared to Euclidean (uniform or log-normal field size) organisations, and the optimal representation size scales logarithmically with neuron count — providing a normative account of why hyperbolic organisation is adaptive.

**Algorithmic level**: The representation is organised as an approximate hierarchical tree where neurons with large place fields occupy higher levels and neurons with smaller, more precise place fields occupy lower levels. Pairwise correlations among neurons reflect the degree of place field overlap, and this correlation structure carries a 3D hyperbolic signature. The radius of the geometry encodes the current depth of the hierarchy and grows as R ~ log(T), tracking the information acquired from the environment. A simple mechanism suffices: a new small place field appears whenever the animal spends a minimum time t₀ in a region; since small fields correspond to the periphery of the hyperbolic geometry, their exponential accumulation drives logarithmic growth of R.

**Implementational level**: The paper does not identify specific cell types, connectivity motifs, or neuromodulatory mechanisms that implement the hyperbolic geometry. The recording data confirm the predicted geometry and dynamics in dorsal CA1 pyramidal cells (putative active pyramidal cells, firing rate 0.1–7 Hz), but the biological substrates of the hierarchical organisation — e.g. synaptic plasticity rules (BTSP is cited but not tested), inhibitory interneuron contributions, or recurrent circuitry — are not directly investigated. This level remains open.

### Limitations & open questions

- The study analyses dorsal CA1 in rats; it is unclear whether the same hyperbolic geometry characterises other hippocampal subfields (CA3, dentate gyrus), ventral hippocampus, or other species including humans.
- The precise biological mechanism by which the hierarchical/hyperbolic organisation is instantiated (which plasticity rules, which cell types, what role interneurons play) is not addressed.
- The topological method requires a sufficient number of simultaneously recorded neurons (>~30–50); this limits the analysis to particular recording configurations and may not generalise to all experimental preparations.
- The paper focuses on place-cell-based spatial coding; whether hyperbolic geometry characterises hippocampal representations of non-spatial or abstract relational information (as suggested by refs. in the discussion) is untested.
- The relationship between hyperbolic geometry in CA1 and the known dorsal-ventral gradient in place field scale, as well as the grid cell scale hierarchy in entorhinal cortex, is noted but not formally reconciled.
- The prediction that the order of spike receipt from different neurons should matter (non-commutativity of hyperbolic addition) is flagged as an important direction for future work but not tested.
- Analyses rely on re-analysed publicly available datasets for most conditions; the novel 48-m track recordings involved only three animals (two excluded from topological analysis due to low neuron count).

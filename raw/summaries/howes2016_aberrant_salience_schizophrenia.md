### One-line summary

This editorial reviews how elevated mesostriatal dopamine synthesis and release capacity may cause psychosis through aberrant attribution of salience to irrelevant stimuli, and outlines outstanding empirical and methodological challenges.

---

### Background & motivation

Presynaptic mesostriatal dopamine dysfunction — specifically elevated dopamine synthesis and release capacity — is robustly implicated in the pathoaetiology of psychosis, yet the mechanistic link between this neurochemical abnormality and clinical symptoms remains poorly understood. The aberrant salience hypothesis proposes that dysregulated dopamine signalling causes abnormal motivational significance to be assigned to otherwise irrelevant stimuli, precipitating delusional and hallucinatory experiences. This editorial synthesises evidence bearing on that hypothesis and identifies gaps between animal electrophysiology, human neuroimaging, and clinical phenomenology that must be closed to rigorously test it.

---

### Methods

This is a brief narrative review (editorial format) drawing on selected empirical and theoretical literature. No systematic inclusion criteria or meta-analytic methods are described.

- Scope: PET studies of dopamine synthesis/release capacity in schizophrenia and high-risk populations; animal electrophysiology of midbrain dopamine neurons; fMRI reward-anticipation studies; behavioural salience attribution paradigms; cognitive theories of psychosis.
- Synthesis method: narrative, integrating neurobiological, computational, and cognitive accounts.

---

### Key findings

- PET studies show robustly elevated striatal dopamine synthesis and release capacity in patients with schizophrenia and in at-risk individuals who later convert to psychosis.
- Animal work demonstrates dopamine neuron heterogeneity: a subset encodes incentive salience for rewards, while others respond to salient non-rewarding (including aversive) stimuli, suggesting dopaminergic dysfunction could produce multifaceted misattribution of salience beyond simple reward over-weighting.
- fMRI studies in schizophrenia show reduced mesolimbic activation to reward-predicting stimuli and exaggerated responses to neutral stimuli; these changes are present in unmedicated and first-episode patients and correlate with positive and negative symptoms.
- Behavioural salience attribution tasks show impaired adaptive salience attribution in medicated patients; aberrant salience is higher in ultra-high-risk individuals and correlates with delusional symptom severity and ventral striatal responses to irrelevant stimuli.
- Aberrant salience attribution may not fully account for thought alienation and hallucinations; reduced ventral striatal reward responses are not specific to schizophrenia (also observed in alcohol dependence and major depression).

---

### Computational framework

The paper invokes a predictive coding / Bayesian brain framework to contextualise the aberrant salience hypothesis. In this account, incoming sensory information is salient when it violates the brain's internally maintained predictive model of the world (represented in cortical regions). Persistent prediction errors drive adaptive updating of the world-model. Subcortical dopamine transmission is proposed to modulate the precision (gain) of prediction errors, so that even subtle dopaminergic abnormalities can cause maladaptive, radical revisions of the world-model — manifesting clinically as delusions and hallucinations. Key variables are: the cortical prior (internal model), the sensory prediction error, and dopaminergic gain on that error signal. The framework assumes that psychotic symptoms arise from aberrant precision-weighting of prediction errors rather than from categorical failures of perception or reasoning.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

Prior to the aberrant salience hypothesis, dopamine's role in psychosis was understood primarily in pharmacological terms: antipsychotic potency scales with D2/3 receptor antagonism, and drugs that elevate striatal dopamine release can precipitate psychosis. The dominant mechanistic account was that elevated striatal dopamine release directly produces psychotic symptoms, but without a satisfying psychological bridge. Earlier formulations of the salience hypothesis (Kapur 2003) focussed narrowly on incentive salience — over-attribution of motivational significance (reward-like) to irrelevant stimuli. The paper's introduction also signals an emerging computational refinement: the Fletcher & Frith (2009) predictive coding account, which situates dopamine as a modulator of cortical-subcortical prediction error signalling rather than a simple reward signal.

---

### What this paper contributes

This editorial updates and extends the aberrant salience hypothesis in three ways. First, it incorporates evidence for dopamine neuron heterogeneity to argue that misattribution of salience is multifaceted — involving both rewarding and aversive dimensions — rather than purely incentive-based. Second, it integrates the predictive coding computational framework to provide a mechanistic account of how subcortical dopamine dysfunction translates into maladaptive belief updating. Third, it surveys the human fMRI and behavioural evidence that directly operationalises aberrant salience, establishing that these abnormalities are present in unmedicated and at-risk individuals and correlate with symptom severity. The paper also clearly delineates remaining gaps: no direct demonstration of aberrant phasic dopamine in patients, methodological incommensurability between animal and human measurement tools, and uncertainty about whether aberrant salience is sufficient for the full symptom spectrum or specific to schizophrenia.

### Brain regions & systems

- Striatum (ventral and dorsal) — primary locus of elevated dopamine synthesis/release capacity; site of aberrant salience attribution signals measured by PET and fMRI.
- Ventral tegmental area (VTA) — origin of mesolimbic dopamine projection; shows reduced activation to reward-predicting stimuli in fMRI studies.
- Ventral striatum / nucleus accumbens — key node of the mesolimbic pathway; exaggerated responses to neutral stimuli and reduced responses to reward cues in schizophrenia.
- Midbrain dopamine neurons (broadly) — site of heterogeneous reward vs. salience encoding in animal electrophysiology studies.
- Cortex (broadly) — proposed site of predictive world-model in the computational framing; specific cortical regions are not individually delineated in this editorial.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not present primary neural data. It synthesises existing fMRI and PET findings and animal electrophysiology, but does not directly pit one algorithm against another with neural recordings. The bar for this section is therefore not met.

The paper proposes a computational account (precision-weighted prediction errors modulated by dopamine) and cites fMRI correlates of aberrant salience, but these imaging measures do not directly validate the specific algorithmic claims (e.g., that dopamine encodes prediction error precision rather than reward value per se). The mechanistic account remains a theoretical framework supported by converging indirect evidence rather than by experiments designed to distinguish between specific algorithmic hypotheses.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- No direct demonstration of aberrant phasic dopamine firing in human patients with schizophrenia exists; methodological gaps between animal electrophysiology and human PET/fMRI are substantial.
- fMRI measures blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) signal, not neuronal activity or dopamine release directly; PET has temporal resolution orders of magnitude coarser than single-unit recordings.
- The relationship between electrophysiological activity, transmitter release (voltammetry, microdialysis, PET), and fMRI BOLD is incompletely understood and confounded by modulatory neurotransmitters and autoreceptor feedback.
- It is unclear whether aberrant salience attribution is sufficient to explain hallucinations and thought alienation, or primarily accounts for delusional atmosphere and delusion formation.
- Reduced ventral striatal reward responses are not specific to schizophrenia (also seen in alcohol dependence and major depression); further comparative work is needed.
- Causal direction remains unproven: longitudinal studies from prodrome through established psychosis are needed to establish whether aberrant salience attribution is causally implicated rather than epiphenomenal.
- The therapeutic implications (presynaptic dopamine-targeting medication plus psychotherapy) are proposed but untested in the framework described.

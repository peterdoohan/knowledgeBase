### One-line summary

This narrative review synthesises clinical, neuropsychological, genetic, and environmental evidence for overlap between ADHD and schizophrenia, arguing that attentional dysfunction may reflect partially shared but distinct neural mechanisms and that ADHD should be screened for in neuroleptic-refractory psychosis patients.

---

### Background & motivation

Although ADHD and schizophrenia are classified as separate disorders, attentional dysfunction is a core feature of both. A growing body of longitudinal and epidemiological evidence indicates that childhood ADHD constitutes a risk factor for later schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, yet the neurobiological relationship between the two conditions has been poorly characterised. The Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework, which organises psychopathology around dysregulated neural circuits rather than categorical diagnosis, provides motivation for examining common and divergent mechanisms. The clinical importance of this question is heightened by uncertainty about whether psychostimulants — first-line treatment for ADHD — are safe or harmful in individuals with comorbid psychosis.

---

### Methods

This is a narrative review. Scope and synthesis approach:

- Literature covered spans clinical epidemiology, neuropsychology (continuous performance tests, event-related potentials, eye movements, affect recognition), genetics, epigenetics, environmental risk factors, and pharmacology (stimulants and psychosis).
- No formal inclusion/exclusion criteria or systematic search strategy are described; the paper synthesises selectively from the published literature.
- The RDoC framework is used as an organising conceptual lens throughout.
- Special attention is given to the stimulants-and-psychosis question, including a review of Curran et al.'s 2004 systematic review of 32 experimental studies on stimulant administration in schizophrenia patients.

---

### Key findings

- Children and adolescents with ADHD are approximately 4.3 times more likely to develop schizophrenia in adulthood than controls; risk is higher in females (RR = 20.1) than males (RR = 2.9).
- More than 50% of adults with schizophrenia met criteria for another psychiatric disorder (most commonly ADHD, conduct disorder, or oppositional defiant disorder) in early adolescence (Kim-Cohen et al.).
- Neuropsychological profiles differ: schizophrenia is characterised by reduced sensitivity on CPTs and inability to suppress P50, while ADHD shows more omission errors, slower reaction times, and (largely) intact P50 suppression — though one recent study found abnormal P50 in ADHD as well.
- Smooth pursuit eye movement deficits and increased anticipatory saccades are present in both conditions, but schizophrenia patients show additional impairment in target selection.
- Emotion perception impairments in the two disorders appear to involve different circuits: schizophrenia is associated with decreased amygdala and medial PFC responses to negative emotions, while ADHD shows amygdala activation during negative emotion processing.
- Shared genetic susceptibility is supported by polygenic overlap (Hamshere et al.) and overlap in rare copy number variants; SNPs associated with dopaminergic drug response are enriched for variants linked to both disorders (Hart et al.).
- Duration of stimulant treatment was not significantly associated with risk of developing schizophrenia.
- Large-dose stimulant administration can precipitate brief psychosis in schizophrenia patients; evidence for a sensitisation mechanism is limited (found in only 2 of 32 reviewed studies); case series show stimulants can be tolerated and may improve cognition when combined with antipsychotics.
- Common environmental risk factors for both disorders include prenatal infections, maternal smoking, low birth weight, nutritional deficiencies (iron, folate, zinc, polyunsaturated fatty acids), and prenatal maternal stress.

### Computational framework

Not applicable. The paper does not employ a computational model or formal mathematical framework. It is a clinical and biological narrative review. The results are framed around circuit-level descriptions of dopaminergic dysfunction (particularly D1 vs. D2 receptor pathways in frontal vs. mesolimbic regions) and the RDoC approach of mapping psychopathology onto underlying neural systems. These findings could constrain or motivate predictive coding or reinforcement learning models of attentional dysfunction that need to account for divergent profiles across disorders sharing dopaminergic involvement.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's introduction signals that the field has historically treated ADHD and schizophrenia as categorically distinct disorders with separate aetiologies. Attention dysfunction in schizophrenia was understood from Kraepelin and Bleuler onwards as a secondary consequence of the core disorder, rather than a defining feature comparable to ADHD. The prevailing view at the time of publication was that while childhood neurodevelopmental difficulties precede schizophrenia, the attentional dysfunction in the two conditions reflects different neurobiological substrates. There was also widespread clinical reluctance to administer psychostimulants to patients with psychosis due to concerns that dopamine agonism would worsen positive symptoms. The introduction is moderately rich in historical framing but less detailed about specific circuit-level models assumed before this review.

---

### What this paper contributes

The review consolidates evidence that the boundary between ADHD and schizophrenia is more porous than categorical classification implies. Key contributions:

- It establishes that childhood ADHD is a meaningful epidemiological risk factor for adult schizophrenia, with quantified risk ratios.
- It documents both convergences (shared genetic susceptibility, overlapping environmental risk factors, some shared ERP abnormalities) and divergences (distinct CPT profiles, P50 suppression differences, divergent emotion-circuit responses) in the neuropsychology of the two disorders.
- It synthesises the stimulants-and-psychosis literature, providing a more nuanced position than either "stimulants cause psychosis" or "stimulants are always safe" — large doses risk brief psychosis in schizophrenia patients, but therapeutic doses can be tolerated and may be beneficial in comorbid cases.
- It raises the clinically actionable recommendation to screen for ADHD in neuroleptic-refractory psychosis patients, and to consider psychostimulant augmentation in stabilised patients with comorbid ADHD symptoms.
- Key unresolved questions identified: whether ADHD comorbid with psychosis represents a more severe subtype or merely an index of psychosis severity; the mechanistic basis of the female-specific elevated risk; differential diagnosis between ADHD, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia and their relationship to stimulant-induced psychosis.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Prefrontal cortex (PFC)** — proposed site of hypodopaminergic dysfunction in both disorders; D1 receptor signalling here is thought to mediate the cognitive benefits of methylphenidate; decreased medial PFC response to negative emotions found in schizophrenia.
- **Amygdala** — decreased activation in response to negative emotions in schizophrenia (contrasting with increased activation in ADHD during negative emotion evaluation); discussed as part of divergent emotional processing circuits.
- **Mesolimbic dopamine system** — implicated in positive psychotic symptoms; D2 receptor blockade by antipsychotics targets this system without affecting PFC D1 signalling.
- **Sensory gating circuits (hippocampus, α7 nicotinic receptor networks)** — P50 suppression deficits in schizophrenia linked to chromosome 15q14 locus encoding α7 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors; perinatal choline supplementation proposed to activate fetal α7 receptors and potentially protect against later psychiatric disorder.
- **Frontal-subcortical circuits** — broadly invoked as mediators of executive function, inhibitory control, and motivational behaviour; proposed locus of differential impairment across disorders.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight. It synthesises observational epidemiological data, neuropsychological profiles, and genetic association findings, but does not formalise an algorithm that could explain the attentional phenomena, and does not present neural recordings, pharmacological manipulations, or imaging data that specifically adjudicate between candidate mechanisms. The paper proposes that D1 vs. D2 dopaminergic dissociations between frontal and mesolimbic circuits partially explain why methylphenidate improves frontal cognition in schizophrenia without worsening mesolimbic-mediated psychosis, but this is a qualitative clinical inference rather than a tested mechanistic model.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- The review is narrative rather than systematic; no formal search strategy or inclusion criteria are reported, raising concerns about selective citation.
- Most reviewed studies used small, heterogeneous samples with variable diagnostic criteria (DSM and ICD versions differ across decades of cited literature).
- The stimulant-psychosis literature is dominated by acute high-dose experimental paradigms that may not generalise to therapeutic dosing in clinical ADHD.
- The mechanisms underlying the markedly higher schizophrenia risk in females with ADHD (RR = 20.1 vs. 2.9 in males) are not explained.
- Causal direction between ADHD and schizophrenia risk cannot be established from the reviewed observational data; confounding by shared genetic liability is not ruled out.
- The paper does not address neuroimaging structural or functional data in detail beyond brief references to amygdala and PFC findings.
- Whether stimulant augmentation in comorbid ADHD+psychosis is safe and beneficial in the longer term remains unresolved; no controlled trials are available.

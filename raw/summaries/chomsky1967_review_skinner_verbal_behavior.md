### One-line summary

Chomsky demonstrates that Skinner's attempt to give a behaviorist functional analysis of verbal behavior fails because its core concepts — stimulus, response, reinforcement — lose all objective meaning when extended from the laboratory to natural language, and proposes instead that linguistic competence requires positing innate, abstract grammatical knowledge in the organism.

---

### Background & motivation

In the mid-twentieth century, many linguists, psychologists, and philosophers hoped that behaviorist psychology could provide a scientific foundation for the study of language and meaning. Skinner's *Verbal Behavior* (1957) was the most ambitious and systematic attempt to fulfil this programme, applying the framework of operant conditioning — developed in controlled animal experiments — to the full range of human linguistic behavior. Chomsky's review aims to evaluate this attempt in detail, using it as a test case for empiricist/behaviorist assumptions about higher mental processes more broadly. The review argues that the failure of Skinner's programme reveals the indispensability of factors internal to the organism — including potentially innate structure — that behaviorism by design excludes.

---

### Methods

This is a theoretical critique and review. The approach is:

- Close conceptual analysis of Skinner's technical vocabulary (stimulus, response, reinforcement, operant strength, mand, tact, echoic operant, intraverbal operant, autoclitic) as defined in controlled laboratory settings.
- Systematic examination of whether these concepts retain their literal meaning when extended to natural language, using counterexamples drawn from ordinary linguistic behavior.
- Survey of the experimental literature on latent learning, imprinting, intrinsic motivation, and sensory-sensory conditioning to assess whether reinforcement is a necessary condition for learning.
- Sketch of an alternative programme: formal grammar as an explicit, generative characterisation of linguistic competence, drawing on Lashley's observations about serial order in behavior.

---

### Key findings

- **Stimulus** loses objectivity: Skinner identifies the controlling stimulus only by observing the response, making the notion circular and unfalsifiable. The claim that verbal responses are "under stimulus control" is a paraphrase for denotation/connotation, adding no new clarity.
- **Response strength** is similarly vacuous: the proposed measures (pitch, speed, loudness, frequency) do not co-vary and cannot be operationalised for language; the term functions as a cover for mentalistic notions like interest, intention, and belief.
- **Reinforcement** is emptied of content: Skinner's examples include self-reinforcement, imagined reinforcement, reinforcement by silence, reinforcement by a listener's future behavior, and reinforcement that never reaches the speaker. The term becomes equivalent to "anything that motivates," making the law of conditioning a tautology.
- **Mand** (command/request) cannot be identified independently of the response it is supposed to explain; the classification of speech acts by listener disposition is demonstrably wrong.
- **Tact** (naming/reference) reduces to a vague paraphrase of reference and meaning, obscuring the traditional denotation/connotation distinction rather than improving on it.
- **Autoclitic** processes (syntax, negation, quantification) are handled by extending the same vacuous framework to grammatical phenomena, adding no explanatory content.
- The empirical claim that reinforcement is a necessary condition for language learning is unsupported: latent learning, imprinting, curiosity-driven exploration in rats and monkeys, and children's rapid acquisition of phonology without explicit instruction all contradict it.
- Children's ability to produce and understand unbounded numbers of novel, grammatically correct sentences cannot be explained by stimulus generalisation or intraverbal chaining — it requires positing an internalized generative grammar of considerable abstract complexity.
- The child's rapid, uniform, intelligence-independent acquisition of language across communities suggests an innate, species-specific language faculty with domain-specific hypothesis-forming capacities.

---

### Computational framework

Not applicable in the strict sense. The review does not itself deploy a formal computational model. However, it sketches the outlines of a framework that would replace Skinner's:

- **Generative grammar** as a formal device (akin to a deductive theory) that enumerates the sentences of a language and assigns each a structural description. This grammar is treated as an internal component of the speaker/listener inferred from behavior, not directly observable.
- The speaker's task is framed as selecting among optional rules within the grammar; the listener's task as recovering which optional rules were applied. This anticipates the competence/performance distinction fully elaborated in Chomsky's subsequent work.
- Lashley's "serial order" problem is invoked to motivate the need for hierarchical, abstract syntactic structure that is not represented directly in the physical sequence of responses.
- The results suggest constraints on what kind of **information-processing system** (hypothesis-forming mechanism) the child's brain must be, given the data available and the speed of acquisition — an early articulation of the learnability/poverty-of-the-stimulus argument.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The prevailing model attacked in this paper is Skinnerian operant behaviorism applied to language: verbal behavior is acquired and maintained entirely through contingencies of reinforcement provided by the verbal community; the speaker's contribution is trivial and elementary; prediction and control of verbal behavior requires only specification of external variables (stimuli, reinforcement history, deprivation states). This model was part of a broader empiricist tradition that treated the mind as a general-purpose associative learning device and denied the need to posit internal structure beyond what could be inferred directly from input-output relationships. Chomsky's introduction makes clear that this view was widely shared by linguists and philosophers who hoped behaviorism would solve the problem of meaning.

---

### What this paper contributes

Chomsky establishes — by working through every major concept of Skinner's system — that the behaviorist framework, in its most careful and explicit form, fails to account for even elementary facts about language. Taken literally, the technical vocabulary covers almost no natural linguistic behavior; taken metaphorically, it is no more scientific than the traditional mentalistic descriptions it was meant to replace. This constitutes a reductio ad absurdum of the entire empiricist programme.

Positively, the review establishes that:
1. Any adequate account of language must acknowledge a substantial, structured contribution from the organism — not merely a history of reinforcement.
2. Linguistic competence is best characterised formally as an internalized generative grammar, which provides a meaningful explanandum for psychological and linguistic research.
3. The speed, uniformity, and intelligence-independence of language acquisition strongly imply innate, species-specific mechanisms for grammar induction — a "language acquisition device" with domain-specific structure.
4. The most tractable scientific programme begins not with the causation of behavior, but with the formal characterisation of what is acquired: the structure of grammars and their properties.

The key unresolved questions identified: What exactly are the built-in structures that permit grammar induction from limited data? How do syntactic and semantic generalisation interact? What is the relationship between formal grammar and the actual processing mechanisms of the speaker and listener?

### Brain regions & systems

Not applicable. The paper operates at the computational and algorithmic levels of analysis, with no anatomical focus. The level of analysis is the cognitive/competence level: what knowledge the language user must possess, and what learning mechanisms could account for its acquisition. Lashley's neuroscientific observations about serial order are cited to motivate the need for hierarchical planning, but no specific brain regions are implicated.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the bar. Chomsky proposes a computational-level characterisation (the grammar as a generative device; the learner as a hypothesis-forming system) and provides behavioural arguments (novelty of sentences, rapid acquisition, absence of adequate reinforcement) that rule out a specific class of alternative algorithms (stimulus-response chaining, intraverbal association). However, no neural data are presented that link the proposed mechanisms to measured brain activity, and the algorithmic and implementational levels remain entirely unspecified. The paper is a programmatic argument for the necessity of an innate grammatical faculty, not an empirical demonstration of its neural basis.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- The review is primarily destructive: it establishes that Skinner's framework fails, but the positive alternative (generative grammar, innate language faculty) is only sketched, not formalised or empirically tested in this paper.
- The learnability argument (poverty of the stimulus) is intuitive rather than formally demonstrated; the precise characterisation of what the child must know innately vs. what can be learned from input is left open.
- Chomsky acknowledges that the factors responsible for the strength and maintenance of verbal behavior "are almost a total mystery," and that the interaction of reinforcement, imitation, inquisitiveness, and innate capacity in acquisition is unknown.
- The review largely sets aside questions of language use, pragmatics, and meaning — the focus is on syntactic competence.
- The alternative rationalist/nativist tradition is invoked but not systematically developed in this paper (Chomsky points to his 1962, 1964, 1966 publications for further elaboration).
- Whether the behaviourist framework could be salvaged with more careful extensions (e.g., via modern computational learning theory) is not considered.

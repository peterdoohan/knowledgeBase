### One-line summary

In healthy young adults performing a virtual navigation task, spatial strategies engage the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (including orbitofrontal cortex), while response/habit strategies engage the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex, revealing a double dissociation that extends known hippocampus–caudate nucleus competition to include distinct prefrontal subregions.

---

### Background & motivation

Navigation can be achieved through two fundamentally different strategies: a spatial strategy that builds a cognitive map via stimulus–stimulus associations (hippocampus-dependent) and a stimulus–response strategy that learns fixed motor sequences from landmarks (caudate nucleus-dependent). While the hippocampus–caudate axis is well established, the prefrontal regions that differentially support these strategies in humans are unknown. Rodent lesion work had implicated ventromedial PFC in spatial learning and dorsomedial PFC in response learning, but no comparable human neuroimaging study had examined PFC contributions to a dual-solution navigation task.

---

### Methods

- **Task**: Concurrent Spatial Discrimination Learning Task (CSDLT) — a virtual 12-arm radial maze with landmarks. Participants learn object locations across six arm-pairs (learning stage), then are tested on recombined pairs (probe stage). High probe performance (≥7/8) indicates spatial strategy; low performance indicates response strategy.
- **Participants**: 23 healthy young adults (14F/9M, ages 18–35, mean 23.9 years), scanned at 1.5 T.
- **Strategy classification**: 6 spatial strategy users (probe ≥7/8), 17 response strategy users (probe <7/8).
- **fMRI**: BOLD contrast of experimental vs. visuo-motor control trials (control condition: identical maze without landmarks, random object placement, concurrent backwards counting task). Analyses focused on early learning (first 3 trials) and late learning (last 2 trials). Regions of interest: hippocampus, caudate nucleus, VMPFC (orbitofrontal cortex + ventral ACC), DMPFC (dorsal ACC + more dorsal regions). Threshold: uncorrected p < 0.001 within ROIs.
- **Voxel-based morphometry (VBM)**: Grey matter density regressed against probe performance and against peak hippocampal BOLD activity.
- **Connectivity analysis**: Peak hippocampal BOLD correlated with whole-brain BOLD activity.

---

### Key findings

- Spatial strategy users showed greater BOLD activity in the right hippocampus (t = 3.76, p < 0.001) and right orbitofrontal cortex (OFC; t = 3.83, p < 0.0005) than response strategy users in the first three learning trials.
- Probe scores positively correlated with right OFC BOLD activity in early learning (t = 3.68, p < 0.001), confirming OFC engagement scales with degree of spatial strategy use.
- Hippocampal peak BOLD activity correlated with right OFC activity (t = 3.76, p < 0.001) and left ventral ACC activity (t = 4.34, p < 0.0005) in the first trial, indicating functional coupling of OFC/vACC with hippocampus during early spatial learning.
- Response strategy users showed greater BOLD activity in the left DMPFC in early learning relative to control trials (t = 4.36, p < 0.0005).
- VBM: probe scores negatively correlated with grey matter density in left DMPFC (t = −4.07, p < 0.0005) and left dorsal ACC (t = −3.72, p < 0.001) — i.e. response learners had more grey matter in DMPFC/dACC.
- Hippocampal peak BOLD positively correlated with grey matter density in right OFC (t = 3.70, p < 0.001) — greater hippocampal engagement during navigation associated with more OFC grey matter.
- No significant BOLD differences were found in late learning (last 2 trials), consistent with prior work showing caudate nucleus activity requires extended overtraining.

### Computational framework

Not applicable — the paper does not adopt an explicit computational or mathematical framework. The results are interpreted descriptively through competing memory systems theory (hippocampal cognitive map vs. caudate stimulus–response habit learning) and reward-guided decision-making accounts of PFC function. The paper speculates that VMPFC encodes stimulus–reward associations (supporting spatial learning) while DMPFC encodes action–reward associations (supporting response learning), drawing on existing reinforcement-learning-adjacent theories of PFC function, but does not formalise this as a model.

The results could constrain models of memory system competition that incorporate a prefrontal arbitration or gating mechanism — for example, models in which a VMPFC–hippocampus circuit suppresses DMPFC–caudate activity early in learning, with the balance shifting as habit formation proceeds.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper builds on a well-established dual-systems framework: the hippocampus supports flexible, map-based spatial navigation via stimulus–stimulus associations, while the caudate nucleus supports rigid, habit-based navigation via stimulus–response associations. Competition between these systems determines which strategy a navigator employs at any given moment. Prior neuroimaging in humans (Iaria et al., 2003) had confirmed this hippocampus–caudate dissociation. Rodent lesion and inactivation studies had additionally implicated distinct medial PFC subregions — VMPFC (prelimbic/infralimbic/OFC) for spatial learning and DMPFC for response learning — but direct evidence in humans was absent. The prefrontal cortex was therefore understood to be important for navigation but its specific subregional contributions in humans remained uncharacterised.

---

### What this paper contributes

The study provides the first human neuroimaging evidence for a double dissociation within the medial PFC: VMPFC (specifically OFC) is functionally and structurally coupled with hippocampus-dependent spatial learning, while DMPFC is associated with caudate nucleus-dependent response learning. This extends the dual-systems model to include distinct prefrontal nodes in each network. The finding that structural grey matter differences mirror functional differences suggests that individual variation in navigation strategy may have a partly anatomical basis. The paper also proposes that DMPFC may support early response learning before the caudate nucleus takes over with extended practice — a temporal division of labour not previously articulated in the human navigation literature.

---

### Brain regions & systems

- **Right orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** — part of VMPFC; shows increased BOLD activity and grey matter density associated with spatial strategy use; functionally coupled with hippocampus during early learning; proposed to encode stimulus–reward associations supporting spatial learning.
- **Left ventral anterior cingulate cortex (vACC)** — part of VMPFC; functionally coupled with hippocampal activity in the first learning trial; implicated in spatial learning network.
- **Left dorsomedial prefrontal cortex (DMPFC)** — shows increased BOLD activity and grey matter density associated with response strategy use; proposed to encode action–reward associations supporting habit-based learning.
- **Left dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC)** — part of DMPFC; grey matter density negatively correlated with probe scores (more grey matter in response strategy users).
- **Right hippocampus** — greater BOLD activity in spatial strategy users in early learning; well-established locus of cognitive map formation and spatial navigation.
- **Caudate nucleus** — no significant BOLD differences detected (consistent with prior work showing caudate activity requires overtraining); established locus of stimulus–response habit learning.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not fully meet the two-part bar. It provides fMRI and VBM data linking VMPFC/DMPFC to navigation strategies, and the functional coupling data (hippocampal BOLD correlated with OFC BOLD and grey matter) goes beyond simple localisation. However, the study does not formalise or test a specific algorithm; it proposes that OFC encodes stimulus–reward associations and DMPFC encodes action–reward associations, but does not pit this account against alternative algorithms or design contrasts to isolate these computations. The study therefore supports a computational-level description (hippocampal system builds a cognitive map; caudate system builds S–R habits; each system has a distinct PFC partner) but does not provide algorithmic or implementational evidence. The speculation about temporal dynamics — VMPFC/hippocampus active early with possible suppression of DMPFC/caudate, the balance reversing with practice — remains untested.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Small spatial strategy group (n = 6 vs. n = 17 for response), limiting statistical power for that contrast.
- fMRI threshold was uncorrected for multiple comparisons (p < 0.001 within ROIs), raising concerns about Type I error.
- 1.5 T scanner limits spatial resolution, particularly for subcortical structures.
- Temporal dynamics between PFC subregions and hippocampus/caudate cannot be resolved with the block-design fMRI analysis used.
- The causal direction of the grey matter correlations is unknown: does navigation strategy use shape grey matter, or does grey matter predispose individuals to a given strategy?
- No caudate nucleus BOLD activity was observed, potentially due to lack of overtraining; this precludes a full within-study dissociation of the two networks.
- The exact computational contribution of VMPFC vs. hippocampus (and DMPFC vs. caudate) to each strategy remains to be determined.
- Whether OFC activity precedes or follows hippocampal activity (i.e. whether OFC initiates spatial learning) cannot be inferred from this design.

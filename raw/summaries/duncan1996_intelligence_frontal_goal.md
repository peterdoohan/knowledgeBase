### One-line summary

Goal neglect — the failure to act on a task requirement that has been understood and remembered — is strongly linked to Spearman's *g* in the normal population and is the predominant deficit following frontal lobe lesions, supporting the hypothesis that frontal action-control functions underlie general intelligence.

---

### Background & motivation

Spearman's *g* (general intelligence) and frontal lobe executive function have long been described in parallel terms — both implicated in planning, problem-solving, and the control of novel behaviour — yet neuropsychologists conventionally held that frontal lesions leave IQ largely intact. Duncan et al. argue this conventional view rests on inappropriate measurement: standard IQ tests (e.g., WAIS) rely heavily on crystallised knowledge, whereas tests of fluid intelligence (e.g., Cattell's Culture Fair) are far more sensitive to frontal damage. Their paper addresses the gap by introducing a direct behavioural assay of goal activation and examining whether its failure tracks both *g* and frontal pathology.

---

### Methods

- **Task design (letter monitoring)**: Subjects monitored a visual stream of letter/digit pairs on two sides of a screen. After 10 frames, a symbolic cue (+ or −, termed the "second side instruction" or SSI) instructed them to either stay with or switch the attended side for the remaining three frames. All subjects demonstrated correct recall of the SSI rule after practice.
- **Goal neglect operationalisation**: A "failed sub-block" was one in which the subject did not correctly use the SSI; complete neglect (failing all 3 sub-blocks) was the key dependent variable.
- **Prompt manipulations**: Across experiments, prompts varied from simply repeating rules, visual arrows co-presented with the SSI, trial-by-trial verbal feedback on errors, and questions asked after each trial.
- **Dual task manipulation (Experiment 3)**: A secondary dot-detection task was added; instruction order (dot instructions first vs. last) was manipulated to vary concurrent goal load.
- **Patient study (Experiment 4)**: 10 frontal patients (3 with focal frontal lesions, 7 with closed head injuries with major frontal component) and 8 posterior lesion patients, each matched to individual controls. Culture Fair IQ was also administered.
- **Intelligence measure**: Cattell's Culture Fair test Scale 2 Form A (established *g* correlation ≈ .81 in large-scale factor analyses) used throughout; Mill Hill vocabulary and Nelson-Denny reading comprehension included in Experiment 2.
- **Populations**: Experiment 1 — 90 normal adults (ages 29–57); Experiment 2 — 41 elderly adults (ages 60–70); Experiment 3 — 38 middle-aged adults (ages 39–49); Experiment 4 — patient and matched-control groups.

---

### Key findings

- **Goal neglect in normals**: 15/90 normal adults (Experiment 1) showed complete neglect of the SSI across all sub-blocks, despite correctly recalling the rule; a bimodal distribution of failure was observed.
- **Link to *g***: The correlation between goal neglect and Culture Fair IQ was r = .41 in Experiment 1, rising to r = .52 in Experiment 2 (elderly sample) and r = .55 in Experiment 3 (dual task); goal neglect as a binary item showed *higher* correlation with *g* than comparably distributed items from the Culture Fair test itself.
- **Fluid, not crystallised, intelligence**: In elderly subjects, fluid intelligence (Culture Fair) predicted neglect (r ≈ .52) whereas verbal knowledge (Mill Hill r = .02; Nelson-Denny r = .30) did not, dissociating current problem-solving ability from accumulated knowledge.
- **Concurrent requirements increase neglect**: Adding a secondary dot task (Experiment 3) increased SSI neglect and raised the *g* correlation; dot neglect was much greater (mean omissions 6.6 vs. 3.2) and more closely tied to *g* (r = .66 vs. .19) when dot instructions were given last, confirming that concurrent goal load is a key determinant.
- **Frontal patients**: 7/10 frontal patients showed complete SSI neglect vs. 1/10 matched controls; F(1,9) = 14.4, p < .005. Posterior lesion patients showed no deficit (F = 0). Frontal patients also showed a mean 9-point Culture Fair IQ deficit (F(1,9) = 14.8, p < .005).
- **Prompt sensitivity**: Verbal prompts (repeating rules, trial-by-trial feedback) eliminated neglect in both normal and frontal subjects; with sufficient prompting, all subjects were capable of correct performance.
- **Speed of resolution**: Once a subject first correctly used the SSI, performance was almost immediately and durably correct (only ~1–2% subsequent failures), suggesting an abrupt transition rather than gradual learning.
- **Awareness not sufficient**: Many subjects who neglected the SSI reported having seen the symbols; conversely, some who did not see them resolved neglect immediately once prompted — showing that stimulus awareness and goal activation are dissociable.

### Computational framework

The paper does not use a formal quantitative framework, but proposes a **hierarchical action-control / goal-competition** model. The core ideas are:

- Behaviour is structured by successive selection of *requirements or constraints* at multiple levels of abstraction (from verbal instructions down to motor commands), following Miller, Galanter & Pribram's (1960) TOTE hierarchy.
- Candidate goals arise both by working backward from active supergoals and by working forward from current environmental state; these candidates compete for control of behaviour.
- This competition is biased by: (a) familiarity / prior experience, (b) strength of environmental prompts, and (c) number of concurrently active requirements.
- A *goal activation* process — converting verbally understood requirements into active behavioural controllers — is the function that is proposed to be impaired in low-*g* individuals and frontal patients.
- The model aligns with Norman & Shallice's (1980) Supervisory Attentional System account of voluntary vs. automatic control; goal neglect occurs when the "contention scheduling" default wins because the supervisory activation process fails.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper pushes against two conventional assumptions in 1990s neuropsychology:

1. **Frontal functions are largely independent of *g***: Based primarily on observations that patients with dramatic frontal dysfunction can produce WAIS IQs in the superior range (e.g., Eslinger & Damasio's patient EVR, Shallice & Burgess's cases), the standard view was that frontal systems are relevant to "executive" functions (planning, problem-solving) but not to the psychometric construct of general intelligence.

2. **Modularity of frontal functions**: Distinct frontal subsystems subserve distinct functions (spatial vs. nonspatial working memory, inhibition, set-shifting, etc.), which sits in tension with the *g* construct's domain-generality.

The paper also engages with the classical distinction between controlled/voluntary and automatic/stimulus-driven processing as a framework for when frontal or *g*-related processes are engaged — a distinction commonly applied to both frontal deficits (Fuster, Luria, Norman & Shallice) and individual differences in *g* (Ackerman, Snow).

---

### What this paper contributes

- Establishes that the apparent independence of *g* and frontal function was partly a measurement artefact: WAIS-style tests are poor measures of *g* in clinical populations, whereas fluid intelligence tests (Culture Fair, Progressive Matrices) reveal substantial and specific frontal deficits.
- Introduces *goal neglect* as an operationalised, quantifiable behavioural phenomenon that is: (a) present in the normal population in proportion to *g*, (b) the predominant failure mode of frontal patients, and (c) sensitive to the same conditions (novelty, weak prompting, concurrent requirements) that hallmark active/controlled processing.
- Shows that the cognitive function at stake is *goal activation* — translating a verbally understood requirement into an active behavioural controller — not problem-solving, working memory capacity per se, or verbal ability.
- Provides an account of why frontal patients can appear unimpaired in standard laboratory tests (structured, prompted, repetitive environments act as surrogate frontal lobes) while being severely disabled in daily life.

### Brain regions & systems

- **Frontal lobes (bilateral, including medial and inferior aspects)** — proposed locus of goal activation; lesions here produce pervasive goal neglect and fluid intelligence deficits regardless of whether damage arises from focal meningioma removal, tumour excision, or closed head injury with major frontal involvement.
- **Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)** — cited via PET literature as activated by a wide range of cognitively demanding tasks; proposed as a candidate region for the domain-general goal-control function underlying *g*.
- **Anterior cingulate** — cited as co-activated with DLPFC in PET studies of demanding tasks; part of the proposed cooperative frontal network.
- **Posterior cortical regions (temporal, parietal, occipital)** — shown to be *not* responsible for goal neglect or fluid intelligence deficits (Experiment 4 posterior patient group), establishing frontal specificity.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper partially meets the bar. It formalises an **algorithm** (goal activation as competition among candidate requirements, biased by novelty, prompts, and concurrent load) and provides **lesion and behavioural data** supporting this over alternatives. However, it does not provide trial-level neural recordings or imaging that link the specific model variables to measured neural activity during the task.

- **Computational level**: The brain must translate abstract task requirements (specified verbally) into active behavioural controllers that persist and guide action. This is needed because environmental cues alone are insufficient to drive correct novel behaviour.
- **Algorithmic level**: A goal-competition process activates requirements selectively; activation is boosted by environmental prompts, prior experience, and low concurrent load. Failure to activate (goal neglect) results in the default action (continuation of the current behaviour stream) taking over. The abrupt, all-or-nothing nature of resolution suggests that once a requirement is successfully instantiated for the first time, a stable subgoal structure is constructed that supports ongoing control.
- **Implementational level**: Not directly addressed. Frontal lesion data implicate the frontal lobes as necessary for goal activation, and the paper speculatively points to DLPFC and anterior cingulate as candidate substrates (based on PET literature), but no cell-type, circuit-level, or neuromodulatory claims are made.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- Patient groups are small (N = 10 frontal, N = 8 posterior) and heterogeneous; lesion boundaries are characterised by CT/MRI but not mapped systematically.
- The letter monitoring task produces a near-binary (pass/fail) outcome with a heavily skewed distribution, restricting the range of the correlation with *g* and making parametric comparisons approximate.
- The mechanism by which a verbal instruction initially fails to activate a goal is unspecified; it is unclear whether the locus is encoding the instruction, maintaining it, or coupling it to action selection.
- Goal neglect was studied only for novel, first-occurrence requirements. Whether the same process underlies longer-term goal forgetting (e.g., after delays, after interleaved tasks) — as in prospective memory failures — is not addressed.
- The relationship to working memory is discussed but not disentangled: the *g* link could in principle run through working memory capacity (Kyllonen & Christal, 1990; Baddeley's central executive) rather than a separate goal-activation process.
- Instruction order effects in Experiment 3 (dot task) suggest a primacy effect in goal loading, but the mechanism for this within the competition framework is not formally modelled.
- It remains unclear which specific frontal subregions are critical — bilateral medial, inferior, dorsolateral? The patient lesion descriptions are too variable and insufficiently granular for region-specific inferences.

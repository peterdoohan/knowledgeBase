### One-line summary

Phosphorylation of Ser368 — but not Ser372 — by protein kinase C (PKC) is solely responsible for closing Cx43 gap junctional hemichannels to solute permeability, through a mechanism distinct from that underlying pH-mediated gating.

---

### Background & motivation

Connexin 43 (Cx43) is a widely expressed gap junction protein whose permeability to hydrophilic solutes is regulated by PKC-mediated phosphorylation. While prior work on intact gap junctions had implicated Ser368 as a PKC target, it was unclear whether Ser372 co-phosphorylation was also required, and whether the same CT domain substructures (particularly the proline-rich region) used for pH gating also mediated PKC regulation. Additionally, very little was known about PKC regulation specifically at the hemichannel level — undocked, plasma-membrane-resident connexons — which have distinct physiological and pathophysiological significance (e.g., activation during ATP depletion causing cell injury). This paper aimed to resolve both the identity of the critical phosphorylation site(s) and the structural basis of hemichannel regulation by PKC.

---

### Methods

- **Expression system**: Wild-type and mutant rat Cx43 cRNAs were expressed in *Xenopus laevis* oocytes using the pOcyt7 vector; endogenous Cx38 expression was suppressed by antisense oligonucleotide injection.
- **Hemichannel activity assay**: Uptake of the hydrophilic fluorescent dye 5(6)-carboxyfluorescein (CF; Mr 376) into single oocytes was used as the functional readout; uptake of Mr 3,000 Texas red-dextran served as a negative control for non-specific permeation.
- **Pharmacology**: Three chemically distinct PKC inhibitors (calphostin C, chelerythrine, bisindolylmaleimide) were applied acutely; gap junction blockers (octanol, 18β-glycyrrhetinic acid) and purinergic receptor blockers (suramin, PPADS) were used to rule out alternative CF uptake routes.
- **Mutagenesis**: Site-directed mutagenesis generated Ser368Ala, Ser372Ala, and Ser368Ala/Ser372Ala single and double point mutants; carboxy-terminal (CT) deletion mutants (Cx43D1: residues 307–360 deleted; Cx43D2: additional deletion 253–270; Cx43D3: further deletion including the proline-rich region 271–287); a proline→alanine mutant (P277/280A); and a poly-glycine replacement of the proline-rich region (Cx43D2-RC).
- **Biochemistry**: PKC activity was measured by a peptide phosphorylation assay (PepTag kit). Plasma membrane Cx43 expression was assessed by membrane biotinylation and Western blot. Serine phosphorylation state of Cx43 was assessed by immunoprecipitation followed by anti-phosphoserine immunoblotting.
- **Statistics**: One-way ANOVA; data expressed as mean ± SE.

---

### Key findings

- PKC inhibitors (calphostin C, chelerythrine, bisindolylmaleimide) increased CF uptake 6–8-fold in oocytes expressing wild-type Cx43, but had no effect in uninjected or water-injected oocytes, confirming the effect is Cx43-specific.
- CF uptake was blocked by gap junction channel inhibitors (octanol, 18β-GA) but not by purinergic receptor blockers (suramin, PPADS), confirming permeation through Cx43 hemichannels.
- Calphostin C treatment for 40 min reduced total oocyte PKC activity from 3.8 to 0.95 pmol Pi·oocyte⁻¹·min⁻¹, confirming functional PKC inhibition.
- Anti-phosphoserine immunoblotting confirmed that calphostin C reduces Cx43 serine phosphorylation and that Ser368Ala abolishes this phosphoserine signal.
- Cx43-S368A (single point mutant, full-length and D1 background) was constitutively CF-permeable and unresponsive to PKC blockers, demonstrating Ser368 is necessary for PKC-mediated hemichannel closure.
- Cx43-S372A behaved identically to wild-type, demonstrating Ser372 plays no role in hemichannel regulation by PKC.
- CT domain deletions removing residues 253–270 and 288–359 (Cx43D1, Cx43D2) did not abolish PKC regulation; deletion of 55% of the CT domain still permitted normal PKC-dependent closure.
- Deletion of the proline-rich region (Cx43D3) produced constitutively high CF permeability, initially suggesting proline involvement, but: (a) Pro277/280Ala substitution, which abolishes pH gating, did not affect PKC regulation; (b) replacement of the proline-rich sequence with a structurally disordered poly-glycine segment (Cx43D2-RC) preserved PKC regulation. Therefore, the proline-rich region itself is not specifically required; the Cx43D3 result reflects the extent of the deletion rather than loss of the proline motif.
- Taken together, the molecular mechanism of PKC regulation (Ser368-dependent, proline-rich-region-independent) is distinct from that of pH regulation (proline-rich-region-dependent, ball-and-chain mechanism).

The results constrain mechanistic models of connexin gating: specifically, they argue against a ball-and-chain model for PKC-mediated closure (since the proline-rich CT region is dispensable) and instead point toward a direct conformational effect of Ser368 phosphorylation, though the precise structural mechanism remains to be determined.

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's introduction conveys the following baseline understanding: Cx43 forms gap junctions (pairs of connexons from adjacent cells) enabling cell-to-cell communication, as well as unpaired hemichannels in the plasma membrane. Phosphorylation of the Cx43 CT domain by PKC was known to reduce dye coupling in multiple cell types, and Lampe et al. (2000) had established that Ser368 phosphorylation was necessary for the reduction in gap junctional communication by PKC agonists, but whether Ser372 also needed to be phosphorylated was unresolved. For pH gating, a "ball-and-chain" mechanism had been proposed wherein the CT domain physically occludes the channel via interaction with the intracellular loop; this mechanism requires the proline-rich region of the CT domain. An implicit assumption was that PKC regulation might employ a similar CT-mediated mechanism. The existence of functional, plasma-membrane-resident Cx43 hemichannels had been demonstrated, and their activation by ATP depletion was linked to dephosphorylation, but the site-specific molecular basis had not been established for hemichannels specifically.

---

### What this paper contributes

The paper establishes three clear advances:

1. **Hemichannel-level PKC regulation**: Demonstrates unambiguously that PKC-mediated phosphorylation acts directly at the hemichannel level to maintain Cx43 in a low-permeability state, extending prior observations from gap junctions to the hemichannel system.

2. **Exclusive role of Ser368**: Resolves the uncertainty about Ser372 by showing it plays no role in hemichannel permeability regulation; Ser368 alone is sufficient and necessary.

3. **Mechanistic dissociation from pH gating**: Shows that the CT domain features required for PKC regulation differ fundamentally from those required for pH gating. The proline-rich region — essential for the ball-and-chain mechanism of acid gating — is dispensable for PKC regulation. This rules out a shared gating mechanism and implies that different molecular events underlie closure by acidification versus PKC phosphorylation. As a corollary, the paper also demonstrates that even extensive CT domain deletions (removing 55% of CT residues) do not eliminate PKC responsiveness, indicating the regulatory information is compact and localised around Ser368.

### Mechanistic insight

The paper does not meet the two-criterion bar for this section. It presents a mechanistic dissection of Cx43 regulation (identifying Ser368 as the critical PKC phosphorylation site and distinguishing the PKC mechanism from pH gating), but the experimental system is *Xenopus* oocytes expressing recombinant protein, not neural tissue. There are no neural recordings, imaging, lesion, or pharmacology data linking the Cx43 hemichannel gating mechanism to specific neural circuits or cellular processes in vivo. The contribution is at the molecular level — identifying which residue and domain are required for hemichannel closure by PKC — without connecting this to an algorithmic- or systems-level neural computation.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- The molecular mechanism by which Ser368 phosphorylation structurally occludes the hemichannel pore is not determined; additional studies are acknowledged as necessary.
- The *Xenopus* oocyte system, while well-controlled, may not fully recapitulate the regulatory environment of native mammalian cells (e.g., differences in accessory proteins, scaffolding, or co-expressed connexins).
- The paper focuses on solute permeability (dye uptake) rather than ionic conductance; given known dissociations between PKC effects on conductance versus permeability in gap junction channels, it remains possible that additional residues influence conductance independently.
- Lysis of oocytes expressing constitutively active Cx43-S368A mutants required altered culture conditions (elevated Ca²⁺), introducing a potential complication; the authors acknowledge they cannot fully explain the discrepancy with prior mammalian cell studies where constitutive hemichannel activity was better tolerated.
- The physiological context in which PKC-mediated hemichannel regulation is most important (specific cell types, pathophysiological triggers) remains to be characterised.
- Whether the findings fully extrapolate to intact gap junctional channels is inferred by analogy rather than directly tested for the full molecular dissection.

### One-line summary

This editorial introduces a special collection of papers arguing that genuine progress in psychiatry requires studying psychopathology as emergent from complex, multilevel systems rather than seeking monocausal explanations.

---

### Background & motivation

Despite a century of psychiatric research, no single cause has been identified for any major mental disorder, and the field increasingly recognises that psychopathology is massively multifactorial. Yet research methodology has remained stubbornly rooted in monocausal and reductionist frameworks. This collection argues that the tools and concepts from complexity science — developed across physics, ecology, and network science — provide a ready toolkit for psychiatry to adopt. The gap being addressed is the mismatch between the known complexity of mental disorders and the simplicity of dominant research paradigms.

---

### Methods

This is an editorial review introducing six inaugural papers in a special collection on complexity in mental health research. The synthesis is narrative rather than systematic.

- Scope: six empirical, theoretical, and methodological papers contributed to the BMC Medicine collection on complexity in mental health
- Topics covered: transdiagnostic risk and resilience, neurobiological mechanisms of resilience, network analysis of resilience factors in adolescents, psychological primitives and suicidality, computational models in psychotherapy, and complexity science principles applied to psychotherapy
- Synthesis method: narrative summary, drawing out cross-cutting themes across the six contributions

---

### Key findings

- A central theme across all six papers is that mental disorders arise from complex systems spanning biological, psychological, and social levels of analysis, intertwined in webs of dynamic interactions.
- Systems exhibit both multifinality (same causal agents, different outcomes) and equifinality (diverse causes, same disorder), making single-factor models fundamentally insufficient.
- McLaughlin et al. highlight transdiagnostic mediators (accelerated biological aging, emotional and social information processing) linking childhood trauma to diverse disorders.
- Ioannidis et al. frame resilience as state-space trajectories and attractor states, arguing for complexity science methods in psychiatric research.
- Fritz et al. find that resilience factors show weaker inter-relations following childhood adversity in early adolescence, suggesting disruption of positive feedback loops.
- Franklin argues that psychological phenomena (including suicidal thoughts) emerge from interactions among irreducible psychological primitives.
- Burger et al. propose computational models as formalised idiographic case conceptualisations useful for treatment selection and outcome prediction.
- Hayes and Andrews introduce complexity science concepts (attractor states, early warning signals, network destabilisation) as actionable frameworks for psychotherapy research.

---

### Computational framework

The editorial does not develop a single formal computational framework itself, but it champions complexity science as the overarching lens. Key concepts invoked include:

- **Attractor states**: stable configurations the mental health system gravitates toward; therapeutic change involves transitioning between attractors.
- **Network analysis**: representing symptoms, resilience factors, or psychological primitives as nodes with weighted edges to capture system structure.
- **Early warning signals**: statistical precursors (e.g., increased autocorrelation, variance) that a system is approaching a tipping point or phase transition.
- **State-space trajectories**: trajectories of an individual's mental health state over time, shaped by underlying dynamical structure.
- **Computational models of case conceptualisation**: formal idiographic models (e.g., differential equation or network models) representing a patient's system for clinical use.

The framework assumes that psychopathology is an emergent property of interacting components, not a direct readout of any single variable, and that the same formal tools from physics and ecology can be translated to psychiatric systems.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper's introduction frames the dominant 20th-century psychiatric paradigm as reductionist and monocausal: researchers sought single underlying causes of mental illness — whether in the Freudian unconscious, the genome, or dysfunctional neurobiology. The Human Genome Project and neuroimaging revolutions implicitly promised that identifying the biological substrate would explain disorder. The authors characterise this as a "monocausal framework" that persists in research design despite mounting evidence of complexity. The baseline assumption being challenged is that mental disorders have a discoverable single cause or biomarker that determines diagnosis and treatment.

---

### What this paper contributes

This editorial does not itself produce new data or models, but it performs a theoretical and agenda-setting function: it establishes the case that psychiatry must move from monocausal to systems-level thinking and that the tools to do so already exist in complexity science. The collection as a whole demonstrates that this shift is already underway. Key conceptual moves the editorial makes explicit are: (1) systems are nested and overlapping — "systems all the way down"; (2) understanding a component is insufficient without understanding its interactions within the system; (3) interdisciplinary collaboration across psychiatry, clinical psychology, and sociology is necessary. The editorial identifies attractor dynamics, network analysis, early warning signals, and computational idiographic modelling as the most promising methodological imports from complexity science.

### Brain regions & systems

Not applicable as a primary focus. The editorial operates at a conceptual and programmatic level. However, several papers it reviews engage neurobiology:

- Ioannidis et al. (reviewed) address neurobiological systems mediating resilience to childhood maltreatment — genetic, endocrinological, and social/psychological influences on neural change.
- McLaughlin et al. (reviewed) implicate accelerated biological aging and emotional/social information processing systems.
- The level of analysis in the editorial itself is systems-level and transdiagnostic, not anatomical.

---

### Mechanistic insight

This paper does not meet the bar for mechanistic insight. It is an editorial that reviews and synthesises six other papers; it presents no original algorithm, neural data, or empirical findings of its own. The closest it comes is advocating for attractor-state and network frameworks as mechanisms for psychopathology and therapeutic change, but these are not formalised or empirically validated within this paper. The cited papers (e.g., Burger et al., Hayes and Andrews) contain more developed mechanistic proposals, but those would need to be assessed separately.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- The paper is programmatic rather than empirical; it does not directly test whether complexity science frameworks improve clinical outcomes.
- The editorial does not address how to operationalise or validate attractor states and network destabilisation in individual clinical cases.
- It is unclear which specific complexity science tools are most tractable for psychiatric research given current data collection constraints.
- The call for interdisciplinary collaboration is not accompanied by concrete proposals for institutional or methodological integration.
- The paper raises but does not resolve how to handle the computational intractability of systems that are "all the way down" — i.e., when does the model of a system become as complex as the system itself?

### One-line summary

Neonatal excitotoxic lesions of the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) do not replicate the schizophrenia-relevant behavioral profile of neonatal ventral hippocampal lesions, but adult mPFC lesions in neonatally hippocampus-lesioned rats normalize their postpubertal hyperlocomotion, implicating mPFC intrinsic neurons as necessary effectors of hippocampal-damage-induced behavioral abnormalities.

---

### Background & motivation

The neonatal ventral hippocampal (VH) lesion rat is an established heuristic model of schizophrenia: animals develop postpubertal hyperdopaminergic behaviours including hyperresponsiveness to novelty, amphetamine, and NMDA antagonists (MK-801). Because schizophrenia involves both temporolimbic and prefrontal pathology, and because the hippocampus and mPFC are closely interconnected, it was unknown whether the mPFC mediates or replicates the VH lesion's effects. This study addresses two related questions: (1) does neonatal mPFC damage itself produce schizophrenia-relevant behavioral abnormalities comparable to neonatal VH damage? and (2) are intrinsic mPFC neurons necessary for the aberrant behaviors driven by prior neonatal VH damage?

---

### Methods

- **Species and surgery**: Male Sprague-Dawley rats; neonatal (postnatal day 7, PD7) excitotoxic lesions of the mPFC (prelimbic/infralimbic subregions, areas Cg1 ventral, Cg3, IL) using ibotenic acid (3 µg/0.3 µl); sham controls received artificial CSF.
- **Behavioral testing — neonatal mPFC lesion group**:
  - *Experiment 1*: Photocell locomotor monitoring at PD35 and PD56; spontaneous locomotion, saline, and amphetamine (1.5 mg/kg IP).
  - *Experiment 2*: Apomorphine-induced stereotypy (0.75 mg/kg SC) rated on a 5-point scale at PD35 and PD56.
  - *Experiment 3*: MK-801 (0.05, 0.1, 0.2 mg/kg SC)-induced hyperlocomotion at PD56.
- **In situ mRNA hybridization**: Striatal D1 and D2 receptor mRNA measured in neonatally mPFC-lesioned and sham rats (n=8 per group) using radiolabeled oligonucleotide and ribonucleotide probes; densitometric analysis of autoradiographic films.
- **Combined VH + mPFC lesion experiment**: A separate cohort received neonatal (PD7) VH lesions (ibotenic acid) or sham; at PD154 (adulthood), they received additional adult mPFC lesions or sham, yielding four groups. Locomotion tested 4 weeks later after novelty exposure, saline, and amphetamine (1.5 mg/kg SC).
- **Statistics**: Multi-way ANOVAs with Fischer PLSD post-hoc tests throughout.

---

### Key findings

- **Neonatal mPFC lesions — locomotion**: Lesioned rats showed *attenuated* spontaneous locomotion in response to novelty (significantly reduced at end of habituation period at PD35 and PD56) rather than the hyperactivity seen after neonatal VH lesions.
- **Neonatal mPFC lesions — amphetamine**: Locomotor response to amphetamine was *reduced* at PD56 (not at PD35) in mPFC-lesioned rats (age × lesion interaction F[1,18]=5.32, p=.03).
- **Neonatal mPFC lesions — MK-801**: Locomotion was generally attenuated in lesioned rats vs. sham (lesion effect F[1,41]=4.1, p<.05); lesioned rats were significantly less active after saline and after 0.2 mg/kg MK-801.
- **Neonatal mPFC lesions — apomorphine stereotypy**: At PD56, mPFC-lesioned rats showed ~25% more stereotypy than controls (p=.02); control rats showed an age-related decline in stereotypy (by 22%) that was absent in lesioned rats.
- **Striatal D1/D2 mRNA**: No significant differences in D1 or D2 receptor mRNA in any striatal subregion (including nucleus accumbens core and shell) between neonatal mPFC-lesioned and sham rats.
- **Combined VH + adult mPFC lesion**: Rats with neonatal VH lesions followed by sham mPFC lesion (in adulthood) showed hyperactivity to novelty and amphetamine. Additional adult mPFC lesion *normalized* both novelty-induced hyperlocomotion and amphetamine-induced hyperlocomotion (p=.005 vs. VH-lesion/mPFC-sham). The mPFC lesion alone did not alter amphetamine-induced locomotion in controls.

### Computational framework

Not applicable. This is a purely empirical lesion-pharmacology study. No explicit computational model is used. The results constrain circuit-level models of how prefrontal-hippocampal connectivity governs subcortical dopamine regulation, and are relevant to cortico-striatal gating models and neurodevelopmental theories of schizophrenia.

---

### Prevailing model of the system under study

The paper operates within a neurodevelopmental model of schizophrenia in which subtle maldevelopment of temporolimbic cortex (particularly the hippocampus) disrupts the normal maturation of prefrontal-subcortical circuits, leading to hyperdopaminergic states that emerge post-puberty. The neonatal VH lesion was already established as producing a delayed-onset hyperdopaminergic profile (hyperresponsiveness to novelty, amphetamine, and NMDA antagonists from puberty onward). PFC dysfunction was known to modulate subcortical dopamine — lesions of the adult mPFC could either increase or leave unchanged amphetamine-induced locomotion depending on lesion extent and location — but whether neonatal mPFC damage would replicate or cause the VH-lesion phenotype was unresolved. The implicit assumption was that hippocampal damage might exert its effect partly via aberrant development of mPFC neurons that project to subcortical dopamine target regions.

---

### What this paper contributes

The study establishes that neonatal mPFC damage (confined to prelimbic/infralimbic areas) does *not* reproduce the schizophrenia-relevant behavioral constellation caused by neonatal VH damage; instead, it causes opposite effects (locomotor attenuation) and only partially mimics adult mPFC lesion effects. This rules out a simple model in which any neonatal prefrontal or limbic lesion produces the same schizophrenia-like phenotype. More importantly, by demonstrating that adult mPFC lesions *reverse* the hyperlocomotor phenotype of neonatally VH-lesioned rats, the paper provides direct causal evidence that intrinsic mPFC neurons — which developed in an abnormal hippocampal connectivity context — are necessary effectors of the VH-lesion behavioral phenotype. This shifts the mechanistic account from subcortical dopamine receptor changes (D1/D2 mRNA are unchanged) toward a circuit-level explanation in which abnormally conditioned mPFC output drives hyperdopaminergic subcortical activity.

---

### Brain regions & systems

- **Ventral hippocampus (VH)** — site of neonatal excitotoxic lesion; established model of schizophrenia-relevant neurodevelopmental disruption; source of direct projections to mPFC.
- **Medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC; prelimbic/infralimbic, areas Cg3 and IL)** — primary site of investigation; receives direct hippocampal input; projects to nucleus accumbens and striatum; proposed locus through which neonatal hippocampal disruption is transduced into postpubertal hyperdopaminergic behaviors.
- **Striatum / nucleus accumbens (core and shell)** — target of mPFC efferents; site of D1 and D2 receptor mRNA measurement; implicated in locomotor and stereotypy outputs.
- **Dopaminergic system (subcortical)** — assessed functionally via drug challenges (amphetamine, apomorphine, MK-801) rather than directly measured; proposed downstream target of mPFC dysregulation.

---

### Mechanistic insight

The paper partially meets the bar. It proposes an algorithm (mPFC intrinsic neurons, conditioned by abnormal hippocampal input during development, drive aberrant subcortical dopamine regulation) and provides lesion data supporting that claim. However, it does not record neural activity, measure dopamine release directly, or identify specific cell types, projections, or biophysical mechanisms.

- **Computational**: The brain must regulate subcortical dopamine tone via cortical gating mechanisms to maintain appropriate motor and motivational responses; following hippocampal maldevelopment, this regulation is disrupted.
- **Algorithmic**: mPFC neurons that develop in the context of abnormal hippocampal afferent input acquire aberrant response properties; their efferent output to striatal/accumbal targets produces exaggerated dopaminergic responses to amphetamine and novelty. Removing these neurons (adult mPFC lesion) normalises the output.
- **Implementational**: Not addressed. No recordings, no pharmacological dissection of cell types, no identification of specific mPFC projection subtypes or neuromodulatory mechanisms. The increase in apomorphine-induced stereotypy without D1/D2 mRNA changes suggests a postsynaptic sensitivity change at a non-transcriptional level, but this is not mechanistically resolved.

---

### Limitations & open questions

- The neonatal mPFC lesion was relatively restricted (prelimbic/infralimbic only) and may allow functional sparing or recovery not possible with larger adult lesions, complicating direct comparison.
- Discrepancy with Flores et al. (1996b), who reported postpubertal hyperlocomotion and elevated D2 mRNA after neonatal mPFC lesion, is attributed to differences in lesion location (more dorsal vs. more ventral mPFC), but this is not directly tested.
- The mechanism by which mPFC neurons, conditioned by neonatal hippocampal deafferentation, produce hyperdopaminergic output is unknown — no recordings or neurochemical measurements from the mPFC itself.
- The study does not assess whether the normalising effect of adult mPFC lesion is permanent or reflects compensation.
- It is unknown which mPFC projection targets (VTA, nucleus accumbens, etc.) are the key effectors.
- Dopamine release and turnover were not measured directly; functional inferences rest entirely on behavioral pharmacology.
